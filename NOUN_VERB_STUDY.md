# Noun/Verb Polysemy in Phi: A Study

*Commissioned 2026-07: Phi uses the same word as noun and verb. Is the
usage sufficient? Does it make the language easier to use? Does it add
true utility or mere confusion? Analysis only; no changes made.*

## The census

Of 770 content words, **129 (17%) carry both noun and verb** in their
`pos` — by far the largest multi-POS class (adjective+noun is second
at 23). 154 words are verb-only, 310 noun-only. 127 of the 129 dual
entries demonstrate both uses in their grammatical notes. The two
living texts lean on the pattern constantly: `thole` (practice)
appears five times across the Metta Sutta and the fable, `laniru`
(journey) four, `lothea` (love) three, and ten more dual words at
least twice.

The manual never teaches it. No chapter states that Phi words can
serve as noun and verb, when they can, or how a listener tells. The
pattern is load-bearing and undocumented.

## What the pattern actually is

"Noun/verb polysemy" is not one phenomenon in the lexicon. At least
five different relations hide under the same mechanism:

| Relation | Examples | Predictability |
|---|---|---|
| action ↔ event/result noun | `hea` hear/hearing, `remo` think/thought, `pula` wish, `helui` change | high — this is ordinary nominalization |
| instrument ↔ to use it | `kiru` chisel, `toka` hammer, `mera` net, `kira` key | moderate — a known cross-linguistic pattern |
| role ↔ to act as one | `phao` parent, `telu` partner, `thema` guardian, `lumishe` mentor | moderate |
| phenomenon ↔ to occur | `pheralu` rain, `phirenu` snow, `neparu` cloud over, `silero` shine like a star | low — "to star" must be looked up |
| substance ↔ to work/apply it | `keluo` work metal, `tinoa` dye, `loesi` oil | low |

The learner's problem is not that these exist; every natural language
does some of this. The problem is that nothing signals *which*
relation a given word uses. `kiru` as a noun is the instrument, not
the act of chiseling. `hea` as a noun is the sense of hearing, not
the ear. Each pairing is correct, and each must be memorized.

## Syntax: the confusion is not where it looks

The fear with flexible word classes is that sentences become
unparseable. In Phi they mostly do not, because the grammar already
assigns roles by position:

- The verb is clause-final. In `mia melira hea.` the final position
  makes `hea` the verb and `melira` the object, whatever their
  dictionary flexibility.
- Slot 1 particles stand immediately before the verb, so any tense,
  aspect, modal, or negation marks verbhood explicitly.
- Noun equipment — plural `lo`, classifiers, quantifiers, deictics,
  prepositions — marks nounhood explicitly: `lo thole` can only be
  "practices."

This is worth saying plainly: **positional word class is native to
Phi's design, not borrowed trouble.** A language built on
modifier-first and announce-then-deliver already asks the listener to
hold role assignments open until the sentence resolves. Chapter 6
calls the sentence a joint construction project; flexible words are
that philosophy applied to the dictionary.

Two genuine ambiguities remain:

1. **Fragments.** `mia pula` parses as subject+verb ("I wish") and as
   possessor+noun ("my wish") — modifier-first makes the two shapes
   identical, and Phi's topic-drop culture makes fragments common. In
   full sentences position resolves it; in two-word utterances only
   context does.
2. **Compound/clause homographs.** The registered compound
   `remo kire` (thought-shape, a fixed view) is string-identical to
   the clause "thought shapes." The compound registry did not create
   this risk, but dual-POS words widen it.

Both are manageable; neither is the real cost.

## The real cost: unprincipled membership

The pattern's membership cannot be predicted, and the lexicon has
begun to contradict itself:

- **`whunei` (breath) lists a verb sense — "mia whunei." (I
  breathe.) — while `whunema` (breathe) was coined in the body batch
  precisely because `whunei` was judged noun-only.** The gap
  inventory records that reasoning. The language now has two ways to
  say breathe, and the dictionary disagrees with its own history.
- `thalo` (walk) is dual, but `wishe` (swim), `wapi` (fly), and
  `kati` (cut) are verb-only. A sampling of nineteen core verbs (eat,
  drink, give, take, fall, burn, find, choose...) found **all
  nineteen verb-only**: Phi currently has no "a cut," "a fall," "a
  gift of speech" — no action noun for most of its action vocabulary,
  except where an entry happens to list one.
- `kuma` (drum) is one word for the instrument and the act, but song
  and sing are two words (`melira`/`meliho`). Both choices are
  defensible; having both with no stated principle is the confusion.

So the honest answer to "utility or confusion?" is: **the syntax
delivers utility; the lexicon delivers confusion.** Sentences parse.
What fails is the learner's ability to generalize: they cannot know,
without a dictionary check, whether a verb has a noun sense, what
that noun sense means if so, and why a neighboring verb lacks one.
129 memorized facts is not a system; it is a habit the lexicon fell
into one entry at a time.

## What sufficiency would look like

The pattern earns its keep if membership becomes rule-governed. A
possible shape, offered for discussion:

1. **A general rule: every verb licenses its event/result noun.**
   "One `remo`" is a thought; "one `thalo`" is a walk; "one `kati`"
   is a cut. This is a grammar-level statement, like plural `lo` —
   taught once in the manual, true everywhere, no per-entry listing
   needed. It converts the largest class of dual entries (roughly
   half) from memorized facts into one rule, and it fills the
   missing action nouns of the core verbs for free.
2. **Everything else stays explicit and per-word.** Instrument, role,
   phenomenon, and substance conversions are real but idiosyncratic;
   they remain listed in `pos` and demonstrated in the entry, as now.
   The entry says what the noun (or verb) sense is, because it is not
   predictable.
3. **Contradictions get resolved.** `whunei` yields its verb sense to
   `whunema` (or the reverse); the handful of doublets gets audited
   the same way.
4. **The manual gains a section** — most naturally in ch13 (nouns) or
   ch15 (verbs) — teaching the rule and the listed exceptions, since
   the texts already use the pattern on every page.

The alternative — pruning to explicit listings only, no general rule
— keeps the dictionary honest but keeps the core-verb gap and leaves
129 entries as trivia. The alternative in the other direction — full
Toki Pona-style flexibility, any word in any role — fits positional
grammar but surrenders the semantic precision the classifier system
and the coin-vs-compose criterion work hard to protect.

## Verdict

Sufficient? No — not because the pattern is overused, but because it
is unstated. Easier to use? The syntax says yes; the dictionary says
only if you memorize which words play. True utility or mere
confusion? Both, currently, and they are separable: the utility lives
in the grammar and the confusion lives in the unprincipled membership
list. Rule the membership and the confusion goes; the 129 words stop
being exceptions and become the visible half of a system.

No changes accompany this study. The four numbered items above are a
proposal awaiting a ruling.
