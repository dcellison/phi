## An audit finding corrected by verification

The audit pass flagged "the truly fluent remain scarce in the studies sampled" as supported by neither meta-analysis, quoting Del Maschio's "Notwithstanding a sufficient variability in our sampled data, we did not find a moderating effect of L2 proficiency on the FLE." Verification against the stored full text found the paper's own limitations passage saying exactly what the chapter compresses: "variables measuring L2 experience were sub-optimally distributed, with some values being little represented (e.g., the vast majority of studies used late bilinguals with an intermediate level of proficiency)." The two passages coexist, sufficient variability for the moderator test beside a skew toward intermediate proficiency, and the chapter's clause stands unchanged. This is the fourth audit finding in the book's series corrected before application, after the Whorf pointer, the Ido percentage, and the Galante title: the verify-the-audit-first rule remains in force.

## Confirmed without change

Everything else verifies cleanly. Every number in the chapter is verbatim-supported: the 725 participants, the 18 and 44 percent, the crossing directions (40 against 18, 47 against 19), the switch dilemma's 81 and 80, the proficiency split of 20 against 32 percentage points, the odds ratio of 1.34 with its interval, the 72.8 percent heterogeneity, the prediction interval reaching 0.29, the Hedges's g of .22 and .28, the sixty Croatian and German bilinguals, the 123-word lexicon, and the six students and four classes. The three direct quotations are exact: the Keysar distance sentence, the Láadan lesson's sentence rule with the *wa* gloss, and Coluzzi's hedged conclusion. The footbridge description matches the paper in substance ("runaway train" for its "small on-coming train," not presented as a quote). The Dylman gloss "which Swedes grow up alongside" matches the paper's own account of English in Swedish schooling and media. The Čavar paraphrase "lived fully inside both their languages" matches the abstract's high proficiency, frequent use, and immersion in both cultures; a 2019 commentary arguing that null was underpowered exists and does not disturb the chapter's "genuinely open." The Elgin 1988 imprint verifies against the hosting site's own copyright page (Second Edition, March 1988, SF3, Madison, WI), with the evidence-morpheme alternatives confirmed from the hosted second-edition dictionary. The claim that the second-face reading is the book's and not Elgin's was checked against the complete 1999 essay and Lesson 1: neither contains it, so the attribution is honest. Coluzzi's study internals remain verified through two convergent secondary sources because the paper itself stayed unobtainable behind its paywall and an empty university-repository entry; the chapter already presents the study at pilot size, which is the size the secondary record supports. All ten reference entries otherwise match Crossref or the publisher and hosting records field by field, including the era-correct PLoS ONE and PLOS ONE casings.

## Method note

The pre-draft verification for this chapter was the series's heaviest, and it shows: the audit found no invented titles, no misattributed figures, and no rebuilt lists, the failure classes of chapters 8 and 9. What survived to the audit was wording-level drift, a halves where the split is uneven, a plural where the study had one language, a subject wider than the chapter's noun, and one citation-form inconsistency between an original and its hosted copy. The overturned finding adds its own lesson: when an audit and the pre-draft pins disagree, the stored full text settles the question in minutes, so the fetched sources should stay in the working directory until the audit closes.
