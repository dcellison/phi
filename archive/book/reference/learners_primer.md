# Phi Learner's Primer

A concise introduction to the Phi language for beginners.

---

## 1. Welcome to Phi

Phi is a constructed language designed for mindful communication. It's not meant to replace your native language, but to offer a different way of thinking—a practice, like meditation or yoga, that shapes how you relate to the world through speech.

### The Organizing Principle

One rule governs everything in Phi: **modifier-first**. Whatever describes, specifies, or frames something else comes *before* that thing. This applies everywhere:

- Adjectives come before nouns
- Objects come before verbs
- Context comes before conclusions

In English, you might say "I see the beautiful tree." In Phi, you'd say something closer to "I beautiful tree see." The describing word (`beautiful`) comes before what it describes (`tree`), and the action (`see`) comes last.

This isn't arbitrary. It creates a natural rhythm of "announce then deliver"—you establish context before reaching your conclusion. The verb, carrying the core action, arrives only after everything else is in place. This slows speech just enough to encourage reflection.

### What You'll Learn

This primer teaches you:
- How Phi sounds
- Essential vocabulary to get started
- How to build simple sentences
- The particle system that shapes meaning

By the end, you'll be able to form basic sentences and understand the structure that makes Phi work.

## 2. The Sounds of Phi

Phi uses a small, carefully chosen set of sounds. Every syllable follows a simple pattern, making the language easy to pronounce once you know the basics.

### The Five Vowels

Phi has five pure vowels, each pronounced clearly and consistently:

| Letter | Sound | Like... |
|--------|-------|---------|
| a | /ä/ | "a" in "father" or Spanish "padre" |
| e | /e̞/ | "e" in Spanish "mesa" (NOT English "bed") |
| i | /i/ | "ee" in "machine" |
| o | /o̞/ | "o" in Spanish "poco" (NOT English "go") |
| u | /u/ | "oo" in "flute" |

These are pure, tense vowels—hold each sound steady without sliding into another. English speakers should avoid diphthongizing: don't let "e" become "ay" or "o" become "oh".

### The Ten Consonants

| Letter | Sound | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| h | /h/ | as in "hat" |
| k | /k/ | as in "kit" |
| l | /l/ | as in "let" |
| m | /m/ | as in "met" |
| n | /n̪/ | as in "net" but dental—tongue touches upper teeth |
| p | /p/ | as in "pet" |
| r | /r/ | a light tap, like Spanish "pero" |
| s | /s/ | as in "set" |
| t | /t̪/ | as in "top" but dental—tongue touches upper teeth |
| w | /w/ | as in "wet" |

Note that `t` and `n` are dental—your tongue touches your upper teeth, not the ridge behind them.

### The Four Fricative Digraphs

These two-letter combinations represent single sounds:

| Digraph | Sound | Description |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| ph | /ɸ/ | a soft "f" made with both lips (like blowing out a candle) |
| th | /θ/ | as in "think" |
| sh | /ʃ/ | as in "ship" |
| wh | /ʍ/ | a breathy "w" (like "which" in some dialects) |

### The Vowel Hiatus Rule

Here's something different from English: in Phi, **every vowel is a separate syllable**. When two vowels appear next to each other, you pronounce both distinctly.

The word `mia` (I) has two syllables: `mi-a`, pronounced /ˈmi.ä/. Not "mya" as one syllable, but "mee-ah" as two.

This creates the flowing, musical quality of Phi speech. It also means words take a bit longer to say, which is intentional—it slows you down just enough to be present with your words.

**Examples:**
- `sorae` (sun) = /so̞.ˈrä.e̞/ = "so-RA-eh" (three syllables, stress on second)
- `phialu` (water) = /ɸi.ˈä.lu/ = "fee-AH-loo" (three syllables, stress on second)
- `welao` (good) = /we̞.ˈlä.o̞/ = "weh-LA-oh" (three syllables, stress on second)

## 3. Your First Words

Let's learn the essential vocabulary you need to start speaking Phi.

### Pronouns

Phi has three core pronouns. Notice they all share the `-ia` ending:

| Phi | Pronunciation | Meaning |
|-----|---------------|---------|
| mia | /ˈmi.ä/ | I, me |
| thia | /ˈθi.ä/ | you |
| shia | /ˈʃi.ä/ | they, them (singular, gender-neutral) |

There's no grammatical gender. `shia` refers to any single person whose identity isn't the speaker or listener.

### Core Verbs

These verbs cover basic actions:

| Phi | Pronunciation | Meaning |
|-----|---------------|---------|

## 4. Building Sentences

Now let's put words together. Remember the organizing principle: modifier-first, verb-last.

### Adjective + Noun

Adjectives come before the noun they describe:

| Phi | Literal | English |
|-----|---------|---------|
| welao melu | good friend | a good friend |
| phelora shiro | beautiful tree | a beautiful tree |
| whalo sorae | big sun | the big sun |
| thiku womu | small home | a small home |

### Simple Sentences: Subject-Object-Verb

The basic sentence order is **Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)**. The doer comes first, then what's affected, then the action:

| Phi | Literal | English |
|-----|---------|---------|
| mia wepu | I go | I go |
| mia shiro nila | I tree see | I see a tree |
| thia phialu hea | you water hear | you hear water |
| shia melu nila | they friend see | they see a friend |

### Combining Adjectives and SOV

Put it together—adjectives before nouns, everything before the verb:

| Phi | Literal | English |
|-----|---------|---------|
| mia phelora shiro nila | I beautiful tree see | I see a beautiful tree |
| thia welao melu nila | you good friend see | you see a good friend |
| shia whalo womu nila | they big home see | they see a big home |

### Intransitive Sentences

Some verbs don't need an object:

| Phi | Literal | English |
|-----|---------|---------|
| mia wepu | I go | I go |
| thia shua | you come | you come |
| shia haolu | they speak | they speak |

## 5. The Particle System

Particles are small words that modify meaning. Phi organizes them into three "slots," each with a different job.

### Slot 0: Sentence Frame

These particles come at the very beginning of a sentence and frame the entire utterance:

| Particle | Meaning | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| wa | question | turns statement into yes/no question |
| no | request | makes a gentle command or request |
| pi | politeness | adds courtesy and care |

**Examples:**

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia wepu | I go |
| wa mia wepu | Do I go? |
| no wepu | (Please) go |
| pi no wepu | Please go (politely) |

You can combine `pi` with `no` for polite requests: `pi no` = "please, would you..."

### Slot 1: Verb Modifiers

These particles come right before the verb and modify the action:

| Particle | Meaning | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| to | past | action happened before now |
| so | future | action will happen after now |

The present tense is unmarked—no particle needed.

**Examples:**

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia shiro nila | I see a tree (now) |
| mia shiro to nila | I saw a tree |
| mia shiro so nila | I will see a tree |

### Slot 2: Word Companions

These particles come right before the specific word they modify:

| Particle | Meaning | Effect |
|----------|---------|--------|
| lo | plural | marks more than one |
| ha | this | proximal (near speaker) |
| ra | that | distal (away from speaker) |

**Examples:**

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| melu | friend |
| lo melu | friends |
| ha shiro | this tree |
| ra womu | that home |

**In sentences:**

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia lo melu nila | I see friends |
| mia ha shiro nila | I see this tree |
| mia ra womu to nila | I saw that home |

## 6. Your First Conversations

Let's put everything together with complete examples.

### Simple Statements

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia wepu | I go |
| thia shua | You come |
| shia haolu | They speak |
| mia phelora shiro nila | I see a beautiful tree |
| thia welao melu nila | You see a good friend |

### Past and Future

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia to wepu | I went |
| thia so shua | You will come |
| shia to haolu | They spoke |
| mia phelora shiro to nila | I saw a beautiful tree |

### Questions

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| wa thia wepu | Do you go? / Are you going? |
| wa shia to shua | Did they come? |
| wa mia so haolu | Will I speak? |

### Polite Requests

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| no shua | Come |
| pi no shua | Please come |
| pi no haolu | Please speak |

### Using Plurals and Demonstratives

| Phi | English |
|-----|---------|
| mia lo shiro nila | I see trees |
| thia ha melu nila | You see this friend |
| shia ra womu to nila | They saw that home |
| mia lo phelora shiro nila | I see beautiful trees |

### A Simple Exchange

Here's what a basic conversation might look like:

**A:** `pi no shua`
*(Please come)*

**B:** `mia shua`
*(I come)*

**A:** `wa thia welao melu to nila`
*(Did you see a good friend?)*

**B:** `mia welao melu to nila`
*(I saw a good friend)*
