# Chapter 12: Verbs of Voice and Modality

## 12.2 The Passive Voice (`se`)

### 12.2.1 Shifting the Spotlight

In the theater of language, every sentence is a stage where different elements compete for the audience's attention. The choice of voice is like directing a spotlight—it determines which character stands center stage and which ones fade into the background. Active voice places the doer in the bright center: "The chef prepared the meal." Passive voice shifts that spotlight to the receiver: "The meal was prepared by the chef."

This shift is never merely decorative. When we choose passive voice, we're making a conscious decision about what deserves our listener's primary attention. Sometimes we want to emphasize the result rather than the process, the affected rather than the actor, the outcome rather than the cause.

Consider the difference between "The committee rejected the proposal" and "The proposal was rejected by the committee." The first frames the event as something the committee did—it's about the committee's action. The second frames it as something that happened to the proposal—it's about the proposal's fate. Same event, completely different emphasis.

This spotlight effect becomes even more powerful when we consider sentences where the agent is unknown, unimportant, or deliberately obscured. "The window was broken" focuses entirely on the state of the window, leaving the question of who broke it in the shadows. Sometimes this ambiguity is exactly what we want—it allows us to discuss effects without getting caught up in debates about causes.

### 12.2.2 The Sound of Receptivity (`se`)

Phi's passive voice particle `se` carries within its sound the essence of its function. The soft 's' that begins the particle suggests something gentle and receptive, like the sound of wind through leaves or water flowing around a stone. This is followed by the open, accepting vowel 'e'—a sound that feels like opening one's hands to receive rather than grasping to take.

When we transform an active sentence into passive voice with `se`, we're not just changing the grammar—we're altering the conceptual framework. The active sentence `mia hauno thiko` (I build the house) places the focus squarely on the builder and their action. But `hauno se thiko` (the house is built/gets built) shifts our attention entirely to the house itself and what happens to it.

This transformation is elegant in Phi because the passive particle `se` takes its place in Slot 1 alongside the tense and aspect particles we've already learned. So we might say `hauno to se thiko` (the house was built) or `hauno so se thiko` (the house will be built), seamlessly integrating voice with temporal information.

The particle `se` creates what we might call "receptive sentences"—sentences that emphasize receiving rather than doing, being affected rather than affecting, undergoing rather than undertaking. This opens up new possibilities for expressing experiences from the perspective of those who are acted upon rather than those who act.

### 12.2.3 When and Why to Choose Passive

The decision to use passive voice is never arbitrary—it reflects specific communicative goals and philosophical choices. In Phi, where every grammatical decision is meant to be conscious and deliberate, understanding when and why to choose `se` becomes important.

One of the most common reasons to choose passive voice is diplomatic communication. When we want to discuss problems or mistakes without directly assigning blame, passive voice provides a gentle way forward. `nusho se peli` (the mistake was made) allows us to acknowledge the error without pointing fingers, creating space for constructive dialogue rather than defensive responses.

Passive voice also serves us well when the agent is unknown or unimportant. `lothei se welaku` (the flowers were planted) focuses on the beautiful result without getting sidetracked by questions of who did the planting. Sometimes the outcome matters more than the process, and passive voice helps us maintain that focus.

Perhaps most importantly, passive voice allows us to center the experiences of those who are affected by actions rather than those who perform them. In discussions of social issues, for example, saying `nala se phelu` (people were harmed) keeps the focus on those who suffered rather than shifting attention to those who caused the harm.

This choice to foreground the affected rather than the actor reflects Phi's deeper values of compassion and attention to those who are often overlooked. By making passive voice an explicit, conscious choice through the particle `se`, Phi encourages speakers to consider whose perspective they're centering in their communication.

### 12.2.4 The Grammar of `se` in Context

Understanding how `se` functions within Phi's grammatical architecture helps us use it with precision. As a Slot 1 particle, `se` takes its place in the ordered sequence of verbal information, working seamlessly with the temporal particles we've already mastered.

The basic transformation is straightforward: where an active sentence places the actor in subject position, a passive sentence with `se` places the affected party in subject position. `mia shako thiko` (I destroy the building) becomes `shako se thiko` (the building is destroyed). The original subject can be omitted entirely, or it can be preserved using appropriate prepositions when the agent remains relevant.

When `se` combines with tense and aspect particles, it creates rich possibilities for temporal and perspective layering. `shako to se ki thiko` (the building was being destroyed) combines past tense, passive voice, and progressive aspect to create a complex temporal-perspective portrait. `shako so se si thiko` (the building will have been destroyed) projects completed passive action into the future.

The elegance of this system lies in its compositionality—each particle contributes its own layer of meaning without interfering with the others. A speaker can choose to mark tense, aspect, and voice in any combination, creating exactly the temporal and perspective profile they intend.

This approach ensures that passive voice in Phi is never accidental or unclear. Every use of `se` represents a conscious choice to foreground the affected rather than the actor, to emphasize reception rather than action, to create space for the experiences of those who undergo rather than those who undertake.
