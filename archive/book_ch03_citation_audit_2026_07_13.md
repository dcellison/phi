# Citation audit: *The invented century*

Audit date: 13 July 2026

## Scope and method

This audit covers every item in the reference list of `03_the_invented_century.md`, the claim for which each item is cited, and the chapter's prominent uncited historical, numerical, legal, and causal claims. I checked publisher or journal records where possible, followed the supplied or discoverable links, compared quoted language with the source text, and kept bibliographic identity separate from evidentiary support. A real book can still be asked to prove more than it says.

The chapter itself was not edited. Line numbers below refer to the version audited on 13 July 2026.

## Overall finding

The fourteen named works, essays, articles, and legal authorities are genuine. The final Duolingo note is not a usable citation: it names neither an author nor an article and gives the reader no stable way to find the item. Most author names and titles are sound. Several entries still need pinpoint pages and stable links; others lack DOIs, fuller publisher data, or a clear edition year.

The sources are mostly real, but the chapter asks them to carry too far. It often begins with a well-supported event and ends the paragraph with a rule about every invented language. Garvía studies Volapük, Esperanto, and Ido; Elgin reports the result of her own Láadan thought experiment; a federal court decided whether `LOGLAN` was generic for particular trademark purposes. Together, they still cannot establish that grammar almost never matters, a proprietor necessarily kills a language, no governance arrangement could have helped Láadan, or every language community follows one fixed funnel.

Before publication, correct the League of Nations account, the Loglan trademark holding, and the 2024 Toki Pona census percentage. The Láadan conclusion and the comparison of Esperanto speaker estimates also need attention. The chapter's broad thesis can remain, but it should be presented as the author's reading of selected histories rather than as a settled empirical result.

## Passage-by-passage audit

### Volapük, lines 3 to 13

#### Supported

The [1911 *Encyclopaedia Britannica* entry by Henry Sweet](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica/Volap%C3%BCk) says that 283 societies existed in 1889, students were estimated at more than a million, and the third congress met in Paris. It also says that everyone there, waiters included, spoke Volapük and that decline began in the year of its zenith. These are estimates reported by the movement's era, not an independent census, so the chapter is right to attribute them.

#### Partly supported

Sweet does not supply the chapter's periodical and textbook details. Other historical accounts give 25 periodicals and 316 textbooks in 25 languages, and Garvía may be the intended source, but the chapter should attach a page number or a direct historical source to those figures. "Textbooks in twenty-five languages" is credible; its present attribution is simply hard to audit.

#### Needs a source

Schleyer's sleepless divine instruction is a familiar part of the history, but neither the Britannica entry nor the bare Garvía citation tells the reader where it appears. Add a page reference.

#### Unsupported superlative

"The fastest-growing language project in history" has no stated measure, comparison set, or source. "One of the fastest early expansions achieved by a constructed language" would preserve the point without claiming an unmeasured record.

#### Overstated

The movement did collapse quickly, but "within a generation the speakers could be counted on fingers" is not supported and reads literally false. Volapük retained groups and publications, and it later underwent Arie de Jong's reform. Describe a very small remnant instead.

#### Causation exceeds evidence

Garvía's book genuinely documents Schleyer's conflict with the academy and his claimed authority. It also covers the reform disputes, defections, and movement split. It does not justify "Volapük died of its inventor" as a complete causal account. Linguistic complexity, disagreements over the project's purpose, organizational conflict, and competition from Esperanto also belong in the history.

#### Overstated transfer

Esperanto attracted former Volapükists and benefited from networks opened by the earlier movement, but "inherited Volapük's disillusioned infrastructure almost whole" needs direct evidence and is much stronger than the sources checked. Narrow it to people, clubs, and expectations that can be documented.

#### Authorial thesis presented as source conclusion

[Penn Press's description of Garvía's book](https://www.pennpress.org/9780812247107/esperanto-and-its-rivals/) confirms that it studies the social and political settings, leaders, organizations, and early choices that shaped Volapük, Esperanto, and Ido. It does not say that their wars were "never really fought over grammar" or establish a law for later art languages and engineered languages. Those sentences may state the chapter's thesis, but they should not be presented as Garvía's general finding without a pinpoint quotation.

### Esperanto and the League, lines 17 to 21

#### Supported

The [1905 Declaration of Boulogne](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Boulogne_Declaration) says that Zamenhof had declined personal rights and privileges, that Esperanto was no one's property, that it depended on no particular person, and that the *Fundamento* was its permanently binding basis. "Placed its future in collective hands" is a fair interpretation if the chapter also preserves the declaration's stabilizing limit: the community did not acquire authority to rewrite the *Fundamento*.

#### Supported but uncited

The congress figures are 688 at Boulogne in 1905 and 4,963 at Nuremberg in 1923. They appear in the congress table in [*Esperanto en perspektivo*](https://tekstaro.com/t?nomo=esperanto-en-perspektivo&sekcio=eep-parto-3) and in later Universal Esperanto Association records. Add a citation where the numbers occur.

#### Historically compressed

The League considered several Esperanto proposals from 1920 onward. Its Secretariat produced the favorable report [*Esperanto as an International Auxiliary Language*](https://openlibrary.org/books/OL7194639M/Esperanto_as_an_international_auxiliary_language.) in 1922, and the Third Assembly adopted the report as a League document while referring the school question for more discussion. Garvía, pages 162 to 163, describes the French delegate's role in moving the matter to the International Committee on Intellectual Cooperation, which recommended national languages instead. French opposition was persistent and consequential, but "used its Council veto to block recognition for three years running" conflates separate proceedings and overstates what was being recognized.

#### Supported with a need for pinpoints

Lins supports Nazi and Stalinist persecution, Hitler's antisemitic treatment of Esperanto, and the destruction of the Soviet movement. The murders of Adam, Zofia, and Lidia Zamenhof are well documented. The exact claim that Stalin's purges "swept up thousands of Soviet Esperantists" should receive a page reference and a definition of "swept up," or be recast as "many Soviet Esperantists were executed, imprisoned, or exiled." [Springer's record for the English volume](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1057/978-1-137-54917-4) confirms the work and its subject.

#### Supported but dated

[Lindstedt's 2006 article](https://researchportal.helsinki.fi/en/publications/native-esperanto-as-a-test-case-for-natural-language) says Esperanto had about 1,000 first-language speakers. It is a scholarly estimate, not a current census, so "the standing scholarly estimate" and "stable base" claim more than this article can establish in 2026.

#### Too broad

"The only invented language that can say so" depends on how "invented language" and "native speaker" are defined. "The only planned international auxiliary language with a documented multigenerational first-language population" is more defensible, provided a current comparative source is added.

#### Incomparable totals

[Nielsen's 2016 model](https://svendvnielsen.wordpress.com/2016/12/10/percountry-rates-of-esperanto-speakers/) estimates 62,983.9 people, with very wide intervals, by combining organization data with four national censuses. Nielsen defines a speaker as someone who would report Esperanto when authorities ask which languages they speak. [Wandel's 2015 paper](https://hrcak.srce.hr/en/138519) extrapolates from Facebook self-identification and estimates about 2 million Esperanto users in the internet community. The two results can illustrate uncertainty, but not a single "honest order of magnitude" because the populations, definitions, and methods differ.

#### Uncited evaluation

"Sustained across five generations," "by nothing but the idea," and "no rival anywhere" are rhetorical judgments. The movement has lasted long enough to span roughly five generations. Institutions and books sustained it too, along with family transmission, congresses, and material networks. Name those mechanisms and soften the exclusivity claim.

### Ido, lines 25 to 27

#### Institutional wording needs correction

The Delegation for the Adoption of an International Auxiliary Language was a private organization, not an intergovernmental or official academy. Its committee was nevertheless elected under the Delegation's rules, not simply self-appointed. The committee adopted Esperanto in principle subject to the Ido reforms, and Louis de Beaufront's concealed role in the proposal is well attested.

#### Figure needs a small correction

The often repeated estimate, traced to page 424 of *Esperanto en perspektivo*, is about 20 percent of movement leaders and 2 to 4 percent of ordinary Esperantists. The chapter's "three or four percent" drops the lower end. The book itself is real; [WorldCat's record](https://search.worldcat.org/de/title/esperanto-en-perspektivo-faktoj-kaj-analizoj-pri-la-internacia-lingvo/oclc/813666199) lists Universala Esperanto-Asocio and the Centro de Esploro kaj Dokumentado as publishers.

#### Interpretation needs labels

"The leaders had followed the better design," reform as Ido's only permitted activity, and each reform bleeding followers are not neutral findings established by the cited percentage. They can remain as the chapter's interpretation if sourced to a history of Ido and separated from the numerical claim.

#### Generalization not demonstrated

One prominent schism does not prove that schisms always recruit elites, communities always resist optimization, or any design-centered community is one proposal from splitting. Narrow the paragraph to what the Esperanto and Ido case suggests.

### Loglan and Lojban, line 31

#### Reference identity is sound

[Brown's 1960 *Scientific American* article](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/loglan/) exists at volume 202, issue 6, pages 53 to 63, DOI `10.1038/scientificamerican0660-53`. It supports Loglan's scientific purpose, not the later ownership dispute.

#### Ownership history needs its own source

A 1992 [Logical Language Group newsletter](https://mw.lojban.org/papri/me_lu_ju%27i_lobypli_li%27u_16_moi) records the group's position that Brown claimed copyright over the vocabulary, while Lojban rebuilt its materials for public use. That is useful primary evidence from one side of the dispute. Add a neutral history before stating the royalty and control account as settled fact.

#### Legal holding is overstated

In [*The Loglan Institute, Inc. v. The Logical Language Group, Inc.*, 962 F.2d 1038](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F2/962/1038/91957/), the Federal Circuit affirmed cancellation of the `LOGLAN` mark. It held that the relevant public used the word generically for a logical language, that a language is not itself goods or services under the Lanham Act, and that the generic name could not indicate the source of dictionaries and grammars. The court did not decide copyright ownership of Loglan words, award the language to the Lojban community, or establish that every name associated with a language is unregistrable for every class of goods or services.

#### Metaphor should not masquerade as law

"The language walked away from its owner" is a strong closing image, but it is the author's interpretation. The legal result was cancellation of a trademark registration.

### Láadan, lines 35 to 37

#### Core citation is accurate

[Elgin's 1999 essay](https://laadanlanguage.com/articles/articles-by-suzette/laadan-constructed-language/) sets out four hypotheses, a ten-year limit running from the novel's 1984 publication to 1994, and her conclusion that the fourth hypothesis was "proved false." The chapter accurately reports that conclusion. The 1982 construction date needs a separate source, since it does not appear in this essay.

#### Rhetoric distorts the result

Elgin says the offered language was neither welcomed and nurtured at the hoped-for level nor replaced by a better women's language. She does not say literally nobody learned, read, extended, or used Láadan. The continuing dictionaries, lessons, and community material on the same site make "Nobody came" misleading unless it is clearly framed as shorthand for the failed adoption hypothesis.

#### Causal diagnosis is unsourced

Elgin does not conclude that women failed to feel a need, experienced no linguistic problem, adopted words rather than grammars, or would have rejected the project under every governance arrangement. Those are plausible questions, not results of her experiment. "No amount of better governance would have fixed" the outcome is an untestable counterfactual.

#### Authority claim needs nuance

"Elgin shared authority" needs evidence. She released the experiment into public discussion and invited a response, but she also retained copyright and authorial control over her works. Specify which authority was shared.

### Toki Pona, lines 41 to 43

#### Identity needs clearer wording

Lang's own [biographical page](https://tokipona.org/lang) says that calling her a linguist can mislead readers into assuming university training. It describes her as a self-taught polyglot with professional work as a translator, conference interpreter, teacher, and publisher. "Canadian translator, polyglot, and language creator" is precise.

#### Origin is supportable, but the citation is misplaced

Lang confirmed the connection between a depressive period and Toki Pona in a [2021 Radio-Canada interview reproduced and translated on her site](https://tokipona.org/universal_language). The 2024 roundtable supports the personal art-project history and the "mapping my brain onto paper" wording. Cite each source for the fact it actually supplies.

#### Book metadata is sound

[The 2014 book record](https://books.google.com/books/about/Toki_Pona.html?id=5P0ZjwEACAAJ) gives Sonja Lang as author and publisher, 134 pages, ISBN `9780978292300`, and a preface that presents the language in a completed form. "Finished it" is defensible in that limited sense. "Fixed the language" is stronger than the source, especially given later dictionaries and community variation; "published a stable, teachable reference" is safer.

#### Public-domain status needs a separate 2026 citation

The 2014 bibliographic entry cannot by itself prove a 2026 rights change. Lang publicly dedicated the 2014 English edition to the public domain in April 2026, and [Wikisource records the CC0 status](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Author%3ASonja_Lang). Cite the declaration directly or through a stable archive rather than adding the claim to the 2014 entry without evidence.

#### Course figure is exact within the survey

The [2024 Toki Pona census](https://tokiponacensus.github.io/results2024/) says 1,997 people responded and 52 percent named jan Misali's *12 Days of Toki Pona* among the resources used at the start. "About half" is fair. It describes respondents to a self-selected community survey, not half of all people who have ever learned Toki Pona.

#### Pandemic causation lacks a source

The transcript and census contain pandemic-era learner accounts, but they do not establish that the pandemic caused Toki Pona's expansion. Either cite time-series evidence and community research or describe the online growth without assigning one cause.

#### ISO history is supportable

The official record shows rejected request `2007-011`, [rejected request `2017-035`](https://glottolog.org/resource/reference/id/557157), and [adopted request `2021-043`](https://glottolog.org/resource/reference/id/641359), which created `tok` on 20 January 2022. "On the third application" is therefore sound. The code is an identifier, not official judgment that Toki Pona is successful or socially established.

#### Latest percentage is wrong

The 2024 census says about 80 percent considered themselves to know Toki Pona, up from 74.2 percent in 2022. "A quarter" describes the older round; the latest figure is about a fifth. The same page reports 53 percent at least at self-described conversation level and says the distribution looks roughly normal. Conversation level is not the same as fluency.

#### Sampling limit is missing

The organizers explicitly note high turnover and difficulty estimating how many people learned Toki Pona from a single census. The sample comes from online community recruitment and allows beginners to answer. It cannot establish total community size, total speakers, or a stable fluent tail.

#### Community language has a source

Lang's [clarification page](https://tokipona.org/clarifying) says speakers prefer "community" to "movement." That supports the statement as the official project's position, not as proof that every speaker agrees.

#### Success story is too tidy

"One unambiguous modern success," "a warm and light-touch authority made forks pointless," and the four-factor causal account are not established by the cited talk or census. The sources show a small vocabulary and published books, along with online communities, an ISO code, and continuing use. They do not isolate which factors caused adoption or show that forks and disagreements were absent.

### High Valyrian, Klingon, and the funnel, line 47

#### High Valyrian enrollment is traceable

Alyssa Pereira's [12 April 2019 SFGATE report](https://www.sfgate.com/tv/article/1-2-million-Game-of-Thrones-fans-have-signed-up-13760730.php) says more than 1.2 million people had signed up for the Duolingo course. The figure came from a report David J. Peterson said he had seen; the same article says 800,000 were actively studying at that time. This is contemporary press evidence, not a public Duolingo dataset.

#### Conversational comparison is unsupported

The chapter offers no evidence for a "tiny" group able to converse in High Valyrian. Course sign-ups and active learners count different things from course completion, self-assessed proficiency, or spontaneous conversation. A community survey or a clearly attributed estimate is needed.

#### Klingon source is genuine but old

[Penguin Random House's record for Okrent's book](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/124272/in-the-land-of-invented-languages-by-arika-okrent/) confirms the author and 2009 publication. Okrent's estimate of roughly 20 to 30 Klingon speakers appears around page 273. Cite the page and reproduce her criterion exactly; "could hold an unscripted conversation" should not be presented as a direct definition unless the book uses it.

#### The funnel is unmeasured

Two figures from different communities, years, and definitions do not establish a universal conversion funnel, much less that "nothing in the tradition's record has ever beaten" it. The paragraph can say that these examples warn against equating exposure or enrollment with conversational use. A quantitative universal claim would require comparable longitudinal data across projects.

### Phi comparison and closing thesis, lines 49 to 51

The claims that Phi is finished and machine-checked are repo-internal facts rather than claims supported by the chapter's bibliography. So are the claims that it is given away, explicitly non-universal, and presented through a shelf of texts. Cite `canon.md`, the repository license, the relevant validator documentation, and the texts index if the book is intended to carry formal citations throughout.

"Nothing about it can be owned the way Brown owned Loglan" is legally too broad. A language system and the project's prose raise different ownership questions; so do its software, marks, and particular publications. State what Phi's license grants and what, if anything, remains protected.

The closing sentence is an effective thesis, but no cited sociological study establishes it as a general result. Introduce it as the conclusion this book draws from the cases, or support it with research on language adoption, communities of practice, voluntary associations, and planned-language movements.

## Sources that should be added

1. League of Nations Secretariat. 1922. *Esperanto as an International Auxiliary Language: Report of the General Secretariat of the League of Nations Adopted by the Third Assembly, 1922*. This anchors what the report said and what the Assembly adopted.
2. *Declaration of Boulogne*. 1905. Cite the adopted text for the ownership and *Fundamento* claims.
3. ISO 639-3 Registration Authority. Change requests `2007-011`, `2017-035`, and `2021-043`. These establish the two rejections and the 2022 adoption of `tok`.
4. Toki Pona Census. 2024. "Results of the 2024 Toki Pona census." This should replace the vague aggregate citation for every current percentage.
5. Pereira, Alyssa. 2019. "1.2 million 'Game of Thrones' fans have signed up to learn High Valyrian, created by Cal grad." *SFGATE*, 12 April. This replaces the uncitable Duolingo note.
6. A neutral history of the Loglan and Lojban split. Brown 1960 and the court opinion do not establish the vocabulary, royalty, and volunteer-breakaway narrative.
7. A stable copy of Lang's April 2026 public-domain declaration. The rights claim should not ride inside the 2014 book entry.
8. A source for Phi's licensing and governance statements, preferably the repository license and the exact sections of `canon.md` on authority and purpose.

## Suggested revision order

1. Correct the League chronology, court holding, census fraction, Ido percentage, and incomplete High Valyrian citation.
2. Separate measured facts from the chapter's interpretations in the Volapük, Láadan, Toki Pona, and funnel sections.
3. Recast the two Esperanto population estimates as method-specific results rather than a shared range.
4. Add pinpoint pages throughout. Begin with Garvía and Lins, then move to Lapenna, Lindstedt, and Okrent.
5. Replace universal claims with bounded conclusions unless a genuine comparative study is added.
6. Normalize the bibliography only after the prose settles, so each entry reflects the edition and page actually used.

## Bottom line

The chapter has a sound shelf of real sources and several memorable, well-supported scenes, but the prose repeatedly outruns that shelf. The argument can keep its force once the text marks, cleanly, where the record ends and this book begins to interpret it.
