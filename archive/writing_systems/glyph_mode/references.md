## Maya writing and visual reference

The preferred project description is **Maya-inspired**. The studio borrows a compositional principle from a real writing tradition whose signs carry their own languages and histories. It does not treat those signs as an anonymous supply of ancient-looking ornaments.

- Peter Mathews and Peter Biro, [A Brief Introduction to Maya Hieroglyphs](https://research.famsi.org/mdp/mdp_briefintro.html), explains the logo-syllabic system, CV syllabograms, roughly square glyph blocks, and ordinary left-to-right, top-to-bottom reading order.
- Christian Prager et al., [IDIOM: A Digital Research Environment for the Documentation and Study of Maya Hieroglyphic Texts and Language](https://academic.oup.com/book/58672/chapter/485388730), distinguishes signs, graphemes, and their many graphic realizations and treats the glyph block as a basic encoding unit.
- The Metropolitan Museum of Art's [Plate with the god Chahk](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/718242) offers a public-domain codex-style example in which painted line, hieroglyphic text, and image share one surface.
- The Metropolitan Museum of Art's [Vessel with water bird and hieroglyphic text](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/761272) provides another public-domain example with carved line and a compact inscription organized around a vessel.
- The Unicode Standard, [Chapter 6: Writing Systems and Punctuation](https://unicode.org/versions/Unicode17.0.0/core-spec/chapter-6/), supplies the working distinctions among syllabaries, abugidas, and featural syllabic systems.

Museum images and epigraphic drawings are references for block density, line movement, negative space, and variation. Identifiable Maya signs and iconography are not templates for Phi forms.
