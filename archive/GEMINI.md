# Gemini's Guide to the Phi Language

This document contains my complete understanding of the Phi language, based on the project's documentation. It will serve as my primary reference for all future tasks, ensuring my actions and contributions are consistent with the language's philosophy, grammar, and vocabulary.

## 1. Core Philosophy: The Five Pillars

Phi is an intentionally designed language built on a foundation of five core philosophical pillars. My primary directive is to ensure all contributions align with these values.

1.  **Solarpunk Values:** A vision of a sustainable, community-oriented, and optimistic future where technology and nature are in harmony. This favors concepts of cooperation, renewable energy (`phalowa`), and convivial tools (`wethau`).
2.  **Buddhist-Inspired Concepts:** A focus on mindfulness (`nosa`), compassion (`lothea`), and interconnectedness. This is embodied in the grammar, which encourages deliberate, non-reactive speech.
3.  **Art Nouveau Aesthetics:** A commitment to organic, flowing, and beautiful forms. This influences the phonology (sound system) and the compositional nature of the vocabulary, prioritizing elegance and natural growth.
4.  **Peace Linguistics Practices:** A conscious effort to build a grammar of nonviolence. This involves avoiding combat metaphors, framing requests cooperatively, and using structures that de-escalate conflict.
5.  **Pre-industrial Wisdom:** An appreciation for animacy, cyclical time, and a deep connection to the natural world. This is reflected in the classifier system and the use of natural prototypes for core concepts.

A key concept is **Transmutation**: the art of re-building an idea from another language from the ground up using Phi's concepts and structures, rather than direct, word-for-word translation.

## 2. Phonology: The Sound System

The sound system is designed for clarity, beauty, and cross-linguistic accessibility.

### 2.1. Inventories
- **Vowels (V):** 5 pure, unwavering vowels: `a` (/ä/ as in father), `e` (/e̞/ as in Spanish mesa), `i` (/i/ as in machine), `o` (/o̞/ as in Spanish poco), `u` (/u/ as in flute).
- **Consonants (C):** 10 simple consonants: `h, k, l, m, n, p, r, s, t, w`.
    - `t` and `n` are **dental** (tongue on back of teeth).
    - `r` can be a trill or a tap.
- **Fricative Digraphs (F):** 4 two-letter units representing single, breathy sounds. They are reserved for abstract/conceptual words.
    - `ph` (/ɸ/): Voiceless bilabial fricative (blowing out a candle). **Not /f/**.
    - `th` (/θ/): Voiceless dental fricative (as in **th**in).
    - `sh` (/ʃ/): Voiceless postalveolar fricative (as in **sh**e).
    - `wh` (/ʍ/): Voiceless 'w' (as in some dialects of **wh**ich).

### 2.2. Core Rules
- **Syllable Structure:** All syllables are open (end in a vowel) and have the structure `(C)CV` or `(C)CVV`. The onset is always a `C` or `F`.
- **The Golden Rule: Vowel Hiatus:** When two vowels appear together, they are **always** pronounced as two separate syllables. There are no diphthongs.
    - `hauno` -> `ha.u.no` (3 syllables)
    - `shei` -> `she.i` (2 syllables)
- **Three-Vowel Constraint:** A sequence of three or more vowels is forbidden.

## 3. Grammar: The Architecture of Mindful Speech

Phi's grammar is strictly **analytical** and **compositional**. Words do not change form. Meaning is derived from word order and particles.

### 3.1. The Verb-Final Foundation
The most important rule is that the **verb always comes last**. The default, unmarked word order is **Subject-Object-Verb (S-O-V)**. This structure encourages speakers to establish full context before stating an action, promoting mindfulness.

- `mathea phino nolea.` (The parent reads the book.)

### 3.2. The Three-Slot Particle System
Grammatical relationships are made explicit through a system of small, single-syllable particles that occupy one of three "slots."

#### Slot 0: Sentence Frame Particles
These appear at the very beginning of a sentence to declare the speaker's intention.
- `wa`: Marks a yes/no question.
- `no`: Marks a cooperative request (imperative).
- `lu`: Marks a real conditional ("if"). `lu whe` marks an unreal/counterfactual conditional.
- `su`: Marks a hope or wish (optative).
- `pi`: Marks the sentence with overall politeness and social mindfulness.

#### Slot 1: Verb Phrase Particles
These are placed immediately before the verb in a strict order to modify the action.
**Order:** `Tense` > `Aspect` > `Voice` > `Modality` > `VERB`

- **Tense:**
    - `to`: Past tense.
    - `so`: Future tense.
    - (Present tense is the unmarked default).
- **Aspect (Internal Texture):**
    - `si`: Imperfective (ongoing action, "-ing").
    - `ki`: Perfective (completed action, "have done").
    - `pa`: Inchoative (beginning of an action, "start to").
    - `te`: Cessative (end of an action, "stop").
- **Voice (Agency):**
    - `se`: Passive voice. Promotes the object to the subject. (`phino se nolea` - The book is read).
    - `ka`: Causative voice. The subject causes the object to act. (`mia thi ka nolea` - I make you read).
- **Modality (Certainty):**
    - `po`: Possibility ("can," "might").
    - `na`: Necessity ("must," "have to").
- **Negation:**
    - `ma`: Negates the verb. It is placed after other Slot 1 particles, directly before the verb.

#### Slot 2: Word-Level Particles
These are placed immediately before the word they modify.
- `lo`: Plural marker for nouns (`lo whelea` - friends).
- `pha`: Proximal deictic ("this"). `pha phino` - this book.
- `tha`: Distal deictic ("that"). `tha phino` - that book.
- `she`: Focus marker. Gently emphasizes a word.
- `mo`: Comparative marker for adjectives (`mo wela` - more beautiful).
- `mo she`: Superlative marker (`mo she wela` - most beautiful).
- `sho`: Quotative marker. Placed before a direct quote.
- **Honorifics:**
    - `sa`: Marks respect for a teacher/elder.
    - `ni`: Marks intimacy for a friend/family.
    - `le`: Marks respect for a community role.

### 3.3. Nouns and Their Modifiers
Noun phrases follow the same **Head-Final Principle**: all modifiers come before the noun.
- **Structure:** `[MODIFIER(s)] [NOUN]`
- **Adjectives:** `wela noshale` (a beautiful garden).
- **Possession (Genitive):** The possessor precedes the possessed. No special markers are needed.
    - `weola thola` (the community's story)
    - `mia phino` (my book)
- **Quantifiers:** Words like `theula` (all), `sheloi` (many), `mawha` (none) precede the noun.
- **Classifier System (Optional but Mindful):** A special noun is placed between a number and the noun being counted to acknowledge its intrinsic nature.
    - **Structure:** `[Number] [Classifier] [Noun]`
    - `himo`: For people (`tho himo whelea` - three friends).
    - `lipha`: For other living things.
    - `themo`: For inanimate objects.
    - `nophe`: For abstract concepts.

### 3.4. Complex Sentences
- **Coordination:** Uses conjunctions like `nela` (and), `thona` (but), `sola` (or).
- **Subordination:** Subordinate clauses (temporal, causal) generally precede the main clause.
- **Nominalization:** The postposition `nai` turns a verb or clause into a noun concept (`pholea nai` - creating).

## 4. Lexicon: A Living Web of Meaning
- **Compositional Transparency:** Complex words are built from simpler, core concepts. This makes the vocabulary interconnected and easier to learn. Example: `nima` (sleep) + `thola` (story) -> `nima thola` (a dream).
- **Sound as Symbol:** The phonetic qualities of words are intentionally chosen to reflect their meaning. Fricatives for abstract concepts, liquids for connection, etc. This creates a "sacred correspondence" between sound and meaning.
- **Lexical Principle: Avoid Derivational Morphology:** Each Phi word should be a unique, holistic concept. Avoid creating new words by systematically modifying existing ones (e.g., turning a noun into a verb by adding a syllable). While occasional shared roots can be noted in the rationale if they are philosophically significant, this is a rare exception. The primary method is to create a new, unique word for each concept.
