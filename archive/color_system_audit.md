# The color system audit (2026-07-06)

The survey behind the *color composes* ruling in `/canon.md`: the color vocabulary examined as a system for the first time, asking whether it suffices, whether it is logical, and whether it should grow.

## What the survey found

**The seven basics are exactly the perceptual universals.** Phi's coined palette is `nuko` (black), `whilo` (white), `rulo` (red), `liro` (green), `soriu` (yellow), `shilu` (blue), `mureli` (brown): precisely stages I through VI of the Berlin and Kay sequence, the cross-linguistic order in which natural languages acquire basic color terms. The four that come next in the sequence (gray, orange, purple, pink) are exactly the ones Phi lacks. The seven are the perceptual ground; everything past them is culturally negotiated territory, which is where an auxiliary language should stop coining.

**The internal design is sound.** Every color is anchored to a natural referent (night, snow and cloud, blood and fire, leaf, sun, sky and water, earth); the entries hedge culture-specific metaphors properly ("melancholy in some cultures, though this metaphor may not transfer"); the physics is right (`whilo` is all colors combined, `nuko` all colors absorbed); and the sound symbolism does real family work (`soriu` opens on the 'so' of `sorae` and says so; `nuko` shares 'nu' with the dark family). The support field exists: `welisha` (color itself, coined in the gap campaign), `loriphi` (rainbow), `tinoa` (dye), `kolai` (pigment), the three metals. The grammar already covers predication, comparison via `sheo`, and the brightness axis (`keru`, `nuelo`, `pholuo`).

**The system was asleep.** Zero occurrences of any color word in the manual's teaching, the primer, the pamphlets, or the ten texts; `welisha` appeared once, in the timbre compound. The same condition the time vocabulary was in before the time commission.

## The gaps

1. **No ruled route to further hues.** The route was already licensed by the nouns-describe rule (manual ch13 §5): a referent noun before `welisha` names the color it carries. But nothing blessed it or refused further coining, so nothing prevented a future session from minting gray, orange, purple, and pink as adjectives and forking the design.
2. **A missing carve**: `nuko` (black) and `nuelo` (dark) never cited each other, and their entries used the same example noun ('nuko shero' black night, 'nuelo shero' dark night). Hue versus illumination, implicit but unstated.
3. **The paleness axis was unruled.** `keru` covers bright and vivid, `nuelo` covers dark, but pale had no stated expression; descriptor stacking ('whilo shilu', pale blue) was a legal reading nothing blessed.
4. **Observation, not actioned: minimal-pair congestion.** Five of the seven sit at edit distance 1 from other adjectives (rulo~nulo, shilu~sholu, liro~liroa/kiro, nuko~puko/muko/tuko/nulo, whilo~weilo/whalo). They survived the opposition pass because colors had zero usage, which is circular: the pairs become live the moment texts use colors. Watch them as the palette enters the corpus; 'liro liru' (green fabric) is a genuine tongue-twister.

## The ruling

*Color composes* lives in `/canon.md`, settled 2026-07-06: the seven adjectives are the whole coined palette; further hues take `welisha` after the referent that carries them (`kerou welisha` gray, `thero welisha` orange, `horathe welisha` pink, `norawhi welisha` violet, all in the compound registry); brightness and paleness stack as descriptors do; hue adjectives beyond the seven are refused by design. The `nuko`/`nuelo` carve was completed in the same PR, and the teaching lives in manual ch13 §7 (the words of color).
