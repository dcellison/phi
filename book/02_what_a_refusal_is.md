# What a refusal is

Arrange to meet a friend in Phi and somewhere in the sentence the meeting time changes character. You cannot say 9:40. The numerals are all present and perfectly capable; what is missing is an hour for the forty to sit inside. The day is told by the sky instead. It passes through `horathe` (dawn), morning, midday, and afternoon, then evening, dusk, night, and `phoemu` (midnight). Ordinary prepositions place you among those eight stations: during the morning, until evening, in the night.

```
mia sui kelua theo.
1SG DUR morning read.
(I read during the morning.)
```

So the meeting is set for during the morning, and both of you know what that means: it means the morning. One of you may still arrive inconveniently early; Phi does not claim to cure personality. The appointment is with a stretch of sky rather than a coordinate on a dial, and whoever arrives first will wait without a number to resent. The language did not mislay the hour. It looked at the hour and said no.

That distinction is the subject of this chapter. Every language has gaps. Nobody decided that English should lack a word for the parents of your child's spouse, and English-speaking families have somehow continued to introduce one another. The gap is simply there. Phi's absences are different: each is a ruling, written in the language's canon with a date and a reason under the recurring phrase refused by design. A natural gap leaves no record. A Phi refusal comes with a paper trail, and the reader is welcome to follow it.

## The measurement family

The clock does not sit alone. Phi's papers ground its refusal in an essay whose title states the position outright, on the psychological violence of measurement. The ruler and the price tag stand beside it. Together, the three make one family of refusals with one set of grounds.

After the hour comes the unit. Core Phi omits the meter and mile, along with the kilogram and liter. Its canon closes the question flatly: unit words will not be coined. This costs precision and can be inconvenient; the design does not pretend otherwise. Extent is stated by comparison or by the world itself. The verb `masue` (measure) means gauging against a shared standard, and the dictionary's own example is a hand of cloth held against an arm rather than a number read off a tape. Distance works the same way:

```
ruela wi philo thalo nai.
path two day walk be.
(The path is a two-day walk.)
```

The path is not eight of anything. It is two days of walking, and the measurement carries information the kilometer leaves out: someone walks it, the path is walkable, and the traveler's legs will verify the figure. A kilometer is wonderfully precise and entirely indifferent to blisters. The Phi speaker gives up the survey's precision and gets back a distance measured through an encounter with it.

The third refusal completes the family, and readers of this book's opening pages have already seen it at work. The core lexicon holds no money or price. The same ruling covers wages as well as buying and selling. What changes hands is said with the hands' own verbs. `loa` means give and `howela` receive; `phowe` means share and `wisola` exchange. Beside them sit `loami` (gift) and `piru` (trader). The grammar leans mutual: the canon's own example is `lo mia wiso wisola.`, we exchange with each other. This is the refusal that met William Morris's boatman in the cold open. `wia` opened a quantity gap, but no payment word supplied its referent. Morris had already written Dick's guess; Phi keeps the missing expectation visible.

Exact counts remain available after the price tag leaves. An exchange uses them whenever a difference affects what someone gives or receives, and speakers can always agree on estimates. Exact economic records, like exact clock times, remain beside the Phi passage as source material whenever coordination or fairness depends on them. The family's claim is narrower and stranger than innumeracy: it concerns what the unmarked core makes ordinary. Giving is plain in Phi, while pricing takes effort. That asymmetry is the design.

## Reading the absences

The list goes on, and every entry keeps its reasoning attached. Phi has no universal gendered person classes. `miona` (person) and `tewema` (partner) do unmarked work, as does `phao` (parent). A person's exact self-description stays available in source material beside the Phi, and the canon keeps the ruling open to case-by-case review. When the language translated Article 1 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, "the spirit of brotherhood" arrived as sibling spirit. The kinship came through; the gender did not hitch a ride.

The lexicon also refuses the offices and tools of domination. It has no root for one person ruling another, and neither lord nor throne gets a neutral home. Binding captives is described as an act rather than stocked as an office. A quieter row of refusals sits nearby, among them hunt and generic bad. A thing may instead be harmful, broken, or unwell, each a diagnosis where bad supplies only a verdict. The blade is declined as a weapon-part, while the knife is left to get on with its work in the kitchen. Marriage as an institution is refused too.

Two refusals show the pattern at its sharpest because they face in opposite directions. The word `lumeo` (die) is the only word the language will ever have for dying. Phi does not soften it into passing away or loss, and it leaves no fog around the one certainty. The sentence shape that would say a person is their suffering, `mia kipona nai`, I am pain, is also refused. Pain in Phi may be felt, located, or carried, but it is never made the person's identity. One refusal declines a comfort that blurs, while the other declines a cruelty that sticks. Both keep the plain sentence easiest to reach.

A refusal, then, is not an absence at all. It is a stated position holding a place open, the way a vow is legible in a life precisely because of what the person keeps not doing. The dictionary can be read the ordinary way, for what things are called. It can also be read backward, entry by missing entry, and backward it reads as a description of its maker's commitments.

## The guardrails

A fair reader should ask the obvious question: what happens when someone needs the very thing a beautiful refusal leaves out? Languages are used by people in trouble, and elegance is worthless if it purchases their silence. Every refusal must therefore leave care and self-identification unobstructed. Consent, safety, testimony, and critique receive the same protection. The rulings above each carry their answer. The clock refusal admits exact time through source records whenever coordination, testimony, or safety depends on it. The gendered-class ruling keeps a person's own words available and reviewable. The measurement family keeps exact figures beside the text, quotable and untranslated, whenever they matter.

The strongest test on the shelf is a familiar custom among language builders. People who invent languages translate Tolkien's Ring Verse the way climbers attempt a famous face. It is something close to a rite of passage, though usually attempted indoors. Phi's shelf answers the custom with a page titled the Ring Verse, refused. The page takes the couplet word by word. Nearly all of it is ordinary vocabulary that the language holds without complaint. Ring composes as finger-circle; one and all are present, as are find, bring, and darkness. What fails is the offices. To rule has no root, and the nearest words, `kulo` (guide) and `theluo` (steward), would falsify the line by replacing power with care. Lord has no one set above; the throne finds only furniture for resting.

So the page does something better than translate. It names the acts beneath the offices:

```
ta thumai sorui theula miona kawhera.
one finger circle UNIV person coerce.
(One finger-ring coerces all people.)
```

```
ta thumai sorui mua nuelo theula miona tiwa.
one finger circle LOC dark UNIV person tie.
(One finger-ring ties all people in the darkness.)
```

`kawhera` (coerce) is an accusation where the original had a title. `tiwa` (tie) is a cord verb, bodily and uncomfortable, held deliberately apart from `nolami` (bond), the connection people form with one another; the Ring gets the rope, not the relationship. The menace of the verse survives entirely, and that is the point the page itself makes in one line: the refusal is narrower than silence. The Ring is present, its acts are audible, and no title makes them clean. Phi can testify about domination in full. What it declines is to keep a clean uniform in the closet for domination to wear.

That is what a refusal is, and why the word gap never fits. A gap can be filled by the first person who needs it. A refusal holds its ground and explains itself, carve by carve, in writing. Read this dictionary forward and it will tell you what things are called. Read it backward, refusal by refusal, and it will tell you what it loves: the unsliced day, the walkable distance, the gift that needs no price. The backward reading was written on purpose.
