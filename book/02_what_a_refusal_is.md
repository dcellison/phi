# What a refusal is

Arrange to meet a friend in Phi and somewhere in the sentence the meeting time changes character. You cannot say 9:40. Not because the numbers are missing, the numerals are all there, but because the language has no hour for the forty to sit inside. The day is told by the sky instead: `horathe` (dawn), then morning, midday, afternoon, evening, dusk, night, `phoemu` (midnight). Eight stations, and the ordinary prepositions place you among them, during the morning, until evening, in the night.

```
mia sui kelua theo.
1SG DUR morning read.
(I read during the morning.)
```

So the meeting is set for during the morning, and both of you know what that means: it means the morning. The appointment is with a stretch of sky, not a coordinate on a dial, and whichever of you arrives first will wait the way people waited for most of human history, without a number to resent. The language did not mislay the hour. It looked at the hour and said no.

That distinction is the subject of this chapter. Every language has gaps, and in a natural language a gap is an accident: nobody decided English should lack a word for the parents of your child's spouse; the gap is just there, unchosen. Phi's absences are not like that. Each one is a ruling, written down in the language's canon with a date and a reason, under a phrase that recurs there like a refrain: refused by design. A gap is silence. A refusal is a sentence with its own paper trail, and paper trails can be followed.

## The measurement family

The clock is the visible corner of a single larger design. The language's papers ground it in an essay whose title states the position outright, on the psychological violence of measurement, and the design retires three instruments together: the clock, the ruler, and the price tag. One family, one set of grounds, three refusals.

After the hour, the unit. Core Phi has no unit words at all, no meter, no mile, no kilogram, no liter, and its canon closes the question flatly: none will be coined. Extent is stated by comparison or by the world itself. The verb `masue` (measure) means gauging against a shared standard, and the dictionary's own example is a hand of cloth held against an arm, not a number read off a tape. Distance works the same way:

```
ruela wi philo thalo nai.
path two day walk be.
(The path is a two-day walk.)
```

The path is not eight of anything. It is two days of walking, and the measurement carries information the kilometer hides: that someone walks it, that it is walkable, that your legs are the instrument that will verify the figure. A speaker gives up the precision of the survey and gets back a world measured in encounters with it.

The third refusal completes the family, and readers of this book's opening pages have already seen it at work. Money, price, wage, buying, selling: the core lexicon holds none of it. What changes hands is said with the hands' own verbs, `loa` (give), `howela` (receive), `phowe` (share), `wisola` (exchange), with `loami` (gift) and `piru` (trader) beside them, and the grammar of exchange leans mutual: the canon's own example is `lo mia wiso wisola.`, we exchange with each other. This is the refusal that met William Morris's boatman in the cold open, where "How much?" could only come out as a count with no object. The scene worked because the dictionary had taken its position long before the novel arrived to test it.

The rulings are not naive about arithmetic. They say plainly that an exchange uses exact counts whenever the difference affects what someone gives or receives, that agreed estimates are always available, and that exact economic records, like exact clock times, stay preserved in source material beside the Phi passage whenever coordination or fairness depends on them. The family's claim is narrower and stranger than innumeracy: it is about what the unmarked core of a language makes ordinary. In Phi, giving is plain and pricing is effortful, and that asymmetry is the design.

## Reading the absences

The list goes on, and every entry keeps its reasoning attached. There are no universal gendered person classes: `miona` (person), `tewema` (partner), and `phao` (parent) do the unmarked work, a person's own exact self-description stays available in source material beside the Phi, and the canon holds the door open for case-by-case review rather than closing it. When the language translated Article 1 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, "the spirit of brotherhood" arrived as sibling spirit, the kinship kept, the gender simply never picked up.

There is no vocabulary of domination: no root for one person ruling another, no lord, no throne, no binding of captives. A short row of quieter refusals sits beside those: hunt; generic bad, where things are instead harmful, or broken, or unwell, each a diagnosis where bad is only a verdict; the blade as a weapon-part, since the knife lives in the kitchen; and marriage as an institution.

Two refusals show the pattern at its sharpest, because they face in opposite directions. The word `lumeo` (die) is the only word the language will ever have for dying, and it is never softened: no passing away, no loss, no fog around the one certainty. And the sentence shape that would say a person is their suffering, `mia kipona nai`, I am pain, is refused outright; pain in Phi is felt, located, carried, but never predicated of a person, because a person has never been their pain. One refusal declines a comfort that blurs, the other declines a cruelty that sticks, and both serve the same end: the plain sentence stays the easiest one to reach.

A refusal, then, is not an absence at all. It is a stated position holding a place open, the way a vow is legible in a life precisely because of what the person keeps not doing. The dictionary can be read the ordinary way, for what things are called. It can also be read backward, entry by missing entry, and backward it reads as a description of its maker's commitments.

## The guardrails

A fair reader should press the obvious worry: languages are used by people in trouble, and a dictionary of refusals could price its aesthetics in other people's silence. The design answers with a standing rule, stated in the language's protocol as a review test every refusal must survive: a refusal must never obstruct care, self-identification, consent, safety, testimony, or critique. The rulings above each carry their carve. The clock refusal ends with a sentence admitting exact time back in through source records whenever coordination, testimony, or safety depends on it. The gendered-class ruling keeps a person's own words for themselves available and reviewable. The measurement family keeps the exact figures beside the text, quotable and untranslated, whenever they matter.

The strongest test on the shelf is the oldest custom in language invention. People who build languages translate Tolkien's Ring Verse the way climbers attempt a famous face, and Phi's shelf answers the custom with a page titled the Ring Verse, refused. The page takes the couplet word by word, honestly. Nearly all of it turns out to be ordinary vocabulary the language holds without complaint: ring composes as finger-circle, and one, all, find, bring, darkness are all present and willing. What fails is the offices. To rule has no root, and the nearest words, `kulo` (guide) and `theluo` (steward), would falsify the line by replacing power with care. Lord has no one set above; the throne finds only furniture for resting.

So the page does something better than translate. It names the acts beneath the offices:

```
ta thumai sorui theula miona kawhera.
one finger circle UNIV person coerce.
(One finger-ring coerces all people.)
```

```
ta thumai sorui mua nuelo theula miona tiwa.
one finger circle LOC dark UNIV person tie.
(One finger-ring ties all people in the darkness.)
```

`kawhera` (coerce) is an accusation where the original had a title. `tiwa` (tie) is a cord verb, bodily and uncomfortable, held deliberately apart from `nolami` (bond), the connection people form with one another; the Ring gets the rope, not the relationship. The menace of the verse survives entirely, and that is the point the page itself makes in one line: the refusal is narrower than silence. The Ring is present, its acts are audible, and no title makes them clean. Phi can testify about domination in full. What it declines is to keep a clean uniform in the closet for domination to wear.

That is what a refusal is, and why the word gap never fits. A gap can be filled by the first person who needs it. A refusal holds its ground and explains itself, carve by carve, in writing. Read this dictionary forward and it will tell you what things are called. Read it backward, refusal by refusal, and it will tell you what it loves: the unsliced day, the walkable distance, the gift that needs no price. The backward reading was written on purpose.
