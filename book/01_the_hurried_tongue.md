# The hurried tongue

Take one sentence about the climate and write it twice. The first version reports: emissions rose again last year. The second version accuses: they are poisoning our future and calling it growth. Post both. By tomorrow morning the second has traveled measurably farther, and the size of its head start can be estimated from its wording alone.

That estimate is not a figure of speech. In 2017, a team at New York University analyzed 563,312 tweets about gun control, same-sex marriage, and climate change and found that each additional moral-emotional word in a message, the vocabulary of blame and outrage, raised its retweet rate by roughly twenty percent (Brady et al. 2017). One word, a fifth again as much reach. Two words, and the message is pulling away from its plainer twin. The effect held within ideological communities more than across them, which means the loaded sentence was not persuading anyone new. It was circulating faster among people who already agreed, getting louder without getting anywhere.

This chapter is about the environment that finding describes, because it is the environment any new practice of communication now enters, and because the case for a slow language begins with an accurate picture of a fast one. The picture has to be built from measurements, not from mood. Everyone feels that public conversation has become hurried and hot; feelings of decline are cheap, and every generation has had them. The measurements are recent, specific, and worth taking one at a time.

## What the feed selects for

Start with what spreads. The moral-emotional-word finding gives outrage a price advantage, and a second study locates the advantage more precisely. In 2021, researchers analyzed 2.7 million posts from the Facebook and Twitter accounts of news outlets and members of the American Congress, asking which features of a post best predicted its sharing. The answer was not emotion in general and not even negativity in general. It was out-group language: references to the other side. Posts about political opponents were shared about twice as often as posts about one's own side, each additional out-group term raised the odds of a share by 67 percent, and out-group language outpredicted every emotional measure the study tested (Rathje, Van Bavel and van der Linden 2021). The single most shareable thing an English sentence can currently do is point at an enemy.

There is a further tilt, and it concerns truth itself. The largest study of rumor spreading yet conducted followed about 126,000 rumor cascades on Twitter, carried by three million people, and measured how truth and falsehood travel. Falsehood diffused farther, faster, deeper, and more broadly than the truth in every category of information; a false story was 70 percent more likely to be retweeted, and the truth took about six times as long to reach fifteen hundred people (Vosoughi, Roy and Aral 2018). At the medium's top speeds, accuracy is at a structural disadvantage. Truth simply takes longer to move.

None of these findings claims the platforms invented the appetite. Gossip is old, and so is the sermon. What the platforms changed is the cost structure, and the clearest statement of that argument is Molly Crockett's: in person, expressing outrage is expensive, it risks confrontation and takes effort, while online the same expression is nearly free, its triggers arrive in an engineered stream, and every reaction returns a small counted reward (Crockett 2017). Her paper is a framework rather than a dataset, and this book flags it as one; the mechanism it proposes has the ring of truth and awaits its full accounting. But the selection data above are not hypothesis. Whatever we intend when we speak online, the machinery between speakers audibly prefers certain sentences, and it prefers the hot ones.

It is worth pausing on what this does and does not establish. Selection is not corruption. No study in this chapter shows that feeds are rewiring the people who scroll them, and the temptation to say so anyway is exactly the kind of inflation this book refuses. What the studies establish is narrower and, for our purposes, more useful: the dominant instruments of public speech reward speed and heat, reliably enough to measure. An instrument that rewards heat will get heat. The question of what the players become can stay open; the tilt of the field is documented.

## The speed of the medium

The pace itself is rising, and not only on any one platform. A 2019 study measured how long topics hold collective attention across very different media: Twitter hashtags, a century of book vocabulary in Google Books, decades of movie ticket sales, Wikipedia views, Reddit comments. The pattern repeated everywhere the data reached: topics rise faster, peak sooner, and fall away sooner than they used to. A hashtag that made Twitter's top fifty stayed there for 17.5 hours on average in 2013 and 11.9 hours by 2016. The mechanism the authors propose is almost embarrassingly simple: more content is produced, attention is finite, and each item exhausts its share sooner, so the whole culture's conversational metabolism runs hotter (Lorenz-Spreen et al. 2019).

So the field is tilted and the tilt is steepening. That the tilt lives in the medium's design, rather than in some general coarsening of people, is what a pair of accidental experiments shows directly.

The first came when Twitter, in 2017, doubled its character limit from 140 to 280. Here was a rare clean test of what compression does to conversation: the same platform, the same people, twice the room. A study of 358,242 tweet replies to American politicians before and after the change found that the longer limit led to less uncivil, more polite, and more constructive discussion, though the same data showed empathy and respectfulness declining across the period regardless (Jaidka, Zhou and Lelkes 2019). Given twice the space, people used some of it on courtesy and reasons. The finding cuts against the easy slogan that less is more, and it sharpens what a slow language should actually want: the problem is not length, it is compression, the pressure to strip a thought of its qualifications until only the hot core is left. Room for nuance turned out to be room people used.

The second experiment measured leaving. Economists paid a randomized group of Facebook users to deactivate their accounts for four weeks ending just after the 2018 American midterm elections, and tracked them against a control group (Allcott et al. 2020). Four weeks off the feed produced a small but real gain in self-reported wellbeing, about a tenth of a standard deviation, alongside more time with friends and family, less political polarization, and, tellingly, less Facebook use after the experiment ended and the payments stopped. People who had tasted four slow weeks bought some more of them at full price. The effect is modest, and the study's authors are careful about it; so is this book. A tenth of a standard deviation does not transform a life. It does establish, with a randomized design, that the ordinary dose of the fast medium costs something, and that people can feel the refund.

Around these measurements sits a broader cultural argument, and it should be cited as what it is: argument. Jenny Odell's How to Do Nothing and Cal Newport's Digital Minimalism gave the decade its vocabulary for the problem, the attention economy, the disciplined retreat from it, and both books are essays of persuasion rather than reports of findings (Odell 2019; Newport 2019). The book you are reading leans on them for names, not for numbers.

## The slow lineage

None of this is the first time a culture has noticed its own acceleration and organized against it. The response has a lineage, and Phi belongs to it, so the lineage deserves an accurate telling.

It begins, by most tellings, with food. In 1986 a McDonald's opened at the foot of the Spanish Steps in Rome, and a Piedmontese food writer named Carlo Petrini organized the protest that grew into Slow Food, its founding manifesto signed by delegates from fifteen countries in 1989. The movement's demand was never merely that people chew slower. It was a standard, good, clean, and fair, and a set of institutions to make the standard livable: convivia, markets, an ark of endangered foods, eventually a university. Slow Food understood from the start that you do not beat a fast system with willpower; you build a slow one next to it.

Carl Honoré's In Praise of Slowness gathered the movement's philosophy in 2004, and its two most durable sentences are both correctives. The goal is the tempo giusto, the right speed for the thing being done, not slowness as an absolute. And the movement is, in his words, not a Luddite attempt to return to a pre-industrial utopia; its members use the machines and refuse the tempo. Six years later three German writers published the Slow Media Manifesto, fourteen theses moving the argument from the plate to the page: slow media are made with the care of artisanal goods, they favor doing one thing at a time, they treat listening as important as speaking, and they are possible precisely because everything else has sped up, islands of deliberate slowness (Köhler, David and Blumtritt 2010). The same year brought the argument to the inbox: John Freeman's The Tyranny of E-mail closes with a manifesto for what he named slow communication, communication that is slower, more nuanced and, above all, more sociable, praising media with texture and intonation against the frictionless rush of the send button (Freeman 2009).

Set side by side, the entries in this lineage agree on three things. Slowness is chosen, not imposed by nostalgia. It is selective, an island, a meal, a practice, not a general retreat from modernity. And it is material: where the slow movements succeeded, they succeeded by building alternatives, kitchens and markets and publications, rather than by asking individuals to grip the fast tools more virtuously.

Now look at where each entry stops. Slow Food rebuilt the kitchen. The Slow Media Manifesto rebuilt the editorial calendar. Freeman asked for fewer, longer letters; Honoré asked for the right tempo in everything from medicine to music lessons. Every one of them treats language itself, the actual material of communication, as a given. The words are assumed to be neutral equipment, equally serviceable at any speed, and the slowness is applied around them: in when we publish, how often we check, how long we make the message. The lineage walks all the way up to the instrument and then asks the player to hold it differently.

## The instrument itself

There is another possibility, and the findings in this chapter point at it from three directions.

The diffusion studies found the advantage of heat in the words themselves: a sentence with shame in it outruns a sentence without. The character-limit study found civility responding to the shape of the writing space: change the form and the conduct changes with it. The deactivation study found that the cost of the fast medium is real but that the refund requires leaving, because within the medium there is nowhere slower to stand. Selection in the vocabulary, conduct following form, no interior escape: three findings that together describe a problem living not in the users but in the material of the medium itself.

The slow lineage already knows what to do with a problem like that; it did it to dinner. It has never done it to grammar. Not because the idea is absurd, but because languages arrive as inheritances, and nobody chooses their inheritance. A language, though, can be built. Its sound system can be tuned so that words cannot be rushed. Its sentences can be shaped so that a thought must be laid out before it is delivered. Its dictionary can decline, on purpose, to stock the words that fast systems run on. Whether any of that would do what the slow movements hope is a question this book will treat as open, with the same discipline it has used on every study above.

But the question itself can no longer be called idle. What would a language built against hurry even look like?
