# The hurried tongue

Take one sentence about the climate and write it twice. The first version reports: emissions rose again last year. The second accuses: they are poisoning our future and calling it growth. Put both on a feed before bed. By morning, if a large study of messages like these generalizes to yours, the second will have traveled farther. It acquired its head start before anyone decided whether it was wiser or even worth reading twice.

That expectation is measurable. In 2017, a team at New York University analyzed 563,312 tweets about gun control, same-sex marriage, and climate change. Each additional moral-emotional word in a message, a word that joins moral judgment to feeling, went with roughly twenty percent more retweets (Brady et al. 2017). A single such word went with roughly one-fifth more reach. Add another and the heated sentence was already pulling away from its plainer twin. The study was observational, so it recorded what spread without proving what made it spread. The association also held within ideological communities more than across them. The loaded sentence was running laps around its own neighborhood.

This chapter is about the environment that finding describes, because every new practice of communication has to enter it. The case for a language of mindful speech begins with an honest look at instruments that reward reaction before attention. Almost everyone feels that public conversation has become hurried and hot, but every generation has suspected that civilization ended shortly after its own childhood. Feelings of decline are cheap. Recent, specific measurements give us more to work with.

## What the feed selects for

Begin with what spreads. The moral-emotional-word finding gives outrage a price advantage, and a second study locates that advantage more precisely. In 2021, researchers analyzed 2.7 million posts from the Facebook and Twitter accounts of news outlets and members of the American Congress. They asked which features best predicted sharing. General emotion and negativity were both weaker predictors than out-group language: references to the other side. Posts about political opponents were shared about twice as often as posts about one's own side, and each additional out-group term went with 67 percent higher odds of a share. Out-group language outpredicted every emotional measure the researchers tested (Rathje, Van Bavel and van der Linden 2021). Within that corpus of American political accounts on two platforms, a sentence became especially shareable when it pointed across the room and said, in effect, "those people."

Truth had its own trouble keeping up. The largest study of rumor spreading yet conducted followed about 126,000 rumor cascades on Twitter, carried by three million people, and measured how truth and falsehood traveled. Falsehood went farther and moved faster than truth in every category of information. It also traveled deeper and more broadly. A false story was 70 percent more likely to be retweeted, while the truth took about six times as long to reach fifteen hundred people (Vosoughi, Roy and Aral 2018). Accuracy was present, but it had arrived after the room was already talking.

The platforms did not invent the appetite. Gossip is old, sermons are old, and nobody needed wireless internet to discover indignation. What changed was the cost structure. Molly Crockett puts the argument plainly: expressing outrage in person takes effort and risks confrontation; online, the same expression is nearly free, its triggers arrive in an engineered stream, and every reaction returns a small counted reward (Crockett 2017). A number in a red circle is a modest thing, though our thumbs have learned to take it seriously. Crockett's paper is a framework rather than a dataset, and it should be read as one. Its proposed mechanism has the ring of truth and still awaits a full accounting. The studies above supply observational data rather than a completed account of that mechanism. Whatever a speaker intends, the machinery between speakers has tastes, and it likes the hot sentences.

The tempting next step is to blame the feed for making people worse. The evidence in this chapter stops before that claim. None of these studies shows that scrolling rewires the people who scroll, and saying so would repeat the very inflation this book is trying to resist. What the studies establish is narrower and more useful here: the main instruments of public speech reward speed and heat, reliably enough for researchers to measure. We do not need a verdict on human nature to document that tilt.

## The speed of the medium

The pace itself is rising, and the pattern reaches beyond any single platform. A 2019 study measured how long topics held collective attention in Twitter hashtags and a century of book vocabulary in Google Books. It also followed decades of movie ticket sales, Wikipedia views, and Reddit comments. The systems differed, but most pointed the same way: topics rose faster, peaked sooner, and fell away sooner than they once had. A hashtag that reached Twitter's top fifty stayed there for 17.5 hours on average in 2013 and only 11.9 hours by 2016. The authors' proposed mechanism is almost embarrassingly simple. People produce more content, attention still gets only twenty-four hours in a day, and each item exhausts its share sooner (Lorenz-Spreen et al. 2019). We might call that a rising conversational metabolism. The metaphor belongs to this book; the measurements belong to them.

Two accidental experiments suggest that the growing tilt belongs partly to the medium's design rather than to some general coarsening of the people using it.

The first arrived when Twitter did something rare for an internet platform: it accidentally made a rather good experiment. In 2017 it doubled the character limit from 140 to 280. The change created a natural test of what compression does to conversation, with the same platform, twice the room, and a before and after to compare. Researchers studied 358,242 tweet replies to American politicians on either side of the change. The longer limit led to less uncivil, more polite, and more constructive discussion, although empathy and respectfulness declined across the whole period regardless of the limit (Jaidka, Zhou and Lelkes 2019). Given twice the space, people spent some of it on courtesy and reasons. They did not spend all of it that way, naturally; Twitter remained Twitter.

The result cuts against the easy slogan that less is more. A language built for attention need not want short messages for their own sake. The problem is compression: the pressure to peel away context and qualification until only the hot centre remains. Room for nuance turned out to be room that people used.

The second experiment measured what happened when people left. Economists paid a randomized group of Facebook users to deactivate their accounts for four weeks ending just after the 2018 American midterm elections, then compared them with a control group (Allcott et al. 2020). The four weeks away produced a small but real gain in self-reported wellbeing, about a tenth of a standard deviation. Participants also spent more time with friends and family, reported lower polarization on policy issues, and used Facebook less after the experiment ended and the payments stopped.

The effect was modest, and the authors treated it that way. A tenth of a standard deviation does not turn an election season into a monastery retreat. One platform in one country is not every feed everywhere. The experiment established something narrower: for these users, in that season, the ordinary dose cost them a little, and they could feel part of the refund.

Around these measurements sits a broader cultural argument. Jenny Odell's *How to Do Nothing* and Cal Newport's *Digital Minimalism* gave the decade much of its vocabulary for the attention economy and disciplined retreat from it (Odell 2019; Newport 2019). They are essays of persuasion rather than reports of findings. That is no insult. A book can help us name a problem without first randomizing its readers into a control group. This one leans on Odell and Newport for names, not numbers.

## A neighbouring slow lineage

People have noticed cultural acceleration before. In this telling, they first organized against it near lunch.

In 1986 a McDonald's opened at the foot of the Spanish Steps in Rome. Carlo Petrini, a Piedmontese food writer, organized the protest that grew into Slow Food, whose founding manifesto was signed by delegates from fifteen countries in 1989 (Slow Food, movement history). The demand was never merely that everyone chew more slowly, which is fortunate because chewing can carry only so much political theory. Over the next decades the movement set out its standard, "good, clean, and fair," then built institutions that made the standard livable: local convivia, markets, an ark of endangered foods, and eventually a university. Its lesson reaches beyond the plate. A fast system is rarely defeated by asking each person to display heroic willpower beside it. A slow alternative needs somewhere to sit down.

Carl Honoré's *In Praise of Slowness* gathered the movement's philosophy in 2004, and its two most durable ideas are correctives. The goal is *tempo giusto*, the right speed for the thing being done, rather than slowness as an absolute. The movement is also, in Honoré's words, no Luddite attempt to return to a pre-industrial utopia. The machines can stay; they simply do not get custody of the timetable.

Six years later, three German writers published the Slow Media Manifesto. Its fourteen theses carried the argument from plate to page: slow media are made with the care given to artisanal goods, favor one thing at a time, and treat listening as seriously as speaking. Their islands of deliberate slowness become possible precisely because everything around them has sped up (Köhler, David and Blumtritt 2010). John Freeman's *The Tyranny of E-mail* had reached the inbox the year before. His closing manifesto called for communication that is slower, more nuanced and, above all, more sociable, with texture and intonation restored against the frictionless rush of the send button (Freeman 2009). Anyone who has pressed send and immediately remembered the missing sentence knows the attraction.

Put these writers beside one another and the family resemblance is clear. They do not ask the whole modern world to stop. Slow Food makes room for a meal; slow media make islands; Freeman asks for fewer, longer letters. Their slowness is chosen and selective, and it has furniture. The movements that lasted built kitchens, markets, publications, and other places where a slower practice could become ordinary. They made the practice easier than self-discipline alone could manage.

Each project also stops in roughly the same place. Slow Food rebuilt the kitchen. The Slow Media Manifesto rebuilt the editorial calendar. Freeman asked for fewer and longer letters, while Honoré asked for the right tempo in activities from medicine to music lessons. All of them treated language itself, the material of communication, as given. The words received an alibi. They were assumed to work equally well at any speed, while slowness was applied around them through when we publish, how often we check, and how much room a message gets. The lineage walks all the way to the instrument and asks the player to hold it differently.

Phi begins elsewhere. Buddhist mindfulness returns attention to present experience, including the act of speaking while it is still happening. Right Speech brings truthfulness, kindness, and benefit into the choice of words; peace linguistics examines what those words do to relationship and power. The Slow Movement offers Phi useful company in resisting needless haste, especially through Honoré's *tempo giusto*. It does not supply the reason for the language.

## The instrument itself

The studies above did not set out to argue for Phi; the language is not hiding in their conclusions. Still, their findings point toward form and medium from several directions.

The diffusion studies found an advantage for heat in the wording itself: within their corpora, a sentence with shame in it outran a plainer twin. The character-limit study found civility responding to the size of the writing space, on that platform in that season. The deactivation study found a small refund that required leaving; it tested departure rather than any more attentive practice inside the platform. These are bounded findings, not pieces of a proof that grammar can rescue public life. That would be heroic arithmetic. They do, however, make one question reasonable: what happens when the material of communication is designed to keep source, intention, and relationship available for notice?

The Slow Movement knows something about protecting a practice from haste because it already did so with dinner. Grammar poses a harder problem: languages usually arrive as inheritances, and nobody chooses an inheritance. A language can be built, though. Its sound system can make breath and full vowels easy to notice. Its grammar can give evidence, relation, and intention audible places. Its dictionary can keep care, refusal, and repair near at hand. Whether those choices help anyone attend more closely remains an open question, and this book will keep it open. Design intentions are invitations to test, not results smuggled in ahead of the experiment.

What would a language built for mindful and compassionate speech look like?
