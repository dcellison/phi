# The invented century

In August 1889, in Paris, delegates from across the world sat through a full congress conducted in a language that had not existed a decade earlier. The language was Volapük, the invention of Johann Martin Schleyer, a German Catholic priest who said the project had come to him in a sleepless night as an instruction from God. By the counts kept in its own era, Volapük then had nearly three hundred clubs, two dozen periodicals, textbooks in twenty-five languages, and something near a million students (Encyclopaedia Britannica 1911; Garvía 2015). The Paris congress was its third and grandest, conducted entirely in Volapük, and the Britannica's retrospect preserved the detail everyone repeats because it deserves repeating: even the waiters spoke it. This was the high-water mark of the fastest-growing language project in history.

Five years later the movement was a ruin, and within a generation the speakers could be counted on fingers. Nothing about the language's grammar changed in those five years. What changed was who owned it.

Phi enters a tradition, and this chapter is that tradition's history told with the question a new language should ask of it: not which invented language was most beautiful, but why they died, and what the survivors did differently. The century of invented languages ran every experiment a designer could want, at full scale, with real people, and its results are unusually consistent. The failures were social. The grammar almost never mattered.

## The emperor of words

Volapük died of its inventor. At the Paris congress the movement's leading figures pressed Schleyer to hand real authority to an elected academy, which could then settle the reforms the language's explosive growth demanded. Schleyer refused; the language, he held, was his, and he would keep a veto over every word of it. The academy proceeded without him, he denounced it, and the movement tore in half. Reform projects multiplied, clubs defected or dissolved, and the million students evaporated; the era's own encyclopedia recorded that the year of the zenith began a decline even more rapid than the rise. The rump academy eventually produced a reformed successor language that history barely remembers, and Esperanto inherited Volapük's disillusioned infrastructure almost whole.

The autopsy has not changed in a century of retellings: a language can survive bad phonology, but it cannot survive a proprietor. Sociologist Roberto Garvía, whose study of this era is the closest thing the field has to a standard account, puts the finding at its most general: the wars between invented languages were never really fought over grammar. They were fought over who holds power in the language, and the projects died or lived by how they answered.

## The language that survived its own failure

The answer that lived came from Ludwik Zamenhof, an ophthalmologist raised in Bialystok, a city whose Polish, Russian, Yiddish, and German communities lived in real mutual hostility. He published Esperanto in 1887 with the opposite instinct to Schleyer's: almost immediately he renounced ownership, and at the movement's first world congress, at Boulogne-sur-Mer in 1905, the community adopted a founding document that froze the language's basis and placed its future in collective hands. Zamenhof wanted more than an auxiliary code; he spoke of an inner idea, the hope that a shared second language would soften the hatreds he grew up inside. Esperantists still call each other samideano, sharer of the idea.

For a generation it looked like the idea might scale. The congresses grew from under seven hundred participants at Boulogne to nearly five thousand at Nuremberg in 1923; the interwar movement pressed the new League of Nations to take the language up, and a favorable League report treated it seriously enough that France used its Council veto to block recognition for three years running, its delegate defending the international standing of French. The League's eventual gesture was small, and the moment passed. What came next was worse than indifference: the dictatorships understood the movement's internationalism perfectly. Hitler had named Esperanto in Mein Kampf as an instrument of Jewish world ambition, and all three of Zamenhof's children were murdered in the Holocaust; Stalin's purges swept up thousands of Soviet Esperantists. The standard history of this period carries the title the language earned: Dangerous Language (Lins 2016).

Esperanto never became what it set out to be. Its universal ambition put it in the path of national interest twice over, and after the war English took the role Esperanto had auditioned for. But the language did not die, and the way it did not die is the century's most instructive fact. The community that the inner idea built kept meeting, publishing, marrying, and raising children in the language: Esperanto today has a stable base of native speakers, about a thousand by the standing scholarly estimate (Lindstedt 2006), the only invented language that can say so. Estimates of total speakers span an honest order of magnitude, from roughly sixty thousand in the most conservative statistical model to about two million by the most generous extrapolation (Nielsen 2016; Wandel 2015), and the organized core, the membership of its world association, has always been small. Read as an auxlang, the numbers are a failure. Read as a voluntary community sustained across five generations by nothing but the idea, they have no rival anywhere in the tradition.

## The improvers

Esperanto's nearest brush with death came not from governments but from its own intellectuals. In 1907 a self-appointed committee in Paris, convened to select a definitive international language, chose instead a reformed Esperanto submitted anonymously under the name Ido, and the anonymous author turned out to be one of the movement's own senior figures. The betrayal drama mattered less than the arithmetic that followed: by the reckoning of the movement's own standard history, the schism took about a fifth of Esperanto's leading activists and only three or four percent of its ordinary members (Lapenna, Lins and Carlevaro 1974). The leaders had followed the better design; the speakers stayed with their community. Ido then spent its energies on the only activity its founding logic permitted, further improvement, and bled followers with each reform until its best minds left to build yet newer projects.

The pattern generalizes across the century: schisms recruit elites and repel communities, and a language optimized by committee attracts exactly the people who cannot stop optimizing. A community anchored in use shrugs off reform; a community anchored in design is one clever proposal away from splitting. It is the same lesson Volapük taught, seen from the other side: the question is never whether the language could be better, because it always could. The question is what a community does with that fact.

## The language you could not own

The property lesson returned, a century after Schleyer, in American courtrooms. James Cooke Brown's Loglan, the logical language a later chapter weighs as a scientific instrument, was also a possession, and Brown treated it as one: he asserted rights over the vocabulary, fell out with his own volunteers over royalties and control, and watched them do what communities do when the founder will not share. They rebuilt the entire lexicon from scratch and continued as Lojban. Brown's institute sued, and in 1992 a federal appeals court settled the question in the community's favor: the name of a language is a generic term, and no one can hold it as a trademark (Loglan Institute v. Logical Language Group 1992). Schleyer's mistake, run again under modern law, produced the modern result: the language walked away from its owner.

## The language nobody came to

The saddest entry in the century's ledger is the one designed with the most scholarly care. In 1982 the linguist and novelist Suzette Haden Elgin built Láadan, a language shaped around women's perceptions, and embedded it in her novel Native Tongue as a deliberate experiment with a deadline: if women found existing languages as inadequate as her hypothesis held, they would adopt Láadan or build something better within a decade. She published the verdict herself, with a scientist's plainness: the decade passed, neither happened, and the hypothesis was, in her words, proved false (Elgin 1999). The grammar was published, the dictionary existed, the novel found readers. Nobody came.

The autopsy here is different from Volapük's, and the difference is the point. Elgin shared authority, wrote the materials, and asked a real question. What Láadan lacked was a felt need: the audience it was built for did not experience their languages as broken, and a language offered as a remedy for an undiagnosed illness recruits no patients. Busy sympathizers adopt words, not grammars. It is the one failure in the century that no amount of better governance would have fixed, which makes it the one every values-built language, Phi included, has to sit with longest.

## The language of good

Against all of this stands the tradition's one unambiguous modern success, and it is instructively small. In 2001 a Canadian linguist named Sonja Lang, working through a depression, began building a language of about a hundred and twenty words as a private instrument for simplifying her own thought, "like mapping my brain onto paper," as she later put it (Lang 2024). Toki Pona spread quietly among hobbyists for a decade, and then Lang did the thing the century's ghosts kept whispering: she finished it. The official book, published in 2014, fixed the language in a form a learner could hold, complete, in two hands; in 2026 she went further and placed the book in the public domain. The community did the rest. A free video course by one YouTuber became the most common first resource, named by about half of all learners in the community's own 2024 census; chat servers gave the language a place to be spoken daily; a pandemic gave the world time to learn a lexicon that fits on one page. In 2022, on the third application, it received an ISO language code, a bureaucratic fact that is also a sentence in history: a hundred-word art project built for one person's peace of mind is now registered infrastructure.

The community's own censuses keep the success honest: each round draws about two thousand respondents, a quarter of whom say they do not yet know the language at all, and the rest spread across a bell curve of proficiency whose fluent tail is small. Toki Pona did not conquer anything. It also never tried: its materials describe a community, kulupu, and decline the word movement, and the language never claimed to be for everyone. Small scope made fluency reachable; a finished book made the target stable; a warm and light-touch authority made forks pointless; modest goals kept it out of everyone's crossfire. Every one of those choices reads as a negative image of some earlier grave.

## The funnel and the epitaph

One more set of numbers calibrates the whole century. When a television fantasy put an invented language in millions of ears, more than a million people started the High Valyrian course on Duolingo, and the people who can actually converse in it are a tiny group even by their own community's accounts. Klingon, the most famous invented language on earth, had twenty or thirty people who could hold an unscripted conversation, at Arika Okrent's estimate from inside the community (Okrent 2009). Curiosity outnumbers learners by orders of magnitude, learners outnumber speakers the same way again, and nothing in the tradition's record has ever beaten that funnel. Communities are not recruited. They condense, slowly, out of use.

Where does a language like Phi stand in this graveyard? It enters with choices that read like a close study of the graves. The artifact is finished and machine-checked rather than perpetually reformed. Authority is explicit and personal, but the design is given away, and nothing about it can be owned the way Brown owned Loglan. The universal ambition that put Esperanto in the path of governments is refused outright in Phi's founding documents, which claim a practice for whoever wants one and a world language for no one. And where Láadan arrived as a diagnosis its audience had not asked for, Phi arrives as a literature first, a shelf of finished texts a reader can weigh without committing to anything. None of that earns it a community; nothing earns anyone a community. The century only says what the price of one is.

If the hundred and forty years have a single epitaph, this book would phrase it the way the sociology keeps pointing: nobody adopts a language. People adopt a community, and the language comes along inside it.
