# The boatman

A man falls asleep in the London of 1890 and wakes in the same house, on the same river, some two centuries on. The novel is News from Nowhere, William Morris's picture of an England that has given up money, wages, and masters, and its narrator spends his first morning doing what any of us would do: trying to pay for things. A young waterman named Dick rows him across the Thames. At the landing-stage, Dick waits, and the narrator knows exactly why he waits, or thinks he does: "as if for the inevitable after-piece that follows the doing of a service to a fellow-citizen." In the Phi translation, that expectation arrives with its epistemology attached:

```
mia mena shia rena pheo naphe kamo thena hasi meno to ho remo.
1SG DECL.COMP 3SG REL POST help arrive thing wait DECL.COMP.CLOSE PST ASSUM think.
(For I took it as given that he was waiting for the thing that follows a service.)
```

Everything in a Phi sentence announces itself before it arrives. `mena` opens the report of the narrator's own thought and `meno` closes it; `rena` and `thena` bracket a relative clause, the thing that follows a service; and the main verb at the end, think, wears the pair `to ho`: past tense, then a particle whose only job is to admit that the knowledge is assumed. The old world's mistake is confessed in the grammar before the hand ever moves.

Then the hand moves. "So I put my hand into my waistcoat-pocket, and said, 'How much?' though still with the uncomfortable feeling that perhaps I was offering money to a gentleman."

Dick's answer is one of the book's quiet jokes. "He looked puzzled, and said, 'How much? I don't quite understand what you are asking about. Do you mean the tide? If so, it is close on the turn now.'"

The joke runs deeper than good manners. In Dick's England the question has lost its object, and he reaches for the only how-much his river still has: the water itself, rising.

A hundred and thirty-odd years after Morris, a small constructed language called Phi began translating his novel. Phi has no word for money. None for price, pay, buy, sell, or wage either, and the gap is not an oversight waiting for a coinage; it is a refusal, recorded in the language's design rulings beside its refusals of the counted hour and the measured mile. So when the translation reached the landing-stage, the narrator's question had exactly one shape available:

```
thelao mia muo wethalu manuwe to sepho. mia shola wia. sholo to haolu.
CONS 1SG INTO garment hand PST send. 1SG QUOT.COMP how many. QUOT.COMP.CLOSE PST speak.
(So I put my hand into my garment, and said, "How many?")
```

`wia.` is the asking word for counted things: how many. Phi puts a question word exactly where its answer will stand, so the sentence asks the world to be countable on the spot. The narrator asks it with his hand in his garment and nothing in the scene to count. And the boatman's reply needs no invention at all:

```
wia. mia rena thia haolu thena po ma shelomu.
how many. 1SG REL 2SG speak thing POT NEG understand.
("How many? I don't quite understand what you are asking about.")
```

Morris wrote that confusion in 1890. The grammar reproduces it unprompted. A translator usually labors to carry a joke across a language border; here the border produced the joke on its own, and the Phi text simply lets Dick continue as Morris has him continue, out to the tide, close on the turn.

This book is about the language that can do that. It is not a textbook; Phi's manual, primer, and texts exist, and this book points to them rather than repeating them. It is a discussion, of why one person built a language that refuses the price tag and the clock, of what the sciences of language and mind actually permit such a language to claim (less than you have heard, and the book will show its arithmetic), of what a person can honestly get from learning it now, and of what might happen if its words travel beyond the small community it was made for. Where the evidence is small, the book says small.

For now, one scene carries the whole argument. A designer wrote his values into a dictionary by leaving things out of it, and when the dictionary finally met the novel those values came from, the scene needed no adaptation. The language and the book had already agreed.
