# The boatman

A man falls asleep in the London of 1890 and wakes in the same house, on the same river, some two centuries on. This is a hazardous thing to do in a utopian novel, since breakfast is unlikely to follow the old rules. The novel is *News from Nowhere*, William Morris's picture of an England that has given up money, wages, and masters. Its narrator meets this enormous rearrangement in a recognizably modest way: he keeps trying to pay for things.

A young waterman named Dick rows him across the Thames. At the landing-stage, Dick waits, and the narrator knows exactly why he waits, or thinks he does: "as if for the inevitable after-piece that follows the doing of a service to a fellow-citizen." In the Phi translation, that expectation arrives with its epistemology attached:

```
mia mena shia rena pheo naphe kamo thena hasi meno to ho remo.
1SG DECL.COMP 3SG REL POST help arrive thing wait DECL.COMP.CLOSE PST ASSUM think.
(For I took it as given that he was waiting for the thing that follows a service.)
```

The Phi sentence tells on him before the story does. Everything that modifies or frames a thought announces itself before the thought arrives. `mena` opens the report of the narrator's own thinking and `meno` closes it. `rena` and `thena` hold the relative clause around the thing that follows a service. At the end comes the main verb, think. It carries `to ho`: past tense followed by a particle whose only job is to admit that the knowledge is assumed. By the time the narrator reaches his conclusion, the grammar has already entered a small objection.

Then the hand moves. "So I put my hand into my waistcoat-pocket, and said, 'How much?' though still with the uncomfortable feeling that perhaps I was offering money to a gentleman."

Dick's answer is one of the book's quiet jokes. "He looked puzzled, and said, 'How much? I don't quite understand what you are asking about. Do you mean the tide? If so, it is close on the turn now.'"

Dick is not making a philosophical point. He genuinely needs a noun. In his England the question has lost its object, and since he is standing beside the Thames, he reaches for the only how-much his river still has: the rising water itself. It is a sensible guess.

More than a century after Morris, a small constructed language called Phi began translating his novel. Phi has no word for money or price. The same refusal covers paying and wages, as well as buying and selling. The gap is not an oversight waiting for someone to mint a word, despite the tempting metaphor. It is a refusal recorded in the language's design rulings beside its refusals of the counted hour and the measured mile. When the translation reached the landing-stage, the narrator's question had exactly one shape available:

```
thelao mia muo wethalu manuwe to sepho. mia shola wia. sholo to haolu.
CONS 1SG INTO garment hand PST send. 1SG QUOT.COMP how many. QUOT.COMP.CLOSE PST speak.
(So I put my hand into my garment, and said, "How many?")
```

`wia.` asks for the number of counted things: how many. Phi puts a question word exactly where its answer will stand, so the sentence asks the world to become countable on the spot. The narrator asks with his hand in his garment and nothing in the scene to count. This gives arithmetic very little to work with. The boatman's reply needs no invention at all:

```
wia. mia rena thia haolu thena po ma shelomu.
how many. 1SG REL 2SG speak thing POT NEG understand.
("How many? I don't quite understand what you are asking about.")
```

Morris wrote that confusion in 1890. The grammar reproduces it unprompted. A translator usually has to coax a joke across a language border and hope it still looks healthy on arrival. Here the border produced the joke on its own. The Phi text can simply let Dick continue as Morris has him continue, out to the tide, close on the turn.

This book stays with the language behind that accident. It is not a textbook; Phi's manual, primer, and texts already do that work. Instead, it asks why one person built a language that refuses the price tag and the clock, and what the sciences of language and mind permit such a language to claim. The answer is less than popular accounts sometimes promise, and the book will show its working. It also asks what someone can honestly gain by learning Phi now, and what might happen if some of its words travel beyond the small community for which it was made. Where the evidence is small, the book says small.

For now, one scene is enough. A man thinks he knows why a boatman waits, and his grammar admits that he is guessing. His hand goes into his pocket anyway. Then the future looks at his perfectly ordinary question and, with considerable patience, offers him the tide. A designer wrote his values into a dictionary by leaving things out of it, and when that dictionary finally met the novel those values came from, the scene needed no adaptation. The language and the book had already agreed.
