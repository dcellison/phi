# The lens, not the cage

In a darkened lab, a volunteer watches three blue squares appear on a screen, one above, two below. One of the bottom squares is the same blue as the top one; the task is to say which, as fast as possible. That is the whole job. Squares, blueness, a button.

The volunteers came in two kinds. English speakers have one everyday word for the region of color in play: blue. Russian speakers do not. Russian has goluboy for the light blues and siniy for the dark ones, there is no plain everyday word that covers both, and a Russian speaker cannot name a blue without deciding which side of the line it falls on. Jonathan Winawer and colleagues ran the square task on both groups and timed them (Winawer et al. 2007).

The Russians were faster, but only in one specific situation: when the two candidate squares fell on opposite sides of their language's boundary, one goluboy, one siniy. English speakers showed no such boundary advantage anywhere. The difference was real, consistent, and on the order of a tenth of a second.

Then came the manipulation that makes this experiment the right place to begin. The researchers had people do the same task while holding something else in mind. When the load was spatial, a pattern to remember, the Russian advantage survived. When the load was verbal, a string of digits silently rehearsed, the advantage vanished. Occupy the inner voice, and Russian speakers sort blues like English speakers.

The most widely discussed replicable result in the study of language and thought comes with an off switch, and the off switch is the finding. Language was not sitting in the eye. It was being recruited, in the moment, as a tool, and when the tool was busy elsewhere the difference went away.

## What a real effect looks like

This chapter is the book's accounting. Phi was built on the idea that a language's structure matters to the people who use it, and a book about Phi owes its reader the current state of the evidence for that idea, stated plainly enough to be checked. The summary is uncomfortable for enthusiasts and for debunkers alike. The strong version of the idea is dead. A weak version is alive, real, and much smaller than the folklore.

The strong version, that language determines thought, that speakers of different languages inhabit different and mutually unreachable worlds, that a concept unnamed is a concept unthinkable, has been abandoned across the field's own reviews (Wolff and Holmes 2011). No serious finding supports it, and the famous anecdotes that seemed to support it are dismantled later in this chapter.

The weak version says a language's obligatory categories can bias attention, memory, and speeded judgment in bounded domains: color, space, time, number, source of knowledge. Its supporting results look like the Russian blues: measured in milliseconds or in memory-error rates, confined to a domain, often visible only while language is actively being recruited, and removable by something as light as a digit string. Terry Regier and Paul Kay, surveying the color work, titled the moderate position exactly: Whorf was half right (Regier and Kay 2009). The half that is right is a lens. Language can tilt a task while it is in your hand. The half that is wrong is the cage. Nothing in the replicated record locks a speaker in.

Two disciplines follow from that summary, and the book holds itself to both. State magnitudes: a tenth of a second is a tenth of a second, not a worldview. And report the corrections alongside the findings: even within color, the once-celebrated claim that the category effect lives mainly in one half of the visual field did not survive a ten-experiment replication series with 230 observers (Witzel and Gegenfurtner 2011). The field corrects itself, and a book that borrows its findings inherits its corrections.

## The sun on the table

In Pormpuraaw, an Aboriginal community on the west coast of Cape York, Lera Boroditsky and Alice Gaby handed people shuffled cards showing a man aging, a crocodile growing, a banana being eaten, and asked them to lay the cards out in order (Boroditsky and Gaby 2010). English speakers given this task arrange time left to right. Hebrew speakers tend to arrange it right to left, following their script. The Kuuk Thaayorre speakers of Pormpuraaw did something else: they laid time out east to west. Seated facing south, the cards ran left to right; facing north, right to left; facing east, toward the body. The sun's path, not the body's left and right, was the axis of time.

Kuuk Thaayorre is described in the spatial-language literature, and in the study's own framing, as a language that runs on cardinal directions: its speakers say the equivalent of north and southwest where English says left and behind, and stay oriented accordingly. The card result is striking because the task never mentions direction at all: a habit built for space shows up organizing time.

Here the honest reader slows down, because the authors did. The samples were small. And what the experiment shows is covariation: a community whose language runs on absolute directions also lays time out absolutely. Whether the language built the habit or rides on it is precisely what a card task cannot say.

That question, cause or passenger, is the subject of the sharpest exchange in the field's modern history. Stephen Levinson and colleagues had shown that speakers of absolute-direction languages, like Guugu Yimithirr in Australia and Tzeltal in Mexico, solve rotated-table puzzles in absolute coordinates, where English speakers solve them in body-relative ones. Peggy Li and Lila Gleitman then turned the tables: they showed that English speakers flip to absolute responding too, when the testing room gives them landmarks to anchor on, and argued the cross-cultural results reflect testing ecology rather than grammar (Li and Gleitman 2002). Levinson's group replied in the same journal, returning the tables, arguing from new data that the flip does not survive properly matched conditions (Levinson et al. 2002).

What the exchange settled is worth more than what it left open. Both sides agree the communities differ, durably, in which spatial frame they habitually use. The dispute is about whether language drives the habit or merely travels with it, and it has not been resolved. That is the mature state of the strongest non-color evidence in the field: a real difference, a live argument about its cause, and two sets of authors holding each other to their data in public. A book about a constructed language should want to be held the same way.

## Number is a technology

The Pirahã, a small Amazonian community, speak a language with no exact number words. Peter Gordon reported that Pirahã adults, asked to match arrays of objects, grew inexact beyond about three (Gordon 2004), and for a while the finding circulated in popular retellings as the strongest determinism story available: no words, no numbers, no thought, a formula that was never Gordon's own conclusion.

The better-controlled follow-up took the story apart and found something more interesting inside it (Frank et al. 2008). First, the Pirahã words that had been glossed as one and two turn out to behave like few and fewer: counting spools down from ten, speakers were still using the smallest word at six. The language does not have inexact words for exact numbers; it has no exact number words at all. Second, and decisively: when the arrays stayed in view, Pirahã adults matched large exact quantities without trouble. The failures appeared only when matching required memory, when the objects were hidden, or transformed, and the exact count had to be carried across time in the head.

The authors' conclusion gave the debate its current frame: number words are a cognitive technology. They do not create the perception of quantity, which the Pirahã plainly have. They are a storage format, a way to hold seventeen in mind after the seventeen things are out of sight, and a mind without the format does fine whenever the world keeps the count for it.

Phi's numerals are a technology chosen in the open, and they are best read with that finding in mind. Three digits, `mu ta wi`, zero, one, two, compose with named powers of three, and the composition stays audible in the word:

```
mia wi phoi ta shao torua phelu.
1SG two nine-group one three-group year hold.
(I hold twenty-one years.)
```

Two nines and a three: twenty-one. The technology is carried openly, and it is carried deliberately short. Exact Phi numbers run through 242 and stop. Beyond that line, by rule, an exact value stays outside the Phi passage in the source's own notation, quoted rather than absorbed. A speaker can count a market day, an age, a flock; a speaker cannot rattle off an invoice, and the language's rulings record the boundary as a decision, not a limit the system failed to clear. Whether one admires the decision or not, it is the right kind of claim: not that the grammar changes what a mind can perceive, but that a language chooses how much counting equipment to hand its speakers, because equipment is exactly what the Pirahã evidence shows number words to be.

## The graveyard

Every popular case for language-shapes-thought leans on a small set of famous stories. Most of them are dead, and a book that intends to be trusted has to bury them in public.

Hopi time. Benjamin Lee Whorf claimed the Hopi language contains no words or forms that refer to time as English knows it, and a generation of readers met the idea of linguistic relativity through that claim. Ekkehart Malotki answered it with a six-hundred-page grammar of Hopi temporal expression, tense, time words, ceremonial calendars, all of it (Malotki 1983). Whorf's Hopi came principally through a seven-year working relationship with one consultant, Ernest Naquayouma, a Hopi speaker then living in New York (McElgunn 2024), and however conscientious that collaboration was, the timeless Hopi of the essays did not survive the grammar written from inside the language.

Snow words. The claim that Inuit and Yupik languages hold some enormous fixed number of words for snow is an academic urban legend, and Geoffrey Pullum wrote its obituary (Pullum 1991): Franz Boas mentioned four lexically distinct snow roots, and retellings inflated the figure to fifty, then to hundreds, each citation borrowing authority from the last. The correction has since been corrected in turn: specialists note that Boas was illustrating word structure rather than issuing a count, and that these languages do hold substantial, culturally exact vocabularies of snow and ice (Krupnik and Müller-Wille 2010). What stays dead is the magic number. The story survives because it flatters the idea it illustrates, which is precisely why this book will not use stories that way.

Mandarin time. Lera Boroditsky reported in 2001 that Mandarin's vertical time metaphors lead Mandarin speakers to think about time vertically (Boroditsky 2001). Three published follow-up papers then challenged or failed to reproduce the result (Chen 2007; January and Kako 2007; Tse and Altarriba 2008), and Chen's corpus counts undercut the premise itself: Mandarin speakers use horizontal time metaphors more often than vertical ones. Later work with better, nonverbal tasks recovered a weaker cousin of the claim, that vertical layouts are more available to Mandarin speakers (Boroditsky, Fuhrman and McCormick 2011). The ledger, kept honestly: the famous version failed replication; a smaller one survives.

The gendered bridges. The single most quoted result in the popular Whorfian canon says German speakers, whose word for bridge is grammatically feminine, describe bridges as elegant and slender, while Spanish speakers, whose word is masculine, say strong and sturdy. The result comes from a chapter in an edited MIT Press volume (Boroditsky, Schmidt and Phillips 2003), published with limited methodological detail and never in a peer-reviewed journal, and a direct replication attempt using two methods failed outright, its authors concluding the original was an artifact or a fluke (Mickan, Schiefke and Stefanowitsch 2014). A systematic review of forty-three grammatical gender studies found the surviving support narrow, task-dependent, and mostly explicable without any Whorfian mechanism (Samuel, Cole and Eacott 2019). The most quotable finding in the genre is the least citable, and this book does not cite it as fact anywhere.

The savings-rate languages. In 2013 the economist Keith Chen reported that speakers of languages without obligatory future tense save more money, as if a grammar that says tomorrow-is-now builds thriftier citizens (Chen 2013). Controlling for the fact that related languages belong to related cultures weakened the association to nonsignificance under the strictest tests, though some specifications kept it, and the correction's coauthors include the original author (Roberts, Winters and Chen 2015). Cross-country correlations between grammar and behavior have earned a standing distrust: languages and cultures descend together, and the grammar is usually the passenger.

The pattern across the graveyard is uncomfortable and clarifying. The size of a claim's fame has been inversely related to its survival. What lived is the modest tier: milliseconds, memory weightings, habits of layout. What died is everything that would have made an easy book.

## The strongest counsel

The skeptics deserve their strongest arguments stated, not summarized from a distance.

Steven Pinker's case begins with the observation that thought cannot simply be inner speech: preverbal infants reason, aphasics reason, and every writer knows the experience of a sentence failing to match the thought it was built to carry, which requires the thought to exist apart from the sentence (Pinker 1994, ch. 3). His sharpest point, though, is about argumentative method. Whorfian writing, he argues, slides between a trivial truth, languages differ in what they oblige speakers to encode, and a dramatic claim, speakers of different languages live in different worlds, borrowing the certainty of the first to fund the thrill of the second. The slide is real, and the only defense against it is the one this chapter practices: keep the magnitudes attached to the claims.

John McWhorter's book-length version presses two further points (McWhorter 2014, chs. 1 and 2). First, inflation: the effects that replicate are tiny, and popularizers read them as worldviews anyway, milliseconds retold as metaphysics. Second, selective wonder: Bulgarian marks evidence grammatically and Greek does not, yet no one writes essays about the skeptical Bulgarian mind; if the inference embarrasses us in Europe, he asks, why do we accept it about languages further away? He also names the moral hazard: inferring how a people thinks from what their grammar marks is condescension, however admiring it sounds.

Evidentiality is the right place to test his point, because it is Phi's own territory and because the evidence runs both ways. Turkish obliges every past-tense report to declare itself: one verb form for what the speaker witnessed, another for hearsay. Sümeyra Tosun and colleagues found the obligation leaves a measurable mark: Turkish speakers remember firsthand-marked information more accurately than hearsay-marked, weight it differently, and late Turkish-English bilinguals carry the asymmetry into English, which has no such grammar (Tosun, Vaid and Geraci 2013). On the other side, Anna Papafragou and colleagues found that Korean children's nonlinguistic ability to track how they know things develops on its own schedule, unaccelerated by the evidential morphology they are busy acquiring (Papafragou et al. 2007), and the same research line concludes that the underlying concepts are universal, with grammar building on them rather than building them (Ünal and Papafragou 2018).

Put the two together and the result reads at its actual size: an obligatory grammatical category can tune a habit of memory. A habit is not a virtue, and McWhorter's Bulgarians remain exactly as credulous as their neighbors.

Phi's designers cannot even borrow the Turkish result, and the language's materials do not try. Phi's evidential particles are optional, and by explicit ruling an unmarked sentence claims no source at all. Nothing in the grammar obliges anyone to disclose how they know, which means the Turkish mechanism, obligation wearing a groove into memory, is unavailable by design. What an optional marker offers instead is a practice: a visible place in the sentence where a speaker may choose to say how they know, every time they speak. Phi keeps four of these markers: `hi` for what the speaker witnessed, `ke` for what they inferred, `ti` for what they were told, and `ho` for what they merely assume. In use, an evidential is one soft syllable set before the verb:

```
shia to ke wepu.
3SG PST INFER go.
(She left [I infer from evidence].)
```

Past marker, inference marker, verb: she left, and the sentence admits the speaker worked that out from evidence rather than watching it happen. The boatman's narrator in this book's opening pages does the same thing in the wild. His moneyed expectation arrives as `to ho remo`, past tense, assumption admitted, and only then the thinking, so that the sentence discloses its own epistemic weakness on the way in. Whether years of that choosing leave any measurable mark on a speaker is an open question. This book will keep calling it one.

## An untested instrument

Constructed languages have been proposed as the clean experiment for seventy years. The record of actually running it is short and instructive.

Loglan was built for the purpose. James Cooke Brown designed a predicate-logic language beginning in 1955 expressly to test whether its speakers would reason differently, and introduced the project to the public in Scientific American (Brown 1960). No controlled test of the question appears anywhere in the project's published record, in Loglan or in Lojban, the community fork that survived its founder's ownership claims; the community's own history records a small number of temporarily conversational speakers and failed attempts to fund research, never the experiment (Logical Language Group, project history). The fluent population the test required never assembled, and the ownership fights that produced the fork filled the years in between.

Láadan came with a deadline. Suzette Haden Elgin, a linguist, built her women's language in 1982 alongside the novel Native Tongue and gave the experiment ten years: if the language answered a real need, women would take it up or improve on it. Her own published verdict is unsparing (Elgin 1999): the ten years ran out, no adoption came, and the one hypothesis she could actually test had failed. Note what was and was not tested. Adoption failed, so the cognitive question, would fluent Láadan change its speakers, was never reached.

Toki Pona, a small philosophical language with a living international community, is surrounded by learner testimony about simplified thinking and gentled attention, and testimony is what it remains: no controlled study of the claim exists (Lang 2014; tokipona.org).

So the clean experiment has not been run by any of the projects that proposed it, and their failures rhyme: the instrument dies of loneliness before it can be read. Each of these languages lost its speakers before the cognition question could be reached, which suggests the bottleneck for such claims is not cognition but community, and that a language project that wants its question answered must first do the unglamorous thing: be worth using, for years, by people who are not thinking about hypotheses at all.

That is the position Phi occupies, and the position its own founding document chose before this book existed. What Phi may claim today is the modest frame this chapter has assembled: thinking for speaking, Dan Slobin's term for the way a grammar's obligations shape what a speaker monitors in the act of speaking (Slobin 1996). Phi's obligations were chosen with exactly that moment in mind. Relations are declared before content arrives. A question stands in the exact place its answer will occupy. There is one word for dying and no way to soften it, one optional slot for saying how you know. If these choices do anything, the effects will be small, domain-bound, and strongest in the practice itself, because that is the size and shape of every effect that has survived this chapter.

The language's protocol says it in one sentence, written into the design rules long before this accounting was assembled: the language's structures are "design intentions to test in use, not guarantees about what the language does to a speaker." Strong determinism is dead. Weak effects are real and small. The instruments built to test them have, so far, lost their speakers first. Those three facts fix the only honest position available to a new language, and Phi's founding document was already standing on it. The science, read to the end, says: stand exactly there.
