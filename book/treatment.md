# The Phi book: chapter treatment

This file is the working plan for a book about Phi written for general readers. Each chapter entry states the chapter's argument, the receipts it rests on (repository artifacts and published research), the scene it opens on, and the turn it ends on. The treatment exists so that weak chapters die cheaply, before they cost drafting time. Nothing here is final prose.

## What the book is

A discussion of the language: why it exists, what it refuses, what it can honestly do for a person now, and what could happen if it travels. It is not an instruction manual. The manual, the primer, and the short road already teach the language, and the book points to them rather than repeating them. Its nearest shelf neighbors are Arika Okrent's In the Land of Invented Languages, narrative nonfiction about invented languages and the people who make them, and Sonja Lang's Toki Pona: The Language of Good, a philosophy carried to general readers by a small language. The empty seat between them is a book that discusses one philosophical language honestly against the current science of language and mind. This book takes that seat.

## The reader

An intelligent generalist who has never built a language: the reader of popular linguistics, the solarpunk reader who has met the manifesto, the person who arrived at the site through one escaped word. The book assumes no Phi. Every Phi example is glossed, and every example does argument work rather than decoration.

## The spine

A language is a practice. Grammar compels nothing; practice cultivates. Every chapter states its claims at the size the evidence supports, and the book earns authority by claiming less than its genre does while showing more.

## Claims discipline

Strong linguistic determinism is dead, and the book says so early and plainly. The replicated language-and-thought effects are small, domain-specific, and task-dependent, and the book quotes magnitudes rather than gesturing at worldviews. Some claims are refused outright no matter how well they would sell: bilingual executive-function advantages, dementia delay, grammar compelling virtue, untranslatable therefore unthinkable. This refusal list binds the book the way the language's own refusals bind the lexicon. The protocol's sentence, design intentions to test in use, not guarantees, is the book's epistemic home.

## Working title

Leading candidate: A Language You Cannot Hurry, where hurry means attention discarded under pressure rather than quick speech. Alternate: Announce, Then Deliver. Decision open.

## Shape and length

A cold open, fourteen chapters in four parts, and a close. Chapters run 4,000 to 6,000 words; the whole lands between 70,000 and 90,000 words.

## The chapters

### Cold open: the boatman

A time traveler in William Morris's News from Nowhere reaches for his money to tip a boatman, and Phi's rendering of the scene produces the question `wia.` (how many) with nothing to count. Morris wrote the boatman's baffled reply, "Do you mean the tide?", in 1890; the grammar produces the same confusion unprompted. One scene, two pages, receipts before philosophy.

Receipts: texts/news_from_nowhere/chapter_02.md and its source witness; the wia entry; the exchange refusal in canon.

Opens on the hand going into the garment; ends on the sentence "the language and the book had already agreed."

### Part I: The case

### 1. The hurried tongue

The communicative environment the book's reader lives in rewards speed and outrage, and this is measured, not felt: moral-emotional words raise a message's diffusion by about twenty percent per word (Brady et al. 2017, PNAS), out-group animosity predicts sharing better than emotion itself (Rathje et al. 2021, PNAS), and when Twitter doubled its character limit, political replies became measurably more civil and constructive (Jaidka et al. 2019, Journal of Communication). A month off the feed buys a tenth of a standard deviation of wellbeing (Allcott et al. 2020, American Economic Review). The chapter sets this out, then draws its boundary: none of this shows that a language fixes anything. The Slow Movement supplies a useful neighbouring account of resisting cultural acceleration, with Honoré's *tempo giusto* keeping the right pace distinct from slowness as a virtue. Phi's centre lies in Buddhist mindfulness and Right Speech, extended by peace linguistics into attention to relationship and power. The chapter examines what grammar and vocabulary can make available to that practice without claiming to manufacture it.

Receipts: Brady et al. 2017; Rathje et al. 2021; Crockett 2017 (flagged as commentary, not data); Jaidka et al. 2019; Allcott et al. 2020; Honoré 2004; Slow Media Manifesto 2010; Freeman 2009; Odell 2019 and Newport 2019 as cultural vocabulary only; the Buddhist-concepts and peace-linguistics pillar chapters for Phi's own direction.

Opens on the twenty-percent finding made concrete in a single feed morning; ends on the question the book exists to take seriously: what would a language built for mindful and compassionate speech look like.

### 2. What a refusal is

Phi's most legible design act is refusal. No hour, no minute, no o'clock: the day is told by eight stations of the sun. No units of measure: extent is stated by comparison or by the world, a path is a two-day walk. No money words: what changes hands is said with the hands' own verbs. No gendered person-words, no vocabulary of domination, no eighth color adjective. A refusal is not a gap but a stated position, readable in a dictionary the way a vow is readable in a life. The chapter establishes that, then shows the guardrails that keep refusal from becoming silence: care, testimony, consent, safety, and critique remain expressible by explicit protocol rule, and the world the language refuses to normalize stays quotable through the source-material boundary.

Receipts: canon's refused-by-design rulings; the measurement-family audits in archive; short_road.md's day and exchange sections; manual ch13 words of the exchange and ch15 words of time; texts/ring_verse_refusal.md; the development protocol's semantic principles.

Opens on meeting a friend during the morning, not at 9:40; ends on reading a dictionary by what it refuses and learning what it loves.

### 3. The invented century

Phi enters a hundred-forty-year tradition, and the tradition's failures were social, not linguistic. Volapük claimed a million students in 1889 and was dead in five years because its inventor would not share authority. Esperanto plateaued as a world auxiliary and survived as the only voluntary conlang community with native speakers. Ido took a fifth of Esperanto's leadership and four percent of its speakers. Loglan's owner claimed the language as property and the community forked it and won in federal court. Láadan targeted a sympathetic values community and its creator published the verdict herself: the hypothesis was invalid, nobody came. Toki Pona, the living counterexample, grew from one finished book, one YouTube course, and a community that calls itself a community rather than a movement. The chapter places Phi in this lineage: a personal language that built a literature before seeking speakers, finished its artifact, and claims nothing an auxlang claims.

Receipts: Garvía 2015, Esperanto and Its Rivals; Okrent 2009; the Toki Pona censuses and the 2022 ISO 639-3 assignment; Elgin's 1999 retrospective essay; Loglan Institute v. Logical Language Group, 962 F.2d 1038; Duolingo enrollment figures against actual speaker counts.

Opens on the 1889 Paris congress conducted entirely in Volapük, five years before the language died; ends on the tradition's one reliable epitaph: nobody adopts a language, they adopt a community.

### Part II: The language as argument

### 4. Announce, then deliver

One structural rule with no exceptions: whatever modifies, specifies, or relates comes before what it affects. The chapter walks a reader through the chisel sentence and lets the rule generalize itself, then gives a plain account of what such a rule can and cannot do to a mind. The frame is Slobin's thinking for speaking: a grammar's obligations shape what a speaker monitors while producing language, and no more than that. Silent-gesture experiments show word order does not restructure how people represent events outside of speech (Goldin-Meadow et al. 2008, PNAS). What the rule provides is a rehearsal, performed in every sentence: state the relations first, then deliver the content. A rehearsal is not a rewiring, and the chapter never blurs the two.

Receipts: short_road.md's one-rule section; the manual's modifier-first chapters; Slobin 1996; Goldin-Meadow et al. 2008; Özçalışkan et al. 2016.

Opens on the chisel sentence read in order, word by word; ends on the sentence as a small ceremony of preparation that a speaker performs thousands of times.

### 5. The web

The lexicon is a designed ecology. Sound symbolism is taught as a teachable association and never as a universal truth, and the manual's own hedges are quoted to show the honesty is native, not retrofitted. Meanings compose: gray is `kerou welisha`, stone color; pink is `horathe welisha`, dawn color; east is `sileta thorui`, the sun's beginning. Words hold to three syllables by hard ceiling, the sound-space is audited for collisions with a purpose-built instrument, and every coinage passes a protocol that asks what the word costs before what it adds. The chapter shows what this buys, a lexicon where pulling one word teaches three more, and what it costs, memory load and the permanent temptation of shortcuts the language will not take.

Receipts: the kerou welisha and horathe welisha registry rows; the sileta and silero kinship ruling; manual ch05's sound-as-symbol hedges; scripts/audit_phonetic_neighbors.py; the word-creation protocol; documents/reference/compounds.md.

Opens on a learner pulling one word and getting three; ends on the discovery that east is the sun's beginning.

### 6. What it will not say

The refusal architecture's hard cases, taken one at a time. How the language analyzes violence and domination without normalizing them. How testimony survives: foreign wording, exact records, scripts, and identifiers stay visibly outside the Phi passage, preserved rather than assimilated, and the chapter argues this is a boundary of language rather than a euphemism. How names work: `ne` marks the next word as a name, a name identifies its bearer without acquiring a dictionary meaning, and a person called `ne sulae` carries a word while a person with a new-made name carries only themselves. The chapter closes the loop opened in chapter 2: every refusal is paired with the mechanism that keeps the refused thing sayable.

Receipts: the development protocol's semantic principles and source-material checklist; the productive name-form charter; texts/human_rights_article_one.md and its ungendered rendering of brotherhood; the naming pamphlet; texts/ring_verse_refusal.md.

Opens on the Ring Verse, what Phi does when handed a text whose subject is domination; ends with the refused thing still sayable, held at arm's length in its own script.

### 7. A literature before a community

Phi inverted the usual order of language projects: it built literature before speakers. Thirteen works stand on the shelf, the Metta Sutta and the Tao Te Ching among them, with a Morris novel in progress, under a two-method doctrine: a translation stays answerable to the source's claims, a transmutation lets the source change under the language's commitments, and every text declares which it is. The chapter spends most of its time on the honesty apparatus: gap logs record what did not survive, translation-limits tables state remaining distance line by line, and a machine checks every labeled citation verbatim against a stored source witness, so a misquote fails the build. The chapter argues this is what it looks like when a language treats its literature as its laboratory.

Receipts: texts/README.md and the shelf's method column; texts/human_rights_article_one.md's translation-limits table; texts/solarpunk_manifesto.md as the newest arrival; the validator's source-citation check; documents/evaluation/active_text_corpus_review.md's review rubric; texts/news_from_nowhere/.

Opens on the machine refusing the build over a misquoted source; ends on literature as the test the language can fail, and keeps choosing to take.

### 8. The workshop

How Phi is made, told the way the colophon tells it: one designer with a veto on everything, an AI instrument that drafts against his designs and does the accounting that makes honesty checkable, a validator that holds the whole corpus at zero tolerance. The Morris and Co. analogy gives the chapter its shape, the designer and the workshop. The costs stay in the open: the training-data and energy critiques are stated, not deflected, and what separates this from slop is put in one sentence: exactly one human, and everything routes through him. Disclosure is argued as both ethics and strategy, because the community this book meets first will ask, and should.

Receipts: colophon.md as the primary document; the validator and CI; the project's own history of corrections as evidence of the veto. Targeted research owed during drafting: current disclosure norms for generative tools in creative work, and a historical accuracy check on the Morris and Co. workshop analogy.

Opens on the colophon's first sentence, "Phi is my language"; ends on its last trade: a fast tool used to build an artifact of attention whose human designer remains answerable for it.

### Part III: What it can honestly do now

### 9. A serious practice

What joining Phi gives a person, tiered by evidence and never inflated. Structured reflective practice reduces distress modestly against doing nothing (about a third of a standard deviation in the strictest pooled analysis) and does not beat other deliberate practices; a minority of practitioners have negative experiences, and the chapter reports it. The strongest, least hedged frame is serious leisure: demanding, identity-conferring pursuits reliably accompany higher wellbeing, and that is what learning a difficult small language actually is. Expressive writing helps a little, under conditions the chapter spells out. Constraints measurably aid creative output. Ritual calms in the moment, with the field's most famous lab result flagged as retracted. The chapter's discipline is to promise smallness and mean it.

Receipts: Galante et al. 2021 and 2023; Goldberg et al. 2022; Van Dam et al. 2018; Farias et al. 2020; Kuykendall et al. 2015; Stebbins' serious-leisure framework; Daykin et al. 2018; Frattaroli 2006; Reinhold et al. 2018; Acar et al. 2019; Haught-Tromp 2016; Hobson et al. 2018; Lang et al. 2020; the Brooks et al. 2016 retraction.

Opens on the primer's cat, asleep through twenty-four chapters, and what that running joke teaches about habit and return; ends on smallness kept as a promise: the practice claim survives the evidence, and it is enough.

### 10. The distance of a learned tongue

Decisions cool in a learned language. The foreign-language effect is real and small: framing biases shrink and moral choices shift when a dilemma arrives in a second language, with a pooled odds ratio of 1.34 and real heterogeneity behind it. The chapter states the limit in the same breath: this is an effect of foreignness, not of any language's design, and it likely fades as fluency grows. Láadan supplies the cautionary mirror: a language whose mandatory disclosure grammar made speakers feel exposed. Phi's evidentials are optional and an unmarked claim carries no source claim by ruling, so distance and disclosure are always available and never forced. The chapter closes with the one training effect a small lexicon has actually demonstrated: learning to say things with few words builds transferable paraphrase skill.

Receipts: Keysar et al. 2012; Costa et al. 2014; Circi et al. 2021; Del Maschio et al. 2022; the non-replications outside the classic dilemmas; Elgin's vulnerability findings; canon's unmarked-claims ruling; Coluzzi 2022 on circumlocution transfer.

Opens on the footbridge dilemma flipping in a second language; ends on Phi keeping a quiet room a speaker can step into, never a room they are locked in.

### 11. The lens, not the cage

The science chapter, and the trial chapter for the whole book. What replicated: Russian speakers discriminate their two blues faster, and the advantage vanishes when the inner voice is occupied, which is the true shape of a real Whorfian effect, online and small. Kuuk Thaayorre speakers lay time east to west. Number words are a cognitive technology for exact quantity across time, which meets Phi's ternary system and its 242 boundary head on. Turkish's obligatory evidentials tune source memory without conferring epistemic virtue. What fell: the Mandarin time study's direct replications, the grammatical-gender bridges that were never peer-reviewed, Hopi timelessness, Eskimo snow, futureless-language savings rates. The frames that survive are thinking for speaking and language as a swappable lens. Then the chapter turns to Phi and refuses the genre's move: the language is an untested instrument, its predecessors died at adoption before cognition could be asked, and its own protocol wrote the honest position first.

Receipts: Winawer et al. 2007; Boroditsky and Gaby 2010; Frank et al. 2008; Tosun et al. 2013 against Papafragou et al. 2007; Chen 2007 and the replication record; Mickan et al. 2014; Samuel et al. 2019; Malotki 1983; Pullum 1991; Roberts et al. 2015; McWhorter 2014 and Pinker 1994 given their strongest arguments; Slobin 1996.

Opens inside the Russian blues experiment, ending its first scene on the detail that occupying the inner voice erases the effect; ends on the protocol's own sentence, design intentions to test in use, not guarantees, as the exact place the science says to stand.

### Part IV: If it catches on

### 12. A language for the commons

Why solarpunk first. The movement's founding documents already call for what Phi is: the manifesto describes solarpunk as a paradigm and a vocabulary, canonizes Art Nouveau, craft, and stewardship, and demands optimism as a discipline rather than a mood. The value alignment is documented pillar by pillar, and the niche is empty: no constructed language occupies this space. Adoption, if it comes, will be register-first, because that is how communities of practice actually mark themselves: words, greetings, mottos, and names travel long before grammar does. Distribution must be gift-first in a community that edited a corporation out of its own utopia advertisement. The bridge artifact already exists: the manifesto reads in Phi on the project's own shelf. The chapter also inherits Esperanto's oldest lesson and answers it in advance: some will want the language without the politics, and the door is built to let them.

Receipts: the 2019 manifesto and Flynn's 2014 notes, quoted directly; the community-size and trajectory evidence; the Dear Alice decommodified edition; Eckert and Wenger on communities of practice; Halliday 1976 on anti-languages; texts/solarpunk_manifesto.md; the hopepunk timeline; Toki Pona's community-not-movement framing.

Opens on the community that removed a brand from its own dream; ends on the manifesto's caretakers-and-gardeners line set beside Phi's own closing benediction.

### 13. Escaping words

Phi refuses the auxlang ambition the way it refuses the clock, and this chapter makes the refusal and defends it. The evidence is the graveyard of chapter 3, compressed to its mechanism: universal ambition invites great-power politics, network effects favor the incumbent, and a language for everyone must shed the values that made it worth having. What Phi keeps instead is the aspect that actually works: words escape. Solarpunk itself escaped a blog post; hopepunk went from a Tumblr joke to a dictionary in two years. The chapter lists Phi's candidates, `womu` (home) as the dwelling-heart, anger as `korua thero`, heart-fire, contempt as `thiku nila`, seeing-small, the eight sun stations, `wia.` (how many) as a saying for unpriceable things, and takes the warning seriously: designed vocabulary can backfire, and the degrowth movement's missile word measurably did. Escaped words are the realistic success criterion, stated with the funnel numbers that make fluency the wrong one.

Receipts: the maintainer's ruling and the protocol's own line that Phi is not an auxiliary language; the adoption-lessons record; Drews and Antal 2016 on missile words; the hopepunk and solarpunk coinage timelines; the compound registry entries behind each candidate word; the curiosity-to-speaker funnel figures.

Opens on how the word solarpunk itself escaped, a blog post to an encyclopedia entry in sixteen years; ends on a language succeeding the way a garden does, by seeding beyond its fence.

### 14. The mind question

The pillars revisited as practice claims, each cut to the size of its evidence. What the modifier-first rule offers is rehearsal in stating relations before content; what optional evidentials offer is a habit of noticing how one knows; what the exchange refusal offers is a daily reframe of what changes hands; what the sky's day offers is attention to where the light actually is. None of this is compelled, all of it is occasioned, and the chapter keeps the analogy fence explicit: the moving findings on Indigenous language revitalization and community health belong to peoples reclaiming their own tongues, and this book does not borrow them. Then the affirmative program: Phi's community, if it forms, is the experiment its predecessors never ran, and the chapter proposes the first small, falsifiable questions with failure imaginable: whether journaling in Phi shifts source-marking habits in a writer's English, whether the composition ethic trains measurable paraphrase transfer, whether announce-first speakers write differently online. Small questions, stated in advance, answerable either way.

Receipts: the four research digests in summary; Tosun et al. 2013; Papafragou et al. 2007; Coluzzi 2022; Hallett et al. 2007 and Whalen et al. 2022, fenced as non-transferable; the protocol's closing reminders.

Opens on the boatman again, and what has changed in the reader since page one; ends on the protocol's last line, the language offers the practice, speakers do the work.

### Close: the door

Three invitations in ascending order of commitment, and the smallest stated first because it is how languages actually travel: borrow a word, read the shelf, or learn at the primer's pace. Where everything lives: the site, the explorer with every word's reasoning attached, the short road for a twenty-minute walk. The close reproduces the short road's own door, the validated farewell block `pi no shua.`, and stops.

Receipts: short_road.md's closing block; kia.md's doors; the explorer.

Opens on the reader closing the book with one word in their pocket; ends on the open door, built open.

## Production method

The book is drafted chapter by chapter, one branch and one pull request each, in treatment order unless review reorders them. Every draft follows `documents/reference/voice_for_models.md` end to end: register identified first, the humanizer skill invoked on the complete draft, the counting pass run against the anti-pattern table, a batch pass across finished chapters for stamping and pet words, and each delivery naming what its audit found. Phi content is quoted from validated texts or validated fresh; `book/` is one of the validator's document roots, so every Phi word and citation is machine-checked from the first file onward. External sources are cited by author and year in the text, with a references list at each chapter's end and a consolidated bibliography closing the book. Each chapter's reference list ends with a `Phi sources` subsection. Repository paths identify the local material behind its claims about the language. Chapters that owe targeted research name it in their receipts.

The book's Phi examples sit deliberately between the primer's simplest sentences and the shelf's full complexity. An example is a natural sentence with real grammar in it, story-grounded where possible, and readable by a stranger at first meeting. The texts shelf does the language's showcasing; the book never runs inventory demonstrations of the particle system. When a scene from the shelf carries the needed grammar, quote the scene.

After the treatment is ratified, drafting begins with a trial: the cold open plus chapter 11. Chapter 11 is the hardest register in the book, science writing in the established voice with claims at exact size. If that chapter works, the book works; if it fails, the treatment gets revised before anything else is written.

## Open decisions

1. Placement: this file proposes book/ as the book's working directory, with one file per chapter as drafting proceeds. Ratifying this pull request ratifies the location.
2. History: teaching prose excludes project history by rule; this book cannot. The colophon set the precedent for a carved, honest-history register outside the teaching corpus, and the book requests the same carve for book/.
3. Title: pick or defer.
4. License and distribution: recommendation is free first, Creative Commons, rendered on the site once chapters exist, with print as a later artifact. The community evidence favors gift-first strongly.
5. Citation format: per-chapter reference lists (recommended) or endnotes.
