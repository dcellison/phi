## 4.5 The Golden Rule: Vowel Hiatus

The single most important rhythmic rule in Phi is **vowel hiatus**.

In many languages, when two vowels meet, they often blend into a single sound called a diphthong (e.g., the "oi" in "coin"). **In Phi, this never happens.**

When two vowels appear in a row, they are always pronounced as two separate syllables with a clean break between them. Every vowel creates its own syllable.

To understand this, let's visualize the syllable breaks using a dot (`.`):
*   `hauno` (house) is pronounced **`ha.u.no`** (three syllables).
*   `shei` (to connect) is pronounced **`she.i`** (two syllables).
*   `theula` (all) is pronounced **`the.u.la`** (three syllables).
*   `kewai` (however) is pronounced **`ke.wa.i`** (three syllables).

The pause between the vowels is small but essential.

---

### Hiatus Practice

The best way to internalize this rule is through practice. Read each word aloud, deliberately exaggerating the separation between the vowels in each pair. As you get comfortable, the separation will feel more natural, but the two sounds must never blend.

| Word | Pronounce As (Syllables) | Focus On |
| :--- | :----------------------- |:---|
| `sheala` | `she . a . la` | The `e` and `a` are two distinct sounds. |
| `phaino` | `pha . i . no` | Feel the sharp transition from open `a` to high `i`. |
| `nolou` | `no . lo . u` | Do not let `o` and `u` merge into an "oo" sound. |
| `whio` | `whi . o` | A high vowel followed by a round one: `whee-oh`. |
| `lisoa` | `li . so . a` | The `o` and `a` are two full, separate beats. |
| `pua` | `pu . a` | A deep sound followed by an open one: `poo-ah`. |
| `thoa` | `tho . a` | Do not let this become the English "though." |

---

### The Three-Vowel Constraint

A direct consequence of the hiatus rule is that a sequence of three or more vowels in a row is **strictly forbidden**.

Because every vowel forms its own syllable, a word like `naoui` would have to be pronounced `na.o.u.i`—a clumsy cascade of four syllables without consonants for structure. To maintain the language's clear, melodious rhythm, a consonant must always separate a vowel pair from another vowel.
