## 4.3 The Consonants: Simple and Clear

Phi uses ten simple consonants to provide structure to its words. The inventory was designed to be cross-linguistically common and easy to pronounce.

---

### Plosives (Stops)
These are sounds made by briefly but completely stopping the airflow in the mouth and then releasing it in a small, clean burst.

*   **`p`** (/p/): This sound is made by closing the lips completely and then releasing the air.
    *   **English:** **p**in, s**p**in
    *   **Practice:** `pota` (table), `wepu` (to go)

*   **`t`** (/t̪/): This sound is **dental**. To produce it, the tip of your tongue should touch the **back of your upper teeth**, not the hard ridge behind them as in English. This creates a much softer sound.
    *   **Practice:** `te` (stopping), `keta` (courage)

*   **`k`** (/k/): This sound is made by raising the back of the tongue to touch the soft palate, stopping the air, and then releasing it.
    *   **English:** s**k**y, **c**at
    *   **Practice:** `kela` (to understand), `keto` (stone)

---

### Nasals
These sounds are made by stopping airflow through the mouth but allowing it to resonate and escape through the nose.

*   **`m`** (/m/): This sound is made by closing the lips, just like for a `p`, but allowing the sound to resonate nasally.
    *   **English:** hi**m**, **m**other
    *   **Practice:** `mia` (I), `minu` (family)

*   **`n`** (/n̪/): Like the Phi `t`, the `n` is also **dental**, produced with the tip of the tongue touching the back of the upper teeth, not the alveolar ridge. This makes it sound softer than its English equivalent.
    *   **Practice:** `nima` (to sleep), `lona` (slow), `kenalu` (to maintain)

---

### Flow Consonants (Liquids and Sibilant)
These are sounds where the airflow is shaped, but not stopped completely.

*   **`l`** (/l/): The **lateral** liquid. This sound is made by placing the tip of the tongue on the ridge behind the teeth and allowing air to flow smoothly past the sides.
    *   **English:** **l**et, **l**ove
    *   **Practice:** `lothea` (love), `sela` (peace)

*   **`r`** (/r/ or /ɾ/): The **rhotic** liquid. This sound has two acceptable pronunciations. It can be a fully **trilled** 'r', made by vibrating the tip of the tongue against the alveolar ridge (like Spanish *pe**rr**o*), or a single, quick **tap** of the tongue in the same position (like the 't' in an American pronunciation of "wa**t**er").
    *   **Practice:** `miro` (beautiful), `shiro` (forest)

*   **`s`** (/s/): The **sibilant**. This is the hissing sound made by channeling air through a narrow groove in the tongue towards the teeth.
    *   **English:** **s**ee, **s**un
    *   **Practice:** `sila` (together), `sela` (peace)

---

### Approximant and Aspirate
These are the most open consonants, acting more as ways to shape the beginning of a vowel.

*   **`w`** (/w/): This sound is made by rounding the lips and raising the back of the tongue.
    *   **English:** **w**et, **w**ay
    *   **Practice:** `wela` (good/harmonious), `weo` (we)

*   **`h`** (/h/): This is the sound of pure breath, with no real obstruction at all.
    *   **English:** **h**at, **h**ome
    *   **Practice:** `hisu` (to feel), `hauno` (house)
