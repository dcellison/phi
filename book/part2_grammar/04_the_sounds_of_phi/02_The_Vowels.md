## 4.2 The Vowels: Pure and Unwavering

The key to mastering Phi's vowels is to understand a single principle: they are always **pure** and **unwavering**. Unlike the gliding vowels of English, which often move from one position to another within a single syllable (a "diphthong"), the vowels of Phi are held steady and clear. Think of them not as glides, but as five pure, resonant musical notes.

---

**`a`** (/Ă¤/)

To produce the `a` sound, the mouth should be open and relaxed, the jaw dropped comfortably, and the tongue resting low and flat in the center of the mouth. It is the sound a doctor asks for when they wish to see your throat.

*   **English:** f**a**ther, c**o**t
*   **Spanish/Italian/Japanese:** The standard `a`, as in *k**a**mi*.

**Practice Words:**
*   `ma` (not)
*   `pa` (beginning)
*   `nosa` (mindful)
*   `thola` (story)
*   `shalai` (purpose)

---

**`e`** (/eĚž/)

This sound requires careful practice for most native English speakers. It is a true-mid front vowel. The body of the tongue is raised to the halfway point, positioned toward the front of the mouth. The lips should be unrounded or spread slightly, as in a gentle smile. It is absolutely **not** the gliding "ay" sound of English words like "h**ay**" or "w**eigh**."

*   **Spanish:** m**e**sa
*   **German:** G**e**bet (first 'e' only)

**Practice Words:**
*   `she` (focus)
*   `le` (honorific for a role)
*   `sela` (peace)
*   `kela` (to understand)
*   `weothe` (law / a shared path)

---

**`i`** (/i/)

This is the highest and most forward of the vowels. To produce it, the main body of the tongue is raised high and pushed forward in the mouth, very close to the hard palate just behind the teeth. The lips should be unrounded and spread.

*   **English:** mach**i**ne, f**ee**t, s**ee**

**Practice Words:**
*   `pi` (politeness)
*   `shi` (four)
*   `lino` (wisdom)
*   `whipi` (to hear)
*   `shilei` (justice)

---

**`o`** (/oĚž/)

This is the rounded counterpart to `e`. It is a true-mid vowel, produced at the back of the mouth with the lips distinctly rounded. English speakers must avoid the tendency to turn it into the gliding "oh" sound of "g**o**" or "sh**ow**." The lips should form a clean, open circle, and the sound should be held steady.

*   **Spanish:** p**o**co
*   **German:** S**o**hn

**Practice Words:**
*   `no` (request)
*   `mo` (comparative)
*   `mosa` (humility)
*   `phola` (power)
*   `thophu` (yesterday)

---

**`u`** (/u/)

This is the highest and most rounded of the back vowels. To produce this sound, the body of the tongue is raised high and pulled far back in the mouth, and the lips must be very rounded and pushed slightly forward. The key is to ensure it is a pure, steady vowel, without the slight "y" sound that can sometimes precede it in English (as in "c**u**te").

*   **English:** fl**u**te, r**u**le, tr**u**th

**Practice Words:**
*   `su` (hope/wish)
*   `lu` (if)
*   `nolu` (to think)
*   `thophu` (yesterday)
*   `phelu` (tendril/spiral growth)
