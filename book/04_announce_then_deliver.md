# Announce, then deliver

Here is the sentence the language's own twenty-minute introduction leads with. It is about as plain as carpentry gets.

```
mia roe kiru wolea kati.
1SG INS chisel wood cut.
(I cut the wood with the chisel.)
```

Take it one word at a time. `mia` is the speaker. `roe` is a function word with a single job: it promises that the next thing named will be an instrument. `kiru` keeps the promise: the chisel. `wolea` is the wood, the thing the sentence is about to act on. Then, last of all, `kati`: cut. The verb is the final word, and it lands in a scene with nothing missing from it. Who would cut, with what, into what: all of it stood settled before the cutting was said.

That ordering is the language's one structural rule, and the language states it in a single sentence: whatever modifies, specifies, or relates comes before what it affects. The rest of the grammar is that sentence having consequences. The quality comes before its bearer: `welao shelu`, good book. The owner comes before the owned: `mia shelu`, my book. A preposition stands before its noun, so the relation is named before the thing being related. An asking word takes the very position in the sentence that its answer will fill. And a dependent clause comes before the clause that depends on it: the reason before the result, the if before the then.

The if before the then is worth hearing at full gentleness, and the language's shelf provides it, in a rendering of Kahlil Gibran's teaching on giving:

```
lu thia miso loa. thia shewo loa.
COND 2SG REFL give. 2SG true give.
(When you give yourself, you truly give.)
```

`lu` opens a condition, and the condition runs all the way to its own period before the consequence draws breath. Nothing in the second clause asks the listener to go back and repair the first. By the time "you truly give" arrives, the ground under it is already laid.

The rule has already worked at full stretch once in these pages. The boatman scene's narrator opened the report of his own thinking with one word, closed it with another, and only then reached the verb think, which carried its confession of assumption ahead of itself. Every piece of that sentence obeyed the same law as the chisel sentence, because there is no other law to obey. Phi has no inversion for drama, no idiom that runs backward, no second pattern held in reserve. A learner who can read the chisel sentence has, in one real sense, met every sentence the language will ever show them.

## The most common order on earth

The verb-final part of this is not exotic. In the largest survey of word order across the world's languages, 564 of 1,376 sampled languages arrange their sentences subject first, object next, verb last, more than any other pattern, English's included (Dryer 2013). Japanese keeps this order, and so do Turkish, Hindi, and Korean. Roughly two of every five languages on earth end their sentences the way Phi does. A designed language that wanted to feel alien had cheaper ways to get there.

What is unusual sits in two other places. First, the world's verb-final languages overwhelmingly put their relators after the noun: in Japanese, the word that does the work of "with" follows "chisel." Phi uses prepositions instead, because under its one rule a relator is a modifier like any other, and modifiers come first. A typologist would file the combination as mixed directionality, an uncommon profile; the language files it as consistency. Second, natural languages bend. Japanese lets phrases move forward for emphasis; Turkish reorders freely to mark what is news and what is already shared. Phi does not bend. What the design added to one of humanity's commonest word orders is not the order. It is the absence of a second one.

So the interesting question is not where typology files the rule, but what living under it could do to the person speaking. The tempting answer is that composing every sentence this way trains a more careful mind. It is a particularly agreeable answer for the person who designed the language. The evidence permits a smaller one, and the rest of the chapter keeps to it.

## Thinking for speaking

Beginning in the 1980s, Dan Slobin and his colleagues put the same wordless picture book, Frog, Where Are You?, in front of children and adults in five countries and asked each of them to tell its story. The pictures never changed: a boy, a dog, and a frog that had left its jar. This modest crisis was enough. Narrators ranged from age three to adulthood and spoke English, German, Spanish, Hebrew, or Turkish. Their tellings sorted themselves by language. English speakers, whose verbs fold the manner of movement into themselves (an English creature creeps, tumbles, or swoops instead of merely moving), built what Slobin called "elaborated trajectories of motion." Spanish speakers, whose everyday motion verbs name the path rather than the manner, produced "elaborated descriptions of the static locations of objects" and let the movement happen between the descriptions (Slobin 1996, 78). The pattern was not a habit of age or schooling; the youngest narrators already told the story their language's way.

Slobin's name for what the frog stories reveal is thinking for speaking: "a special form of thought that is mobilized for communication" (Slobin 1996, 76). In the act of turning experience into an utterance, a speaker selects the features of the scene that fit what their grammar can readily carry. The grammar does not decide what the speaker saw. It decides what the sentence needs, and so it directs the speaker's attention, during composition, toward what it needs. His summary of the whole research line fits in one sentence: "each native language has trained its speakers to pay different kinds of attention to events and experiences when talking about them" (Slobin 1996, 89). And he bounds the claim in the same chapter: the training is laid down in childhood and is "exceptionally resistant to restructuring" when adults learn a second language (Slobin 1996, 89). The claim ends there: attention while talking.

Read Phi's rule through that frame and the design's ambition becomes precise. To speak the chisel sentence, the speaker has to settle the instrument before the act can be voiced. To speak Gibran's line, the whole condition must be composed before its consequence can begin. What this grammar obliges a speaker to attend to, in the moment of building a sentence, is relations: which word will modify which, what depends on what, what frame the coming content will stand in. That is the entire mechanism on offer: attention ordered for as long as the sentence is being made.

## The silent gesture

If a grammar's training ran deeper than speaking, it should survive the removal of speech. That experiment has been run, and its result sets a useful boundary.

Susan Goldin-Meadow and her colleagues asked forty adults, ten each native speakers of English, Spanish, Chinese, and Turkish, to watch short videos of simple events and describe them using only their hands, no speech allowed. Forty more reconstructed the same events by stacking picture transparencies on a peg while the experimenter pointedly paid no attention: a task with no communication in it at all. English and Spanish order their spoken sentences actor, act, patient; Chinese mixes its orders; of the four groups only Turkish speaks with the act last. Yet in silent gesture every group, whatever its language, produced one order: actor, patient, act. The stacking task produced the same order again. The authors' finding is stated without hedging: "the word orders speakers used in their everyday speech did not influence their nonverbal behavior" (Goldin-Meadow et al. 2008). A lifetime inside English word order had not rearranged how English speakers laid out an event once the words were taken away.

A later study narrowed the finding further. English and Turkish package motion differently, and when speakers of the two languages gesture while speaking, their hands follow their grammars. Şeyda Özçalışkan and colleagues had the same people describe the same motion events in gesture alone, and the difference vanished: the silent gestures of English speakers and Turkish speakers were, in the authors' word, identical in how they packaged and ordered the elements of motion (Özçalışkan, Lucero and Goldin-Meadow 2016). Language shapes the gesture that travels with speech. Take the speech away and the shaping leaves with it.

For Phi, that boundary matters. A lifetime under the chisel sentence's order will not restructure how a speaker lays out events when they are not speaking. The rehearsal Phi offers happens in language and stays there. Anything larger would outrun the data.

The experiments also reveal a smaller, stranger fact. The order every silent gesturer fell into, actor before patient before act, is the chisel sentence's own arrangement, the pattern the study's authors note is "analogous to the subject-object-verb pattern found in many languages of the world" (Goldin-Meadow et al. 2008). When people lay out an event with no grammar watching, they lay it out roughly the way Phi's rule requires. Taken on its own terms, this takes something away from Phi rather than adding something: the order cannot be a special training, because it appears to be everyone's wordless default already, English speakers included. What it means is smaller and still worth having. The rule does not fight the grain. Phi asks its speakers to build every sentence in an order the unoccupied mind seems to reach for on its own, and to do it deliberately, sentence after sentence.

## A rehearsal, not a rewiring

The evidence leaves Phi with a narrower claim. The rule cannot reach the wordless mind; the gesture studies close that door. The rule does direct attention during speech; the frog stories show grammars doing exactly that to their speakers, from early childhood, durably. Between those findings sits what Phi actually provides: a rehearsal run in every sentence, with the relations stated first and the content delivered into them. The next sentence asks for the same preparation again.

A rehearsal is not a rewiring, and this book will not blur the two. Nothing about performing a sequence, however many times, is proof that the performer is being changed by it; that claim would need evidence this chapter has been careful to say does not yet exist. What a rehearsal unarguably does is structure the performance itself. Every Phi sentence, by rule, is a small act of preparation completed before an act of delivery, and a speaker in ordinary conversation performs that shape as many times as they have things to say.

Say the chisel sentence once more at speaking pace and hear the preparation in it. The speaker comes first, the tool is promised and named, and the wood is laid on the bench. Only when every piece is in place does the last word fall, and it falls like the act it names. Actual carpentry may still go badly, because grammar has never steadied anyone's hand. None of the waiting changes the wood. What the sentence holds open, for exactly as long as it lasts, is the moment before the act, made audible every time. Phi declines to let that moment go unspoken. Whether the refusal leaves anything behind in the speaker is a question for later chapters, and those chapters will have to earn their own answer. The sentence, meanwhile, is already better prepared than it had to be.
