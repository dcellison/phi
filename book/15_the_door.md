# The door

This book began with a hand going into a pocket for money that was not there. If the chapters have done their work, something is now traveling the other way: you are closing the cover with a word in your pocket. Maybe it is `womu`, for the home you are building rather than buying. Maybe it is the bare `wia.` you will deploy, someday, on someone who asks what a Sunday afternoon was worth. Maybe it is only `kia`, two open syllables at a threshold. Whichever it is, it is yours now. The license was written that way on purpose, and chapter 13 already told you the whole distribution strategy.

So here are the three invitations this book ends on, in ascending order of commitment, the smallest first because the smallest is how languages actually travel.

Borrow a word. That is the entire first invitation. No account, no course, no creed; take the word that named something you already felt and use it where you live. If it earns its keep in your English, it was built well, and if it spreads from you to someone else, you have personally conducted the only kind of adoption the record has ever shown to work.

Read the shelf. The literature came before the community, and it asks nothing of you but reading: the Metta Sutta that opened it, the fables and the Tao, the Ring Verse held at its careful distance, Morris's riverbank where a boatman is still, patiently, offering a confused man the tide. Every Phi line sits beside its gloss and its source. You can weigh the whole language from a chair, which is exactly how its maker intended a stranger's first visit to go.

Learn it. This is the long invitation, and the book will not pretend it is small: the sounds ask for unhurried vowels, the sentences ask you to hold your relations in mind before you speak, and the lexicon asks your memory for room it will not give back. Chapter 9 told you honestly what that kind of pursuit returns. If you want the gentle way in, the primer teaches through one household, where a child asks at the door and a cat is, at this hour as at most hours, asleep. If you want the map first, the short road walks the whole design in about twenty minutes.

Everything lives in one place, and the front door is a single word: the site opens at `kia`, hello, and offers its doors from there. One leads to the twenty-minute walk, one to the lexicon explorer where every word keeps its meaning and its relations close at hand, one to the primer's household, one to the manual's full machinery, one to the shelf, one to the pamphlets, and one to this book, filed where it belongs, under Consider.

The short road ends at a door of its own, and this book has saved it for last because nothing better exists to say. The language you have been reading about was built out of refusals, a day without a clock, a lexicon without a price, a grammar that will not force a confession, and among all those closed doors its maker built exactly one that never shuts. It is the sentence the whole project keeps at its threshold, and it is built open.

```
pi no shua.
POL IMP come.
(Please, come.)
```
