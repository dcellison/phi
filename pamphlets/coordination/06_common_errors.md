# Part 6: Common errors

Coordination has one deep rule, equals only, and a small family of relation mix-ups. These seven cover the errors that actually occur.

### Error 1: unlike elements joined

**Wrong:** `*mia peloru nela thia theo nila.` intended as "I see the flower and you read."

`nela` joins comparable structure, and an object noun cannot pair with a clause. Give each thought its full clause and join those: `mia peloru nila nela thia theo.` Two verbs, two complete sides, one level join.

### Error 2: the single `nela` in a list

**Wrong:** `*shiro nela peloru whelina.` intended as "trees, flowers, and grass."

English drops its and until the last boundary; Phi sounds every joint: `shiro nela peloru nela whelina.` A missing `nela` is not a lighter list but a different structure, `peloru whelina` collapsing into one phrase, grass of the flowers, and the list quietly loses a member.

### Error 3: `thona` stretched over a redirect

**Wrong for the shape:** a paragraph of assertions forced into one sentence so that `thona` can carry the turn.

`thona` marks a local contrast between two coordinated elements. When the turn redirects a whole line of thought, end the sentence and open the next with `whekai`. The conjunction and the marker split one job by scope, exactly as `lao` and `thelao` split reason, and choosing the period is often the stronger contrast.

### Error 4: `we` asked to build a pair

**Wrong:** `*shiro we peloru` intended as "trees and flowers."

`we` reaches back to an established alternative; it does not stand between two elements named now. The pair belongs to `nela`: `shiro nela peloru.` Save `we` for the second sentence, where the newcomer joins what the first has set up.

### Error 5: `we` on the wrong edge

**Wrong for the meaning:** `lohau we wile.` intended as "the dog joins the players."

Grammatical, and it adds the playing to what the dog already does. To add the dog to the players, the participant is the news, and `we` stands before the subject: `we lohau wile.` The particle's position names what is being added; put it at the edge of exactly that.

### Error 6: `sola` read as a command to choose

**Wrong for the meaning:** treating `mia so theo sola mia so thalo.` as a vow to do exactly one.

The fork leaves both paths open. Exclusivity is a further claim, and when it matters, a sentence of its own states that one path excludes the other. `sola` presents; it does not choose for anyone.

### Error 7: `nela` asked to carry cause

**Wrong for the meaning:** `mia to theo nela to shelomu.` offered as "I read, and so I understood."

The sentence is grammatical and says less than the intent: two past facts, level, unrelated. Cause has its own grammar: `lao mia to theo mia to shelomu.` inside one sentence, or the conclusion drawn across two with `thelao`. English lets and smuggle therefore; Phi asks the claim to wear its own word.
