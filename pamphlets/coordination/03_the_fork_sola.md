# Part 3: The fork: `sola`

`sola` draws a fork and leaves both paths on the page:

```
mia theo sola sheluo.
1SG read DISJ listen.
(I read or listen.)
```

```
thia shea sola maeli pula.
2SG peace DISJ quiet wish.
(You wish for peace or quiet.)
```

Like its two siblings, it joins equals at any level: words, phrases, or full clauses, each side keeping its own structure.

### Open or closed

An or can allow both paths or permit only one, and `sola` itself does not decide. Context usually settles the reading, and when the difference matters, a sentence of its own states whether both are possible or one must be chosen. The fork stands; the rules of the fork are said aloud when someone needs them.

### Alternatives inside a question

With the question particle, `sola` builds the choice question:

```
wa thia theo sola sheluo.
Q 2SG read DISJ listen.
(Do you read or listen?)
```

`wa` opens, the alternatives stand inside, and the listener may take a path or refuse the fork entirely; the grammar has asked, not cornered.

### Clause against clause

```
mia so theo sola mia so thalo.
1SG FUT read DISJ 1SG FUT walk.
(I will read, or I will walk.)
```

```
thia po mia naphe sola mia po miso naphe.
2SG POT 1SG help DISJ 1SG POT REFL help.
(You can help me, or I can help myself.)
```

The second example is the machinery meeting itself: two full clauses, each with its own modality, and the reflexive standing exactly where the second path bends the help back home. Every part was taught somewhere else in this shelf; `sola` just holds the fork open between them.

### Practice: draw the fork

1. I read or listen.
2. Do you read or listen? Ask it.
3. I will read, or I will walk.

Answers: 1 `mia theo sola sheluo.` 2 `wa thia theo sola sheluo.` The question particle opens and the fork stands inside it. 3 `mia so theo sola mia so thalo.` Both futures stay live, and the evening will pick one without the sentence having promised it.
