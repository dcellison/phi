# Part 2: The contrast: `thona`

Where `nela` says and, `thona` says but: two equally ranked elements, the second presented against an expectation the first has raised.

```
shiro mioru nai thona thiku nai.
tree beautiful be ADVRS small be.
(The tree is beautiful, but it is small.)
```

```
shia shaelo thona thesua nai.
3SG brave ADVRS mindful be.
(They are brave but mindful.)
```

### Both sides stay asserted

The contrast subtracts nothing. The tree is beautiful, and the tree is small; the person is brave, and the person is mindful. `thona` adds one thing only: the speaker's sense that the second claim runs against what the first led the listener to expect. And that expectation belongs to the speaker, not the world. Bravery and mindfulness are not natural opposites; this sentence, in this conversation, treats the second as the surprise. A different speaker may hear no tension at all, and the grammar leaves them free to say the same facts with `nela`.

### Clause against clause

Between full clauses, `thona` sets one complete assertion against another:

```
mia to theo thona to ma shelomu.
1SG PST read ADVRS PST NEG understand.
(I read, but did not understand.)
```

```
shia haolu thona lo miona ma sheluo.
3SG speak ADVRS PL person NEG listen.
(They speak, but the people do not listen.)
```

Notice the first example lightening itself: the second clause drops its established subject and keeps its own tense and negation, coordination and topic-drop working together the way real speech does.

### One sentence or two

`thona` marks a local contrast inside one sentence. When a turn arrives after a stretch of sentences and redirects the whole line of thought, the discourse marker `whekai` opens the new sentence instead. The pattern is the same scope choice the adverbial-clauses pamphlet drilled for reason and concession: inside one sentence, the conjunction; across the boundary, the marker. Local surprise takes `thona`; a change of direction takes the period.

### Practice: set the contrast

1. I understand, but you do not.
2. The sun is warm, but the wind is cold.
3. Say the same two facts about the tree with no contrast claimed at all.

Answers: 1 `mia shelomu thona thia ma shelomu.` 2 `sileta sulae nai thona howeli pelui nai.` 3 `shiro mioru nela thiku nai.`, or two plain sentences; the facts are identical, and only the speaker's framing has stepped aside. That the choice exists is the lesson: contrast is something you add, and Phi makes the adding visible.
