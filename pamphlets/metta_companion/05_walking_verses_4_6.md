# Part 5: Walking verses 4–6: omitting none

### Verses 4–5 — the doors close

```
welua sola kema. whalo sola thiku.
fragile DISJ strong. large DISJ small.
(Fragile or strong. Large or small.)

[rena se nila] sola [rena se ma nila].
[REL PASS see] DISJ [REL PASS NEG see].
(The seen or the unseen.)

noshi sola wuero.
near DISJ far.
(Near or far.)

[rena lima nai] nela [rena wea lioru shua].
[REL alive be] COORD [REL toward life come].
(Those who are alive and those coming toward life.)

su theula [rena lima nai] towe nai.
OPT UNIV [REL alive be] well be.
(May all that lives be well.)
```

Verse 3 already wished joy on *all* that lives. What are these two verses for, then? Read the pairs as doors: every one of them is an exclusion someone might quietly make while saying "all" — the far away, the invisible, the not yet here — and the text walks the row shutting them one by one. Only after the inventory does the refrain repeat, because only now has `theula` been proofed against the ways a reader shrinks it.

Notice the lines have no verbs. They are not broken sentences; they are the list a wish is checking itself against, spoken as pure pairs — and Phi's pauses being free, recitation gives each pair its own breath without a single mark on the page. The passive relatives from Part 3 do their work here; if `[rena se ma nila]` still costs you a beat, run Part 3's drill again before going on.

**Micro-drill:** recite the three short pair-lines with full hiatus — `welua sola kema` wants every vowel it owns. Then close one more door: compose your own pair in the same shape, some axis along which you are tempted to ration care, and say it between verse 5 and the refrain where it belongs.

### Verse 6 — the three refusals

```
su mawha miona wei shia peshu.
OPT NONE person DAT 3SG lie.
(May no person lie to another.)

su mawha miona shia thiku nila.
OPT NONE person 3SG small see.
(May no one see another as small.)

su mawha miona lue korua thero wei shia peloma pula.
OPT NONE person ABL heart fire DAT 3SG harmful wish.
(May no one, from the fire of the heart, wish harm upon another.)
```

The wish-shape flips from blessing to refusal, and the grammar of the flip matters: subject `mawha miona`, *no person*, still under `su`. The language holds a stronger tool — canon's modal negation `na ma`, *must not* — and the text declines it three times in a row. Even its prohibitions are hopes. Nothing in this text is forbidden to anyone; some things are wished away from everyone, which is a different act, and the only one consistent with verse by verse leaving every listener free.

Two readings to secure before moving on. First, `thiku nila` — hear it against verse 3's `peloma nila`: the same seeing-as pattern, now naming contempt as an act of the *seer*. The grammar contrast is the whole ethics: `shia thiku nai.` says they are small; `mia shia thiku nila.` says I see them small, and locates the smallness in me. Second, `lue korua thero` — the ablative names anger as a place one acts *from*, not a thing one is. The compound is canonized (`documents/compounds.md`); the preposition is the mercy.

**Micro-drill:** say the confession the ablative makes possible — `mia lue korua thero to haolu.` (I spoke from heart-fire.) — and notice what the sentence does not say: it does not say *I am*. That is a sentence for the journal (Pamphlet 3, Part 7), the day it is true.
