# Part 8: Misreadings

A reader's companion owes you the errors of *reading*, and they are not the errors of production. Every one below is a way an English-trained eye makes the text say something it carefully does not.

### Misreading 1: `su` as a soft command

*"May they speak gently"* read as *"they should speak gently."* The English word *should* smuggles obligation back into the one text built to refuse it. `su` claims nothing and demands nothing; it is not a polite command, it is a different act. The test: a command can be disobeyed. Nothing anyone does can disobey `su shia moli haolu.`: the wish simply stands, unbroken, which is why the text can wish things for the unborn and the unseen without absurdity. If your inner translation contains *should*, *must*, or *ought* anywhere `su` stands, re-read Part 2; the text has exactly one *must*, and it spent it in the first line on its own practitioner.

### Misreading 2: the seer in the seen

`[rena se nila]` read as *the one who sees*. The `se` is doing the whole job: PASS flips the clause, and the phrase is *that which is seen*. Miss it and verses 4–5 invert: the text becomes a wish for observers instead of a wish that reaches the observed and the unobservable alike. Doctrine: manual ch16 §1; drill: Part 3. The pair to burn in: `[rena nila]` heads toward a seer; `[rena se nila]` is the seen; `[rena se ma nila]` is the unseen, three particles deep and completely regular.

### Misreading 3: hunting for "beings"

Somewhere in the second reading, the English-trained mind goes looking for the noun (*beings, creatures, sentient things*) and starts treating `theula [rena lima nai]` as a paraphrase deployed to cover a hole. Read the text's own findings: the clause is not the workaround, it is the design. A noun for "beings" would carve the world into the class it names; *all that is alive* refuses the carving and stays checkably true at the edges (the text's inventory verses exist to check it). Phi did not fail to have this word. It declined to.

### Misreading 4: the compounds as verdicts

`korua thero` read as *an angry person's anger*; `thiku nila` read as *being a contemptuous person*; `remo kire` read as *dogma, that thing others have*. All three compounds are observations of acts and states, and their grammar says so: the fire takes `lue`, a source one acts *from* and can be free *of*; the smallness lives in `nila`, the seeing, not in the seen or the seer's nature; the shapes are things a heart *holds* and can put down. Read as verdicts, verse 6 becomes an indictment of other people. Read as grammar, it stays what it is: a wish about acts, from which anyone, including the reader, may be free.

### Misreading 5: the fragments as broken Phi

`wei leo waero.` has no verb, and neither do the inventory pairs. A learner fresh from the primer's full sentences may hear errors. They are the text breathing: lists and directions spoken as pure relations, each preposition announcing its object and nothing else claimed. Pauses are free in this language and no pause changes a meaning, so recitation gives each fragment its breath at no doctrinal cost. Do not repair them into sentences when you recite; their verblessness is what lets the blessing move without asserting anything on the way.

### Misreading 6: the gap log as apology

The table at the text's end, read as a list of failures: as if a finished language would have needed no log. Backwards. The log is transmutation working in the open: what was missing, what was built, what was later coined, and the one place a doctrine was set down deliberately, on the record. Where the log says a later word exists (`thowia`, born; `ruemi`, lie down) and the text's phrase *stands as chosen poetry*, you are watching the language refuse to retrofit its first text into blandness. The log is the text's evidential: it tells you how it knows what it says. Phi does not ask its texts to be seamless. It asks them to be exact about how they are not.

### Misreading 7: racing

Reading all ten verses in one sitting, the way you finished the fable. The fable is a story; this is an instrument, and the capstone's counsel (a little each day) is not pacing advice but part of the semantics: a text about sustaining a practice, absorbed in a way that is one. The tenth verse means differently to someone who arrived at it over ten days. The constraints are the teaching; the tempo is one of the constraints.
