# Part 3: The refrain: all that lives

Phi has no word for "beings," and the text's own findings treat that as a discovery, not a gap: the headless relative does the work better. This part makes sure you can read the construction at speed, because the text's most repeated line is built from it:

```
su theula [rena lima nai] towe nai.
OPT UNIV [REL alive be] well be.
(May all that lives be well.)
```

### Reading it apart

`rena` opens a describing clause; `lima nai` is that clause, *is alive*; and where a noun would normally arrive to close the frame (`[rena lima nai] miona`, the person who is alive) nothing does. The clause stands headless, a description with no noun under it, and `theula` quantifies it whole: *all [that is alive]*. The doctrine is manual ch21 and the relative clauses pamphlet, part 4; the reading skill is hearing `rena` open a door and knowing the noun may never come, because the description itself is the referent.

One honesty about the square brackets: they are scaffolding, printed by the text as a reading aid, and they are not Phi punctuation: nothing marks the clause edges in speech except `rena` at one end and whatever arrives, or does not, at the other. When you recite, the brackets are silent; when you are fluent, they are gone.

### The refrain's relatives

The text builds its whole cast of referents this way, and verse by verse you will meet:

```
[rena se nila] sola [rena se ma nila].
[REL PASS see] DISJ [REL PASS NEG see].
(The seen or the unseen.)
```

`se` is the passive (manual ch16 §1), and inside a relative it flips who the clause is about: `[rena nila]` would head toward *the one who sees*; `[rena se nila]` is *that which is seen*. The unseen (`se ma nila`) is a thing English needs a fossilized participle for, and Phi builds live from three particles.

```
[rena wea lioru shua]
[REL toward life come]
(those coming toward life)
```

The unborn, routed through `wea`: motion toward without promised arrival, exactly the preposition honesty requires of a future that is not guaranteed.

### Headed and headless, side by side

The text's first line keeps its noun (`[rena shea ruela sano] miona`, the *person* who knows the path of peace) because the opening verse is about a particular practitioner. The refrain drops the noun because its wish refuses to be about any particular kind of thing. Headed narrows; headless opens. The text uses each exactly where its scope needs it, which is the fluency this part is drilling toward: reading the presence or absence of the head noun as meaning, not as syntax.

### Drill: build the referents

Produce each in Phi; answers below.

1. The one who tells the stories. (The primer's riddle game owns this one.)
2. The one who gave seeds to the earth.
3. That which is not seen.
4. All that comes toward life.
5. The one who guides.

Answers: 1. `[rena lo nophi shane] miona`: primer 22's own riddle, headed, because the answer is a person. 2. `[rena wei muila thinoe to loa] miona`: the next riddle in the same game. 3. `[rena se ma nila]`: headless; the whole point is not knowing what kind of thing is there. 4. `theula [rena wea lioru shua]`: headless under `theula`, the refrain's own machinery. 5. `rena kulo miona`: canon's standing example: the role refused as a noun, alive as a clause.
