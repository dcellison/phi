# Part 1: The first text

The primer's capstone calls the texts shelf a door, and this text the language's heart. Before walking in, three things about the room.

### It is a transmutation, not a translation

The text rebuilds the Karaniya Metta Sutta from Phi concepts rather than mapping English words onto Phi ones: the doctrine of that method lives in `documents/language_guide.md` §6 and manual ch24, and its receipts live at the bottom of the text itself, in the gap log. Read the gap log as part of the text, not as an apology appended to it: every place the lexicon had no word, the log records what was built instead (heart-fire for anger, *coming toward life* for the unborn) and every place the source's doctrine sits outside Phi's worldview, the log says so plainly. The text ends on the path of peace instead of rebirth, and tells you it chose to. Honesty about how you know is this language's oldest habit; the gap log is that habit applied to how a text knows.

### How to use this companion

Keep the text open beside this pamphlet; the companion quotes every verse but replaces none of them. The primer's own reading rules apply doubly here:

- **Aloud, always.** This is a recitation text. Reading it silently is reading the sheet music and skipping the song.
- **A little each day.** The capstone's counsel, and the household's method. One verse per sitting is not slow; it is the tempo the text was built for.
- **Gloss lines are scaffolding.** Use them on the first pass, cover them on the second. The pamphlet's goal is their removal.
- When the romanization starts to feel easy, switch the texts shelf to tengwar and read the verse again. Same text, second door.

### The shape of the whole

Ten verses, one arc. The text opens by describing its practitioner (one relative clause, announced before its person). It wishes them character, then contentment. It widens: the wish goes to all that lives, then to an inventory built precisely to omit no one: fragile or strong, seen or unseen. It refuses three harms. Then it turns, for the first time, to *you*, and hands over the famous image: the parent, the only child, the heart with no boundary. It tells you how to sustain the practice in every posture, and it closes with the wishing done, three plain statements about the one who sees clearly, ending on the same two words it opened with: `shea ruela`, the path of peace.

And under the arc, an engine with exactly two settings, which the next part is about: the text contains one `na` (one *must*) and nineteen `su`. It obliges its practitioner once, to practice. Everything else it hopes.
