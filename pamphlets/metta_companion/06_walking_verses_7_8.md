# Part 6: Walking verses 7–8: the turn to you

### Verse 7 — the boundless heart

```
phea [phao ta lopia shorupo].
AS [parent one child protect].
(As a parent protects their only child.)

su thia [mawha norui] korua phelu.
OPT 2SG [no boundary] heart hold.
(May you hold a heart of no boundary.)

su thia theula [rena lima nai] theama.
OPT 2SG UNIV [REL alive be] care.
(May you care for all that lives.)
```

Stop at the second word of the second line. `thia`. Six verses of wishes have gone to `shia`, to `theula`, to `mawha miona` — to them, to all, to no one — and the reader has been safely watching hope travel outward. At the text's most famous image, it turns around. The largest capacity ever wished for is wished on *you*, exactly at the moment you were most comfortably a spectator. Read the verse again knowing that; it does not feel the same.

The grammar of the turn is worth its own pass. `phea` hangs the whole comparison in the air before the wish it shapes — announce the *as*, then deliver the *may* — so that by the time your heart is asked for, the parent and the only child are already standing in it. The same equative carries the language's other great universal (the human-rights text: `theula miona phea lomea wiso na lothea.` — all people, as siblings, must love one another), and it takes a clause as readily as a noun; nothing else in the reference row does more work with two syllables. And `[mawha norui] korua` — *no-boundary heart* — is the possessor-first pattern from your first week of Phi, holding the sutta's central image in nine syllables.

**Micro-drill:** `su thia mawha norui korua phelu.` is this text's sentence to own for life. Say it until it is one breath. The brackets, as always, were never audible.

### Verse 8 — radiating

```
su thia wei theula muila lothea loa.
OPT 2SG DAT UNIV earth love give.
(May you give love to the whole earth.)

wei leo waero. wei phou nulo. wei theula noa.
DAT ABOVE sky. DAT BELOW deep. DAT UNIV position.
(To the sky above. To the deep below. To every place.)

su thia lue korua thero ralu nai.
OPT 2SG ABL heart fire free be.
(May you be free from the fire of the heart.)
```

Hear the first line with the primer in your ear. The household's oldest sentence-shape is *give to the earth*: `no wei muila thinoe loa`, thinoe's own teaching — seeds to the earth, water to the earth. Verse 8 picks up that exact construction — `wei … loa` — and puts `lothea` where the seeds were. Love, in this text, is not a feeling radiated abstractly; it is a thing *given*, in the grammar the language uses for bread, for flowers, for water, for gifts. The gardening sentence grew up. This is what transmutation means, one construction at a time.

Then three fragments with no verbs — prepositions as poetry, each relation announced before its object, each direction its own breath. And the verse closes by returning verse 6's fire, this time from the release side: `ralu` with the ablative, free *from* — the same mercy of the source reading, completed.

**Micro-drill:** write your own three directionals in the verse's shape — `wei … .` three times — for where your love actually has to travel: a room, a distance, a person's direction. Then say verse 8 whole, yours included, and notice the fragments carry it without a single verb missing anyone.
