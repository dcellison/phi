# Part 2: The engine: `su`

`su` is a Slot 0 particle: it stands at the very front and frames everything after it as a wish (manual ch11 §3). The primer taught you its siblings: `wa` frames a question, `no` a request, `pi` politeness. `su` is the one it saved for the texts, and this text runs almost entirely on it.

### The one must

Before the wishing starts, the text spends its only obligation:

```
[rena shea ruela sano] miona welao na thole.
[REL peace path know] person good NEC practice.
(The one who knows the path of peace must practice goodness.)
```

One `na`, first verse, aimed at the only person the text ever obliges: its own practitioner. Everything that follows (nineteen `su`, count them yourself) is hope. The text does not order the world to be well; it does not even order *you* to love it. It requires one thing, practice, and hopes the rest into the room.

### Why hope, grammatically

Manual ch23 §5 teaches the register: for the largest requests, frame a wish instead of a question: the optative *does not even ask; it hopes aloud, and lets the listener volunteer*. This text makes the largest request a sentence has ever carried (that every living thing be well) and so it never once leaves the optative to make it. A command can be disobeyed and a question declined; a wish leaves everyone entirely free, which is the only footing love can honestly stand on. The grammar is the ethics.

And notice what `su` costs the speaker: nothing to prove. The evidential system (Pamphlet 3) has no purchase on a wish, because a wish claims nothing about the world, which is why the journal triad ends its three lines *witness, feeling, wish*. `su` is the mood whose honesty is tested nowhere but in the wisher.

### The distances of one wish

The same wish, at three ranges:

```
no moli haolu.
IMP gentle speak.
(Speak gently.)

su thia moli haolu.
OPT 2SG gentle speak.
(May you speak gently.)

su shia moli haolu.
OPT 3SG gentle speak.
(May they speak gently.)
```

The first instructs a listener. The second blesses one. The third (the sutta's own line) blesses someone who is not even present to hear it, which is the reach that makes the text possible: most of its wishes are for the absent, the unseen, the not yet born.

### Drill: hear the engine

1. Open the complete text and count the `su` aloud: there are nineteen. Then find the verse that has none, and read it twice. (It is the last one. Part 7 is about why the wishing stops there.)
2. Convert each request into the sutta's mood: `no sheluo.`; `no wei muila phialu loa.`; `no shea ruela thole.`
3. Take `su shia towe nai.` and walk it inward: say it for a stranger, then with `thia` for a friend across the table, then (the primer's oldest lesson) decide what remains when the wish needs no words at all.

Answers for 2: `su thia sheluo.`; `su thia wei muila phialu loa.`; `su thia shea ruela thole.` Each conversion trades an instruction for a freedom, and that trade, made nineteen times, is the whole text.
