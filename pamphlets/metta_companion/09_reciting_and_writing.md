# Part 9: Reciting and writing

The exercises of a reader's companion are the two things readers of this text have always done with it: say it, and extend it. Parts B and C have a key; A and D are between you and the text.

## Part A: The recitation ladder

Ten sittings, matching the text's own tempo. The standing rules: aloud, full hiatus on every vowel pair (`su.la.e`-style care throughout), a genuine breath at every period, and the fragments given their own beats. Hurried Phi is casual by definition, and this is the least casual text the language owns.

1. The refrain only: `su theula [rena lima nai] towe nai.` and its `siora phaelo` variant, from memory before you sleep.
2. Verse 1. The opening relative until the brackets are inaudible.
3. Verse 2, then 1–2 together.
4. Verse 3, then 1–3. The refrain arrives; you already own it.
5. Verses 4–5, then 1–5. The pairs get their breaths.
6. Verse 6, then 1–6. The refusals stay wishes in your mouth: if they start sounding like laws, slow down.
7. Verse 7, then 1–7. The turn: mark the first `thia` however your voice marks such things.
8. Verse 8, then 1–8, your own three directionals silently included or not.
9. Verses 9–10, then 1–10. Let the last verse's flatness be audible: the wishing is over; say the statements as statements.
10. The whole text, once, at the pace of someone with nowhere else to be. Then once more from the tengwar, where it looks the way it has always sounded: nothing taller than anything else.

## Part B: Parse at reading speed

The brackets below have been removed. Restore them aloud (say where each relative opens and closes) then name what each marked particle is doing.

11. `su theula rena lima nai towe nai.`
12. `su shia rena lo phue peloma nila thena ma phoa.`
13. `rena se ma nila`: name the work of `se` and of `ma`.
14. `ha shea menui nai.` No relative here: name the mood, and what is absent.

## Part C: Translate, keeping the mood's force

15. `su mawha miona shia thiku nila.`
16. `wei theula noa.`
17. `mia henoi sano.`
18. `shia shea ruela thole.`

## Part D: Your household's triad

Write three wishes of your own in the text's three shapes, then keep them where your journal lives:

- One for a named person, announced properly (Pamphlet 5 owns the machinery): `su ne … .`
- One for a headless relative of your own drawing: choose the description honestly, since it decides who your wish can reach.
- One refusal in the `su mawha` shape: the harm you would wish away from everyone, forbidden to no one.

One possible household's triad, for shape only:

```
su ne sulae towe nai.
OPT NAME sulae well be.
(May sulae be well.)

su theula [rena mua thepalu lima nai] siora phaelo.
OPT UNIV [REL LOC garden alive be] joy feel.
(May all that lives in the garden feel joy.)

su mawha miona sonu nai.
OPT NONE person alone be.
(May no one be alone.)
```

There is no key for Part D. Read yours aloud a season from now; a wish you still mean is the only grade this part gives.

## Answer key

**Part B.**

11. `su theula [rena lima nai] towe nai.`: the relative opens at `rena`, closes headless before `towe`; `theula` quantifies the whole clause.
12. `su shia [rena lo phue peloma nila] thena ma phoa.`: opens at `rena`, closes at its head `thena`; `lo phue` is the clause's own subject; `ma` stands in Slot 1 against `phoa`, denying the doing, not the wisdom.
13. `se` flips the clause to its patient (the *seen*, not the seer) and `ma` denies the seeing: the unseen. Passive first, negation against the verb, completely regular.
14. A plain statement: no `su`, no evidential, nothing. The absence is the register: settled knowledge, unmarked, exactly as verse 10 will speak.

**Part C.**

15. "May no one see another as small." Not *no one should*: nothing is forbidden; a harm is wished away from everyone.
16. "To every place." A fragment, and complete: the relation announced, nothing asserted.
17. "I know enough." The quantifier as a knowable; say it only on days it is true.
18. "They walk the path of peace." Bare, and meant to be: the text's last fact, in the register facts deserve.
