# nophi lue mawha lokue — News from Nowhere, ch. 3: The Guest House and Breakfast Therein (in progress)

*The narrator steps indoors, and the utopia comes down to table height: a hall whose good building gives the feeling of space and freedom, three young hosts who greet a stranger like a friend home from a long journey, a breakfast given without ceremony, and, carved behind the honored table, a writing that stands exactly where the narrator's own past stood. Two words are coined, both for the table and garden the whole novel keeps returning to: `napa` (bread) and `perola` (rose). The narrator takes his name here, the name the book knows him by: `ne phemi`, Guest, a man named by his welcome, staying in the guest-house that shares it. The beautiful housekeeper is named at Morris's own moment for her, `ne luwae` (Graceful); the golden dustman bears his joke-name `ne solai` (Gold) over a deliberately plain true name, `ne keruko` (Sturdy). And where Morris sorts the hosts by sex, Phi cannot and does not: the narrator's close attention survives whole, and the sorting goes the way of the refused vocabulary.*

*Each block below carries four lines: the Phi sentence, its word-by-word gloss, a back-translation into English, and Morris's own original wording, so a reader can see exactly what the transmutation kept and what it changed.*

---

**Notes:** the house is read from the water-side in, and the transmutation keeps the reading order. Brick is the Babel text's own `mueri kerou` (clay-stone), and the lead roof is `tumoa keluo`, the heavy metal, the same carve that named the lead-works in the last chapter: the metal Phi never names is described by its weight. Tracery is what the eye verifies, stone shaped into threads (`kerou sima kire`), and the frieze is baked clay by way of the kitchen's own verb: `rena se pharelo mueri`, clay that has been cooked. The frieze runs (`wepu`) along the wall the way Morris's does. What the narrator recognizes in those figures, and why he knows them so particularly well, Morris leaves unsaid, and the Phi leaves it just as unsaid.

**Notes:** marble mosaic composes as what a floor of it is, many small smooth stones built together; the open timber roof is a roof that shows its wooden bones (`lo wolea tholau`), the skeleton Morris's word "open" invites the eye to see. The arches are the last chapter's `kerou loriphi`, stone rainbows, and here they do work no dead stone does in English: they guide (`kulo`) into the chambers. Fresco becomes the narrator's own guess in plain materials, pigment laid on soft clay, held by `remo` because Morris marks it as a guess. The famous closing sentence keeps its whole shape: the hall's gift is a feeling of space and freedom (`tholu nela ralu`), the feeling makes the heart rise, and good building gives it habitually (`ro`), but only to the one who is without fear and habitually uses their eyes; both habits ride the same particle, because Morris's sentence is about practiced attention, not luck. Crosby Hall yields to the honest comparison the narrator can still make, the great halls he had known.


**Notes:** this section carries the chapter's deepest refusal, and it should be named plainly. Morris's narrator sorts the hall by sex: these are "the first of the sex I had seen," clothed "like women," compared against "the male men." Canon refuses gendered person-words outright, so none of that sorting can be said, and the transmutation does not smuggle it back in through a composed workaround. What survives is everything the sentence actually observes: the newness of these three among everyone met so far, the close and deliberate attention, the judgment that gardens, buildings, and friends could be no better. The armchair joke survives whole because `meipa` (seat) was already in the lexicon: no one had stuffed these people full the way a seat is stuffed, though in the narrator's own world many people habitually are. The dress stands between two old worlds' shapes and follows neither, the fourteenth century dissolving into `serao punoa` exactly as the last chapter's costume note did. The handshake scene keeps its simile at full length, a friend only now returned from a long journey, and the glance at the narrator's clothes is what askance honestly is, inquiring eyes he could not help sensing.

**Notes:** `perola` (rose) is coined here, at the flowers' first entrance onto the table the novel will keep setting: the rose recurs across the book, and this same section hands it a second image within the hour, lips as red as the roses she had brought in. The word opens on `peloru` (flower), the family it crowns. `napa` (bread) is the second coin, two syllables off `napuro` (grain) because bread belongs to the daily round, and the two kinds Morris names survive at full weight: the farmhouse loaf keeps all four of its adjectives stacked before it, and the pipe-stems become thin breads shaped like little flutes (`phea thiku phui`), with Turin dissolving into the far southern town where such bread was eaten. The strawberries are red berries (`rulo mirulo`) on a great leaf, the cabbage generalized and the berry kept at species-list distance per the species test; the blackbirds, though, compose exactly (`lo nuko pelori`, the black birds), because the joke needs the birds themselves, racing the housekeeper to the fruit. Her little speech keeps its engine: the thought of the berries fell out of her head (`lue mia koma to lepa`) when the stranger climbed into the boat, the same falling the last chapter's relief used. The buttery is the food-place; the delicate glass is a glass bowl made with care, standing in the table's middle.

Two carve-notes: Morris's "fine lady," a rank-and-gender figure Phi refuses twice over, becomes the behavior it names, some people never tell their own years, and I am not that person; and the proverb "a woman is as old as she looks" is told of a person, `miona`, the only citizen Phi's grammar knows, with the reportative `ti` marking it as the received saying it is. Her parting keeps the book she began yesterday, the work she means to finish, and the farewell inside `pholeni`; and the sun she takes from the table as she goes is credited, as Morris credits it, to an old storyteller who wrote of such taking.

## What the transmutation changed

*Gap log: two new words, both cleared of collisions before coining. `napa` (bread), two syllables opening on the `na-` of `napuro` (grain), its parent: the grain made home, the most given of foods, coined because bread belongs to the daily round and this chapter dwells on it kind by kind, from the dark farmhouse loaf to the flute-thin crusts. `perola` (rose), three syllables opening on the `pe-` of `peloru` (flower), the family it crowns: the layered garden flower with the guarded stem, coined because the rose recurs across the whole novel and twice in this hour, first heaped on the breakfast table, then lending its red to a woman's lips. Everything else composes: the hall is `lona lokue` (the meeting-place); the arches remain `kerou loriphi` (stone rainbows) and here they guide (`kulo`) into the chambers; marble mosaic is many small smooth stones built into a floor; the open timber roof shows its wooden bones (`lo wolea tholau`); the frieze is cooked clay (`rena se pharelo mueri`) holding person-shapes; fresco is pigment laid on soft clay; tracery is stone thread-work; the lead roof is the heavy metal, as the lead-works were; blackbirds are `lo nuko pelori` (the black birds); strawberries are red berries (`rulo mirulo`) on a great leaf; the buttery is the food-place; pollards are trees whose heads are cut, and the commoners' rights the old sharing-custom (`serao phowe keno`); cobwebs are the head's little nets; the philistines are `rena mioru thiku nila lo miona`, the people who see beauty small, the registry's contempt compound turned outward; the lie is `peshu` with a little truth for its guardian (`thema`); mathematics stays `tawi remo` and gains its cousin the blowpipe, `thero whesu keli`, the fire-blowing device; flattery is over-sweet speech (`ru kaelo haolu`); being moved is the heart's motion (`korua roke`); affectation is watching oneself (`miso nila`), so its absence is moving without self-seeing; the Golden Dustman is `solai muhena miona`; local colour is the time's color (`thimu welisha`); reins are the horse's ties; and the grey horse, the sandy hair, the dark blue cloth all ride the color rule.*

*The names land at Morris's own naming moments, under the ratified policy: the narrator himself becomes `ne phemi` (Guest), the family name that is also his condition, chosen in the same breath in which Morris chose it, with "add William to it if you please" kept as the offer of a second name and released as the author's untranslatable self-portrait; Annie is `ne luwae` (Graceful), named in Boffin's mouth; Boffin is `ne solai` (Gold), the joke, over `ne keruko` (Sturdy), the plain true name standing where "Henry Johnson" stands; Jim the horse-keeper is `ne woru` (Keeper). Places dissolve into their functions as ruled: Epping Forest into the great forest at whose edge the narrator was born, Walthamstow and Woodford into villages the sentence never needed, Queen Elizabeth's Lodge and High Beech into the very old house and the high place, the Corporation of London into the great city's council, Bloomsbury into the great town's middle, Turin into a far southern town, Crosby Hall into the great halls the narrator had known, Oxford's High Table into the elders' table, Europe into these lands. Dickens becomes the old stories, Scott an old storyteller, and Morris's Latin tag "Ne quid nimis" an old saying that happens to be Phi's own doctrine: nothing must be too much.*

*Three refusals carry structural weight. The gendered sorting of the hall, "the first of the sex," "clothed like women," "the male men," "a woman is as old as she looks," "well, lads," "both men and women," cannot be said in a language that refuses gendered person-words, and it is not smuggled back in: what survives is the newness of these three among all yet met, the deliberate attention, the armchair joke (which needed only `meipa`), and a proverb told of persons. The ranks fall with the genders: the "fine lady" becomes the behavior she names (some people never tell their years), the baron a gold-wrapped man of a far older world, the senator an elder-councillor, the "sir" a courtesy Phi's grammar carries in `pi` instead. Calendar identifiers remain in Morris's source lines rather than becoming Phi numerals: the passage replaces 1955 only at the level of its relevant elapsed interval (`pai ta rei wi lau torua`, near 135 years), renders twenty-five years as near one twenty-seven (`pai ta lau torua`), gives ages in ternary (fifty-six as two twenty-sevens and two; forty-two as twenty-seven, nine, and two threes; twenty as two nines and two; eighty as exact `wi lau wi phoi wi shao wi`), and paraphrases the inscription's "May 1962" as `mua wirae lumae`, at spring's end, while the exact source inscription remains visible.*

*Disclosed simplifications, each a real loss taken knowingly: machine printing and composing flatten into device-work for books, a craft distinction with no Phi press vocabulary yet; the "radical" of the radical cobblers is absorbed into the trampling of custom the sentence itself describes; hornbeam and holly generalize into head-cut trees and dense small growth, lavender into sweet-smelling herbs, the plums into great blue fruits, the balm into an herb whose name the narrator's boyhood knew, since the naming-memory, not the species, is what the scene turns on (the species test holds: none of the five recurs doing image-work); ivory becomes smooth bone, cheeks and lips become face and mouth, the wrist becomes the hand, all body-words Phi deliberately keeps few; and Morris's "eventful morning" is carried as the morning of wonders the chapter has already earned. The fighting whose approach the weaver's book skirts is rendered the only way Phi will ever render it, by the deed: before the time when people did harm to one another. That sentence is the novel's first step toward the war chapters, and the language did not need a single weapon to take it.*
