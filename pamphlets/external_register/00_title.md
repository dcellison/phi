# The marked threshold

## Guest and exact material in Phi

Phi is strongest when it builds an idea from its own concepts. It is honest only if it also admits when the source itself matters: a person's name, a formula, a safety-critical quantity, a quotation, an identifier, or a term under philosophical examination.

This pamphlet teaches the two external frames that make that admission audible:

- `hasha … hasho` adapts a guest to Phi pronunciation without adopting it into the lexicon.
- `patha … patho` preserves an exact payload without interpreting it as Phi.

The threshold is not a warning label. It marks provenance. A term may be welcomed, criticized, quoted, honored, or merely recorded; the frame itself makes none of those judgments.

## What you will be able to do

By the end, you will be able to choose the right layer, adapt a pronounceable guest form, preserve arbitrary exact material safely, place an external atom in a Phi sentence, distinguish exact material from Phi quotation, and explain why carrying a concept is not the same as giving it a core word.
