# Part 6: Pointing: `ha` and `ra`

The guest is shown around, and the pointing words come out. `ha` selects what the speaker treats as near or current; `ra` points beyond the immediate frame:

```
ha shiro mioru nai.
PROX tree beautiful be.
(This tree is beautiful.)
```

```
ra shiro ru whalo nai.
DIST tree INTS large be.
(That tree is very large.)
```

The tree by the door, the tree across the valley. Two words divide the whole field of pointing, and everything finer is said with a locational phrase when the room needs a better map.

### Standing alone as places

With `nai`, the same pair supplies here and there as predicates:

```
mia ha nai.
1SG PROX be.
(I am here.)
```

```
shia ra nai.
3SG DIST be.
(They are there.)
```

Two of the most useful sentences in the language, and each is three words long. Answering a call, placing a companion, promising presence: this little pattern carries all of it.

### Inside the phrase, and alone

Before a noun, the deictic leads the whole phrase:

```
ha lo melu
PROX PL friend
(these friends)
```

Standing with no noun after it, the deictic takes `lo` in front, exactly the way the pronouns do, and the phrase points at a plurality without naming a class. Babel's builders use it for their own bricks:

```
su lo mia lo ha ru thape.
OPT PL 1SG PL PROX INTS burn.
(Let us burn these thoroughly.)
```

The two orders divide the work cleanly: `ha lo thena`, these things, when the noun follows; `lo ha`, these, when nothing does. Hear which job the phrase is doing and the order comes with it.

### Days and years

The pointing field includes time. `ha philo` is this day, today; `ra torua`, that year; and the tense-and-aspect pamphlet has already put `ha philo` at the head of a journal page. Near and far serve the calendar as readily as the room, one more place the two words quietly cover.

### Practice: point the visit

1. This friend.
2. That mountain.
3. I am here. Say it as the whole answer it is.
4. These things, with the noun; then these, without it.

Answers: 1 `ha melu.` 2 `ra tomora.` 3 `mia ha nai.` 4 `ha lo thena`, then `lo ha`. The deictic leads when a noun follows; `lo` leads when the pointing stands alone. Two small orders, two different jobs, no exceptions to remember.
