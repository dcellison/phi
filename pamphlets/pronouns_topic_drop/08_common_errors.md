# Part 8: Common errors

Each of these is an English reflex meeting a Phi rule. The rule wins, and every repair is short.

### Error 1: the fronted possessor

**Wrong for the meaning:** `mia lohau nai.` intended as "my dog."

A bare pronoun opening a predicate clause is the subject, so that sentence means "I am a dog." Ownership as its own claim takes `phelu`: `mia lohau phelu.` A possessor inside a larger phrase reads cleanly: `mia lohau welao nai.`, my dog is good. Give the possessive reading structure, and it is unmistakable.

### Error 2: the lonely reciprocal

**Wrong:** `*mia wiso nila.`

A relation needs at least two, and `wiso` brings no plurality of its own. Build the plural subject first: `lo mia wiso nila.`, we see each other, or coordinate: `mia nela thia wiso lothea.`

### Error 3: the mirror where the meeting belongs

**Wrong for the meaning:** `lo mia miso nila.` intended as "we see each other."

Grammatical, and it means we see ourselves, each gaze returning to its own face. The meeting of eyes is `wiso`: `lo mia wiso nila.` Ask whether each actor receives their own action or the actions cross.

### Error 4: the cold-open drop

**Wrong as an opening:** `shelomu.` beginning a conversation.

Topic-drop spends credit, and an opening line has none: no subject is established, so none is recoverable. Establish first, then lighten: `mia shelu theo. shelomu.` The first sentence pays; the second rides.

### Error 5: the drop over rivals

**Wrong for the listener:** `melu to shua. lopia to shua. to meliho.` intended to say the child sang.

Two referents stand live, and the bare verb leaves the singer to chance. Say the name: `lopia to meliho.` Explicitness here is not heaviness; it is the same care for the listener that allowed the drop while only one candidate stood.

### Error 6: `rena` dropped as if it were a subject

**Wrong:** `*mia to nila peloru mioru nai.` intended as "the flower that I saw is beautiful."

Dropping an established subject is topic-drop; dropping the relativizer is a different operation, and the grammar does not offer it. Without `rena`, the listener receives content words with no signal that a whole clause is standing in a noun's position. Keep the marker: `rena mia to nila peloru mioru nai.`

### Error 7: the pointing order reversed

**Wrong:** `*lo ha melu` intended as "these friends."

Before a noun, the deictic leads the phrase: `ha lo melu.` The `lo`-first order belongs to the deictic standing alone: `lo ha`, these, with no noun after it. Each order has its one job, and the presence or absence of the noun tells you which you are in.
