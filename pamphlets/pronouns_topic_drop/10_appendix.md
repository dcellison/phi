# Appendix: quick reference

## The personal inventory

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| first | `mia` (I, me) | `lo mia` (we, us) |
| second | `thia` (you) | `lo thia` (you all) |
| third | `shia` (another, it) | `lo shia` (they, them) |

One form per person, in every role: subject, object, prepositional object, possessor. Position announces the role.

## The object pair

| Word | Gloss | Position | Says |
|---|---|---|---|
| `miso` | REFL | object | the action returns to each actor |
| `wiso` | RECP | object | the action passes between the actors |

`wiso` requires a plural subject, built with `lo` or `nela` coordination.

## The pointing pair

| Use | Near | Far |
|---|---|---|
| before a noun | `ha melu` (this friend) | `ra tomora` (that mountain) |
| as a place, with `nai` | `mia ha nai.` (I am here) | `shia ra nai.` (they are there) |
| alone, plural | `lo ha` (these) | `lo ra` (those) |
| in time | `ha philo` (today) | `ra torua` (that year) |

Before a noun the deictic leads; standing alone, `lo` leads.

## Working rules

- A bare pronoun opening a predicate clause is the subject; ownership as its own claim takes `phelu`, and a possessor inside a larger phrase reads cleanly.
- Two plurals in one clause keep their seats: the first `lo` phrase is the subject, the second the object.
- `lo mia` gathers the speaker and others; membership is said aloud when it matters, by `nela` coordination or a contrast clause.
- Topic-drop needs a subject established and recoverable; the drop is always optional, and `shia` stands ready when a third person needs saying.
- Frames and relative clauses hand a subject forward exactly as a period does; `rena` itself never drops.
- The moment two referents compete, restore the noun or the pronoun rather than leave the listener to choose.
