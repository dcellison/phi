# Part 2: Case from position

One pair of sentences carries the whole lesson:

```
mia thia nila.
1SG 2SG see.
(I see you.)
```

```
thia mia nila.
2SG 1SG see.
(You see me.)
```

Same two words, traded places, opposite meaning. Where English swaps I for me, Phi moves the unchanged word to a different seat. Subject first, object second, and the form never bends: that is the entire case system, and it is already yours from chapter 10.

### The four seats

| Role | Phi | English |
|---|---|---|
| subject | `mia thia nila.` | I see you. |
| object | `thia mia nila.` | You see me. |
| prepositional object | `thia wei mia shua.` | You come to me. |
| possessor | `mia shelu welao nai.` | My book is good. |

The third row puts `mia` after a preposition, and the fourth puts it before a noun it possesses. Four grammatical relationships, one form, position doing all the work.

### The possessor needs company

The possessor row rewards a careful ear. A bare pronoun opening a predicate clause is the subject, so:

```
mia lohau nai.
1SG dog be.
(I am a dog.)
```

That sentence says what it says. The possessive reading arrives only when the structure forces it, and two structures do. Ownership as a claim of its own uses `phelu`:

```
mia lohau phelu.
1SG dog hold.
(I have a dog.)
```

And a possessor inside a larger phrase reads cleanly because the sentence has somewhere else to put the subject meaning:

```
mia lohau welao nai.
1SG dog good be.
(My dog is good.)
```

No ambiguity survives: "I am a good dog" would order the words `mia welao lohau nai.`, so the grammar has already separated the readings for you. Hear the two shapes a few times and the right one starts arriving unbidden.

### Practice: trade the seats

1. I know them.
2. They know me.
3. You come to me.
4. My book is good. Then say what `mia shelu nai.` claims instead.

Answers: 1 `mia shia sano.` 2 `shia mia sano.` 3 `thia wei mia shua.` 4 `mia shelu welao nai.` And `mia shelu nai.` is the subject reading, "I am a book": a fine sentence for a story, and a reminder that the first bare pronoun of a predicate clause has claimed the subject's seat.
