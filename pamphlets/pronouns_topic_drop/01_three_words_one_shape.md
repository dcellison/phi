# Part 1: Three words, one shape

A knock, the door opens, and the visit begins with the two people it takes to have one:

```
mia thia nila.
1SG 2SG see.
(I see you.)
```

```
wa thia shea nai.
Q 2SG peace be.
(Are you at peace?)
```

`mia` is the speaker and `thia` the addressee, and each keeps its form wherever the sentence puts it. The greeting question is the oldest use of the pair: one person locating another and asking after their state, four words in all.

### The third word

`shia` brings in one more being or thing, anyone or anything the two of you speak of:

```
shia shua.
3SG come.
(They arrive.)
```

```
mia shia sano.
1SG 3SG know.
(I know them.)
```

English needs to pick he, she, it, or they before the sentence can move; `shia` mentions the person without describing them, and the conversation supplies who is meant. When more identification would help, a noun or a name does that work, and the naming pamphlet gives names their own ground. The pronoun's job is presence, and it does that job with one word for everyone.

### One shape, heard

All three pronouns are hiatus disyllables: a stressed first syllable, then a separate open `a`. Say them slowly, `mi.a`, `thi.a`, `shi.a`, and hear the family resemblance the grammar built in. The `th` of `thia` asks the tongue for a moment of deliberate placement, and some speakers like it that the word for you begins with a sound that cannot be said carelessly.

### Practice: the door

1. I thank you.
2. You know me.
3. Are you at peace? Ask it of the friend on the step.
4. They arrive. Announce the third guest.

Answers: 1 `mia thia whaline.` 2 `thia mia sano.` 3 `wa thia shea nai.` 4 `shia shua.` Four sentences into the visit and every person present already has a word, the same word they will keep all evening.
