# Part 7: Having and holding

Possession lives twice in the grammar: inside the phrase, and as a claim of its own. Inside the phrase, the possessor simply comes first:

```
mia serao shelu
1SG old book
(my old book)
```

The pronoun keeps its ordinary form, the possessor leads, and the rest of the phrase follows in its usual order. The sequence is fixed: `serao mia shelu` would break the canonical nesting, and a contrast worth emphasis takes the focus particle or its own clause rather than a scrambled phrase.

### Having as a predicate

To assert the having itself, the possessed thing becomes the object of `phelu`:

```
mia lohau phelu.
1SG dog hold.
(I have a dog. This dog is mine.)
```

```
mia wi themo wiru phelu.
1SG two THING.CLF basket hold.
(I have two baskets.)
```

The verb names present holding, control, or keeping. The pronouns pamphlet has already drilled the neighboring trap, so one reminder suffices here: a bare clause-initial pronoun is the subject, and `mia lohau nai.` says "I am a dog." The having claim belongs to `phelu`, and the phrase-internal possessor belongs before its noun.

### Nesting, and its natural limit

Possessors nest:

```
mia melu shelu
1SG friend book
(my friend's book)
```

`mia melu` is itself a phrase, and it possesses the book entire. One level reads effortlessly. Beyond that, the ear starts doing bookkeeping: a chain like community-parent-friend-book is grammatical and asks the listener to resolve three relations before the noun arrives. Two short clauses carry the same facts in comfort, and choosing them is a courtesy the grammar leaves to you.

### Practice: hold it rightly

1. Your garden.
2. My friend's book.
3. I have two baskets, counted and classified.
4. Say "I have a dog," and say what the near-miss sentence would claim instead.

Answers: 1 `thia thepalu.` 2 `mia melu shelu`, the inner phrase possessing the outer noun. 3 `mia wi themo wiru phelu.` 4 `mia lohau phelu.`; the near miss, `mia lohau nai.`, would make you the dog, which is why the having verb earns its keep.
