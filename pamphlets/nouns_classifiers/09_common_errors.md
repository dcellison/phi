# Part 9: Common errors

The phrase has few rules, so its errors are few, repeated, and easy to name. These seven cover nearly everything a learner writes wrong in a noun phrase.

### Error 1: two quantity markers

**Wrong:** `*wi lo melu` intended as "the two friends."

One phrase, one quantity claim. The numeral carries it: `wi melu.` The same repair serves every pairing: `*sheloi lo melu` becomes `sheloi melu`, `*theula lo melu` becomes `theula melu`. English layers its quantity words; Phi elects one.

### Error 2: a classifier on a quantifier

**Wrong:** `*sheloi himo melu` intended as "many people who are friends."

Classifiers ride on counts, and a quantifier judges instead of counting. `sheloi melu` says many friends complete. When the human category itself needs saying, count: `ta shao himo melu`, or give the category its own sentence.

### Error 3: the classifier inside the number

**Wrong:** `*ta himo shao melu` intended as "exactly three friends, classified."

The number expression finishes before the classifier begins: `ta shao himo melu.` However many words the count takes, it is one block, and the classifier waits at its end.

### Error 4: the scrambled possessor

**Wrong:** `*serao mia shelu` intended as "my old book."

The possessor leads the phrase: `mia serao shelu.` The canonical order is not a style preference; each position means something, and a possessor placed mid-phrase reads as a different structure, not an elegant emphasis. Emphasis belongs to the focus particle or a clause of its own.

### Error 5: the approximate count read as exact

**Wrong for the claim:** `shao kerou` reported after a careful tally of three.

Bare `shao` claims a scale, roughly a three-group. The tally licenses the exact form: `ta shao kerou.` Both phrases are honest; each belongs to a different state of knowledge, and swapping them misreports what you know.

### Error 6: `ha` worn as "the"

**Wrong for the meaning:** `ha shelu` for every definite book, near or far, present or absent.

`ha` points at what is near or current; it is not an article with a Phi accent. The book across the room takes `ra shelu`; the book identified by ownership takes `mia shelu`; the book already under discussion is usually just `shelu`, context doing what context does. Point when pointing is true, and let the bare noun carry the rest.

### Error 7: `themo` for everything physical

**Wrong for the category:** `*wi themo luphore` intended as "two rivers."

`themo` covers detached and crafted objects: stones, baskets, books, tools. A river, a cloud, or the rain is physical without being that kind of thing, and the counting pamphlet's nature-now drills settle each case. When the category is unclear or beside the point, the best classifier is none: `wi luphore` counts the rivers perfectly.
