# Part 4: The classifier's place

A count can carry one more word: a classifier, which names the broad category Phi assigns to what is counted.

```
ta shao himo melu
one three-group HUM.CLF friend
(exactly three friends, explicitly classified as people)
```

```
wi nophe torua
two ABST.CLF year
(two years, explicitly classified as time units)
```

Four classifiers cover the field: `himo` for people, `lipha` for other living beings, `themo` for detached and crafted objects, `nophe` for concepts, time, and events. The counting pamphlet drills the choosing in full, edge cases included; this part settles what the phrase needs to know, which is where the word goes and when it appears at all.

### After the whole number expression

The classifier stands after the complete number, exact or approximate, and before the noun:

| Without | With | Claim |
|---|---|---|
| `wi shiro` | `wi lipha shiro` | exactly two trees |
| `ta shao kerou` | `ta shao themo kerou` | exactly three stones |
| `shao kerou` | `shao themo kerou` | approximately a three-group of stones |

`ta shao themo kerou` reads left to right as the grammar builds it: the count, then the category, then the thing. The number expression finishes before the classifier begins, however many words the number takes.

### Optional is the rule

Every phrase in this part is equally grammatical without its classifier, and the bare form is not a casual shortcut; optionality is the design. A speaker adds the classifier when the category itself matters to the discussion, and omits it when the category is beside the point, unclear, or not the speaker's call to make. The word foregrounds an analysis. It never grades the speaker's manners.

### Who takes one

Classifiers ride on numerals and on the question `wia`, which asks for the kind of answer a numeral gives. The ordinary quantifiers make a different kind of claim and never take one: `sheloi melu`, never `*sheloi himo melu`. The dividing line is exactness: where a count could stand, a classifier may stand with it.

### Practice: place it, or leave it out

1. Exactly two trees, category made audible.
2. Approximately a three-group of stones, classified as objects.
3. Many friends, and say why no classifier joins the phrase.
4. Exactly one story, classified.

Answers: 1 `wi lipha shiro.` 2 `shao themo kerou.` The approximate number finishes, then the category. 3 `sheloi melu`; a quantifier judges rather than counts, and classifiers ride only on counts. 4 `ta nophe nophi.` One story, held as the abstraction it is, and the phrase would be just as correct with the classifier gone: `ta nophi.`
