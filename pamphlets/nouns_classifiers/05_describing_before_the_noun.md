# Part 5: Describing before the noun

Description is the narrowing art. An adjective stands before its noun, and the two-books morning takes its first real step:

```
serao shelu
old book
(an old book)
```

One word in front, and the newer book on the table drops out of the running.

### Stacking

Descriptors stack, each before the next:

```
serao mioru shelu
old beautiful book
(an old, beautiful book)
```

Phi sets no universal hierarchy among adjective meanings; the order is yours, and it does real work. The earlier descriptor takes the wider scope and the earlier attention, so keep the sequence that groups the qualities the way you mean them. An old beautiful book and a beautiful old book pick out the same volume while framing it differently, which is exactly the kind of choice a speaker gets to make on purpose.

### Every adjective is also its quality

The quality-noun rule doubles the vocabulary at no cost: every adjective is also its own quality noun, form unchanged. `thua` is fair and fairness; `wesoma` is mutual and mutuality; `siloma` is simple and simplicity. Position tells the listener which use is live: before a noun, the word describes; in a noun's own position, it is the quality itself.

```
mia siloma lothea.
1SG simple love.
(I love simplicity.)
```

The adjective walked into the object position and became the thing loved. One lexical entry, two working lives, and the grammar arbitrates by place alone.

### Practice: narrow with description

1. Good food.
2. An old, beautiful book, and say which quality frames the other.
3. Many good friends.
4. Say that you love fairness.

Answers: 1 `welao nuora.` 2 `serao mioru shelu`; the age frames the beauty, an old thing that is beautiful, and reversing the order would frame the age instead. 3 `sheloi welao melu`, the quantity claim first, then the description, then the noun. 4 `mia thua lothea.` The adjective stands in the object position and means the quality entire.
