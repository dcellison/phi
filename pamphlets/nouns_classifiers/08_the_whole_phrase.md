# Part 8: The whole phrase

Every position this pamphlet has taught belongs to one canonical order:

`[Possessor] [Quantifier] [Deictic] [Number or lo] [Classifier] [Descriptors] [Noun]`

No phrase fills every position; the quantity positions are alternatives, and a classifier appears only after a numeral or `wia`. But every phrase you build sits inside this frame, and reading it left to right is reading the reference narrow itself: whose, how judged, where, how many, what kind, what like, and at last what.

### The two-books morning, settled

The morning this pamphlet opened on can now be finished properly. Two books on the table, and the phrase grows only as far as the field demands:

```
shelu
book
(a book)
```

```
serao shelu
old book
(the old book, if only one is old)
```

```
mia ha serao shelu
1SG PROX old book
(this old book of mine)
```

Three words was enough the moment one book on that table was old; the full form waits for the harder morning when age alone cannot decide. This is the discipline of the whole system: the canonical order holds a position for everything, and the speaker fills only the positions the situation calls for.

### Kindness to the ear

A fully loaded phrase is grammatical and heavy. Every word before the noun is a promise the listener must hold open until the noun lands, so a long approach asks for real working memory. When a reference needs many facts, consider spending two sentences instead of one procession: name the thing simply, then say more about it. The phrase narrows; the discourse elaborates; both are Phi.

### Where the phrase meets the particles

Inside real sentences, the Slot 2 particles ride within this same order, the intensifier and focus among them, and the corpus attests phrases as full as `ha lo ru mioru peloru`, these very beautiful flowers. Their nesting has its own fixed sequence, and the three-slots pamphlet drills it; what this pamphlet adds is the frame those particles ride inside.

### Practice: build to order

1. Many good friends.
2. Exactly three beautiful gardens.
3. This old book of mine.
4. All stories, then narrow to the community's stories in a second phrase.

Answers: 1 `sheloi welao melu.` 2 `ta shao mioru thepalu.` The count completes, then the description, then the noun. 3 `mia ha serao shelu.` Possessor, pointer, quality, thing: the canonical order in four words. 4 `theula nophi`, then `sila nophi`; the quantifier claims the whole set, and the possessor phrase carves the community's share of it. Word by word, the field narrows, and the noun arrives exactly where the grammar promised it would.
