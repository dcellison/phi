# Part 3: Counting and quantifying

The storeroom is being taken in hand, and every phrase in it makes a claim. Phi offers a ladder of them, from silence about number to a count a granary keeper could audit:

| Expression | Claim |
|---|---|
| `melu` | friend or friends; number unstated |
| `lo melu` | more than one; no count |
| `ta melu` | exactly one |
| `wi melu` | exactly two |
| `ta shao melu` | exactly three |
| `shao melu` | approximately a three-group |
| `soli melu` | some; an indefinite nonzero number |
| `sheloi melu` | contextually many |

Two rows deserve a careful ear. `ta shao melu` counts exactly three; bare `shao melu` claims only the scale of a three-group, a handful rather than a tally. The exact form and the approximate form are both honest, about different states of knowledge, and choosing between them is the whole skill: say what you counted, estimate what you gauged, and let the phrase show which you did.

### The quantifiers

Where numerals count, quantifiers judge. The set is ten strong:

| Phi | Gloss | Range |
|---|---|---|
| `theula` | UNIV | the whole relevant set |
| `sheloi` | MANY | contextually large |
| `shelami` | MOST | the larger part |
| `soli` | SOME | indefinite, nonzero |
| `phina` | FEW | contextually small |
| `theli` | EACH | one by one |
| `wheli` | ANY | free choice within the set |
| `mawha` | NONE | no member or amount |
| `henoi` | ENOUGH | sufficient for the purpose |
| `wia` | how many | the question |

Each stands directly before its noun: `shelami wiru`, most baskets; `theli lopia`, each child, taken one at a time. `henoi` is the quietly remarkable one: it measures against a purpose instead of a scale, so `henoi sulopa` for one bowl and `henoi sulopa` for a full table are different amounts and the same true claim. And `wia` turns any of these phrases into the question that a numeral will answer.

### Zero and none

Even absence offers two claims. `mu melu` counts zero friends, a numeral's exactness; `mawha melu` quantifies none at all. The count belongs in an audit, the quantifier in an ordinary sentence about an empty bench.

### Practice: claim what you know

Choose the phrase each situation supports.

1. You counted the baskets one by one: three.
2. You glanced at the woodpile: something like a three-group of stones.
3. Soup for the table tonight, and there is sufficient.
4. Ask how many stories the elder holds.

Answers: 1 `ta shao wiru.` The tally happened; say the exact count. 2 `shao kerou.` The glance supports the scale, and the phrase claims no more. 3 `henoi sulopa.` Sufficiency, measured against the meal it serves. 4 `wia nophi.` The question stands where its answer will, and any row of the ladder may answer it honestly.
