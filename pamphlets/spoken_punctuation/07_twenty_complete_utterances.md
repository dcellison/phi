# Part 7: Twenty complete utterances

English keeps its smallest words in the margins. It dresses them in italics, props them up with exclamation marks, and spells the same sound three ways in one novel. Phi took the opposite decision: every one of these sounds is a word, with an entry, a period, and a job no other word does. There are twenty, the smallest of the lexicon's three inventories, and each is a full utterance on its own.

| Word | Meaning | Word | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| `kia` | hello | `nea` | aha |
| `pao` | goodbye | `nuo` | um |
| `lia` | yes | `wao` | wow |
| `tua` | no | `lua` | lovely |
| `mio` | mmhm | `sai` | ouch |
| `hao` | pardon | `halu` | aww |
| `mea` | thanks | `phuo` | phew |
| `soa` | sorry | `pio` | hehe |
| `hei` | hey | `shiu` | shh |
| `teo` | watch out | `hoi` | onward |

Part 6 met six of these doing the exclamation mark's work. The rest of the class deserves the same daylight, because the difference between neighbors is where the meaning lives.

### Meeting and parting

`kia` opens an encounter at any hour and grades nobody by rank. `pao` closes one without predicting how long the separation will last.

```
pao. su thia towe nai.
goodbye. OPT 2SG well be.
(Goodbye. May you be well.)
```

The farewell is complete at its first word; the wish after it is the speaker's own addition, opened by `su` exactly as Part 6 taught.

### Answering

`lia` answers yes and `tua` answers no, each a complete turn. The primer's household runs on them:

```
lia. misheko nulae.
yes. cat sleep.
(Yes. The cat is asleep.)

tua. lopia thalo.
no. child walk.
(No. The child is walking.)
```

Two neighbors keep these honest. `mio` is the listener's sound while someone else is still speaking: it says the words are landing, and it claims neither the floor nor agreement. A nod is not a signature. And `hao` asks for a sentence again, whether the gap was in the hearing or in the sense; the listener need not pretend comprehension. English writes "sorry?" for that job, but `hao` is not an apology, and the difference matters at the door of the next section.

### Feeling aloud

Seven words let feeling speak before a sentence is ready, and each keeps its own territory. `wao` says expectation was exceeded; `nea` says something clicked. Surprise is not understanding:

```
nea. mia sano.
aha. 1SG know.
(Aha. Now I know.)
```

`sai` speaks from the hurt itself; `halu` answers from beside it, warmth for someone else's tenderness without claiming their feeling. `lua` admires the thing, steam lifting from a good meal, a hinge moving cleanly, while `mea` thanks the person who gave it; appreciation and gratitude are different debts. `phuo` releases the breath after the effort ends, and `pio` gives a small laugh its own word, audible playfulness that still cannot make a hurtful joke harmless.

```
sai. mia kipona phaelo.
ouch. 1SG pain feel.
(Ouch. I am in pain.)
```

The cry is complete before the sentence arrives; whether help is wanted belongs to what follows.

### Reaching others

`hei` asks for attention; `teo` says why the attention matters, danger, and Part 6 already put it to work. `shiu` asks the room to settle, a request that grants no authority over other speakers. `hoi` is encouragement while effort is still underway, offered from beside the path rather than above it.

```
shiu. lopia nulae.
shh. child sleep.
(Shh. The child is sleeping.)
```

### Repair and grace

`mea` and `soa` carry the small ceremonies. The primer's market shows thanks folded into an ordinary turn:

```
mea. kona lopia. kia.
thanks. VOC child. hello.
(Thanks. Child, hello.)
```

`soa` says sorry and opens a repair, but the entry itself is plain about the limit: the words and actions that follow must do the repairing. For a small bump, `soa` may be the whole exchange; for a larger harm, it is the first sentence of several.

That leaves `nuo`, the audible um, the one interjection English refuses to admit is a word at all. It holds the floor while a word is found, and either vowel may lengthen to match the wait. English spells it with an ellipsis; Phi hears it as vocabulary, and Part 8 returns to it.

### Practice: the nearest neighbor

Each situation offers two candidates. Choose one and say why its neighbor loses.

1. A friend lifts the lid and the soup smells wonderful: `lua` or `mea`.
2. The elder is mid-story and you want them to continue: `mio` or `lia`.
3. The recipe's confusing step suddenly makes sense: `nea` or `wao`.
4. A loose stone starts sliding toward the path below: `hei` or `teo`.
5. The stuck jar finally opens: `phuo` or `hoi`.
6. You bump the table and spill a guest's tea: `soa` or `sai`.

Answers: 1 `lua`. Nothing has been handed to you yet; the admiration is for the soup, and thanks can follow the bowl. 2 `mio`. It shows the story is landing without taking the floor or promising agreement. 3 `nea`. The step did not exceed expectation; it came loose. 4 `teo`. The attention matters because of danger, and the warning says so. 5 `phuo`. The effort is over; `hoi` belongs while the lid was still fighting. 6 `soa`. The hurt, if any, is the guest's; `sai` would report your own.

Twenty words, none needing an exclamation mark to be heard. English keeps such sounds in the margins; Phi shelved them in the dictionary, where a learner can look each one up and find out exactly what it does not mean.
