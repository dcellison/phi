# Part 8: The marks with no counterpart

Parts 2 through 6 found a Phi word doing the work of an English mark. Five marks are left over: the colon, the semicolon, the parentheses, the dash, and the ellipsis. For these, Phi offers no word, and the reason is worth hearing in each case. Their work was already done, by the grammar, by the sentence boundary, or by the discourse markers of manual chapter 18.

### The colon

An English colon is a promise: what follows will deliver what was just set up. Phi spent its whole grammar on that promise. `wa` promises a question before the proposition arrives, `ne` a name, `lu` a condition, `shola` exact words, `mena` a proposition. A language built to announce before delivering has no leftover work for a colon to do; the mark's one idea is the sentence's standing order.

### The semicolon

The semicolon is English's half-boundary, heavier than a comma, lighter than a period. Phi keeps no silent in-between strength. A boundary is either the full period or an audible join, `nela`, `thona`, or `sola`, and the choice between them is itself information the listener receives. Part 5's warm sun and peaceful wind already showed both; there is no third thing between them to learn.

### The parentheses

English lowers its voice inside brackets, usually to slip in an example or pin down a case. Phi raises the aside to a sentence of its own and names the relation aloud. `phisu` opens the example:

```
lo shiro lima nai. phisu ha shiro thuroa.
PL tree alive be. EXMP PROX tree grow.
(Trees are alive. For example this tree grows.)
```

`shekoi` narrows to the case the speaker means:

```
mia lo shiro lothea. shekoi mia ha serao shiro lothea.
1SG PL tree love. SPEC 1SG PROX old tree love.
(I love trees. Specifically I love this old tree.)
```

What English whispers, Phi says at full voice with the relation named. An aside that does not deserve its own sentence can wait for a better moment; one that does gets a marker the listener can hear.

### The dash

The interrupting dash is the mark of a sentence changing its mind. A Phi sentence changes its mind aloud. `shorela` opens the second try:

```
shia ma haolu. shorela shia maeli nai.
3SG NEG speak. REPH 3SG quiet be.
(They do not speak. In other words they are quiet.)
```

In live speech the same word serves self-correction: finish the sentence or let it stand unfinished, then let `shorela` introduce the repaired version. The complementizers pamphlet drills that recovery for dropped closers; the move is the same everywhere. The aside dash, the pair that works like soft parentheses, takes the `phisu` and `shekoi` treatment above.

### The ellipsis

English gives three dots two different jobs, and Phi splits them. The hesitation is a word: `nuo`, from Part 7, holds the floor audibly while a word is found.

```
nuo. mia remo.
um. 1SG think.
(Um. I am thinking.)
```

The trailing off is not a word, and Phi does not spell it. An unfinished sentence is honestly unfinished; the page records what was said and leaves the silence unspelled, exactly as Part 1 promised. One more English habit deserves a warning here: three dots inside a quotation, marking words silently removed. An exact `shola ... sholo` frame claims the original wording, so a silently shortened quotation breaks the claim it is making. When only part of the wording matters, report the content with `mena ... meno` instead, where no verbatim claim was ever on the table.

### Practice: give the mark its Phi

Say what each English habit becomes in Phi, and which word, if any, does the work.

1. "Trees are alive (this one, for instance, grows)."
2. "The sun is warm; the wind is peaceful."
3. "They don't speak, I mean, they're just quiet."
4. "And then I, um, thought about it."
5. "The plan has one requirement: patience."

Answers: 1 The bracketed aside becomes its own sentence opened by `phisu`; the first example block above is the answer in full. 2 Either two periods or audible `nela`, as in Part 5; the semicolon's in-between weight does not exist to be translated. 3 The correction becomes a new sentence opened by `shorela`, as in the third block. 4 The hesitation is the word `nuo`, its own utterance before the sentence resumes. 5 No word at all: state the plan, then state the requirement, and the grammar's announce-then-deliver order carries the promise the colon was making.

Five marks came asking for work, and every position was filled: the grammar had taken the colon's job, the period and the audible joins the semicolon's, the discourse markers the parentheses' and the dash's, one honest little word and one honest silence the ellipsis's. The page stays quiet. The sentence never was.
