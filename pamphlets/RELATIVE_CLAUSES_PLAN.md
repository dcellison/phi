# Pamphlet Plan: Relative Clauses in Phi

## Overview

**Purpose:** A deep-dive companion to the manual chapter, providing extended explanation, abundant examples, and practice material for learners struggling with Phi's pre-nominal relative clauses.

**Target audience:** Learners who have completed Parts I-IV of the manual and find relative clauses challenging. Also useful for linguistics enthusiasts interested in how Phi handles relativization differently from European languages.

**Estimated length:** 25-35 pages

## Structure

### Part 1: Understanding the Concept (5-7 pages)

#### What is a relative clause?
- A sentence transformed into a description
- The difference between "the old book" and "the book that I read yesterday"
- How languages turn verbs into adjective-like modifiers

#### How English does it
- Post-nominal position: noun first, description after
- Relative pronouns: who, which, that, whom, whose
- The ambiguity problem: "the person who I met who helped me who lives nearby"
- Processing burden: listener must hold the noun while waiting for description to end

#### How Phi does it differently
- Pre-nominal position: description first, noun last
- Single relativizer `rena` for all cases
- Natural bounding: the noun itself closes the clause
- Processing benefit: listener knows they're receiving a description before learning what's described

#### Cross-linguistic perspective
- Japanese, Korean, Turkish: pre-nominal relatives (like Phi)
- Chinese: both strategies available
- Why this correlates with verb-final word order
- The typological logic: modifiers before heads, consistently

### Part 2: The Mechanics of `rena` (4-5 pages)

#### The word itself
- Sound symbolism: rolling `r` connects, grounding `na` anchors
- Gloss: REL (relativizer)
- Category: complementizer (not a pronoun!)

#### Position and scope
- Always clause-initial
- Everything between `rena` and the noun is the description
- Structural formula: `[rena CLAUSE] NOUN`

#### No closer needed
- Why `mena` needs `meno` but `rena` doesn't
- Pre-nominal position creates natural boundary
- The noun IS the closer

#### The gap strategy
- Where the head noun would appear inside the clause
- Gap in subject position: `rena ___ nophi kealo miona` (the person who creates stories)
- Gap in object position: `rena mia ___ nila shelu` (the book that I saw)
- Gap in oblique position: `rena mia ___ mua thalo shelira` (the forest that I walked in)
- No case marking on `rena`: context does the work
