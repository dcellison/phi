# Part 5: Approximation and sufficiency

The manual treats approximation as an important part of the system, and this pamphlet has been building toward it: everything exact you learned in Parts 1–2 helps you recognize when you are making an estimate rather than an exact claim. A scale unit standing bare (no digit in front) means *about that many*:

```
shao philo
three-group day
(some three days)

phoi miona
nine-group person
(nine or so people)

lau shiro
twenty-seven-group tree
(many trees)

rei silero
eighty-one-group star
(a great many stars)
```

All four rows are scale estimates without fixed rounding intervals. Bare `lau` often has the pragmatic force of *many*, and bare `rei` often means *a great many* or *too many to count for this purpose*. Neither says that the quantity is literally uncountable. When no scale estimate is intended, use a quantifier such as `sheloi` (many) instead.

### Exact or about: the judgment

The system offers internal precision through 242 and preserves larger exact values in source material. Its longer forms make some precision conspicuous, but length does not decide truth. Ask: *Do I know the exact value? Does the difference affect action, safety, access, allocation, accountability, or faithful reporting? Is this information mine to disclose? Would an estimate erase something important?*

```
ta shao wi lipha powea.
one three-group two LIFE.CLF egg.
(Five eggs — exact, because the recipe requires five.)

shao lipha powea.
three-group LIFE.CLF egg.
(About a three-group of eggs — an estimate when the precise count is not known or needed.)
```

The first form asserts an exact quantity of five. The second gives only an approximate scale. Either could be accurate or misleading depending on the speaker's knowledge and the practical stakes.

### Quantifiers: less specific quantity claims

Below even the about-scale sits the quantifier row: `sheloi` (MANY), `soli` (SOME), `phina` (FEW), `shelami` (MOST), `theli` (EACH), `wheli` (ANY), `theula` (UNIV), `mawha` (NONE), for when magnitude itself is more than you know or need. And one of them is a philosophy in a word: `henoi` (ENOUGH). Sufficiency is directly sayable, no number attached:

```
henoi sulopa nai.
ENOUGH soup be.
(There is enough soup.)
```

No count appears because the sentence makes a sufficiency judgment directly. That judgment still needs a purpose and affected people: enough for whom, for what, and under what conditions? The manual's meditation chapter also ends a session with the same word standing alone (`henoi.`), where the practitioner makes a personal judgment rather than proving a numerical fact.

### Asking how many, and how much to ask

`wia` (HOW MANY) before a classifier or noun asks the quantity question:

```
wia himo miona so shua.
how many HUM.CLF person FUT come.
(How many people will come?)
```

Note what a Phi speaker hears in the answer's *form*. `ta phoi himo miona` asserts nine; `phoi miona` estimates around a nine-group; `sheloi miona` says many without proposing a scale. These gradations are information. A questioner may still need an exact answer for planning or safety, while privacy or uncertainty may limit what the speaker can provide.

### When precision is the wrong instrument

The philosophy document on measurement (see the appendix's cross-references) argues that some institutional uses of counting can diminish the people or relationships being described. The grammar cannot diagnose that harm for you. Its finite exact range, visible grouping, optional classifiers, bare-scale estimates, and word for sufficiency keep several alternatives available. For one day, note each quantity you express and why you chose an exact numeral, estimate, quantifier, qualitative description, or source record. Audit the consequences rather than assuming that shorter or less precise wording is better.
