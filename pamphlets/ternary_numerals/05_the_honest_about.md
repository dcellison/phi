# Part 5: The honest about

This part is the pamphlet's center of gravity, and everything exact you learned in Parts 1 and 2 was built toward it: when you round in Phi, it is a choice and not a failure, and the exact forms are what make the choice visible. A scale unit standing bare (no digit in front) means *about that many*:

```
shao philo
three-group day
(some three days)

phoi miona
nine-group person
(nine or so people)

lau shiro
twenty-seven-group tree
(many trees)

rei silero
eighty-one-group star
(a great many stars)
```

All four rows are scale estimates without fixed rounding intervals, and each step up the ladder trades precision for reach. Bare `lau` often has the pragmatic force of *many*, and bare `rei` often means *a great many* or *too many to count for this purpose*. Neither says that the quantity is literally uncountable. When no scale estimate is intended, use a quantifier such as `sheloi` (many) instead.

### Exact or about: the judgment

The system never forbids precision; it prices it, in breath. The judgment drill is to hear the price and answer honestly, remembering that length does not decide truth: an exact count can be owed, and an estimate can erase something someone needs. Three questions sort most cases: *Do I know the exact value? Would the difference change what anyone does? Is it mine to give?*

```
ta shao wi lipha powea.
one three-group two LIFE.CLF egg.
(Five eggs — exact, because the recipe requires five.)

shao lipha powea.
three-group LIFE.CLF egg.
(About a three-group of eggs — an estimate when the precise count is not known or needed.)
```

The first form asserts an exact quantity of five; the second, only a scale. Either can be accurate or misleading, and what decides between them is knowledge: when a scale is all you have, the sentence gets shorter as it gets truer, and when the recipe is owed its five, the length is the honesty.

### Quantifiers: less specific quantity claims

Below even the about-scale sits the quantifier row: `sheloi` (MANY), `soli` (SOME), `phina` (FEW), `shelami` (MOST), `theli` (EACH), `wheli` (ANY), `theula` (UNIV), `mawha` (NONE), for when magnitude itself is more than you know or need. And one of them is a philosophy in a word: `henoi` (ENOUGH). Sufficiency is directly sayable, no number attached:

```
henoi sulopa nai.
ENOUGH soup be.
(There is enough soup.)
```

No count of bowls, no census of appetites: the one quantity that mattered, asserted directly. Enough for whom, for what, under what conditions: the word holds a judgment, not a number, which is why the manual's meditation chapter can end a session with it standing alone (`henoi.`), the practitioner judging the present sitting rather than proving a numerical fact.

### Asking how many, and how much to ask

`wia` (HOW MANY) before a classifier or noun asks the quantity question:

```
wia himo miona so shua.
how many HUM.CLF person FUT come.
(How many people will come?)
```

Note what a Phi speaker hears in the answer's *form*. `ta phoi himo miona` asserts nine; `phoi miona` estimates around a nine-group; `sheloi miona` says many without proposing a scale. These gradations are information, and matching your question's need to the answer's precision is the fluency this part practices. A questioner may still need the exact answer, and privacy or uncertainty may limit what the speaker can give.

### When precision is the wrong instrument

The philosophy document on measurement (see the appendix's cross-references) argues that some institutional uses of counting can diminish the people or relationships being described. The grammar cannot diagnose that harm for you; what it keeps within reach is alternatives: a finite exact range, visible grouping, bare-scale estimates, and a word for sufficiency. The drill for this part runs one day long. Each time a number rises in you, ask the three questions before you say it, and write the day's one necessary number in your journal, if there was one. Some days there is.
