# Part 6: Calculating aloud

Phi narrates integer arithmetic in sentences with five verbs: `sholei` (gather), `leiro` (release), `welura` (spread), `phanoi` (portion), and `kelai` (result or equality). They are household verbs: nothing in Phi is added or subtracted; quantities are gathered, released, spread, and portioned, the way eggs and bowls actually move through a kitchen. Every operation has two clauses: the operation, then its result.

### The operand boundary

The coordinating conjunction `nela` is mandatory between the two operands:

`[operand 1] nela [operand 2] [operation]. [result] kelai.`

This is a grammatical boundary, not an optional pause. Without it, adjacent operands can have the same form as one compound numeral: `ta shao wi` is five, so only `ta shao nela wi` unambiguously presents three and two as separate operands. `nela` never occurs inside a numeral. Part 1's hear-it-whole drill pays its rent here: precisely because `ta shao wi` arrives as one shape, the boundary between shapes must be spoken.

### The four operations, spoken

```text
wi nela ta shao sholei. ta shao wi kelai.
two COORD one three-group gather. one three-group two equals.
(Two plus three results in five. — 2 + 3 = 5)

ta phoi nela ta shao leiro. wi shao kelai.
one nine-group COORD one three-group release. two three-group equals.
(Nine minus three results in six. — 9 − 3 = 6)

ta shao nela wi welura. wi shao kelai.
one three-group COORD two spread. two three-group equals.
(Three multiplied by two results in six. — 3 × 2 = 6)

ta phoi nela ta shao phanoi. ta shao kelai.
one nine-group COORD one three-group portion. one three-group equals.
(Nine divided by three results in three. — 9 ÷ 3 = 3)
```

A plain equality uses the same boundary: `ta shao ta nela ta shao ta kelai.` means "Four equals four." A result question replaces the result with `hina`: `ta shao nela wi sholei. hina kelai.` means "Three plus two; what results?"

### Addition and subtraction: commensurable quantities

Addition and subtraction operate on the same kind of quantity. Classifiers remain optional, but when nouns or classifiers appear, both operands and the result must keep a compatible referent:

```text
wi lipha powea nela ta shao lipha powea sholei. ta shao wi lipha powea kelai.
two LIFE.CLF egg COORD one three-group LIFE.CLF egg gather. one three-group two LIFE.CLF egg equals.
(Two eggs plus three eggs results in five eggs.)
```

Long? Yes, deliberately: counting eggs into the bowl aloud is exactly this long in every language, and Phi declines to offer a shorter written form than the spoken truth. What the rule refuses is a different thing: two people and three bowls are not a sum. Use an ordinary sentence to relate them instead. The same rule applies to subtraction: the removed quantity and the remainder have the starting quantity's kind.

### Multiplication: quantity and factor

In multiplication, the first operand may name what is counted and the second is a bare factor. The product inherits the first operand's referent:

```text
ta shao themo noru nela wi welura. wi shao themo noru kelai.
one three-group THING.CLF bowl COORD two spread. two three-group THING.CLF bowl equals.
(Three bowls multiplied by two results in six bowls.)
```

Requiring the same classifier on both operands would be wrong here: the factor two is not two bowls. It tells how many copies of the counted quantity the operation uses.

### Division: total, groups, quotient, remainder

Division is the household's daily arithmetic: portioning among. The first operand names the total being portioned and the second may name recipients or groups. The quotient inherits the total's kind, not the second operand's:

```text
ta phoi wi lipha powea nela ta shao himo miona phanoi. ta shao lipha powea kelai. wi lipha powea therilu.
one nine-group two LIFE.CLF egg COORD one three-group HUM.CLF person portion. one three-group LIFE.CLF egg equals. two LIFE.CLF egg rest.
(Eleven eggs divided among three people results in three eggs for each, with two eggs remaining.)
```

The remainder is a separate fact with a future: in this kitchen, `wi lipha powea therilu.` is tomorrow's breakfast. Whether the distribution is also sufficient, fair, or agreed to are separate sentences; equal portions state none of them.

### Comparing magnitudes

Two more verbs share the operand boundary: `sharoi` (exceed) and `thenoi` (fall short). No result clause follows, because the comparison is the whole claim:

```text
ta phoi nela ta shao sharoi.
one nine-group COORD one three-group exceed.
(Nine exceeds three.)

wa ta shao nela wi shao sharoi.
Q one three-group COORD two three-group exceed.
(Does three exceed six?)
```

The verbs measure magnitude and stop there: what exceeds is not thereby better, and what falls short is not thereby less. Negation states the inclusive readings: `ta shao nela ta shao ma thenoi.`, three does not fall short of three.

### Current mathematical limits

Phi has internal exact integers from 0 through 242. It currently has no negative or fractional numeral syntax. A subtraction stated entirely in Phi therefore assumes a nonnegative result, and an uneven division uses an integer quotient plus remainder. Preserve negative values, fractions, out-of-range results, formulas, and exact technical calculations as source material outside the Phi passage when their form matters.
