# keiro — from the Tao Te Ching

*The Tao Te Ching has been in the public domain for twenty-four centuries, and no book was ever a more natural guest here: a short text about water, emptiness, quiet guidance, and the strength of the soft, written by a sage who would have recognized every one of Phi's refusals. These are Phi's excerpts: five chapters — 8, 11, 17, 63, 76 — each transmuted with notes. The tenth shelf entry, and the fourth to need no new words.*

*Because the original is Chinese, the English beneath each Phi passage is our own plain rendering of the chapter's sense — Phi transmutes the idea, never somebody's translation. And the title is a single word: the Tao is the Way, and the lexicon carved its path-words this very season — `ruela` the walked ground, `shoeka` the chosen course, `keiro` the way in its guiding sense. The Tao is `keiro`, and the carve made the choice before the text arrived.*

*This text is also written to be a working guide: each note names the transmutation move it demonstrates — the refusal, the reframe, the carve, the grammar-led choice, composition — so that a reader transmuting by hand can see not just what was done but which kind of thing was done. The moves are gathered at the end.*

## Chapter 8 — the water

> *The highest good is like water. Water benefits all things and does not contend. It dwells in the low places that people disdain — and so it is near the Way.*

```
no phialu nila.
IMP water see.
(Look at water.)

phialu theula thena naphe. phialu mawha pilu.
water UNIV thing help. water NONE take.
(Water helps all things, and takes nothing.)

phialu mua mulu lokue menui. miona ra ma menui.
water LOC low place dwell. person DIST NEG dwell.
(It dwells in the low places. People will not dwell there.)

thelao phialu pai keiro nai.
CONS water NEAR way be.
(And so, water is near the way.)
```

**Notes:** two moves. First, *the reframe*: Laozi's simile ("the highest good is *like* water") becomes Phi's observing mood — `no phialu nila`, look at water — because Phi prefers to point at the teacher rather than assert the resemblance; the reader does the comparing, which was always the simile's job. Second, *the refusal doing the philosophy*: Phi has no word for contending, so "does not contend" cannot be said — and does not need to be. What the language offers instead is `mawha pilu`, takes nothing, which is the same virtue stated from the giving side. When a refusal blocks a line, ask what the line's virtue looks like in Phi's own economy; it is usually already there. The consequence marker `thelao` then does the "therefore" — a discourse adverb, standing before the subject where the ruled position puts it.

## Chapter 11 — the use of what is not there

> *Thirty spokes share one hub, and it is the hole that makes the wheel useful. Clay is shaped into a vessel, and it is the hollow that makes it useful. Doors and windows are cut for a room, and it is the openings that make it useful. What is there gives advantage; what is not there gives use.*

```
miona muo tomi muila kire. tomi muwi whemoa nai. thelao tomi po phelu.
person INTO pot earth shape. pot interior empty be. CONS pot POT hold.
(One shapes earth into a pot. The pot's hollow is empty — and so the pot can hold.)

miona womu lorima. miona ponu kire. ponu whemoa nai. thelao miona po koema.
person home build. person door shape. door empty be. CONS person POT enter.
(One builds a home and shapes a door. The doorway is empty — and so one can enter.)

mua tomi mu thena nai. thelao tomi po howela.
LOC pot zero thing be. CONS pot POT receive.
(In the pot there are zero things — and so the pot can receive.)

thena naphe. whemoa we naphe.
thing help. empty ALSO help.
(What is there serves. What is not there serves also.)
```

**Notes:** the chapter this set was chosen for — the queue called it *mu's homecoming*, and here it is: `mu`, the number of absence, counting the pot's contents honestly at zero, in a sentence a market-day child could parse. Three moves. *The reframe*: the lexicon does hold `rolu`, the wheel — but this household throws pots and hangs doors, so the argument keeps the two examples its readers shape with their own hands, which is faithful to the chapter's method: Laozi too reached for the objects at hand. *The grammar-led choice*: "makes it useful" becomes `po`, the possibility particle — the emptiness does not *add* a function, it *opens* one, and Phi has a particle whose entire meaning is an opened door. And the closing couplet is *the quality-noun rule* doing metaphysics: `whemoa`, empty, stands in the subject seat and becomes emptiness itself — no abstract noun needed to say that absence serves, because every adjective in this language already knows how to name itself.

## Chapter 17 — the guide no one notices

> *The best leader, the people barely know exists. The next best, they love and praise. The next, they fear. The worst, they despise. And when the best leader's work is done, the people say: we did it ourselves.*

```
rena welao kulo miona. lo miona shia thiku sano.
REL good guide person. PL person 3SG small know.
(The one who guides well — the people barely know they are there.)

mo thiku. lo miona shia lothea. lo miona shia keluro.
CMPR small. PL person 3SG love. PL person 3SG honor.
(A lesser one — they love, they praise.)

mo thiku. lo miona sukima phelu.
CMPR small. PL person fear hold.
(Lesser still — they hold fear.)

mo thiku. lo miona shia ma nawea.
CMPR small. PL person 3SG NEG respect.
(The least — they do not respect.)

lu rena kulo miona lo miona ma theomi. lo miona shia ma theomi.
COND REL guide person PL person NEG trust. PL person 3SG NEG trust.
(When the guide does not trust the people, the people do not trust the guide.)

lo miona shola lo mia miso to riola sholo haolu.
PL person QUOT.COMP PL 1SG REFL PST labor QUOT.COMP.CLOSE speak.
(And the people say: we ourselves did the work.)
```

**Notes:** *the refusal* carries the whole chapter. Phi has no leader — no ruler, no lord, refused by ruling — and so the text is about `rena kulo miona`, the one who guides: the canon's own phrase, minted when guiding was adjudicated verb-first. Laozi would not object; his point is that the best leader is precisely not a lord. The descending ranks are `mo thiku` fragments — *lesser* — set down alone the way the journal sets down dates. The trust line is a mirror built from one negation used twice. And the ending is *the quotation*: `shola … sholo` preserving the people's exact words, with `miso` leaning on them — we *ourselves* — so that the guide's finest achievement is a sentence in which the guide does not appear.

## Chapter 63 — the great begins small

> *Act without striving. Repay injury with kindness. The difficult things of this world begin while they are easy; the great things of this world begin while they are small. The sage does not aim at greatness — and so achieves it.*

```
no maeli phoa.
IMP quiet do.
(Do, quietly.)

lu miona wei thia peloma thena loa. no wei shia phena loa.
COND person DAT 2SG harmful thing give. IMP DAT 3SG kind give.
(If someone hands you a harmful thing, hand them back kindness.)

theula whalo riola lue thiku riola shua.
UNIV large labor ABL small labor come.
(Every great work comes from small work.)

phewo whalo ma remo. thelao phewo whalo pilewa.
sage large NEG think. CONS sage large make.
(The sage does not think of greatness — and so the sage makes it.)
```

**Notes:** the untranslatable *wu wei* gets the shortest transmutation on the shelf: `no maeli phoa`, do quietly — the manner descriptor standing before the verb where this season's ruling placed it. But the honest note is that Phi barely needed to translate it at all: a language whose every sentence announces before it delivers, whose grammar cannot rush, is already acting without striving; *wu wei is Phi's resting state*. The injury line is *composition* on the gift economy: Phi frames the harm as a thing handed over — `peloma thena`, a harmful thing — so that the response can be the same verb with a better object: `phena`, kindness, the quality-noun again. Two givings, one shape, and the ethics live in what you choose to load.

## Chapter 76 — the soft outlasts the hard

> *A person is born soft and yielding, and dies stiff and hard. Grasses and trees are born tender, and die brittle. So the hard and stiff are companions of death; the soft and yielding are companions of life. The stiff tree breaks in the storm; the supple tree bends and stands. The soft overcomes the hard.*

```
miona wuloe thowia. miona kethua lumeo.
person soft born. person hard die.
(A person is born soft, and dies hard.)

thiku shiro luwi nai. serao shiro kethua nai.
small tree flexible be. old tree hard be.
(The young tree is supple; the old tree is stiff.)

lu whalo howeli shua. rena kethua nai shiro lepa. rena luwi nai shiro manolu.
COND large wind come. REL hard be tree fall. REL flexible be tree stay.
(When the great wind comes, the stiff tree falls — and the supple tree stays.)

kethua lumeo melu nai. wuloe liona melu nai.
hard die friend be. soft live friend be.
(The hard is death's companion; the soft is life's companion.)

wuloe sheo kethua mo mureo nai.
soft THAN hard CMPR stable be.
(The soft outlasts the hard.)
```

**Notes:** the opening pair is *depictive manner* — `wuloe thowia`, born soft; `kethua lumeo`, dies hard — the descriptor in its ruled place before the verb, describing the one it happens to. Laozi's felled tree is softened by *the reframe*: no axe arrives, because none is needed — the great wind was always the better teacher, and it lets the supple tree demonstrate `manolu`, to stay, the staying that is an action and not an absence. The companions couplet seats `kethua` and `wuloe` as quality-nouns beside `lumeo` and `liona`, death and life by the event-noun rule — four abstractions, zero abstract vocabulary. And the last line is the lexicon's comparative doing the paradox plainly: not that the soft defeats the hard — Phi would refuse a conquest — but that it is `mo mureo`, more lasting, which is what Laozi meant by winning.

## What the transmutation changed

*Gap log: the Tao → `keiro`, by the path-carve (the guiding way, not the walked ground). Contend → unsayable and unneeded; water's virtue restated as `mawha pilu`, takes nothing. The wheel → kept to the pot and the door, the argument's own household examples. Useful → `po`, possibility: emptiness opens rather than adds. Leader → `rena kulo miona`, the one who guides, per the domination refusal — the chapter agrees. Praise → `keluro`, honor. Wu wei → `no maeli phoa`, and a note that Phi's grammar is already wu wei. Repay injury → the gift economy: a harmful thing handed over, kindness handed back. The felled tree → the great wind, with the supple tree's virtue as `manolu`, staying. Overcomes → `mo mureo`, more lasting: endurance, not conquest. New words coined: none, and `mu` counts in literature.*

## The moves, for transmuters working by hand

*Gathered from this text's notes and the shelf before it: **the refusal** — when Phi cannot say a line, ask what the line's virtue looks like in Phi's own economy (contending → taking nothing; the leader → the guide; conquest → endurance). **The reframe** — replace the source's imagery with imagery Phi already owns, at the same depth (simile → the observing mood; the wheel → the pot; the axe → the wind; the archer → the tree, in the ninth text). **The carve** — when several Phi words cover one English word, the lexicon's carve notes decide (`keiro`, not `ruela`). **The grammar-led choice** — let a ruling shape the sentence (possession through `phelu`; usefulness through `po`; manner before the verb). **Composition** — build the missing abstraction from rules instead of coining (quality-nouns for the soft and the hard; event-nouns for life and death; `mu thena` for honest emptiness). Coin only when all five moves fail, and then by the full protocol — this text needed none.*
