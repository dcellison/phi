# phewo phelui — from The Prophet

*Kahlil Gibran's The Prophet (1923) has counseled a century of readers in twenty-six short teachings, and it enters the public domain speaking freely. These are Phi's excerpts: three of the teachings (on love, on children, on giving), each transmuted with notes. It needed no new words at all.*

*One transmutation happens before the first line, as it did for the Little Prince: the title. Gibran's Almustafa is called a prophet, but Phi has no offices and no anointings; it can only say what the man does: `phewo`, the sage, the one who has grown wise and speaks. So the shelf calls this text `phewo phelui`, the sage's words, and loses nothing the story needed: the people of Orphalese never wanted his title either. They wanted the words.*

---

## On love

> *"Love gives naught but itself and takes naught but from itself. Love possesses not nor would it be possessed; for love is sufficient unto love."*

```
lothea li miso loa. lothea li lue miso pilu.
love RESTR REFL give. love RESTR ABL REFL take.
(Love gives only itself; love takes only from itself.)

lothea ma phelu. lothea se ma phelu. lothea lothea heno.
love NEG hold. love PASS NEG hold. love love satisfy.
(Love holds nothing, and is not held. Love satisfies love.)
```

**Notes:** the restrictive `li` does Gibran's "naught but" twice over, fencing the giving and the taking to the self alone; and `miso`, the reflexive, is both the gift and the source. The second verse is the passive `se` earning its place in literature: love does not hold, love is not held, the same verb turned once. And the closing sentence is three words in the shape Phi loves best: subject, object, verb, and all three of them the same love.

> *"And think not you can direct the course of love, for love, if it finds you worthy, directs your course."*

```
thia lothea po ma kulo. lothea thia kulo.
2SG love POT NEG guide. love 2SG guide.
(You cannot guide love. Love guides you.)
```

**Notes:** English directs; Phi guides. The lexicon settled this distinction on principle (directing-someone was adjudicated into `kulo`, the guide's verb, when qualities stopped naming acts), so Gibran's line arrives pre-transmuted: not command of a course, but guidance along a way. Eight words, mirrored around the turn.

> *"To wake at the dawn with a winged heart and give thanks for another day of loving…"*

```
su thia mua horathe siora rihe. su thia lao lothea philo liona whaline.
OPT 2SG LOC dawn joy rise. OPT 2SG BECAUSE love day live thank.
(May you rise at dawn with joy, and thank Life for a day of loving.)
```

**Notes:** the optative `su` (the mood the Metta Sutta runs on) turns Gibran's infinitives into blessings, which is what they always were. The winged heart cannot fly straight into Phi (hearts here are `korua`, and they stay in the chest), but its work survives as manner: `siora rihe`, to rise joyfully. The thanking follows the grammar of gratitude the lexicon already teaches: reason marked with `lao`, the one thanked as the object; and Phi, asked *who* receives the thanks, answers with `liona`: Life, the giver two teachings later.

## On children

> *"Your children are not your children. They are the sons and daughters of Life's longing for itself."*

```
lo thia lopia thia thena ma nai. shia liona lopia nai.
PL 2SG child 2SG thing NEG be. 3SG live child be.
(Your children are not your possessions. They are the children of Life.)
```

**Notes:** two refusals shape the opening. *Sons and daughters* collapses into `lo lopia` (Phi's children have no genders to be sorted into, by settled ruling), and nothing is lost: Gibran's very point is that the sorting doesn't matter. And "not your children" becomes *not your things*, `thia thena ma nai`, because Phi's grammar refuses to let a fronted pronoun claim ownership in a predicate; the language pushed the line toward what Gibran meant rather than what he wrote.

> *"They come through you but not from you, and though they are with you yet they belong not to you."*

```
shia thue thia shua. shia lue thia ma shua.
3SG THROUGH 2SG come. 3SG ABL 2SG NEG come.
(They come through you — not from you.)

shia nua thia nai. thia shia ma phelu.
3SG COM 2SG be. 2SG 3SG NEG hold.
(They are with you; you do not hold them.)
```

**Notes:** the prepositions carry the theology: `thue` through, `lue` from; one letter of English, a whole relation of Phi, and the second sentence denies exactly what the first affirmed. *Belong* is the canon's own example working in the wild: possession that predicates must use `phelu`, to hold, so "they belong not to you" becomes `thia shia ma phelu`, you do not hold them, which is both the grammar's requirement and the verse's meaning.

> *"You may give them your love but not your thoughts, for they have their own thoughts."*

```
thia wei shia lothea po loa. thia wei shia remo po ma loa.
2SG DAT 3SG love POT give. 2SG DAT 3SG think POT NEG give.
(You may give them your love. Your thoughts you cannot give them.)

shia miso remo phelu.
3SG REFL think hold.
(They hold their own thoughts.)
```

**Notes:** the giving-sentence twice, once opened and once closed (`po loa` may give, `po ma loa` cannot give), with `wei` announcing the receiver before either gift is named. Thoughts are `remo` by the event-noun rule, thinking's own noun; and *their own* is `miso` standing in the owner's place, the reflexive doing quiet possessive work.

> *"Their souls dwell in the house of tomorrow, which you cannot visit, not even in your dreams."*

```
shia norea mua wireo womu menui. thia ra po ma nai. we mua whemura thia ra po ma nai.
3SG soul LOC future home dwell. 2SG DIST POT NEG be. ALSO LOC dream 2SG DIST POT NEG be.
(Their souls dwell in the house of tomorrow. You cannot be there — not even in dreams.)
```

**Notes:** `wireo womu`, the future's house, is possession-grammar building Gibran's architecture with no new bricks. The visiting becomes being (`thia ra po ma nai`, you cannot be there), the predicative deictic the canon settled, wearing the full modal stack. And *not even in your dreams* is `we`, also, standing before the dream-phrase: the addition that closes the door twice.

> *"You are the bows from which your children as living arrows are sent forth. The archer sees the mark upon the path of the infinite… for even as He loves the arrow that flies, so He loves also the bow that is stable."*

```
thia shiro nai. lo thia lopia liona thinoe nai.
2SG tree be. PL 2SG child live seed be.
(You are the tree; your children are living seeds.)

howeli lo thinoe kau wireo kolua.
wind PL seed ALL future carry.
(The wind carries the seeds toward tomorrow.)

liona rena wapi thinoe lothea. liona we rena mureo nai shiro lothea.
live REL fly seed love. live ALSO REL stable be tree love.
(Life loves the seed that flies — and Life also loves the tree that stands.)
```

**Notes:** the transmutation this text exists for. Gibran's most famous image is an armory (bow, arrow, archer), and Phi has no word for any of it, by its oldest refusal. But the image was never about archery; it is about a launching that is also a letting-go, an instrument that stays while what it loved goes far. Phi has owned that image since primer chapter eight: the household gives seeds to the earth. So the bow becomes the tree, the arrows become living seeds, the archer becomes the wind that carries them toward tomorrow; and Gibran's closing mercy survives whole: Life loves the seed that flies, *and also* the tree that stands. `mureo`, stable: the bow's virtue, unchanged, in a form the language can bless.

## On giving

> *"You give but little when you give of your possessions. It is when you give of yourself that you truly give."*

```
lu thia thena loa. thia thiku loa.
COND 2SG thing give. 2SG small give.
(When you give things, you give little.)

lu thia miso loa. thia shewo loa.
COND 2SG REFL give. 2SG true give.
(When you give yourself, you truly give.)
```

**Notes:** two conditions, mirrored; and the manner slot does the philosophy. `thiku loa`, to give small; `shewo loa`, to give truly: the descriptors stand before the verb the way the ruling placed them, and the whole distinction between property and self fits in the one word that changes.

> *"All you have shall some day be given; therefore give now, that the season of giving may be yours and not your inheritors'."*

```
theula rena thia phelu so se loa.
UNIV REL 2SG hold FUT PASS give.
(All that you hold will one day be given.)

nosa. no loa. su loa thimu thia thimu nai. lumira thimu ma nai.
now. IMP give. OPT give time 2SG time be. descendant time NEG be.
(Now. Give — so the season of giving is yours, and not your heirs'.)
```

**Notes:** the first line is a headless relative under `theula` wearing future and passive together (*all that you hold will be given*), canon's particle stack in its ruled order. Then the journal's oldest gesture: a time-phrase set down alone. `nosa.` Now. The blessing that follows uses the event-noun rule to make giving its own season (`loa thimu`, the giving's time), and hands it to `thia` rather than `lumira`, the descendant, exactly as Gibran urges.

> *"There are those who give with joy, and that joy is their reward."*

```
lu miona siora loa. ha siora shia loamira nai.
COND person joy give. PROX joy 3SG gift be.
(When one gives with joy, that very joy is their gift.)
```

**Notes:** English needs *reward*; Phi does not have one and does not want one: a reward is payment, and this economy runs on gifts. `loamira`, the gift with the giving folded inside it, says what Gibran meant more exactly than his own word: the joy is not wages for giving. It is another gift, and the giver receives it.

> *"For in truth it is life that gives unto life — while you, who deem yourself a giver, are but a witness."*

```
liona wei liona loa. thia li rena nila miona nai.
live DAT live give. 2SG RESTR REL see person be.
(Life gives to Life. You are only the one who watches.)
```

**Notes:** the teaching's floor, and the text's quietest sentence. Life gives to Life: subject, receiver, gift-verb, with `liona` on both sides of `wei`. And the giver's ego is dissolved by a fence: `li rena nila miona`, only the one-who-sees, the restrictive particle and a headless-headed relative doing in four words what Gibran needed a subordinate clause to say.

## What the transmutation changed

*Gap log: prophet → refused as office; the title describes instead: `phewo phelui`, the sage's words. Sons and daughters → `lo lopia`, by the gendered-words refusal, which is also Gibran's point. Life's longing → Life's love, folded into Life as giver and lover throughout. Belong → `phelu` possession, per the clause-initial-pronouns ruling. Bow, arrow, archer → tree, living seeds, and the wind: the launching-and-letting-go rebuilt from the primer's own planting scene, with the bow's stability kept as `mureo`. The sword among love's pinions was not carried across; what love holds, in Phi, is `tiso`, the sharp, and that verse waits for a reader who needs it. Direct → `kulo`, guide, per the qualities-do-not-act adjudication. Reward → `loamira`, gift, because this economy has no wages. Winged heart → `siora rihe`, rising joyful. The baptism verse stayed behind: Phi keeps rites unnamed so every speaker may bring their own. New words coined: none. The language as it stands carries three whole teachings.*
