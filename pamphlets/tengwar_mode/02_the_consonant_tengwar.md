# The consonant tengwar

Every Phi syllable opens with a consonant, so every tengwa's real job is to be that opening: a vowel only ever rides on top of it. Fifteen tengwar cover the whole inventory: nine consonants that keep one shape everywhere, four fricative digraphs that each collapse into one shape, and `r`, which alone takes two different shapes depending on where it sits in a word. Chapter 5 is that shape's own chapter. Learn the shape, not the keypress; a shape read at a glance is the entire point of this mode.

Four of the shapes carry two sounds each, because Phi's four digraphs (`ph`, `th`, `sh`, `wh`) are two letters in romanization but one sound and one tengwa in speech and in Tengwar alike. Formen, thúlë, harma, and hwesta never split.

| Grapheme | Tengwa | Example |
|---|---|---|
| p | parma | `pelua` (paper) |
| t | tinco | `towe` (well) |
| k | calma | `keruko` (sturdy) |
| m | malta | `manuwe` (hand) |
| n | númen | `nomei` (name) |
| r | rómen / órë | see chapter 5 |
| ph | formen | `phari` (pen) |
| th | thúlë | `thekiro` (write) |
| sh | harma | `shelu` (book) |
| wh | hwesta | `whunei` (breathe) |
| s | silmë nuquerna | `silero` (star) |
| h | hyarmen | `hurao` (patient) |
| w | vala | `woru` (keeper) |
| l | lambë | `lei` (harmony) |

Two of these rows hide a choice, and both choices are settled for you before you ever pick up a pen. Silmë has a plain form and a nuquerna form (the "inverted" shape written low enough to carry a tehta underneath it comfortably), and chapter 6 explains why Phi always writes the second one. Rómen and órë are one sound wearing two positions: the `r` that opens a word and the `r` that never does. Speech may trill or tap either one freely; the page does not care. Chapter 5 is where the difference becomes a rule you can apply without listening for it first.

Practice the shapes on their own before chapter 3 adds tehtar to them. Say each example aloud, find its tengwa in the table above, then look for it again in the rendered lines ahead: every example sentence in this pamphlet shows its Tengwar directly above its romanization, so the shape itself is always there to check against. A hand that traces the real form stops needing the table faster than a hand that only reads about it.
