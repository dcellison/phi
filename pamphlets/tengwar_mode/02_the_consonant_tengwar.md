# The consonant tengwar

Every Phi syllable opens with a consonant, so every tengwa's real job is to be that opening — a vowel only ever rides on top of it. Fifteen tengwar cover the whole inventory: nine consonants that keep one shape everywhere, four fricative digraphs that each collapse into one shape, and `r`, which alone takes two different shapes depending on where it sits in a word — chapter 5 is that shape's own chapter. Learn the shape, not the keypress; a shape read at a glance is the entire point of this mode.

Four of the shapes carry two sounds each, because Phi's four digraphs — `ph`, `th`, `sh`, `wh` — are two letters in romanization but one sound and one tengwa in speech and in Tengwar alike. Formen, thúlë, harma, and hwesta never split.

| Grapheme | Tengwa | Example |
|---|---|---|
| p | parma | `pelua` (paper) |
| t | tinco | `towe` (well) |
| k | calma | `keruko` (sturdy) |
| m | malta | `manuwe` (hand) |
| n | númen | `nomei` (name) |
| r | rómen / órë | see chapter 5 |
| ph | formen | `phari` (pen) |
| th | thúlë | `thekiro` (write) |
| sh | harma | `shelu` (book) |
| wh | hwesta | `whunei` (breathe) |
| s | silmë nuquerna | `silero` (star) |
| h | hyarmen | `hurao` (patient) |
| w | vala | `woru` (keeper) |
| l | lambë | `lei` (harmony) |

Two of these rows hide a choice, and both choices are settled for you before you ever pick up a pen. Silmë has a plain form and a nuquerna form — the "inverted" shape written low enough to carry a tehta underneath it comfortably — and chapter 6 explains why Phi always writes the second one. Rómen and órë are not two options for the same sound at all; they are two different sounds, the trilled `r` that opens a word and the tapped `r` that never does, and chapter 5 is where the difference becomes a rule you can apply without listening for it first.

Practice the shapes on their own before chapter 3 adds tehtar to them. Say each example aloud, find its tengwa in the table above, and toggle the script to see the shape itself rather than a description of it — a hand that traces the real form stops needing the table faster than a hand that only reads about it.
