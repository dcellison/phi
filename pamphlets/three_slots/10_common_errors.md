# Part 10: Common errors

Some particle errors are structural and can be caught by a validator. Others are errors of interpretation: the sentence is grammatical, but a reader assigns a particle a meaning it does not have. Both deserve repair, though only one will make a script complain.

### Error 1: reversing the Slot 1 order

**Wrong:** `mia ma to nila.`

**Right:** `mia to ma nila.`

Tense precedes negation. English places "not" among its auxiliaries in ways that do not map onto Phi, so the fixed rank order must do the work: tense, aspect, voice, evidentiality, modality, negation.

### Error 2: putting two particles in one rank

**Wrong:** `suliwa hi ti nai.`

`hi` and `ti` are both evidentials. If the speaker intends to make both source claims, each needs its own clause: `suliwa hi nai. suliwa ti nai.` One clause presents direct perception; the other presents a received report. The one permitted same-rank pair is `se ka` in the voice rank.

### Error 3: dropping a needed tense

**Wrong for "They left":** `shia wepu.`

**Right:** `shia to wepu.`

The bare verb is present. A past main clause therefore needs `to`. The reverse error is adding past tense to a general statement that is not located in the past.

### Error 4: treating an unmarked source as direct evidence

**Misreading:** taking `mia thia sano.` (I know you) to claim direct perception.

An unmarked assertion names no source. It is not hidden `hi`, and it is not weaker than an evidential clause. If source matters, choose the corresponding particle; if it does not, leave the rank unmarked.

### Error 5: confusing an ongoing event with a habit

**Wrong for a recurring pattern:** `misheko si nulae.`

**Right:** `misheko ro nulae.`

`si` presents one sleeping event in progress. `ro` presents sleep as a recurring pattern. Neither says that sleeping is the cat's permanent nature, and `ro` does not mean always.

### Error 6: treating passive voice as proof of evasion

**Grammatical:** `phialu to se ma loa.`

The sentence says that the water was not given without identifying a giver. That may omit information required by a particular question, or it may be exactly right when the giver is unknown. Passive voice supplies a structure, not a verdict about the speaker.

### Error 7: restricting a quantity with `li`

**Wrong:** `li ta shao lipha powea.`

`li` restricts identity, not quantity. The count is `ta shao lipha powea`. If the amount fails to meet a relevant need, `henoi ma nai.` adds that judgement. The two thoughts are clearer when neither is asked to impersonate the other.

### Error 8: filing the vocative under Slot 0

**Wrong:** `kona melu wa thia towe nai.`

**Right:** `kona melu. wa thia towe nai.`

`kona` is an extra-clausal utterance, not a particle. The period marks a structural boundary between the call and the question.

### Error 9: overreading `ka ma`

**Form:** `mia lopia to ka ma nulae.`

This says, "I did not cause the child to sleep." It does not say whether the child slept for another reason. Negation applies to the causal relation expressed by `ka`; the caused event remains open.

### Error 10: treating Slot 2 as a one-word slot

**Form:** `li ha lo melu`

`li` has scope over the whole phrase "these friends," not merely over `ha`. Slot 2 modifies a constituent, which may contain several words. Reading the scope order from left to right prevents the wider particle from being mistaken for a neighbour with no family obligations.
