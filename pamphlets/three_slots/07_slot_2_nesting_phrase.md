# Part 7: Slot 2: the nesting phrase

`ha lo melu` is not a loose handful of particles. `ha` reaches across `lo melu`, while `lo` supplies the plurality inside that demonstrative phrase. Slot 2 orders such layers from wider scope to narrower scope:

**`we` / `li` > `ha` / `ra` > `lo` / numeral > `ko` > `ru` / `mo` > word**

The broadest addition or restriction comes first, followed by deixis, quantity, focus, and degree. The content word arrives last.

```
ha lo melu
(these friends)

ra nu wi shiro
(that second tree)

ha mo ko mioru peloru
(this most beautiful flower)

we ha melu
(this friend too)

li ha lo melu
(only these friends)
```

`we` and `li` do not stack on the same phrase. Three local constructions also keep fixed internal orders: `mo ko` forms the superlative, `ne` precedes an honorific, and `nu` precedes the numeral it makes ordinal.

### `li`: restriction

`li` restricts the identity of the constituent in its scope.

```
li shia sano.
RESTR 3SG know.
(Only they know.)

li ne sulae ruela sano.
RESTR NAME sulae path know.
(Only sulae knows the path.)
```

The restriction does not apply directly to a quantity. English "only three eggs" may mean an exact count or an insufficient amount. Phi gives the count as `ta shao lipha powea`; when it falls short of a need, `henoi ma nai.` says so. The number does not have to perform the disappointment as well.

### `ko`: focus

`ko` selects a constituent for contrast or correction without claiming that all alternatives are excluded.

```
mia ko lothea sano.
1SG FOC love know.
(Love is what I know, specifically.)

ko mia thia nila.
FOC 1SG 2SG see.
(I am the one who sees you.)
```

The difference between `ko` and `li` is therefore practical. `ko mia naphe.` selects me as the helper in the present contrast. `li mia po naphe.` says that only I am able to help. One points; the other restricts.

### Practice: build the constituent
