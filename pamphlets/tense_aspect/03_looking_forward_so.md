# Part 3: Looking forward: `so`

A friend is expected:

```
melu so kamo.
friend FUT arrive.
(The friend will arrive.)
```

`so` is `to` facing the other way. It places the event after the present moment, and like its partner, it carries nothing but the direction.

### A place, not a wish

The future is where plans, promises, predictions, and harvests all live, and `so` serves every one of them evenly:

```
mia lunei so nila.
1SG moon FUT see.
(I will see the moon.)
```

Whether that sentence is an astronomer's certainty or a hope spoken at a window, `so` settles only the timeline. When the sentence needs possibility, `po` joins the stack in the modality rank:

```
mia so po theo.
1SG FUT POT read.
(I may be able to read.)
```

The two particles do two jobs in one breath: `so` locates the reading ahead, `po` opens the question of ability. Keeping them separate is what lets Phi say a confident future, a doubtful one, and a merely possible one without ever bending the verb.

### The same openness

Everything part 2 said about `to` holds mirrored here. A future event can be about to begin, or already scheduled as a custom, or complete by some coming evening, and those views belong to the aspect rank:

```
thia so pa theo.
2SG FUT INCH read.
(You will begin to read.)
```

The beginning lies ahead. Change one particle and the same skeleton promises a finished book instead.

### Practice: face forward

1. You will come.
2. We will see the water.
3. I may be able to sing. Keep time and possibility in their own words.

Answers: 1 `thia so shua.` 2 `lo mia phialu so nila.` The plural marker builds we, and the water stands before the particles as objects always do. 3 `mia so po meliho.` `so` sets the direction, `po` opens the door, and neither does the other's work.
