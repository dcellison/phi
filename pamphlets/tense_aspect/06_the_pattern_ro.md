# Part 6: The pattern: `ro`

The most famous cat in the grammar:

```
misheko ro nulae.
cat HAB sleep.
(The cat sleeps habitually.)
```

`ro` presents recurrence across separate occasions. Not this nap: the institution of napping. The particle steps back from any single moment and reports the shape a life keeps, and with it Phi says customs, practices, seasons of behavior, and everything a household would call the way things go.

### One occasion or many

`si` and `ro` divide territory that English often leaves to context, so the boundary deserves its own drill. `si` looks inside one occasion while it unfolds. `ro` looks across occasions and reports a pattern. The claims are different enough to picture:

```
lopia si wile.
child IPFV play.
(The child is playing.)
```

```
lopia ro wile.
child HAB play.
(The child plays regularly.)
```

The first sentence needs a game in progress somewhere. The second is true on a rainy afternoon with the child asleep, because a pattern goes on holding between its instances. Ask which claim you are making, one unfolding now, or many across time, and the particle chooses itself. A habit is a pattern by definition, so a habit takes `ro`, always.

### Patterns move through time too

A pattern can belong to the past or lie ahead, and tense does that work exactly as it does for single events:

```
mia to ro meliho.
1SG PST HAB sing.
(I sang habitually then.)
```

```
lo mia so ro meliho.
PL 1SG FUT HAB sing.
(We will sing regularly.)
```

The first is the custom of an earlier span of life. The second is an arrangement waiting to begin: rehearsals not yet started, a promise a choir makes to itself. Grammar this small can hold a former practice and a future one apart, and the verb stays whole through both.

### Practice: the standing shape

1. The elder rises habitually.
2. The dog was swimming. One occasion, mid-flow.
3. Every generation in one clause: the elder sang regularly in those days.

Answers: 1 `wheo ro rihe.` 2 `lohau to si wishe.` Past and in progress: the swim was underway at the story's moment. 3 `wheo to ro meliho.` The pattern itself is placed in the past, and the sentence mourns nothing; it remembers precisely.
