# Part 5: The edges: `pa` and `te`

Every event has a first moment and a last one, and Phi gives each edge its own particle. Dawn:

```
sileta pa rihe.
sun INCH rise.
(The sun begins to rise.)
```

And the end of the storm:

```
pheralu te lepa.
rain CESS fall.
(The rain stops falling.)
```

`pa` catches the entry, `te` the ceasing. Between them lies the whole middle that `si` watches and `ki` closes.

### `pa`: the first note

`pa` places the attention on beginning. The lips close and open abruptly, onset in the mouth as well as the clause:

```
lo pelori pa meliho.
PL bird INCH sing.
(The birds begin to sing.)
```

Not the song: the first note of it. A beginning is its own event, worth its own sentence, and mornings are full of them. The primer builds an entire chapter out of `pa` at daybreak, because that is when a household's verbs wake one by one.

### `te`: the release

`te` presents the event as it ceases:

```
shia te meliho.
3SG CESS sing.
(They stop singing.)
```

Here is the distinction that repays careful practice: ceasing is its own fact, separate from completion. A reader can set a book down halfway, so `mia te theo.` reports that the reading stopped and stakes no claim on the book being finished. When both things are true, the evening's reading ended and the book stands read, each claim takes its own clause with its own particle. `te` opens the hand; `ki` sets the finished work on the shelf. The two say different things, and Phi lets you say exactly the one you mean.

### Practice: name the edge

1. The child begins to read.
2. The wind ceases.
3. I stopped singing. The song was not over. Say only what happened.

Answers: 1 `lopia pa theo.` 2 `howeli te shua.` 3 `mia to te meliho.` Past, then the edge: the singing ceased at a past moment, and nothing in the sentence claims the song was complete. That silence is precision, not absence.
