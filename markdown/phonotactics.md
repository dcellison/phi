# phonotactics

## groups

| group | pattern
| ----- | -------
| C     | `h/l/m/n/p/r/s/t/w`
| B     | `sp/st/sl/sw/`
| F     | `ph/th/sh/wh`
| V     | `a/e/i/o/u`
| D     | `[VV]^aa^ee^ii^oo^uu`

## syllable structure

`(C)CV(V)`

a syllable in phi can start with either one or two consonants. if two, the pair will be either a blend or a fricative. a syllable can end with either a single vowel or a diphthong.

because diphthongs in phi are always pronounced as separate letters, pseudo-syllables are produced consisting of a single vowel. but because words in phi must start with a consonant and end with a vowel, a single vowel can never be a word. this syllable structure produces a more rhythmic language.

## word patterns

### nouns

`[C/F][V/D][F][V/D]`

this pattern tends to produce soft words of two to four syllables. there are no consonant blends and words can possibly have two fricatives, giving nouns an airy feel.

examples:
```
nouphu tawho whiphi paethe ruetha phowho riowhio luishe waiwhai
```

### verbs

`[C/B][V/D][C][V]`

this pattern produces words of two or three syllables. these words include consonant blends and have no fricatives, reflecting that verbs are more utilitarian in phi.

examples:
```
mipa nora tumu wuesu stuahe pearu stene ripu swiapu nuipu hauni
```

### short words

these patterns produce words of two syllables.  they are also used for very common nouns, such as numbers and colours.

`[C/F][V][C/F][V]`

examples:
```
thele theshi lushe phati taphe rila whori whunu pewhe thira shopha rutho
```

### utility words

these words are used for everything else in the language, like particles, pronouns, prepositions, etc.
