# Section 6: Slot 1 - Verb Phrase Particles

## Overview

Slot 1 particles occupy the middle position in phi's particle hierarchy, appearing between Slot 0 sentence frame particles and Slot 2 core word particles. These particles establish the **verb phrase context** - determining temporal reference, aspectual perspective, modal attitudes, and negation scope for predicates.

## Core Function

**Verb phrase** particles answer the essential question: *When, how, and in what manner does this action or state occur?*

- **Temporal reference**: When does this happen? (past, present, future)
- **Aspectual perspective**: How does this action unfold? (ongoing, completed, habitual)
- **Modal attitude**: What is the speaker's stance? (obligation, desire, command)
- **Negation scope**: Does this action occur or not?

## The Complete Slot 1 Inventory

Phi's verb phrase system contains **14 particles** organized into four functional categories:

| Category | Particles | Function |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Tense** | `li`, `ta`, `su` | Temporal reference |
| **Aspect** | `we`, `la`, `ni`, `po`, `pu`, `ri`, `wi`, `wu` | Aspectual perspective |
| **Mood** | `to`, `ru` | Modal commands and obligations |
| **Negation** | `me` | Action/state negation |

## Structural Properties

### Hierarchical Ordering
Slot 1 particles follow strict internal ordering based on semantic scope:

**Tense > Aspect > Mood > Negation**

```
li pu me whera
PST PFV NEG learn
"had not learned (completely)"
```

### Verb Phrase Scope
All Slot 1 particles apply to the **entire verb phrase**, including:
- The main verb
- Any objects or complements
- Slot 2 particles that modify verb phrase elements

### Optional vs. Required
- **Optional in informal speech**: Tense particles (`ta` often omitted)
- **Required for precision**: Negation (`me`), aspect in formal contexts
- **Context-dependent**: Modal particles vary by speech situation

## Cross-Linguistic Perspective

### Universal Patterns
Phi's verb phrase particles follow cross-linguistic patterns found in:
- **Bantu languages**: Complex aspectual systems
- **Slavic languages**: Perfective/imperfective distinctions  
- **East Asian languages**: Particle-based temporal marking
- **Austronesian languages**: Mood and aspect particles

### Innovative Features
- **Systematic ordering**: Unlike natural language variation
- **Clear scope boundaries**: Each particle has precise domain
- **Compositional semantics**: Particle combinations are predictable
- **Register flexibility**: Formal precision, informal efficiency

## Learning Progression

### Foundation Level (Required)
1. **Basic tense**: `li` (past), `ta` (present), `su` (future)
2. **Essential negation**: `me` (not)
3. **Common aspects**: `la` (progressive), `ni` (perfect)

### Intermediate Level (Recommended)
4. **Modal attitudes**: `we` (want), `to` (command), `ru` (should)
5. **Aspectual nuances**: `po` (habitual), `pu` (perfective)

### Advanced Level (Sophisticated)
6. **Complex aspects**: `ri` (imperfective), `wi` (inceptive), `wu` (cessative)
7. **Aspect combinations**: Multiple particles in sequence
8. **Register variation**: Formal vs. informal usage patterns
