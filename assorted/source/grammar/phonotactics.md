# phonotactics

> phonotactics refers to the rules governing the permissible combinations of phonemes in a language. it defines which sound sequences are allowed to form syllables and words, and which combinations are prohibited. these patterns are often unconsciously followed by native speakers and give each language its distinctive sound.

## introduction to phi phonotactics

phi implements a revolutionary phonotactic system where **constraint hierarchies systematically encode grammatical categories**. unlike natural languages where phonotactic patterns emerge historically through usage and sound change, phi's system is deliberately designed for transparent grammatical function. this means that the permissible sound sequences for any given word are not arbitrary but are direct reflections of its grammatical role, making word structure itself a primary indicator of grammatical category.

## theoretical framework

### constraint-based architecture
phi phonotactics operates through hierarchical constraint systems that interact systematically to generate grammatical category templates:

**template constraints**: define the systematic sound patterns assigned to each grammatical category.
**boundary constraints**: regulate syllable structure and word-level phonological integrity.
**sequence constraints**: control permissible segment combinations within templates.
**categorical constraints**: prevent pattern overlap between distinct grammatical functions.

## systematic constraint architecture

### template generation constraints
phi employs systematic template constraints that generate grammatical category patterns through principled rule interactions:

**content word constraints**: 
- nouns: require fricative-final and vowel-sequence complexity to signal referential importance (e.g., `whethea` `FVFP` with final fricative `th` and vowel pair `ea`).
- verbs: demand fricative-initial patterns to mark predicate function systematically (e.g., `shose` `FVCV` with initial fricative `sh`).
- adjectives: employ fricative-medial templates to distinguish property attribution (e.g., `hashe` `CVFV` with medial fricative `sh`).
- adverbs: utilize three-syllable patterns to encode systematic modification scope (e.g., `napine` `CVCVCV` with three CV syllables and no fricatives).

**function word constraints**:
- particles: receive minimal CV templates to reflect grammatical simplicity
- prepositions: employ fricative-initial + vowel-sequence patterns for systematic recognition
- determiners: combine fricative-sequence + consonant-final structures for definitive marking
- conjunctions: utilize systematic CVCV patterns to encode logical relationship functions

### boundary constraint hierarchy
phi implements systematic boundary constraints that maintain template integrity:

**word boundary preservation**: systematic spacing prevents phonological processes that could blur categorical distinctions between adjacent words in SOV structures.

**syllable boundary regulation**: vowel hiatus maintenance preserves syllable count transparency essential for template recognition in verb-final processing.

**morpheme boundary respect**: phonological templates accommodate systematic morphological marking without violating categorical sound pattern requirements.

### sequence constraint systems
phi employs hierarchical sequence constraints that control segment combination possibilities:

**fricative distribution constraints**: systematic restrictions on fricative placement create template-specific positions that enable categorical identification through sound pattern location.
For example, an initial fricative as in `shose` (see) strongly signals a verb (`FVCV`), whereas a medial fricative often indicates an adjective (e.g., `hashe` - `CVFV`) and a final fricative before a vowel pair is characteristic of nouns (e.g. `whethea` - `FVFP`).

**vowel sequence constraints**: prohibition of identical vowel pairs maintains systematic template clarity while permitting maximal vowel sequence exploitation for categorical encoding.

**syllable duplication constraints**: systematic prohibition against identical syllable repetition ensures template uniqueness and prevents categorical ambiguity through pattern recognition.
