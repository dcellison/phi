# adjectives

> adjectives are a major word class found in virtually all human languages that modify nouns by attributing properties and qualities to them. they denote observable features such as size, color, shape, and emotional states, as well as evaluative judgments. cross-linguistically, adjectives may show agreement with nouns or remain invariant, participate in comparative constructions, and appear in different positions relative to the nouns they modify.

## introduction to phi adjectives

adjectives in phi adhere to the structure `[C][V][F][V]` where:

- C = consonant (h, l, m, n, p, r, s, t, w)
- V = vowel (i, u, e, o, a)
- F = fricative digraph (ph, wh, th, sh)

this pattern creates words like `mipho` (blue), `hashe` (green), and `pisha` (soft), which always begin with a consonant followed by a vowel, then a fricative digraph, and ending with a vowel. this structure gives adjectives a consistent two-syllable rhythm with a characteristic "flowing" quality in the middle due to the fricative sound.

adjectives in phi serve to modify nouns by describing their qualities, properties, or attributes. they express color, size, shape, physical qualities, temporal aspects, and evaluative or emotional characteristics. unlike in some languages, phi adjectives do not change form to agree with the nouns they modify.

in phi's SOV (subject-object-verb) word order, adjectives typically precede the nouns they modify: `mipho noshea` (blue food), `tophe thephoa` (big person). this consistent placement makes adjective identification straightforward in context.

the distinctive phonological pattern of adjectives helps listeners immediately identify descriptive words in speech, even without explicit grammatical markers. this consistent structure is part of phi's systematic approach to word formation, where each part of speech has its own unique sound signature.

## design philosophy

the phi adjective system prioritizes **essential communication** over exhaustive description. this design:

- focuses on universal basic concepts found across human languages
- eliminates redundancy by providing one clear word per meaning
- covers fundamental semantic domains needed for daily communication
- maintains systematic phonotactic integrity
- enables precise expression without overwhelming complexity

research into basic-level categorization shows that certain descriptive concepts are universal across cultures. phi's adjective system concentrates on these essential categories while avoiding overly specific or culturally-bound terminology.

## usage notes

**natural usage principles**:

phi embodies the **minimal marking principle** - use only what's needed for clear communication. this creates authentic, flowing speech patterns rather than rigid descriptive structures.

**context sensitivity**: adjectives can be omitted when properties are obvious from context or situation. this includes dropping color when objects have default colors, size when relative scale is clear, or condition when state is apparent.

**progressive complexity**: beginners use basic adjectives; advanced speakers add nuanced description only when communicatively valuable.

**examples with natural simplification**:

**Full form:**
```
mipho hashe liphai
blue green tree
Blue green tree
```

**Natural form:**
```
liphai
tree
Tree
```

**Full form:**
```
tophe pesha phuawhai
big heavy stone
Big heavy stone
```

**Natural form:**
```
pesha phuawhai
heavy stone
Heavy stone
```

**beginner-friendly usage**: 
- Start with single adjectives: `tushe noshea` (good food)
- Add complexity gradually: 
```
mao tushe miwhi noshea
EMPH good fresh food
Very good fresh food
```
- Context often eliminates need for obvious descriptors

**semantic precision**: each adjective covers one clear semantic domain without overlap. related concepts are distinguished by separate lexical items rather than contextual interpretation.

**SOV positioning**: adjectives precede their nouns in phi's subject-object-verb word order. examples: `hashe liphai` (green tree), `waphi phuawhai` (small stone), `tushe noshea` (good food).

**no derivational morphology**: phi adjectives are not derived from other parts of speech. each maintains its distinct phonotactic pattern and semantic independence.

**systematic coverage**: the eight core categories provide comprehensive descriptive capability for essential human communication needs.
