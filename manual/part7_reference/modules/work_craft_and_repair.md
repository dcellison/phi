# Work, Craft, and Repair

A chair comes back from the workshop with one leg pale and new. Whoever sits in it tonight may want to know who cut that leg, whether the wood came from the elm the storm took, and when the chair can stand in the hall again. This module is the vocabulary those questions run on: tasks and projects, skill and its proof, stock and its story, and plain words for work that wears a person down.

Like every module, its specialized vocabulary is optional and uses ordinary Phi grammar. A speaker who has not learned it can always ask for a core paraphrase. Learning the words gives no skill or authority over another person's labor; they describe work without handing it out.

The chapter table below introduces 28 module roots. Current classifications bring the optional learning list to 70 words; base `winora` plan and `hasoru` pending are taught alongside it. The complete machine-generated list is available under [Work, Craft, and Repair in the module lexicon](../lexicon/by_module.md#work-craft-and-repair). The JSON entries in `vocabulary/content/` remain authoritative for each word's form and full definition.

## Planning and pending status belong to base vocabulary

Neither entry has a `modules` field. Someone can plan tomorrow's meal or a journey; a request can remain pending without becoming a work project. The chapter keeps both words close because organized work uses them constantly, not because work owns them.

The adjectives `hasoru`, `noraku`, `pokera`, `nukesa`, and `kiranu` likewise name their states through the quality-noun rule.

## Tasks, projects, plans, and status

`riporu` is the workshop's smallest whole thing: one bounded piece of work, with a stated action and something it acts on. A task can be proposed, chosen, assigned, refused, or finished, and the noun itself takes no side in any of that.

```
mia ha riporu kelomi.
1SG PROX task accept.
(I accept this task.)
```

`rinoka` gathers tasks, people, materials, and an intended result into one undertaking. `winora` arranges the intentions; the plan it produces may change, split into two, or wait for a season, and none of that is failure.

```
lo mia ha rinoka winora.
PL 1SG PROX project plan.
(We plan this project.)
```

`wepuri` is movement toward a named stage. A project can progress and still be late, and change alone is not progress. That narrowness is the word's whole discipline.

```
ha rinoka wepuri.
PROX project progress.
(This project progresses.)
```

`hasoru` and `noraku` split what English flattens into waiting. A pending task waits its turn in an agreed order. A blocked one has met an obstacle, and saying `noraku` invites the next question: what stands in the way?

```
ha riporu hasoru nai.
PROX task pending be.
(This task is pending.)
```

```
lao muralo to ma shua ha riporu noraku nai.
BECAUSE material PST NEG come PROX task blocked be.
(Because the material did not come, this task is blocked.)
```

`lumethi` and `thimora` hold time the way Phi holds it, without the clock. A deadline is a claimed latest time, and someone claimed it; a schedule arranges the work across days. Both leave room to ask who set them and who agreed.

```
ha riporu lumethi pheo wi philo nai.
PROX task deadline POST two day be.
(This task's deadline is in two days.)
```

```
lo mia ha thimora tukiro.
PL 1SG PROX schedule review.
(We review this schedule.)
```

## Assignment, coordination, supervision, and handoff

`rilowa` reports that someone named an expected performer, and it stops exactly there. Whether the assigner had the standing to assign, whether the person accepts, and whether the conditions are fair are all separate sentences, and Phi likes them separate.

```
kowela wei mia ha riporu rilowa.
council DAT 1SG PROX task assign.
(The council assigns this task to me.)
```

Acceptance and refusal keep their own verbs, and either can answer an assignment.

```
mia ha riporu naweri.
1SG PROX task refuse.
(I refuse this task.)
```

`wephari` arranges order, timing, and dependencies among activities; the coordinator holds threads, not people. `someru` is nearer: attention to another's work under a claimed responsibility. Supervision can be requested and kind, or imposed and contested; the word carries the claim, not its legitimacy.

```
mia lo riporu wephari.
1SG PL task coordinate.
(I coordinate the tasks.)
```

```
shia ha shiroka someru.
3SG PROX repair supervise.
(They supervise the repair.)
```

`sethoni` passes the next stage to other hands. What actually moved is worth saying aloud, because the verb does not promise that the piece, the information, and the responsibility all traveled together.

```
mia wei thia ha riporu sethoni.
1SG DAT 2SG PROX task handoff.
(I hand the task off to you.)
```

A full account of one afternoon's work may take several clauses: one person assigns, another accepts, a third coordinates what depends on what, and the handoff happens when the material really moves. Phi will not compress all of this into one managerial verb, and the refusal is deliberate.

## Skill, competence, expertise, and qualification

`thesani` is a capacity a person built by practice, and it stays theirs in bad weather, without permission, and between uses. `pokera` is narrower: able to meet this stated criterion, here, with these tools. `nukesa` runs deeper than any single criterion; it is the kind of practice that has opinions about the grain of the wood. `kiranu` says the stated prerequisites are met, which is a fact about a list. A person can be any of the four without the other three, and the workshop is where the differences show.

```
mia ha thesani phelu.
1SG PROX skill hold.
(I have the skill.)
```

```
shia pokera nai.
3SG competent be.
(They are competent.)
```

```
shia nukesa nai.
3SG expert be.
(They are expert.)
```

```
shia kiranu nai.
3SG qualified be.
(They are qualified.)
```

Certification and authorization stand apart from all four. `sikanu` is a formal claim that criteria were met, made by someone with a name; commons `kanuwa` is a grant of bounded power. Six relations, six words, and none of them ever does another's work.

## Training, assessment, and certification

`thunaro` organizes practice toward a skill, and the learner stays a party to it, free to question the method or stop. `kisome` reads evidence against a criterion for a stated purpose; an assessment can be careful or sloppy, and the verb endorses neither the assessor nor the result.

```
mia thia thunaro.
1SG 2SG train.
(I train you.)
```

```
shia ha theko kisome.
3SG PROX craft assess.
(They assess the craft.)
```

`sikanu` is the formal act at the end, and the certificate it produces is a source artifact: the issuer, scope, identifier, and date live in the document itself, outside the Phi passage.

```
punoki shia sikanu.
institution 3SG certify.
(The institution certifies them.)
```

One apprenticeship can hold teaching, training, supervision, testing, assessment, and certification at different moments. They still name different relations, and a credential from elsewhere does not melt them together.

## Design, testing, and quality

`noporu` says what the thing should be: its form, its joins, what it is for. A design can be provisional or shared. The drawing that carries the exact dimensions is a source artifact; the designing is Phi's.

```
mia ha rolu noporu.
1SG PROX wheel design.
(I design the wheel.)
```

`somaki` puts the wheel through a defined procedure and records what happened. What the results mean is `kisome`'s work, a second act with its own verb.

```
mia ha rolu somaki.
1SG PROX wheel test.
(I test the wheel.)
```

`kirero` is always character against a purpose, and the dimensions can conflict honestly: a beautiful thing hard to repair, a durable one wasteful of material, a precise one nobody can use. Naming which quality is meant keeps the argument about the thing. And quality never grades the maker; Phi has no sentence in which a person is their work's score.

```
mia ha kirero kisome.
1SG PROX quality assess.
(I assess the quality.)
```

Where the object is technical, the Systems words join in: `masuro` calibrates, `nilaki` inspects, `kiphira` names the fault, `kisholu` holds the specification.

## Workload, drudgery, and exploitation

`ritako` is the whole weight of an interval: the tasks, their timing, their interruptions, the coordination they drag behind them. Two people with the same task count can carry different workloads, and the word exists so that sentence can be said.

```
mia ha ritako ma kelomi.
1SG PROX workload NEG accept.
(I do not accept the workload.)
```

`rimawu` is work that drains and means nothing to the one doing it. It is a report from inside the work, and it deserves the standing of any testimony: whose experience, under what conditions. It is not a verdict on all repetition; a potter's hundredth bowl may be practice or drudgery, and only the potter knows which. Phi's warm word for labor, `riola`, must never be laid over that report to silence it.

```
mia ha rimawu naweri.
1SG PROX drudgery refuse.
(I refuse the drudgery.)
```

`pilora` makes a claim about power: someone takes disproportionate benefit through another's labor, need, or dependency. It is an analysis, not a mood, and it asks for its evidence the way any strong claim in Phi does. Beside it stand base `kawhera` (coerce) and `whepelo` (retaliate), so criticism of work can be exact instead of vague.

```
punoki lo miona pilora.
institution PL person exploit.
(The institution exploits people.)
```

Drudgery does not prove exploitation, and exploitation does not need every worker to name their work drudgery. The two claims can support each other without collapsing into one.

## Stock, inventory, supply, offcuts, and provenance

`muphera` is what the shelf actually holds; `sitawi` is what the ledger says it holds. A workshop can have stock and no inventory, or an inventory that outlived the stock it described, and honest work sometimes begins by reconciling the two.

```
keritha henoi muphera phelu.
workshop ENOUGH stock hold.
(The workshop has enough stock.)
```

```
mia ha sitawi tukiro.
1SG PROX inventory review.
(I review the inventory.)
```

`sephori` makes material available for a stated use. Whether it arrives in good condition, whether it is enough, and whose it remains are each their own sentence.

```
shia wei keritha muralo sephori.
3SG DAT workshop material supply.
(They supply material to the workshop.)
```

`katemu` is what the cut set aside. An offcut may become stock for a smaller task, feed the stove, or end as `whemori` (waste); the noun holds the material open while its afterlife is decided.

```
mia lo katemu phelure.
1SG PL offcut store.
(I store the offcuts.)
```

`luseri` is the story a material carries: where it came from, whose hands held it, what was done to it, told with its gaps showing. Provenance can serve repair, attribution, and safety without proving ownership, which remains a commons question.

```
ha muralo luseri se sirami.
PROX material provenance PASS record.
(The material's provenance is recorded.)
```

## Useful transparent compositions and source boundaries

| Phi expression or strategy | Practical meaning | Why it remains compositional or external |
|---|---|---|
| polite whole-clause question naming a proposed action | request or offer | The answer stays the addressee's to give. |
| ordered `lo riporu` tasks with actions and `pherami` dependencies | steps or work sequence | The order and its dependencies stay visible instead of hiding in a sequence word. |
| task or project plus existing `sholu` and a stated `kirema` criterion | completion under a criterion | Done under a criterion is not yet good, safe, or accepted, and the composition keeps them apart. |
| `sithora` role plus tasks, skill, authority, and work relations | job, occupation, or profession analysis | One Phi word would erase the institutional weight an exact job title carries. |
| `moloi` apprentice, `thunai` student, or explicit practice and skill statements | novice or learner status | Limited experience is scoped to a craft, not turned into a fixed identity. |
| exact source credential plus `kiranu`, `sikanu`, and `kanuwa` where each fits | license or professional credential analysis | The exact credential keeps its issuer and scope; Phi keeps qualification, certification, and authorization distinct. |
| exact drawing, dimension, tolerance, or revision plus `noporu` and Systems `kisholu` | technical design or specification | The notation carries the exact artifact; Phi carries its meaning. |
| bodily self-report, refusal, requested change, Medical or Accessibility terms, and Commons authority terms | fatigue, accommodation, ergonomic limit, or stop-work relation | A body's limit outranks a schedule, and the grammar keeps it able to say so. |
| exact wage, rate, contract, employment category, warranty, liability term, or collective agreement | economic or legal work relation | The exact terms remain operative in their own document; Phi analyzes what they do to people. |
| exact tool, component, software, automation system, or protective equipment identity plus Systems and work relations | modern technical work object | Exact identification matters more than a catalogue of borrowed names that would age in a season. |

Source material stays outside the Phi passage here as everywhere: the drawing with its dimensions, the certificate with its issuer, the contract with its terms. Phi points at them, reads them, and argues with them; it does not swallow them.

## Using the module well

A useful account starts from the concrete relation: who is doing what, and for whom. Tools and timing follow in their own clauses. The module's distinctions enter when they earn their place. Authority, consent, and the body's limits stay in their own clauses, where Phi can see them. When a speaker has not learned a module word, a core paraphrase is ordinary courtesy: the people, actions, and conditions become explicit, and the meaning walks across.

The module ends where the workshop begins. `shiroka`, repair, is core Phi, not module Phi: every speaker already carries the conviction that things are worth mending. Task and schedule, stock and provenance are scaffolding around that conviction. The mended chair back in the hall is the point.

```
mia ha tenoa shiroka.
1SG PROX tool repair.
(I repair the tool.)
```
