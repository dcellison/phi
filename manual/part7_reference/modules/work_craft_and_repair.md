# Work, Craft, and Repair

The Work, Craft, and Repair module gives Phi speakers optional vocabulary for organizing work, describing practical capacities, examining labor conditions, evaluating made or repaired things, and tracing materials through use. The words use ordinary Phi grammar. A speaker who has not learned them can still ask for a core paraphrase, and learning this module does not confer skill, qualification, authority, ownership, employment status, or permission to direct another person's work.

The complete machine-generated list is also available under [Work, Craft, and Repair in the module lexicon](../lexicon/by_module.md#work-craft-and-repair). The JSON entries in `vocabulary/content/` remain authoritative for each word's form and full definition.

## Core vocabulary used by the module

The module does not replace Phi's existing language of labor, craft, making, repair, learning, choice, refusal, safety, evidence, community, or exact source material. It adds narrower work relations around those familiar resources.

| Repair and continuity | `shiroka` repair, `whori` mend, `talome` restore, `thiranu` maintain, `thenoki` sustain, `whoa` whole, `pukate` break, `helui` change |

| Choice and responsibility | `lesawi` consent, `kanu` choose, `nawo` agree, `naweri` refuse, `kelomi` accept, `thekao` acknowledge, `seru` commit, `thonai` willingly responsible, `ralu` free |

Systems and Shared Infrastructure supplies technical system, dependency, state, capacity, load, calibration, fault, failure, inspection, reliability, hazard, and specification words. Commons and Collective Governance supplies role, delegation, authority, authorization, rule, decision, ownership, obligation, accountability, review, coercion, and retaliation words. ## Module vocabulary

| Phi | Part of speech | Working sense | Essential contrast |
|---|---|---|---|
| `riporu` | noun | task; bounded unit of intended activity | A task is not purposeful labor, a role, obligation, technical dependency, accepted assignment, or proof that the work is useful. |
| `rinoka` | noun | project; bounded coordinated undertaking | A project is not a task, system, institution, indefinite aspiration, or commitment by every participant. |
| `winora` | verb | plan; organize intended future actions | Planning is not a method, schedule, design, specification, promise, decision, or guarantee. |
| `wepuri` | verb | progress; move toward a stated work criterion | Progress is not completion, generic improvement, efficiency, growth, motion, or moral advancement. |
| `hasoru` | adjective | pending; awaiting a needed action or condition | Pending is not merely future, unstarted, blocked, complete, or evidence of who is responsible for delay. |
| `noraku` | adjective | blocked; currently unable to proceed because of an obstacle | Blocked is not pending, refusal, technical failure, cancellation, or proof of fault. |
| `lumethi` | noun | deadline; claimed latest time for an action or result | A deadline is not a schedule, obligation, commitment, exact timestamp, or proof that the time is legitimate or feasible. |
| `thimora` | noun | schedule; planned arrangement across time | A schedule is not a plan's full content, deadline, promise, exact calendar artifact, or proof of availability. |
| `rilowa` | verb | assign; designate an expected performer for a task | Assignment is not a request, allocation, delegation, authorization, accepted obligation, competence, or consent. |
| `wephari` | verb | coordinate; arrange relations among dependent activities | Coordination is not collaboration, supervision, technical control, representation, or authority over participants. |

| `somaki` | verb | test; apply a defined procedure to gather evidence | Testing is not observation, assessment, inspection, review, calibration, proof, approval, or a safety guarantee. |

The verbs receive event or result nouns through Phi's ordinary event-noun rule: `winora` can name a plan, `wepuri` progress, `rilowa` an assignment, `wephari` coordination, `someru` supervision, `sethoni` a handoff, `thunaro` training, `kisome` an assessment, `sikanu` certification, `noporu` a design, `somaki` a test, `pilora` exploitation, and `sephori` supply as an activity. The adjectives `hasoru`, `noraku`, `pokera`, `nukesa`, and `kiranu` likewise name their states through the quality-noun rule.

## Tasks, projects, plans, and status

`riporu` gives Phi a neutral bounded work unit. It can be proposed, assigned, accepted, refused, started, blocked, completed, or handed onward, but none of those relations follows merely from naming the task.

```
mia ha riporu kelomi.
1SG PROX task accept.
(I accept this task.)
```

`rinoka` gathers related tasks, participants, dependencies, resources, and an intended result into one bounded undertaking. `winora` is the act of organizing intended actions; a plan may change, fail, remain unaccepted, or contain several possible paths.

```
lo mia ha rinoka winora.
PL 1SG PROX project plan.
(We plan this project.)
```

`wepuri` measures movement relative to a stated stage or criterion. A project can progress while remaining late, harmful, inefficient, or unfinished, and change alone is not progress.

```
ha rinoka wepuri.
PROX project progress.
(This project progresses.)
```

`hasoru` says that something awaits a needed action or condition. `noraku` says it cannot presently proceed because of a named obstacle. The distinction matters: a pending task may simply be waiting in an agreed order, while blocked status calls for an account of what prevents movement.

```
ha riporu hasoru nai.
PROX task pending be.
(This task is pending.)
```

```
lao muralo to ma shua ha riporu noraku nai.
BECAUSE material PST NEG come PROX task blocked be.
(Because the material did not come, this task is blocked.)
```

`lumethi` names a claimed latest time; `thimora` names an arrangement of work across time. State who set either one, what source artifact carries its exact value, what assumptions it uses, and whether affected people accepted it.

```
ha riporu lumethi pheo wi philo nai.
PROX task deadline POST two day be.
(This task's deadline is in two days.)
```

```
lo mia ha thimora tukiro.
PL 1SG PROX schedule review.
(We review this schedule.)
```

## Assignment, coordination, supervision, and handoff

`rilowa` reports that a source designated an expected performer. It deliberately does not say whether that source had authority, whether the recipient accepted, whether an obligation exists, whether the person is able to act, or whether compensation and conditions are fair.

```
kowela wei mia ha riporu rilowa.
council DAT 1SG PROX task assign.
(The council assigns this task to me.)
```

`wephari` concerns order, timing, information, resources, interfaces, and dependencies among activities. Coordination may be distributed. It does not make the coordinator a supervisor or decision authority.

```
mia lo riporu wephari.
1SG PL task coordinate.
(I coordinate the tasks.)
```

`someru` concerns attention to another participant's work under a claimed responsibility. State the exact scope and source of that responsibility. Supervision can be supportive and requested, imposed and contested, competent or incompetent, legitimate or illegitimate.

```
shia ha shiroka someru.
3SG PROX repair supervise.
(They supervise the repair.)
```

`sethoni` transfers a bounded next stage, object, information set, or control relation. The recipient's receipt, understanding, acceptance, authority, and later accountability remain separate.

```
mia wei thia ha riporu sethoni.
1SG DAT 2SG PROX task handoff.
(I hand the task off to you.)
```

A complete work account may therefore need several clauses: one person assigns, another accepts or refuses, a third coordinates dependencies, someone supervises within a stated scope, and a handoff occurs only when the relevant material and information are actually transferred. Phi does not compress these into a single managerial relation.

## Skill, competence, expertise, and qualification

`thesani` is a learned repeatable practical capacity. Name its domain, conditions, tools, and claimed level. A person may have a skill but be unable to use it under present conditions, lack permission, or not meet a particular performance criterion.

```
mia ha thesani phelu.
1SG PROX skill hold.
(I have the skill.)
```

`pokera` is a situated competence claim: the person can meet a stated criterion for a stated action under stated conditions. Evidence should be available, but competence never implies consent or authorization.

```
shia pokera nai.
3SG competent be.
(They are competent.)
```

`nukesa` marks deep domain practice and understanding. Expertise is scoped and revisable; it does not make a person infallible or grant authority outside the evidence and social relations separately stated.

```
shia nukesa nai.
3SG expert be.
(They are expert.)
```

`kiranu` says that a person meets stated prerequisites under a named source or system. A qualification may be formal and still fail to demonstrate present competence, or a competent person may lack a source-recognized qualification.

```
shia kiranu nai.
3SG qualified be.
(They are qualified.)
```

Commons `kanuwa` authorization answers another question: who grants permission or bounded power to act. Work `sikanu` certification reports a formal claim that criteria were met. Keep all six relations distinct: skill, competence, expertise, qualification, certification, and authorization.

## Training, assessment, and certification

`thunaro` organizes instruction and practice toward a skill or performance criterion. The learner's consent, access, method, conditions, feedback, and ability to stop or change participation remain explicit.

```
mia thia thunaro.
1SG 2SG train.
(I train you.)
```

`kisome` compares evidence, performance, or a condition with a criterion for a stated purpose. An assessment can be uncertain, disputed, methodologically weak, or attached to unjust consequences; the verb does not endorse the assessor or result.

```
shia ha theko kisome.
3SG PROX craft assess.
(They assess the craft.)
```

`sikanu` is an act by a named person or institution that creates or maintains a formal criteria claim. Preserve the exact certificate, issuer, identifier, scope, date, and validity outside Phi when those details matter.

```
punoki shia sikanu.
institution 3SG certify.
(The institution certifies them.)
```

Teaching, training, supervision, testing, assessment, inspection, review, certification, and authorization may occur in one learning process, but each names a different relation. A source credential does not erase this structure.

## Design, testing, and quality

`noporu` specifies intended form, relations, function, materials, behavior, or making process. A design can be provisional or collaborative. It may be represented by an exact drawing or specification outside Phi, but it is not identical to that artifact.

```
mia ha rolu noporu.
1SG PROX wheel design.
(I design the wheel.)
```

`somaki` carries out a defined evidential procedure under stated conditions. State the method, object, conditions, criterion, observations, interpretation, and limits rather than saying only that something was tested.

```
mia ha rolu somaki.
1SG PROX wheel test.
(I test the wheel.)
```

`kirero` is always quality relative to purpose, users, conditions, criteria, or values. Several dimensions may conflict: an artifact may be beautiful but difficult to repair, durable but materially wasteful, precise but inaccessible, or compliant with a specification that users reject.

```
mia ha kirero kisome.
1SG PROX quality assess.
(I assess the quality.)
```

Systems `masuro` calibration, `nilaki` inspection, `kiphira` fault, and `kisholu` specification can join this vocabulary. Testing gathers evidence through a procedure; assessment interprets evidence against a criterion; inspection examines technical condition or conformity; review reconsiders a prior matter; certification issues a formal claim. None automatically approves safe return to use.

## Workload, drudgery, and exploitation

`ritako` gathers the amount, intensity, timing, complexity, interruption, and coordination burden of work over an interval. It can include assigned, chosen, paid, unpaid, visible, and invisible work. State whose workload is described and resist reducing it to a task count.

```
mia ha ritako ma kelomi.
1SG PROX workload NEG accept.
(I do not accept the workload.)
```

`rimawu` gives direct lexical space to work experienced or structured as tedious, exhausting, unwanted, degrading, denied agency, or disconnected from intelligible purpose. Identify whose experience or analysis grounds the term. Phi's positive `riola` labor must not be used to overwrite that judgment.

```
mia ha rimawu naweri.
1SG PROX drudgery refuse.
(I refuse the drudgery.)
```

`pilora` makes an analytical claim about power: one party secures disproportionate benefit or shifts burden through another's labor, vulnerability, dependency, exclusion, or resource relation. State the actors, power relation, benefit, burden, realistic alternatives, evidence, and affected account.

```
punoki lo miona pilora.
institution PL person exploit.
(The institution exploits people.)
```

Drudgery does not by itself prove exploitation, and exploitation need not depend on every affected person describing the work as drudgery. Commons `kawhera` coercion concerns constrained practical choice, while `whepelo` retaliation concerns disadvantage imposed in response to an earlier action. These terms support criticism without making criticism semantically vague.

## Stock, inventory, supply, offcuts, and provenance

`muphera` is the physical collection held for future use. `sitawi` is a time-bounded representation of that collection. A workshop can have stock without an accurate inventory, and an inventory can remain on record after the stock changes.

```
keritha henoi muphera phelu.
workshop ENOUGH stock hold.
(The workshop has enough stock.)
```

```
mia ha sitawi tukiro.
1SG PROX inventory review.
(I review the inventory.)
```

`sephori` makes material, tools, information, or another resource available to a recipient, process, or place. Adequacy, condition, provenance, receipt, acceptance, ownership, and restrictions require separate claims.

```
shia wei keritha muralo sephori.
3SG DAT workshop material supply.
(They supply material to the workshop.)
```

`katemu` names material separated outside the intended main form during cutting or shaping. An offcut may become stock, enter another design, require hazardous handling, be recycled, or become `whemori` waste; the noun does not decide its afterlife.

```
mia lo katemu phelure.
1SG PL offcut store.
(I store the offcuts.)
```

`luseri` traces a claimed source and history of custody, transfer, transformation, or contribution. State sources, stages, gaps, confidence, and exact identifiers. Provenance can inform attribution, repair, safety, ecological analysis, and ownership disputes without proving any of them.

```
ha muralo luseri se sirami.
PROX material provenance PASS record.
(The material's provenance is recorded.)
```

Ecological `muralo` material, `panuri` resource, `whemori` waste, and `tukelu` recycling remain relational ecological concepts. Work stock and offcuts describe practical material positions; neither grants ownership or disposal authority.

## Useful transparent compositions and source boundaries

| Phi expression or strategy | Practical meaning | Why it remains compositional or external |
|---|---|---|
| polite whole-clause question naming a proposed action | request or offer | Keeps the addressee's answer, conditions, and alternative visible instead of treating the proposal as work already owed. |
| ordered `lo riporu` tasks with actions and `pherami` dependencies | steps or work sequence | Shows the actual order and relation rather than hiding them in a generic sequence word. |
| task or project plus existing `sholu` and a stated `kirema` criterion | completion under a criterion | Does not turn completion into quality, safety, acceptance, approval, payment, or freedom from follow-up responsibility. |
| `sithora` role plus tasks, skill, authority, and work relations | job, occupation, or profession analysis | Exact employment or professional categories may carry institutional and legal force that one Phi word would erase. |
| `moloi` apprentice, `thunai` student, or explicit practice and skill statements | novice or learner status | Keeps limited experience scoped to a domain and avoids making it a fixed personal identity. |
| exact source credential plus `kiranu`, `sikanu`, and `kanuwa` where each fits | license or professional credential analysis | Preserves issuer, title, identifier, scope, and validity while keeping qualification, certification, and authorization distinct. |
| exact drawing, dimension, tolerance, or revision plus `noporu` and Systems `kisholu` | technical design or specification | Source notation carries the exact artifact; Phi describes its role and interpretation. |
| bodily self-report, refusal, requested change, Medical or Accessibility terms, and Commons authority terms | fatigue, accommodation, ergonomic limit, or stop-work relation | Prevents work status and technical capacity from overriding the person's body, access, consent, or safety. |
| exact wage, rate, contract, employment category, warranty, liability term, or collective agreement | economic or legal work relation | Exact terms may be operative and contested; Phi can analyze transfers, commitments, burdens, benefits, ownership, and authority around them without replacing them. |
| exact tool, component, software, automation system, or protective equipment identity plus Systems and work relations | modern technical work object | Exact identification and instructions matter more than creating a rapidly aging catalogue of borrowed object names. |

Source material remains outside the Phi passage. Phi may point to, describe, translate, or analyze an exact schedule, drawing, standard, certificate, contract, wage, identifier, formula, measurement, warning, record, or source-script name, but its tokens do not occupy Phi syntax. Preserve source spelling, script, notation, precision, version, and provenance.

## Using the module well

Begin with the concrete work relation: who proposes, assigns, accepts, refuses, performs, coordinates, supervises, receives, observes, tests, assesses, records, or is affected by which action. Add the relevant task or project boundary, plan, dependencies, conditions, schedule, deadline, status, materials, evidence, and uncertainty. Then state authority, obligation, ownership, compensation, bodily limits, access, safety, and acceptance separately when they matter.

Use the critical terms directly when they fit. `rimawu` and `pilora` exist so positive words for craft, service, cooperation, commitment, and responsibility do not sanitize unwanted or extractive relations. At the same time, identify the affected standpoint and evidence rather than treating condemnation as a substitute for analysis.

Ask for a core paraphrase when a module word is unfamiliar. A useful paraphrase does not need to recreate the dictionary entry in one sentence; it can expose the people, actions, criteria, sources, conditions, and consequences that the module word gathers.

Keep testing the vocabulary in craft narratives, project discussions, shared maintenance, learning relationships, labor criticism, material histories, household work, software work, and ecological repair. Coin another module word when a recurring concept deserves lexical presence or when a compound is cumbersome, ambiguous, or aesthetically wrong for Phi. Retain a composition when its parts expose a useful boundary or responsibility.
