# Household and Daily Life

Yesterday's soup is in a jar on the shelf. Before anyone calls it waste, somebody lifts the lid, smells it, and decides whether it can become tonight's meal. In the next room, a blanket dries over two seats while the floor waits for the broom.

The Household and Daily Life module gives Phi speakers optional vocabulary for the small arrangements that keep a dwelling usable: rooms and storage, the work of washing, things kept on the bed, and the path from recipe to leftovers. These words use ordinary Phi grammar. A speaker who has not learned them can still ask for a core paraphrase, and learning the module does not decide who belongs in a home or who owes its work.

The chapter table below introduces 30 roots. Current classifications bring the learning list to 42 words; the complete machine-generated list is available under [Household and Daily Life in the module lexicon](../lexicon/by_module.md#household-and-daily-life). The JSON entries in `vocabulary/content/` remain authoritative for each word's form and full definition.

## Core vocabulary used by the module

The new words enter a crowded house. Phi already names the dwelling, the people around it, the furniture beneath them, the food on the table, and most of the work that makes those things last.

| Area | Core Phi resources |

| Interior and furniture | `nuroa` kitchen, `nuwera` bed, `wonepa` table, `meipa` seat, `ponu` door, `wira` window, `moru` wall, `tomae` floor, `toru` roof, `tholu` space |
| Food and vessels | `nuora` food, `napa` bread, `sulopa` soup, `phialu` water, `nuola` eat, `milau` drink, `pharelo` cook, `noru` bowl, `soeli` cup, `palu` plate, `tomi` pot, `lopha` vessel, `molu` container, `wiru` basket |
| Cloth, rest, and temperature | `wetha` cloth, `liru` fabric, `wethalu` garment, `nulae` sleep, `therilu` rest, `sukaro` hot, `sulae` warm, `wuloe` soft, `kurathi` dry, `wirua` wet |
| Upkeep and repair | `sawa` wash, `hewe` wipe, `hiso` clean, `muhena` dust, `phuri` brush, `shiroka` repair, `talome` restore, `tenoa` tool, `thinu` put, `phelu` hold |
| Choice and relations | `mirewu` private, `kanu` choose, `lesawi` consent, `nawo` agree, `naweri` refuse, `theama` care, `phowe` share, `nuawe` together |

Work, Craft, and Repair can identify a domestic task and show who carries its burden. Ecological Systems and Material Life follows what happens to matter after household use. Commons and Collective Governance handles claims of property and institutional authority inside a home, while Accessibility and Participation tests whether the actual arrangement works for the person using it. The base rows keep those ordinary facts outside optional study. None of those module relations turns a household into an institution or a person into an assigned domestic role.

## Privacy belongs to shared core

**mirewu** private has no module field. A person may need privacy at home, in a clinic, or among friends. Meetings and public systems need the same boundary, so the word belongs in base vocabulary.

```
ha lokue mirewu nai.
PROX place private be.
(This place is private.)
```

Privacy is a chosen limit on access or attention. It can belong to a room with an ordinary door, a conversation that is not repeated, or a record held within agreed bounds. `kupe` hide concerns concealment, `kupela` secret concerns information deliberately untold, and `sonu` alone concerns company. None of them can do the exact work of `mirewu`.

## A household shares daily life

`womuri` can be one person. It can also be people joined by kinship, friendship, partnership, or no short source label. The noun begins with what they keep together rather than with a family tree.

```
lo mia womuri nai.
PL 1SG household be.
(We are a household.)
```

Affection, permanence, and freedom of choice come from the surrounding sentences. `womuri` gives the domestic unit a name without settling those facts in advance.

## Rooms, visits, and locks

`tholupi` is an interior room before anyone decides what the room is for. A kitchen keeps its own word, but a room used for sleep at night and weaving in daylight can remain `tholupi` throughout.

```
ha tholupi thiku nai.
PROX room small be.
(This room is small.)
```

A visitor crosses into that interior without becoming part of the household. The full sound of `phemi` guest sits inside `phemiru` visit, and the final syllable keeps the visit moving toward its end.

```
mia ha womu phemiru.
1SG PROX home visit.
(I visit this home.)
```

The door can be closed with `tapu` and held closed by `kirato` lock. A lock can protect privacy, confine someone, or deny them their own home. The noun identifies the mechanism; people and authority remain visible around it.

```
mia roe kira ha kirato phae.
1SG INS key PROX lock open.
(I open this lock with a key.)
```

## Things kept above the floor

A shelf is not a small table pushed against the wall. Its job is keeping: the book waits there without asking anyone to sit down.

```
ha phelasi shelu phelu.
PROX shelf book hold.
(This shelf holds a book.)
```

`lirupa` bag and `molupa` box begin from two different kinds of container. The bag yields around what it carries and folds afterward. The box keeps its own edges.

```
mia mua ha lirupa nuora phelu.
1SG LOC PROX bag food hold.
(I keep food in this bag.)
```

```
ha molupa lo tenoa phelu.
PROX box PL tool hold.
(This box holds tools.)
```

The distinction does not decide material by itself. A leather case may behave like a box, while a tightly woven sack still behaves like a bag. Shape under use carries the choice.

## Bottles, jars, buckets, and lids

`lophani` bottle narrows the general liquid vessel at its neck. The smaller opening helps it travel and pour, but the material and closure can change from one bottle to another.

```
ha lophani phialu phelu.
PROX bottle water hold.
(This bottle holds water.)
```

A hand or spoon can reach into `tapulo` jar. The jar may close under `tapuri` lid, though the fit itself still needs to be described when keeping food depends on it.

```
ha tapulo morisa phelu.
PROX jar salt hold.
(This jar holds salt.)
```

```
ha tapuri tomi tapu.
PROX lid pot close.
(This lid closes the pot.)
```

`kolupe` bucket carries its load from a handle and remains open for the next filling. That makes it useful for water, ash, garden soil, or laundry without turning every handled container into the same thing.

```
mia roe ha kolupe phialu kolua.
1SG INS PROX bucket water carry.
(I carry water with this bucket.)
```

## Sweeping, washing, and sanitation

Dust on a floor needs a different motion from water on a plate. `phurilo` is the broom, and `phurewa` is the returning stroke that moves the dust.

```
mia roe ha phurilo tomae phurewa.
1SG INS PROX broom floor sweep.
(I sweep the floor with this broom.)
```

Soap enters an action Phi already knows. `sawathi` is the cleansing material; `sawa` remains the verb wash.

```
mia roe sawathi lo soeli sawa.
1SG INS soap PL cup wash.
(I wash the cups with soap.)
```

The wet cloth in the hand may be `wethaki` towel. Once garments and household cloth are gathered into the washing cycle, they become `wethate` laundry, and the work can then be named with Work vocabulary if its assignment or burden matters.

```
mia roe wethaki ha palu hewe.
1SG INS towel PROX plate wipe.
(I wipe this plate with a towel.)
```

```
mia ha wethate sawa.
1SG PROX laundry wash.
(I wash this laundry.)
```

Folding returns each piece from the gathered load to its next use.

```
mia ha wethalu lupaki.
1SG PROX garment fold.
(I fold this garment.)
```

`muneki` latrine gives ordinary sanitation a household noun. It covers the place or fixture, while source instructions carry any exact design, maintenance, or health requirement.

```
ha womu muneki phelu.
PROX home latrine hold.
(This home has a latrine.)
```

## The bed after dark

The bed is already `nuwera`. `wethamo` blanket lies over the sleeper, and `komalu` pillow supports the head or whichever part of the body needs a smaller resting place.

```
ha wethamo sulae nai.
PROX blanket warm be.
(This blanket is warm.)
```

```
mia nia ha komalu koma thinu.
1SG ON PROX pillow head put.
(I put my head on this pillow.)
```

Neither object promises comfort. The blanket can be damp, and the pillow can be too firm. Phi says those conditions where the body meets them.

## A meal has parts

`nurome` meal gathers food into one occasion. It can happen at any time of day, so morning and evening meals use ordinary time words rather than separate inherited schedules.

```
lo mia ha nurome nuola.
PL 1SG PROX meal eat.
(We eat this meal.)
```

The spoon carries its little bowl into the hand. A carved serving spoon and a small eating spoon are both `norupe`; size and use separate them when they sit together.

```
mia roe norupe sulopa nuola.
1SG INS spoon soup eat.
(I eat soup with a spoon.)
```

An ingredient is food seen from inside a preparation. The onion in the basket is `nuora`; the onion chosen for soup is also `phareme` ingredient.

```
mia ha phareme kanu.
1SG PROX ingredient choose.
(I choose this ingredient.)
```

`thekopa` recipe keeps the ordered knowledge of what happens next. It may live in a book, in speech, or in someone's practiced hands. Exact quantities and source wording stay beside the Phi account when changing them would change the dish.

```
mia ha thekopa theo.
1SG PROX recipe read.
(I read this recipe.)
```

## Food after the meal

Food does not become waste because the meal ended. `pheomu` leftovers gives the remainder a name while its next use is still open.

```
ha pheomu mua tapulo nai.
PROX leftovers LOC jar be.
(These leftovers are in the jar.)
```

Condition can close that possibility. `mukesi` spoil is the practical judgment that a perishable thing no longer suits the intended eating or use. The reason may need more evidence than smell alone.

```
ha nuora to mukesi.
PROX food PST spoil.
(This food spoiled.)
```

Ecological `morume` decompose follows the material process, while `mukesi` follows the lost household use. A spoiled food may decompose, but the two claims answer different questions.

## Heat, mixing, and flow

The broad verb `pharelo` cook still carries most kitchen work. Four narrower verbs help when the method changes the result.

`therapi` boil names the liquid state that can be heard and seen in the pot.

```
mia phialu therapi.
1SG water boil.
(I boil water.)
```

`napako` bake takes its opening from `napa` bread but reaches any food cooked by surrounding dry heat.

```
mia napa napako.
1SG bread bake.
(I bake bread.)
```

`welotu` mix moves ingredients through one another. They may remain distinct, as beans and herbs do, or become one dough; the desired result says when the work is done.

```
mia lo phareme welotu.
1SG PL ingredient mix.
(I mix the ingredients.)
```

`seluto` pour gives direction to `selu` flow. The water moves, but the hand and vessel begin its path.

```
mia phialu seluto.
1SG water pour.
(I pour water.)
```

## Useful transparent compositions

Some familiar objects and arrangements are clearer with their parts showing.

| Concept | Phi strategy | What the composition keeps visible |
|---|---|---|
| Chair, stool, bench, or sitting cushion | Use `meipa` seat with material, height, back, or shape where needed | The existing word already covers whatever is made for sitting. |
| General container | Use `molu` container | Shape matters only when a bag, box, bottle, jar, bucket, or named vessel is intended. |
| Basket | Use `wiru` basket | Its woven, self-supporting construction already has a core word. |
| Cupboard, pantry, or closet | Use `tholupi`, `phelasi`, `molupa`, and `tapu` around the actual arrangement | The room, shelf, container, and closure remain separate enough to describe many designs. |
| Morning or evening meal | Use `nurome` with `kelua` morning, `howai` evening, or another time phrase | The language does not impose one culture's meal schedule. |
| Household chore | Use Work `riporu` task with the concrete action | Assignment, acceptance, workload, and drudgery stay available when the work is contested. |
| Frying, roasting, steaming, or another named method | Use `pharelo` cook with a Phi description or preserve the source method beside it | The heat source, vessel, liquid, and movement can be explained without pretending the methods are identical. |
| Bathing place or washing fixture | Use `sawa`, `tholupi`, `noru`, `molu`, and source information as needed | The action, room, basin, water path, and exact fixture do not collapse into one assumed design. |
| Household membership or domestic role | Use `womuri` with Commons `wemari` member or `sithora` role only when those relations are actually claimed | Sharing a dwelling does not silently assign authority or work. |
| Rent, tenancy, lease, or exact address | Use Commons relations and preserve the source document or address | The legal category, exact terms, and identity of the place keep their source force. |

## Source material at home

A household meets source material constantly. An address may sit on an envelope and a medicine bottle keeps its own label; repair instructions, meter readings, food names, and an exact copied recipe do the same. Those artifacts stay outside the Phi passage when their spelling, value, or authority matters. Phi can say where the paper is, who read it, what action followed, and which word still needs explanation.

At night, the leftovers go into a jar and the dry towel returns to its peg. The lock turns, and behind the door, `mirewu` leaves one room unanswered until morning.
