# The passive 'se'

## Shifting the spotlight

Every sentence is a small stage where different elements compete for attention. Active voice places the doer in the center: "The gardener planted the flowers." Passive voice shifts that spotlight to the receiver: "The flowers were planted by the gardener."

This shift is never merely decorative. When we choose passive voice, we make a conscious decision about what deserves the listener's primary attention. Sometimes we want to emphasize the result rather than the process, the affected rather than the actor, the outcome rather than the cause.

Consider the difference between "The committee rejected the proposal" and "The proposal was rejected." The first frames the event as something the committee did. The second frames it as something that happened to the proposal. Same event, different emphasis.

The passive becomes especially powerful when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or deliberately obscured. "The window was broken" focuses entirely on the window's state, leaving the question of who broke it in the shadows. Sometimes this ambiguity is exactly what we want. It allows us to discuss effects without getting caught up in debates about causes.

## The sound of receptivity

Phi's passive particle **se** /ˈse̞/ carries within its sound the essence of its function. The soft 's' suggests something gentle and receptive, like the sound of wind through leaves or water flowing around a stone. The open vowel 'e' feels like opening one's hands to receive rather than grasping to take.

The vocabulary definition captures this beautifully: *se* begins with "the soft sibilant that flows without aggression—appropriate for marking the gentleness of receiving rather than acting."

When we transform an active sentence into passive voice with *se*, we alter the conceptual framework. The active sentence places focus on the builder and their action:

> **mia womu lorima**  
> I house build  
> "I build the house"

Adding *se* shifts our attention entirely to the house:

> **womu se lorima**  
> house PASS build  
> "The house is built"

The passive particle creates what we might call "receptive sentences." These emphasize receiving rather than doing, being affected rather than affecting, undergoing rather than undertaking.

## When and why to choose passive

The decision to use passive voice is never arbitrary. In Phi, where every grammatical choice should be conscious and deliberate, understanding when to choose *se* becomes important.

### Diplomatic communication

When we want to discuss problems or mistakes without directly assigning blame, passive voice provides a gentle way forward:

> **nophi se shonela**  
> story PASS learn  
> "The story was learned" / "Lessons were learned"

This acknowledges what happened without pointing fingers, creating space for constructive dialogue rather than defensive responses.

### Unknown or unimportant agents

Sometimes the outcome matters more than the process:

> **peloru se thuroa**  
> flower PASS grow  
> "The flowers were grown"

This focuses on the beautiful result without getting sidetracked by questions of who did the planting.

### Centering the affected

Perhaps most importantly, passive voice allows us to center the experiences of those who are affected by actions rather than those who perform them:

> **lopia se naphe**  
> child PASS help  
> "The child was helped"

This keeps focus on the one who received help rather than shifting attention to the helper. The choice to foreground the affected rather than the actor reflects Phi's deeper values of compassion and attention to those who are often overlooked.

## The grammar of 'se' in context

As a Slot 1 particle, *se* takes its place in the ordered sequence of verbal information, working seamlessly with temporal particles.

The basic transformation is straightforward: where an active sentence places the actor in subject position, a passive sentence with *se* places the affected party in subject position. The original subject can be omitted entirely, or preserved when relevant.

When *se* combines with tense particles, it creates rich possibilities for temporal and perspective layering:

> **womu to se lorima**  
> house PAST PASS build  
> "The house was built"

> **womu so se lorima**  
> house FUT PASS build  
> "The house will be built"

When combined with aspect particles:

> **womu to se ki lorima**  
> house PAST PASS PROG build  
> "The house was being built"

> **womu so se si lorima**  
> house FUT PASS PERF build  
> "The house will have been built"

The elegance of this system lies in its compositionality. Each particle contributes its own layer of meaning without interfering with the others. A speaker can choose to mark tense, aspect, and voice in any combination, creating exactly the temporal and perspective profile they intend.

## De-escalation through grammar

The vocabulary entry for *se* notes its connection to peace linguistics: "By making passive voice easily available, 'se' enables speakers to discuss harmful actions without focusing on blame. Instead of 'who did this?', we can explore 'what was experienced?'—shifting from accusation to understanding, from perpetrator-focus to healing-focus."

This isn't about avoiding accountability. It's about creating space for honest conversation. When someone feels accused, they become defensive. When we describe what happened without immediate blame, we open room for acknowledgment and repair.

Every use of *se* represents a deliberate choice to foreground the affected rather than the actor, to emphasize reception rather than action, to create space for the experiences of those who undergo rather than those who undertake.
