# Aspect: views of an event

Tense locates an event. Aspect chooses the view a clause takes of it.

| Particle | Gloss | View | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **si** | IPFV | in progress | **mia si theo.** I am reading. |
| **ki** | PFV | complete at the reference time | **mia ki theo.** I have read. |
| **pa** | INCH | beginning | **mia pa theo.** I begin to read. |
| **te** | CESS | ending | **mia te theo.** I stop reading. |
| **ro** | HAB | customary across occasions | **mia ro theo.** I read regularly. |

Only one aspect may appear in a clause. If an account needs two views, it needs two clauses.

## si: in progress

**si** presents the event from within while it is underway:

**shia si meliho.** — They are singing.

The sustained **s** can continue as long as the breath does, a physical mnemonic for the progressive view. The particle says neither that the action is temporary nor that it will finish. It simply catches the present stretch.

Habit does not use **si**. An action occurring now and an action characteristic across many occasions are different claims.

## ki: complete at the reference time

**ki** presents the event as a completed whole at the time established by tense:

**shia ki meliho.** — They have sung.

The complete closure and quick release of **k** give the word a clipped contour that can help memory. Completion need not mean success, natural conclusion, or praise. It means that the event is presented as complete.

Bare **ki** means "have done," **to ki** "had done," and **so ki** "will have done."

## pa: beginning

**pa** places attention on entry into an action or state:

**mia pa theo.** — I begin to read.

The lips close and open abruptly, an embodied mnemonic for onset. **pa** does not say whether the beginning was deliberate, sudden, or successful.

## te: ending

**te** presents an action or state as it ceases:

**shia te meliho.** — They stop singing.

Ending differs from completion. A person can stop reading halfway through a book, so **te theo** need not imply **ki theo**. The particle also leaves cause open: someone may choose to stop, be interrupted, or simply reach a point where the event ceases.

## ro: habit or characteristic pattern

**ro** presents recurrence across separate occasions:

**shia ro meliho.** — They sing regularly.

The tap may recur and the trill already does, which gives the whole form a useful mnemonic for return. **ro** reports a pattern without turning it into identity, virtue, or permanence. A former habit takes **to ro**; a future recurring arrangement takes **so ro**.

Aspect lets two speakers describe the same activity differently without either changing the verb. One may catch the singing in progress, another its ending, and a third the custom of singing at dusk. The choice says which view the clause takes.
