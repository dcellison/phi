# Building numbers

A number is digits counting units, largest unit first, remainder after, and nothing else:

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | *ta shao* | one three-group |
| 4 | *ta shao ta* | one three-group and one |
| 5 | *ta shao wi* | one three-group and two |
| 6 | *wi shao* | two three-groups |
| 7 | *wi shao ta* | two three-groups and one |
| 9 | *ta phoi* | one nine-group |
| 14 | *ta phoi ta shao wi* | a nine, a three, and two |

No conjunction joins the parts; position does the work, as it does everywhere. Read *ta phoi ta shao wi* left to right and you watch a quantity assemble the way a Phi sentence assembles: the big frame announced first, the detail delivered inside it.

A scale appears at most once, scales descend from largest to smallest, and only `ta` or `wi` may count a scale. A zero coefficient is shown by leaving that scale out: 82 is *ta rei ta*, not a form containing `mu`. Standalone `mu` still means exactly zero.

The named scales set a finite exact range. The longest canonical numeral is *wi rei wi lau wi phoi wi shao wi* (242). Phi does not recursively count groups of `rei`; 243 and larger exact values remain source material outside the Phi passage. A speaker can instead choose a bare scale or a quantifier when approximate magnitude is sufficient.

Say the numbers aloud and match them to quantities arranged in equal triads. Ordered counting helps recall, but fluent understanding also needs shuffled practice: hear a form and identify its magnitude, then see a quantity and produce its form. Do not expect automaticity after a fixed number of examples, and do not use decimal-conversion speed as the measure of Phi fluency.
