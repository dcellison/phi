# Approximation: choosing useful precision

A scale unit standing alone, with no digit before it, means *about that many*:

*shao philo* — some three days

*phoi miona* — nine or so people

*lau shiro* — a twenty-seven of trees: many

*rei silero* — stars on the scale of an eighty-one-group: a great many

This is not sloppiness; it is an explicit choice of precision. A bare scale names rough magnitude around that scale without defining a rounding interval. In ordinary context, bare *lau* often has the force of “many,” and bare *rei* often has the force of “a great many” or “too many to count for this purpose.” Neither form literally claims that the quantity is uncountable.

Beyond *ta lau* (twenty-seven), exact counting becomes long-winded on purpose. Exact Phi remains available through 242; larger exact values belong in source material outside the Phi passage. Approximation is appropriate when the exact value is unknown or irrelevant, but it is not inherently more honest than precision. Safety, dosage, accessibility, allocation, accountability, and faithful reporting may require an exact source value even when the surrounding Phi account uses a rough magnitude.

The journal practice (chapter 23) can use `nu ta shao philo`, the third day, when exact position matters, or *shao philo*, approximately three days, when only the scale of the interval matters. An exact calendar identifier may remain in source material when later identification requires it.
