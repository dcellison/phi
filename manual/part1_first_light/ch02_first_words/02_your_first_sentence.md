# Your first sentence

Most languages start you somewhere safe: the weather, the location of a pen, a hotel you are not actually staying at. Phi starts you at I love you. That is a design decision, not an accident. If a language is going to put one sentence in your mouth first, it may as well be one you might someday need to mean.

**mia thia lothea.**

Three words, in an order worth noticing: I, then you, and then, only when both of you are already in the sentence, the love.

Phi places the verb last in every sentence, and here that means the loving arrives in a room where both people are present. English delivers its verb early and lets the object trail after; Phi lets the participants gather first. The practice, in this sentence and every one after it, is to use that order for what it offers: notice who is involved before the action completes.

The sentence also introduces the broad pattern that organises Phi: **a dependent comes before what it depends on.** Objects precede verbs. Descriptors precede nouns. Most particles announce their scope before it; paired complement and quotation frames also mark where that scope closes. The language announces, then delivers. Once that pattern feels familiar in a three-word sentence, much of the later grammar has somewhere to settle.

**mia** means I or me. Close the lips for nasal `m`, release into high-front `i`, then keep the following `a` in its own syllable: *mi-a*, with stress on the first syllable. Position tells the listener whether `mia` is subject, object, or possessor.

**thia** means you, addressing one person. Place the tongue tip lightly between the teeth for `th`, move into high-front `i`, then open a separate `a`: *thi-a*, with stress on the first syllable. The form carries no rank or case ending; whoever you address, the word is the same size.

**lothea** means love as deep affection or active care. Release `l` into rounded `o`, place the tongue at the teeth for stressed `the`, then keep final `a` separate: *lo-the-a*, with stress on the middle syllable. The full contour moves from voice through breath into an open vowel, but its parts are sounds, not smaller words for kinds of love.

Put them together and you have a complete clause: `mia thia lothea.` I love you.

Now reverse the participants:

**thia mia lothea.**

You love me.

The words themselves do not change. Their positions identify the subject and object in these complete clauses.

## A question

To ask a yes or no question, place **wa** at the beginning:

**wa thia mia lothea.**

Do you love me?

The clause keeps its ordinary order. `wa` changes the assertion into an inquiry before the proposition arrives, so no one mistakes the question for a claim. To say it, round the lips for `w` and then open into `a`. That widening can recall the room a real answer needs.

## Building more

With the same order and a few new words, you can make other short clauses:

**mia thia nila.**

I see you.

**wa thia mia sano.**

Do you know me?

**mia shea wilao.**

I long for peace.

Word order distinguishes the participants; context, as everywhere in human speech, carries the rest. Three words in, you already hold the shape of every Phi sentence you will ever meet.
