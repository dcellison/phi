# The learning relationship

A learner asks why `ke` cannot mark a general expectation. The teacher can point to the distinction: `ke` begins with evidence from the present case, while `ho` rests on background assumption. If the question exposes an unclear lesson, the lesson needs repair. Expertise does not become less real when it is required to explain itself.

Phi keeps teaching and learning as separate events. `thumela` (teach) means demonstrating a pattern or arranging experience so another person can learn. `shonela` (learn) is what the learner does through instruction, experience, or study. A clear explanation may fail to produce learning, and a learner may discover something the teacher did not supply.

That distinction gives each role responsibility. A teacher owes accurate explanation and useful practice. Honest correction belongs to the work too. A student brings questions and prior knowledge, along with the right to say when an explanation has not worked. The roles are unequal in experience at a particular task without becoming a ranking of human worth.

`thunai` (student) applies to anyone engaged in learning, whether the study is self-directed, shared with peers, or guided by a teacher. The role can move from one person to another as the subject changes. Someone who teaches pronunciation in the morning may need help with Tengwar after lunch. This is not a collapse of expertise; it is a reasonably accurate account of lunch.

Mentorship can add continuity and trust. A mentor may know common difficulties and offer a practice that once helped, while the learner remains the person whose understanding is at stake. Phi's two verbs keep the boundary plain: teaching may prepare the way, but it cannot impersonate learning.
