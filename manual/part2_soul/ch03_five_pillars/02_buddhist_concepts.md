# Pillar two: Buddhist concepts

## A practice of attention

Someone begins a reply and notices that irritation has already chosen the first sentence. The pause does not solve the disagreement, but another choice has become possible. Phi draws openly from Buddhist philosophy and practice to cultivate that kind of attention, without requiring faith or metaphysical agreement.

The borrowed tools include mindfulness, compassion, equanimity, and attention to the conditions from which an action arises. The aim is practice, not conversion. What a sentence can do is offer the speaker a place to notice a mental state while it is still choosing words, and Phi builds such places deliberately.

## Mindfulness as grammar

Phi calls deliberate attention to present experience `thesua` (mindful). The word includes attention to speech while it is still happening, before habit has carried the sentence farther than its speaker intended.

In a Subject-Object-Verb clause, the participants arrive before the action that joins them. A speaker can use that order as a small exercise in keeping the scene present until the verb comes. The order asks for neither a long pause nor a completed thought before speech begins; fluent Phi may move quickly. Its value is the occasion for attention, not delay.

The evidential particles provide another such occasion. When a source matters, `hi` marks direct witness, `ke` inference, `ti` report, and `ho` assumption. Choosing one invites the speaker to inspect a claim before presenting it. Leaving all four off remains grammatical and makes no source claim.

## Right Speech

An accurate sentence can still be timed to humiliate, and a gentle one can conceal what the listener needs to know. Buddhist traditions place Right Speech within the path of practice and ask speakers to attend to truth, care, and purpose. Phi carries that concern into the present utterance: what does the speaker know, what do they intend, and how may the words enter a relationship?

Source markers can distinguish witness from assumption. Clause-initial frames make questions and directives audible. The lexicon gives ordinary words to pain, consent, and refusal, while keeping forgiveness separate from repair. Each keeps a relevant choice within reach at the moment of speaking.

Compassion does not require a pleasant answer. A speaker may need to state pain, refuse contact, name harm, or preserve exact testimony. Right Speech asks for truth without turning truth into permission to dominate. In disagreement, Phi usually keeps needs, consequences, boundaries, and possible repair nearer than the language of victory.

## Conditions, not isolation

A flower on the table did not arrive alone. It began as a seed in soil. Water and sunlight followed under particular weather and human care, with chance among the conditions too. Dependent origination directs attention to such conditions and to the changes that follow when one of them changes. It is more exact than the pleasant but vague claim that everything is simply one.

Phi borrows that attentiveness to relation. It also records a few deliberate sound kinships, such as `whemura` (dream), `wheiru` (mist), and `whemoa` (empty). Their shared opening can recall things whose shapes are hard to hold, but no hidden root supplies their meanings. In the Heart Sutra, the text carries the argument about emptiness; the adjective `whemoa` keeps its ordinary image of an emptied vessel.

Buddhist belief is not required. Phi names the source because disguising an inheritance as an invention would be poor practice, and because mindfulness becomes thinner when it is reduced to a fashionable synonym for calm.

The speaker, not the grammar, undertakes Right Speech.
