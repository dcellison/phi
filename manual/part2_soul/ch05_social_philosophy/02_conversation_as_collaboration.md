# Conversation as collaboration

English keeps a well-stocked cupboard of combat metaphors for dialogue. We "attack" ideas, "defend" positions, "demolish" arguments, and "defeat" opponents. We speak of "winning" debates as though understanding were a zero-sum game. These metaphors make a contest readily available as one model of disagreement.

Phi offers different resources. Modifier-first grammar lets many relations arrive before the element they affect, while the final verb asks the listener to hold the participants in mind. Evidentials can state the claimed source of knowledge. `pi no` can mark a polite request. None of these decides whether the conversation is collaborative; they give collaboration explicit material to work with.

None of this prevents disagreement. Phi speakers can and should challenge ideas. A `hi`- or `ke`-marked claim is actually one syllable longer than an unmarked declaration. Its value is not convenience but specificity: the listener knows whether the speaker claims witness or inference and can respond to that basis.

When people use those distinctions well, understanding can emerge that none of them brought to the table alone. That is more than compromise; different perspectives may produce an account that was unavailable to any one speaker. Phi cannot arrange the meeting on their behalf. It can help them keep track of what each person brought.
