# Repair across difference

A familiar word passes unheard across a noisy room. An unfamiliar one arrives perfectly and helps no more. In either case, `hao` can ask for repetition or clarification without pretending the listener already knows which part failed.

Shared background never appears automatically, even among people from the same household or town. Across different languages and experiences, speakers may need to build it more deliberately. Phi has repair forms for that work. Among them are `pi no reo lilea`, "please clarify the meaning," and the plain admission `mia ma sano`, "I do not know."

## What a new word does not reveal

An unknown Phi word remains unknown. A documented sound sibling can give a learner a useful hint after the relationship has been taught. Most resemblance is not a code, and nobody can recover a definition by assigning meanings to its syllables.

This makes explanation necessary. A speaker can give an example, contrast the word with a nearby one, or use a base-vocabulary paraphrase. The lexicon helps by placing distinctions in descriptions and examples rather than trusting sound to introduce a concept on its own.

## What grammar can contribute

Predictable particle positions can help a learner locate clause force, verb marking, and word-level modification once the slot system is familiar. Verb-final order also carries a cost: earlier participants may have to remain in memory until the action arrives.

Evidentials can locate one kind of difference. A witnessed claim and a reported claim invite different questions, while `ho` marks background assumption rather than a degree of low confidence. Repair becomes more exact when the next question addresses the source actually claimed.

The remaining work is social: noticing confusion, pausing long enough to ask, accepting that understanding may arrive without agreement. Grammar keeps the repair tools on the table where both speakers can reach them. Picking one up is the conversation.
