# The ethics of communication

After a difficult meeting, a friend asks what happened. You heard a voice rise, saw one person leave, and inferred that they were upset. A truthful account keeps those three claims apart instead of reporting the inference as though it were witnessed.

Phi's evidentials make that separation concise when the source matters. `hi` claims direct perception, `ke` a conclusion from particular signs, `ti` information received from elsewhere, and `ho` an assumption based on expectation. The particles describe provenance, not honesty or confidence. A speaker can misuse any of them.

## Truth with care

Right Speech refuses the familiar bargain in which honesty excuses cruelty and kindness excuses evasion. Truth remains answerable to what happened. Care enters through timing, detail, manner, and attention to what the listener can safely do with the account.

Sometimes the careful choice is blunt. Testimony may need exact names and actions. A person setting a boundary may have no reason to cushion a refusal until it becomes doubtful. Compassion includes the speaker, the listener, and anyone affected by what remains unsaid.

## Courtesy and agency

Putting `pi` before directive `no` adds courtesy. It does not turn an order into an invitation. A considerate request leaves practical room for refusal and does not punish the answer for being inconvenient.

The grammar cannot inspect that room. It can only mark the courtesy and directive force openly enough for both people to discuss. Power, consent, and consequence remain facts of the situation.

No wording can guarantee a gentle reception. The listener may be hurt, disagree, ask for time, or correct the account. Ethical speech includes that uncertainty. A person is not a doorstep where a carefully wrapped sentence can be left and declared delivered.
