# A limited palette, infinite expression

## The philosophy made audible

Phi's sound system embodies its core philosophy in physical form. Ten consonants, four fricative digraphs, and five vowels: a modest palette, chosen deliberately. Most of the sounds are among the most common in the world's languages; the four fricatives are rarer, and are there for their breath-like character rather than their universality.

This isn't just about easy pronunciation. When you speak Phi, you're doing philosophy with your breath.

## Cross-linguistic accessibility

The sounds of Phi appear in languages across the world. If you speak Spanish, Japanese, Italian, or Swahili, most of Phi's sounds will feel familiar immediately. If you speak English, German, or Mandarin, you'll find some small adjustments, but nothing that can't be mastered with practice.

This accessibility isn't accidental. A language designed for peace must welcome speakers from everywhere. Sounds that require years of practice create barriers. Sounds that most humans already produce create bridges.

## What makes Phi distinctive

While individual sounds are common, Phi combines them in ways that create a distinctive sonic texture:

- **Pure vowels** that never glide or blur
- **Open syllables** that always end in vowels
- **Vowel hiatus** where adjacent vowels stay separate
- **Breathy fricatives** reserved for abstract concepts
- **Penultimate stress** creating predictable rhythm

The result sounds flowing yet clear, like a stream over smooth stones. Each sound has space to be heard. Nothing crowds or collides.

## Learning through attention

Mastering Phi's sounds requires presence. You'll need to notice the shape of your mouth, the position of your tongue, the flow of your breath. This isn't extra work; it's the practice itself.

The same attention that produces correct pronunciation develops the mindfulness that Phi encourages in all communication. Even mechanical drills become meditation when approached with awareness.
