# The five vowels

## One target for each letter

Phi uses **a, e, i, o, u**. In careful reference speech, each letter aims at one vowel quality and does not turn into a diphthong. An English speaker may need particular care with `e` and `o`, since the vowels in words such as "day" and "go" usually move before they finish.

Each accent will land around these targets in its own way. Phi asks that the five categories remain distinct and that adjacent vowels stay in separate syllables.

| Vowel | IPA | Mouth position | Approximate model |
|-------|-----|----------------|-------------------|
| a | /ä/ | open, central, unrounded | Spanish *padre* or English "father" |
| e | /e̞/ | mid, front, unrounded | Spanish *mesa*, without the glide of English "hay" |
| i | /i/ | high, front, unrounded | English "machine" |
| o | /o̞/ | mid, back, rounded | Spanish *poco*, without the glide of English "go" |
| u | /u/ | high, back, rounded | English "food" without an initial "y" sound |

## The open vowel: a

For **a** /ä/, let the jaw open and keep the tongue low near the centre of the mouth. The lips stay unrounded. English "father" is a useful approach for many speakers, though the exact vowel varies by accent.

- `shea` /ˈʃe̞.ä/ (peace)
- `mia` /ˈmi.ä/ (I)
- `lothea` /lo̞.ˈθe̞.ä/ (love)

## The front vowels: e and i

For **e** /e̞/, raise the tongue to mid height toward the front of the mouth and leave the lips unrounded. Hold that target rather than following it with the small upward glide common in English "hay".

For **i** /i/, raise the front of the tongue close to the hard palate without making contact. The lips remain unrounded. English "machine" gives a serviceable model.

- `shiro` /ˈʃi.ro̞/ (tree)
- `shea` /ˈʃe̞.ä/ (peace)
- `thesua` /θe̞.ˈsu.ä/ (mindful)

## The back vowels: o and u

For **o** /o̞/, raise the back of the tongue to mid height and round the lips. English speakers can use Spanish *poco* as an approach, or shorten English "go" before its glide begins.

For **u** /u/, raise the back of the tongue higher and round the lips a little more. Begin directly with the vowel, without the "y" heard before the vowel in some pronunciations of English "cute".

- `womu` /ˈwo̞.mu/ (home)
- `nulae` /n̪u.ˈlä.e̞/ (sleep)
- `wepu` /ˈwe̞.pu/ (go)

## A circuit through the vowels

Say `a e i o u`. Give each vowel enough time to hear where the tongue and lips have settled. Then reverse the route: `u o i e a`. The point is not to make five identical performances. It is to learn five positions well enough that one vowel does not wander into another.

Once the positions are familiar, shorten the vowels to an ordinary speaking length and listen for the five categories to remain apart.
