# Correlative constructions

Simple coordination handles most needs, but sometimes you want to emphasize the relationship between connected elements. Correlative constructions do this through dedicated word pairs: an opener that announces the structure and a closer that completes it. The listener knows from the first word exactly what kind of connection is coming.

Phi has four correlative pairs. They share a consistent pattern: the opener always ends in `-a`, the closer always ends in `-o`. Once you learn this rule, you can recognize any correlative's role by its final vowel.

## Both...and: `lera...lero`

This pair emphasizes completeness, the sense that both elements matter equally and together form a whole:

```
lera shea lero lothea phaelo
BOTH peace AND.CORR love feel
(i feel both peace and love.)
```

```
lera sorae lero pheralu welao nai
BOTH sun AND.CORR rain good be
(both sun and rain are good.)
```

```
lera mia theo lero thia sheluo
BOTH 1SG read AND.CORR 2SG listen
(both i read, and you listen.)
```

Where simple `nela` connects items casually, `lera...lero` insists on the pairing. The speaker is deliberately saying: these two belong together and carry equal weight.

## Either...or: `sera...sero`

This pair frames a genuine choice between two alternatives:

```
mia sera theo sero thalo
1SG EITHER read OR.CORR walk
(i either read or walk.)
```

```
sera thia mia naphe sero mia so wepu
EITHER 2SG 1SG help OR.CORR 1SG FUT go
(either you help me, or i will go.)
```

Where simple `sola` mentions alternatives in passing, `sera...sero` presents them as a deliberate decision point. Each option is given equal structural weight.

## Neither...nor: `mira...miro`

This pair jointly excludes two elements. The negation is built into the correlative itself, so no additional `ma` particle is needed:

```
mia mira theo miro sheluo wilao
1SG NEITHER read NOR.CORR listen long-for
(i want neither to read nor to listen.)
```

```
mira sorae miro pheralu nai
NEITHER sun NOR.CORR rain be
(there is neither sun nor rain.)
```

The `m-` onset echoes Phi's negation particle `ma`, giving these words an acoustic family resemblance with denial.

## Not only...but also: `whera...whero`

This pair signals expansion, that the first element is significant but the second adds something beyond it:

```
shia whera shaelo whero thesua nai
3SG NOTONLY brave ALSO.CORR mindful be
(they are not only brave but also mindful.)
```

```
whera mia theo whero mia shelomui
NOTONLY 1SG read ALSO.CORR 1SG understand
(not only do i read, but i also understand.)
```

The breathy `wh-` onset carries a quality of revelation, the whispered beginning of something larger unfolding. Where `lera...lero` says "these two are equal," `whera...whero` says "there's more here than you expected."

## The system at a glance

| Type | Opener | Closer | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| both...and | `lera` | `lero` | emphatic addition |
| either...or | `sera` | `sero` | emphatic choice |
| neither...nor | `mira` | `miro` | joint exclusion |
| not only...but also | `whera` | `whero` | expansion |

The pattern is consistent: the onset consonant tells you the type (`l-` connection, `s-` choice, `m-` exclusion, `wh-` expansion), and the final vowel tells you the position (`-a` opens, `-o` closes).

For everyday coordination without special emphasis, the simple conjunctions `nela`, `thona`, and `sola` remain the right choice. Correlatives are a tool for precision and rhetorical weight, not a replacement for basic coordination.
