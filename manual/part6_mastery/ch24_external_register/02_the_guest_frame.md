# The adapted guest frame

The guest frame welcomes outside material into Phi's sound system without adding it to the dictionary. `hasha` opens the atom and `hasho` closes it.

```
mia ne hasha toronoto hasho nila.
1SG NAME EXT.GUEST [toronoto] EXT.GUEST.CLOSE see.
(I see Toronto.)

ne hasha samira hasho so shua.
NAME EXT.GUEST [samira] EXT.GUEST.CLOSE FUT come.
(Samira will come.)
```

The second line is still valid, but a bearer who accepts `samira` as a single Phi-form name may use the lighter `ne samira so shua.` Productive name-forms have two or three syllables and need no guest boundary. The frame remains necessary for longer or multi-token adaptations and useful when the speaker wishes to mark the form's external provenance explicitly.

The payload is lowercase and uses only Phi's consonants, fricative digraphs, and vowels. It begins with an onset, uses open syllables, permits no run of three vowels, and takes penultimate stress. Unlike a core word, it may repeat a syllable; the surrounding frame already protects the lexicon from confusion.

The adaptation should be recognizable to the person or community whose form it carries. There is no mechanical transliteration that can settle every name. Ask the bearer when possible, record the source form separately, and prefer the exact frame when the adapted form would hide an important distinction.

`hasho` cannot stand as a payload token because it would close the frame. Change the adaptation instead. Guest frames cannot nest, and a guest payload never appears without its boundaries. A separately chosen productive onym may happen to share its sounds, but that is a `ne` construction rather than an elided guest frame.

## Stress and writing modes

The full guest payload follows the ordinary penultimate-stress rule token by token. Because its sounds are Phi sounds, Roman, Tengwar, and future Phi writing modes can all render it. That cross-mode pronounceability is the reason to choose guest rather than exact form.
