# Grammar and responsibility

A complete external frame behaves as one nominal atom. It can occupy the same positions as a noun phrase, but material inside it contributes no Phi syntax.

```
mia roe patha ISO 8601 patho siru theo.
1SG INS EXT.EXACT [ISO 8601] EXT.EXACT.CLOSE marker read.
(I read the marker by means of ISO 8601.)

patha photosynthesis patho noetha nai.
EXT.EXACT [photosynthesis] EXT.EXACT.CLOSE essential be.
(Photosynthesis is essential.)

shia patha lord patho to haolu.
3SG EXT.EXACT [lord] EXT.EXACT.CLOSE PST speak.
(They said the word "lord.")
```

Subjects, objects, preposition objects, possessors, noun modifiers, and predicates before `nai` are available. `ne` goes before the complete atom when it is a proper name. Phi particles stay outside and scope over the atom. An English verb inside exact payload does not become a Phi verb; use a Phi support verb to say what happens with it.

## Quotation and external preservation

`shola … sholo` quotes Phi speech as Phi speech. Its contents remain subject to Phi grammar, spelling, and punctuation. `patha … patho` preserves source material without making that claim. A translation or paraphrase belongs in `mena … meno`; unchanged non-Phi wording belongs in the exact frame.

## Refused concepts

Core refusal and faithful report are different acts. A speaker may need to name a harmful historical category, quote a law, record testimony, respect someone's self-description, or analyze domination. Exact framing permits that work. The frame marks where the source concept enters and leaves; it does not tell the listener whether the speaker approves of it.

Prefer a core Phi analysis when its composition reveals something useful. Prefer exact material when replacing the source would falsify the record, obstruct care or safety, or erase an identity. Prefer guest material when pronunciation across Phi modes matters more than exact spelling. These choices are part of responsible philosophical speech.
