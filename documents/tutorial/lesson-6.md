## Lesson 6: The Full Verb Phrase

You have learned about tense and aspect particles. These all belong to a category called **Slot 1 particles**, which come directly before the verb. Now, we will learn how to combine them and add the final two types: **modality** and **negation**.

### Particle Stacking: Creating Nuance

You can use more than one Slot 1 particle before a verb to create a more specific meaning. When you do this, they must always appear in a specific order:

**TENSE (`to`, `so`)  →  ASPECT (`si`, `ki`, `pa`, `te`)  →  [VOICE]  →  [EVIDENTIALITY]  →  VERB**

Let's see an example we've already hinted at: "I was sleeping."
This combines the past tense (`to`) with the ongoing aspect (`si`).

*   **Phi:** `mia to si nima.`
*   **Gloss:** `1SG PST IPFV sleep.`
*   **Translation:** I was sleeping.

The order is fixed: `to` must come before `si`.

### Modality: "can" and "must"

Modality particles express the speaker's attitude about the action, like whether it's possible or necessary. These come after tense and aspect, but still before the verb.

Let's learn two modality particles:
*   `po`: expresses possibility ("can" / "is able to")
*   `na`: expresses necessity ("must" / "have to")

**Example with `po` (can):**
*   **Phi:** `sha po pholea.`
*   **Gloss:** `3SG POSS create.`
*   **Translation:** The other can create.

**Example with `na` (must):**
*   **Phi:** `mia na shele.`
*   **Gloss:** `1SG NEC give/receive.`
*   **Translation:** I must give.

### Voice: Shifting Focus

The **voice** particle changes who or what is the subject of a sentence. In Phi, we use `se` to mark the **passive voice**.

In an active sentence, the doer (agent) is the subject:
*   **Phi:** `mia nophi kealo.`
*   **Gloss:** `1SG story create.`
*   **Translation:** I create a story.

In a passive sentence, the receiver (patient) becomes the subject, and the doer is typically left out:
*   **Phi:** `nophi se kealo.`
*   **Gloss:** `story PASS create.`
*   **Translation:** A story is created.

The passive is useful when the action itself matters more than who performed it, or when the doer is unknown.

### Evidentiality: Knowing How We Know

Phi includes a special set of particles for marking *how you know* what you're saying. These **evidential** particles express epistemic humility—they acknowledge the source of your knowledge. They are **optional**; when you leave them out, direct experience is assumed.

There are four evidential particles:
*   `hi`: marks direct experience ("I witnessed this myself")
*   `ke`: marks inference ("I conclude from evidence")
*   `whi`: marks hearsay ("someone told me")
*   `ho`: marks assumption ("I suppose")

**Example with `ke` (inference):**
*   **Phi:** `sha to ke wepu.`
*   **Gloss:** `3SG PST INF leave.`
*   **Translation:** She left [I infer from evidence].

Perhaps you found an empty room and a note. You didn't see her leave, but the evidence tells you she did.

**Example with `whi` (hearsay):**
*   **Phi:** `lo miona whi shele.`
*   **Gloss:** `PL person REP help.`
*   **Translation:** People helped [I was told].

You weren't there, but someone reported this to you.

**Example with `ho` (assumption):**
*   **Phi:** `thi ho kela.`
*   **Gloss:** `2SG ASM understand.`
*   **Translation:** You understand [I assume].

You're making a reasonable assumption, but you acknowledge you don't actually know.

### Negation: `ma`

The final particle in this slot is `ma` (not). It is used to negate the verb. It is placed directly before the verb, after any other particles.

*   **Phi:** `mia lothea ma kela.`
*   **Gloss:** `1SG love NEG understand.`
*   **Translation:** I do not understand love.

### Putting It All Together

You can stack all of these particles to create very precise meanings. The full order is:

**TENSE → ASPECT → VOICE → EVIDENTIALITY → MODALITY → NEGATION → VERB**

Let's look at a complex example: "I will not be able to stop understanding."

*   **Phi:** `mia so te po ma kela.`
*   **Gloss:** `1SG FUT CESS POSS NEG understand.`
*   **Translation:** I will not be able to stop understanding.

This follows the order: `so` (future tense), `te` (cessative aspect), `po` (possibility modality), `ma` (negation), and finally the verb `kela`.

Now let's add evidentiality: "She could have understood [I infer]."

*   **Phi:** `sha to ke po kela.`
*   **Gloss:** `3SG PST INF POSS understand.`
*   **Translation:** She could have understood [I infer].

Here we have `to` (past tense), `ke` (inferential evidential), `po` (possibility), and the verb `kela`. The evidential `ke` tells the listener you're drawing a conclusion from evidence, not reporting something you witnessed directly.

### Lesson 6 Summary

*   You can stack multiple Slot 1 particles before a verb.
*   The order is always: **TENSE → ASPECT → VOICE → EVIDENTIALITY → MODALITY → NEGATION → VERB**.
*   `po` means "can," and `na` means "must."
*   `se` marks passive voice (the receiver becomes the subject).
*   `ma` means "not."
*   Evidentials mark knowledge source: `hi` (direct), `ke` (inference), `whi` (hearsay), `ho` (assumption).
*   Evidentials are optional—leaving them out implies direct experience.

With this knowledge, you now have control over the entire verb phrase, allowing you to express not just *what* happened, but *how you know* it happened. In the next lesson, we will focus on modifiers for nouns and verbs.
