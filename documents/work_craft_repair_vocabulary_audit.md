# Work, Craft, and Repair Vocabulary Audit

**Status:** Evidence-gate audit underlying the active [Work, Craft, and Repair profile](modules/work_craft_and_repair.md), current to 2026-07-10. `archive/` excluded. No vocabulary proposal.

## Purpose and Method

This audit was conducted against Phi's 937-entry pre-module inventory to determine what could support a zero-new-root Work, Craft, and Repair profile. It reviews documented roots for labor, making, repair, tools, materials, qualities, teaching, learning, coordination, consent, safety, exchange, and responsibility. It now serves as the before-state and concept inventory for a future vocabulary pass.

The later [Systems and Shared Infrastructure module](modules/systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md) now supplies optional terms for components, functions, states, dependencies, capacity, technical load, calibration, faults, failures, inspection, reliability, hazards, and specifications. Those additions resolve several lexical absences recorded below but do not settle work-specific questions of tasks, authority, competence, consent, acceptance, safe return to use, labor conditions, or responsibility. The active Work profile records the current cross-module division.

The audit is not a list of English headwords to translate. A missing term matters only when connected Phi use cannot preserve the needed distinction through ordinary clauses, an existing conversion, a source-faithful external form, or a deliberate refusal to conventionalize it.

## Findings at a Glance

- Concrete craft is one of Phi's strongest lexical domains. Speakers can name a workshop, general and specific tools, many materials, numerous making processes, physical qualities, measurement, teaching, learning, repair, restoration, and maintenance.
- Work organization is much thinner than material craft. Phi lacks conventional distinctions for task, project, assignment, delegation, supervision, handoff, progress, completion criterion, inspection, qualification, schedule, workload, and institutional responsibility.
- Several central work roots are ethically directional rather than neutral. They describe purposeful contribution, skilled attention, harmonious cooperation, joyful service, mutual respect, or willingly accepted responsibility. Those meanings support Phi's values but do not adequately name drudgery, coercion, exploitation, unsafe instruction, conflicted collaboration, or labor performed without endorsement.
- Exact specifications, safety instructions, certifications, contracts, prices, wages, identifiers, and technical role names can be preserved externally. Exact carriage does not establish understanding, competence, consent, authority, compliance, or safe completion.
- The existing inventory is strong enough to justify testing a zero-new-root profile. It is not strong enough to claim that work relations, occupational safety, quality assurance, or economic life are already well covered.

## Semantic Pressure and Unsafe Inferences

- **`riola` is not neutral work.** Its entry defines labor as purposeful contribution serving beyond the self and contrasts it with drudgery. Using it for coerced, alienated, extractive, meaningless, or harmful labor may falsely dignify the relation or erase the worker's judgment.
- **`pheloma`, `molawi`, and `wenola` presuppose positive relations.** Joyful service, harmonious cooperation, and mutually respectful collaboration cannot neutrally describe compelled service, conflict inside a team, sabotage, domination, or nominal collaboration without shared control.
- **`pilewa` and `theko` carry responsibility and skill ideals.** Naming an act as making or craft does not establish good design, competence, consent, safety, ecological benefit, or responsibility for consequences.
- **`moloi` does not define an apprenticeship institution.** The root identifies a learner under skilled guidance. It does not specify age, contract, duration, compensation, dependency, assessment, safeguarding, exit rights, or whether the guidance is welcome.
- **`seru` and `thonai` are voluntary-direction roots.** A self-binding commitment and willingly carried duty must not be used as if they already encoded assigned labor, imposed liability, inherited role, or coercive obligation.
- **Tool and teaching roots grant no authority.** Knowing a method, teaching, facilitating, or holding a tool does not license assigning another person's labor, touching their body, directing an unsafe action, or deciding that they are competent.
- **Repair is not safe return to use.** `shiroka`, `whori`, `talome`, and `thiranu` describe useful work but do not establish inspection, testing, specification compliance, independent review, or acceptance by the affected user.
- **Measurement is not certification.** `masue`, comparison roots, and an exact dimension can support checking, but they do not establish instrument validity, tolerance, applicable standard, qualified interpretation, or approval.
- **`temu` does not create jurisdiction.** A guild is an association around shared skill, not automatically a licensing body, employer, owner, disciplinary authority, or representative of every practitioner.

## Missing or Thin Relations

| Vocabulary family | Distinctions currently missing or thin | Initial zero-root handling |
|---|---|---|
| Work units and progress | task, job, project, step, dependency, progress, pending, complete, deadline | Name each concrete action, participant, time, condition, and resulting state; test whether repeated clauses remain recoverable. |
| Role and authority | assignment, delegation, supervisor, coordinator, decision authority, handoff, accountable role | State who requests or proposes what, who accepts or refuses, the claimed source of authority, and who later acts; compare with Commons and Collective Governance. |
| Skill and qualification | skill, expertise, novice, qualified, certified, authorized, competent for a specific action | State knowledge of method, prior practice, observed demonstration, and current practical possibility; preserve exact credentials and source standards externally. |
| Plan and specification | plan, design, drawing, component, sequence, dependency, dimension, tolerance, revision | Use future clauses, conditions, `norae`, `norelu`, exact specifications, and written commitments without calling the result a complete plan. |
| Inspection and quality | inspect, test, defect, fault, quality, tolerance, acceptance criterion, approval, safe return to use | State observations, measurements, exact criteria, remaining uncertainty, and who accepts the result; retain exact technical and legal terms. |
| Safety and capacity | hazard, exposure, protective equipment, fatigue, ergonomic fit, work limit, stop-work authority, incident | Use pain, rest, danger, safety, careful action, refusal, bodily self-report, and exact safety instructions; do not infer capacity or consent from role. |
| Labor conditions and harm | drudgery, coerced labor, exploitation, workload, shift, break, retaliation, harassment, unsafe pressure | Preserve testimony exactly where needed and use explicit clauses about desire, refusal, necessity, authority, harm, benefit, and consequence rather than positive work roots. |
| Economic relation | wage, payment, price, compensation, contract, employment, ownership, debt, profit | Use exact external terms and concrete transfers or commitments until civic and economic scenarios establish which relations require shared core treatment. |
| Materials and logistics | raw material, stock, inventory, supply, component, offcut, waste, reuse, recycling, disposal | Follow each material through holding, movement, transformation, use, repair, and final handling; compare with Ecological Systems and Material Life. |
| Access and participation | adapted tool, communication format, pacing, work accommodation, assistance, representation | State the person, intended activity, barrier, requested change, accepted or refused help, timing, and participation outcome; compare with Accessibility and Participation. |

## Current Composition and External Strategies

| Need | Current strategy | Limit to record |
|---|---|---|
| Describe practical skill | State that a person knows the method, has practiced the craft, can perform the action, and can explain or demonstrate it. | Knowledge, repetition, ability, and qualification remain distinct; none grants authority over another worker. |
| Divide shared work | Name every participant and concrete action; let each person choose, accept, refuse, or commit; record dependencies and future order explicitly. | This can avoid a task noun but may not preserve assignment, coverage, handoff, or who ensures omitted work is done. |
| Ask before tool use or teaching | Use a polite whole-clause question before observation, touch, instruction, assistance, or transfer; preserve refusal and an alternative action. | Practical possibility is not permission, and agreement to learn is not blanket consent to tool use, bodily contact, or evaluation. |
| State a safety condition | Name danger, safety, pain, rest, careful action, and the prohibited or delayed act; carry the exact source instruction or standard in `patha ... patho`. | Exact wording does not establish access, understanding, applicability, competence, enforcement, or safe completion. |
| Check dimensions or fit | Measure, compare, state fitting or non-fitting relations, and preserve values, units, tolerances, and specifications exactly. | A matching dimension is not complete inspection, quality assurance, or approval for use. |
| Describe completion | State the actions performed and the resulting whole, sturdy, smooth, fitting, repaired, or restored condition; state remaining uncertainty separately. | Phi has no conventional work-status, acceptance, handoff, sign-off, or warranty relation. |
| Discuss compensation or institutional terms | Preserve the exact wage, price, contract, credential, role, or rule and describe concrete giving, receiving, exchange, commitment, and authority in core Phi. | A paraphrase may erase legal force or exploitation; external carriage does not make the arrangement legitimate or understood. |

## Scenario Evidence

[The wheel, the chisel, and the promised repair](philosophical_test_corpus.md#15-the-wheel-the-chisel-and-the-promised-repair) applies this audit in connected Phi. It tests method knowledge, apprenticeship, an imposed sharp-tool task, refusal, hand pain, a prior commitment, urgency, exact dimensional and safety instructions, self-selected work, teaching boundaries, shared recording, and unresolved safe return to use.

The scenario confirms that concrete craft, refusal, capacity, teaching, measurement, and planned contribution are compositional. It also confirms that task identity, assignment authority, skill or qualification, bounded consent to training, workload, inspection, completion criteria, and final safety responsibility remain thin.

## Audit Conclusion

The inventory and scenario together supplied the before-state and boundaries for the zero-new-root [Work, Craft, and Repair profile](modules/work_craft_and_repair.md). Existing vocabulary is dense enough to organize and teach, while its idealized work semantics and thin organizational relations now provide clear opportunities for transparent compounds and module coinage.

No root follows from this audit. The profile indexes current resources, preserves coercive or institutional source language exactly, refuses to treat meaningful craft as the only kind of work, and makes work-sharing, consent, capacity, safety, authority, and completion visible in clauses. Vocabulary should remain closed until the same distinction fails in this scenario and an independent transformed or non-workshop case.
