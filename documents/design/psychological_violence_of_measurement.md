# The Psychological Violence of Measurement

## Status of this argument

This document records a design argument, not a demonstrated causal law. It uses *violence* deliberately for measurement practices that participate in coercion, humiliation, surveillance, exclusion, or extraction. Counting and precision are not violent in themselves, and Phi's grammar cannot make their use ethical.

Phi responds to the critique by making some exact enumeration conspicuous, providing direct approximate and sufficiency expressions, and preserving exact records that do not fit its internal system outside the Phi passage. The design is an experiment in linguistic attention. Claims about its psychological or social effects require observation and evidence from actual use.

## Measurement as instrument and institution

A measurement can support care, coordination, comparison, memory, scientific inquiry, environmental protection, accessibility, public accountability, and fair distribution. The same measurement can become harmful when an institution gives it authority beyond what it validly represents.

Risk increases when a metric:

- makes unlike people, experiences, or ecosystems appear fully commensurable;
- substitutes a proxy for the purpose it was meant to serve;
- ranks people while concealing who chose the criterion;
- exposes private information or enables surveillance;
- creates thresholds whose small numerical differences carry disproportionate consequences;
- presents uncertain, sampled, modeled, or context-dependent data as complete fact;
- turns a descriptive measure into a target, quota, diagnosis, entitlement, or judgment without adequate review;
- obscures the people affected, the conditions of collection, and the possibility of refusal or appeal.

These harms come from the measurement practice, its interpretation, and its power relations, not from decimal notation or precision alone. A rounded or qualitative claim can also mislead, erase inequity, hide danger, or frustrate accountability.

## What research supports

### Numeral systems vary

The world's numeral systems are not uniformly decimal, although decimal systems dominate the typological sample in the World Atlas of Language Structures. WALS also records vigesimal, mixed, other-base, body-part, and restricted systems. This shows that decimal expression is not the only human possibility; it does not show that every alternative is equally common or equally easy for every learner.

Phi's finite pure ternary system is deliberately unusual. The project has not established a natural-language precedent with the same inventory, exact range, approximation rule, and value commitments. Its learnability must be evaluated on its own structure and teaching practice.

### Small exact quantities and grouped quantities are different

Research on subitizing distinguishes the rapid enumeration of very small sets, commonly fewer than four items, from slower counting of larger sets. This supports a cautious claim that three can often be recognized without serial counting. It does not support claims that people directly "see" nine, twenty-seven, or eighty-one as exact quantities.

Visual grouping can improve enumeration of larger sets when people combine small subgroups, a learned process sometimes called *groupitizing*. Triads and triads-of-triads may therefore help teach Phi's structure. The arrangement is a learning aid, not evidence that powers of three are innate perceptual units.

### Exact number relies more heavily on language

Studies of bilingual arithmetic and of speakers with restricted exact number vocabularies support a distinction between approximate numerical representation and language-supported large exact number. The findings do not imply that a language rigidly determines what its speakers can think. They do show why an unfamiliar exact numeral system needs explicit teaching and why source notation remains important.

### Novel compositional rules require learning

A 2025 study trained adults on novel numeral systems with bases 2, 3, 4, 5, 8, and 10. Base size alone did not produce a simple learnability ranking across most conditions, while exact generalization of the full compositional rule set was uncommon. One experiment cannot predict Phi proficiency, but it warns against assuming that transparent rules become automatic after a few examples.

A bilingual study found that number-word length affected digit-span performance and proposed consequences for mental calculation. Phi's longer exact forms therefore present a plausible verbal-memory cost that use should test, not merely a philosophical atmosphere. Written grouping, visible triads, reference tables, and permission to use source notation are prudent accessibility measures.

## What Phi's numeral system actually does

Phi defines the following linguistic mechanisms:

- Three digit words: `mu` (0), `ta` (1), and `wi` (2).
- Four scale units: `shao` (3), `phoi` (9), `lau` (27), and `rei` (81).
- Canonical exact integers from 0 through 242, with each scale used at most once and counted only by `ta` or `wi`.
- Bare scale units for contextual approximation without fixed rounding intervals.
- Quantifiers including `sheloi` (many), `soli` (some), `phina` (few), and `henoi` (enough).
- Four optional classifiers that can foreground whether a count concerns people, living beings, detached or crafted things, or abstract entities and events.
- Source material outside the Phi passage for larger exact values, fractions, negative numbers, units, formulas, identifiers, measurements, and records whose original form matters.

These mechanisms change what is short, internally expressible, or explicitly marked. They do not establish the truth, relevance, ownership, fairness, sufficiency, or consequences of a number.

## Observable effects and open hypotheses

Several effects follow directly from the grammar:

- Exact internal forms become longer as more ternary places are used.
- The internal exact range ends at 242.
- Bare scales distinguish an estimate from an exact numeral.
- `henoi` makes sufficiency directly sayable without a count.
- Classifiers make a selected category audible when a speaker chooses one.
- A source record remains visibly outside the Phi passage instead of being normalized into Phi syntax.

Several hoped-for effects remain hypotheses:

- Longer exact forms may prompt some speakers to reconsider unnecessary precision.
- Ready access to `henoi` may support discussion of sufficiency.
- Classifiers may remind some speakers of what or whom a count concerns.
- Separating source records from Phi discussion may make the relation between data and interpretation easier to inspect.

No current evidence shows that these features cause contentment, reduce greed, prevent dehumanization, increase generosity, improve ecological judgment, or make distribution fair. A community can hoard resources while speaking approximately, rank people with numbers below 243, or use a human classifier inside a coercive record. Institutions and actions carry those outcomes.

## When approximation is unsafe or unjust

Phi should not treat resistance to precision as a higher value when exactness supports care or accountability. Preserve or state exact information when differences matter to:

- medical dose, timing, symptoms, test results, or clinical thresholds;
- accessibility dimensions, tolerances, schedules, capacity, or compatibility;
- safety limits, engineering specifications, maintenance, or emergency response;
- ecological samples, concentrations, trends, thresholds, or model outputs;
- allocation, inventory, voting, representation, budgets, compensation, or distribution;
- dates, legal obligations, testimony, citations, identifiers, or versioned records;
- research reproducibility, uncertainty, error, and comparison with a baseline;
- any affected person's ability to verify, challenge, consent to, or act on a claim.

In those settings, an approximate Phi summary may accompany the exact source record but must not replace information needed for action. Conversely, exact disclosure can violate privacy or create risk; the right response may be access control, consent, aggregation, or non-disclosure rather than inaccurate wording.

## A practical decision sequence

Before expressing a quantity, ask:

1. What decision or understanding will this quantity support?
2. Do I know the value exactly, approximately, or only qualitatively?
3. What uncertainty, unit, method, time, place, population, or baseline gives it meaning?
4. Could rounding erase danger, need, inequity, or accountability?
5. Could exact disclosure violate privacy, consent, safety, or legitimate control of information?
6. Is the value within Phi's exact range and adequate without external notation?
7. Should the Phi passage use an exact numeral, a bare scale, a quantifier, `henoi`, a qualitative clause, or a reference to separately preserved source material?

This sequence does not privilege the shortest answer. It asks for the representation that is accurate, proportionate, usable, and accountable in context.

## Cultural care

Phi must not recruit Indigenous, gift-economy, Buddhist, disability, ecological, or slow-movement communities as proof of a general theory without careful, community-specific sources. A restricted numeral lexicon does not demonstrate that a community lacks complex thought, and it does not prove that its members are more egalitarian or content. Likewise, a Buddhist-inspired metaphor does not turn arithmetic into Buddhist practice.

The design may take inspiration from sufficiency, non-attachment, qualitative judgment, and resistance to optimization while naming these as Phi's commitments. Any future case study should distinguish linguistic description, historical evidence, design analogy, and Phi's own normative choice.

## Conclusion

Phi's ternary system creates deliberate friction around some exact enumeration and makes approximation and sufficiency easy to name. That is a real linguistic intervention with a real learning cost. It may help speakers notice questions that familiar decimal wording leaves unmarked, but the grammar cannot answer those questions for them.

The defensible aim is narrower and stronger: keep the choice of precision visible, preserve exact information wherever accuracy supports care or accountability, and refuse to confuse a measurement with the whole reality it describes.
