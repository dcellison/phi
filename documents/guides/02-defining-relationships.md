# The Art of Speaking Phi, Part 2: Defining Relationships

This guide covers the use of honorifics. In Phi, honorifics are not about hierarchy or social status, but about clearly and respectfully defining your relationship to the person you are speaking about. Their use is a sign of social awareness and care.

## The Honorific Particles (`sa`, `ni`, `le`)

These Slot 2 particles are placed directly before a person's name or title. Choosing which one to use (or not to use) is a key part of speaking Phi with social grace.

### 1. `sa`: Respect for a Teacher or Elder

Use `sa` to show deference and respect for someone you consider a source of wisdom, mentorship, or guidance. It acknowledges their role as a teacher in your life.

- **Example:** `sa Thala mia whaushe.` (RESPECT Thala teaches me.)
- **Context:** You are speaking about your mentor, Thala, and wish to show your respect for her wisdom.

### 2. `ni`: Intimacy and Closeness

Use `ni` for close friends, family members, partners, or anyone with whom you share a deep, chosen bond of mutual affection. It is a sign of warmth and intimacy. Using it for a stranger would be overly familiar.

- **Example:** `ni Hino to wepu.` (INTIMACY Hino has left.)
- **Context:** You are speaking about your dear friend, Hino.

### 3. `le`: Respect for a Community Role

Use `le` to show respect for the *function or role* a person holds within the community. This is more formal and less personal than `sa` or `ni`. It separates the person from their function, allowing you to show respect for their work even if you don't know them well.

- **Example:** `le Mako noshale pholea.` (ROLE Mako creates the garden.)
- **Context:** You are speaking about Mako, the community gardener. You are showing respect for the important work Mako does for the community.

### When Not to Use an Honorific

In many neutral situations, or when speaking about yourself, no honorific is needed. The absence of an honorific is simply the neutral, default state.

By choosing your honorifics mindfully, you make the social context of your relationships clear, fostering communication based on respect and understanding.
