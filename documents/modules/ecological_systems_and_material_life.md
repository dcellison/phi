# Ecological Systems and Material Life

**Status:** **Established vocabulary module** with 27 module roots coined here, 46 current memberships, and base-vocabulary `howenu` weather, `muralo` material, and `panuri` resource; ordinary Phi grammar.

## Charter

This module gives Phi speakers optional vocabulary for describing ecological organization, land and water processes, weather and climate, pollution and exposure, material uses and afterlives, and repeated ecological observation. It supports discussion of living beings and nonliving conditions together without treating a named place as a complete system, a green intention as an ecological result, or scientific terminology as self-interpreting authority.

The module is not a scientific ontology, environmental standard, conservation doctrine, climate model, land-management plan, professional credential, or claim that solarpunk values settle an ecological question. It does not certify that an action is sustainable, regenerative, harmless, just, or effective merely because a valued Phi word describes its intention.

The speaker-facing guide is [Ecological Systems and Material Life](../../manual/part7_reference/modules/ecological_systems_and_material_life.md), and the complete current optional inventory appears in the [module lexicon](../../manual/part7_reference/lexicon/by_module.md#ecological-systems-and-material-life). The vocabulary table below records every root established for this profile, including three that belong in base vocabulary. The generated inventory also includes older roots classified after the profile pass and words shared with other learning paths. Learning-path placement changes where a learner finds a word, not what it means. Canonical definitions remain in `vocabulary/content/`.

## Boundaries

- Phi retains one grammar. The module adds no ecological particle, scientific classifier, system syntax, inflection, parser mode, or incompatible construction.
- A module word is ordinary Phi vocabulary. Speakers outside the module may ask for a core paraphrase and are not obliged to learn its specialized terms.
- "Material life" means material conditions, flows, transformations, uses, and afterlives within lived systems. It adds no grammatical animacy and does not assert that every material is biologically alive.
- `litero` names a selected ecosystem, not a naturally bounded, balanced, healthy, self-sufficient, or harmonious whole. Its place, time, scale, components, relations, and omissions remain part of the analysis.
- `likori`, `liloni`, `liweso`, and `philima` require stated categories and scope where those choices matter. They do not create a universal taxonomy, rank kinds of life, translate source-community knowledge, or make a list of organisms into proof of biodiversity or ecological health.
- `panuri` resource and `whemori` waste are relational classifications, not inherent material essences. Neither grants ownership, disposal authority, access, entitlement, or permission to erase another ecological, cultural, or future relation.
- `hisophi` contaminant, `pesenu` pollution, `pekira` toxicity, `perawi` exposure, and `pemaru` hazard remain distinct. Technical purity language must not be used to stigmatize people, cultures, migrants, or disliked organisms.
- `somethi` monitoring concerns stated ecological indicators, places, and conditions. It does not authorize surveillance of people, establish complete coverage, or grant authority to interpret or publish a record.
- Exact species names, taxonomies, chemical names, analytes, values, units, site and sample identifiers, methods, datasets, models, standards, thresholds, legal categories, and source quotations remain in source material outside the Phi passage when their identity or precision matters.
- Infrastructure and technical system relations overlap [Systems and Shared Infrastructure](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md). Base vocabulary carries claims, evidence, reasons, and criteria; inference, validity, definition, uncertainty, and competing values overlap [Philosophical Reasoning](philosophical_reasoning.md). Authority and allocation overlap [Commons and Collective Governance](commons_and_collective_governance.md).
- Ecological decisions affect bodies, access, work, shelter, water, food, and shared places. Those consequences remain shared with [Medical and Bodily Care](medical_and_bodily_care.md), [Accessibility and Participation](accessibility_and_participation.md), [Household and Daily Life](household_and_daily_life.md), and [Work, Craft, and Repair](work_craft_and_repair.md).
- Names, classifications, testimony, and ecological concepts belonging to Indigenous peoples, local communities, or knowledge traditions require source-faithful carriage and attribution when a core paraphrase would erase origin, authority, identity, restriction, or contested history. Phi's values do not license extraction or universalization of that knowledge.

## Earlier roots classified here

Seven older roots now sit in this learning path. `keshoa` reptile, `moreke` mammal, and `teluno` amphibian name biological classes; `shaemu` organic describes living-derived or growth-formed matter. `nunoa` forager and `telo` farmer join ecology to the Work module, while `theluo` steward joins it to Commons. Their canonical meanings remain unchanged.

## Optional Cross-Module Dependencies

The established [Systems and Shared Infrastructure module](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md) supplies `terura` system, `monaki` component, `mirela` state, `pherami` dependency, `koewi` input, `thirulo` output, `sekaru` transfer, `ketora` technical control, `turelo` feedback, `henora` capacity, `poruli` efficiency, `nilaki` inspection, `koluri` reliability, `pemaru` hazard, and `kisholu` specification. These words trace the mechanics beneath an ecological account. `litero` ecosystem and `menuro` habitat still require ecological relations, while authority to intervene comes from elsewhere.

Base `sherewa` claim, `thesori` evidence, `remotha` reason, and `kirema` criterion support ecological discussion before the established [Philosophical Reasoning module](philosophical_reasoning.md) adds inference, validity, contradiction, definition, doubt, confidence, tradeoffs, and compromise. These terms help examine an ecological conclusion. The evidence still has to do its own work, whatever a model or community decision says.

The established [Commons and Collective Governance module](commons_and_collective_governance.md) supplies membership, representation, institutions, roles, authority, authorization, jurisdiction, legitimacy, rules, procedures, decisions, allocation, entitlement, ownership, obligation, accountability, and review. Ecological speakers may use these terms when a collective decision concerns land, pollution, or restoration. Governance vocabulary cannot validate the ecological account or make the intervention work. Base `sirami` record, `kawhera` coerce, and `whepelo` retaliate remain available when the issue crosses institutional boundaries.

## First Vocabulary Pass

The first pass admits 27 optional roots for recurring ecological distinctions and places weather, material, and resource in base vocabulary. It does not attempt to minimize coinage, and its choices may be revised through ecological writing, observation, land and water discussion, or material practice.

| Family | Roots established for this profile | What the pass now distinguishes |
|---|---|---|
| Ecological organization | `litero` ecosystem, `menuro` habitat, `likori` species, `liloni` population, `liweso` ecological community, `philima` biodiversity | Ecosystem versus named place or harmony; habitat versus observed location; species versus exact taxon; population versus species; ecological versus human community; biodiversity versus list, abundance, or health. |
| Land, water, weather, and climate | `luphano` watershed, `wiralu` runoff, `wheraku` erode, `munari` sediment, `howenu` weather, `meluri` climate, `phaluwe` flood, `kuramo` drought | Drainage area versus water body; surface flow versus rain or stream; erosion versus weathering or excavation; sediment versus soil or pollution; weather versus climate; flood and drought versus their exact thresholds or consequences. |
| Pollution and exposure | `pesenu` pollute, `hisophi` contaminant, `pekira` toxic, `nomiki` concentration, `perawi` expose | Pollution versus contamination; contaminant versus pollutant; toxicity versus hazard or harm; concentration versus total amount or load; exposure versus dose, uptake, or effect. |
| Material life and afterlives | `muralo` material, `panuri` resource, `whemori` waste, `morume` decompose, `lurepa` nutrient, `tukelu` recycle | Material versus thing or substance grade; resource and waste as relational statuses; decomposition versus breakage, death, or composting; nutrient versus food or fertilizer; recycling versus return, reuse, or a closed cycle. |
| Observation and interpretation | `phisuri` sample, `mothami` baseline, `herani` trend, `reteru` model, `somethi` monitor | Sample versus example or evidence; baseline versus original or ideal state; trend versus cause or forecast; model versus represented reality; monitoring versus one observation, inspection, proof, or surveillance. |

All 30 roots established in this pass follow the complete vocabulary schema, include explicit semantic contrasts and grammatical examples, pass the lexical validator, and have no character edit-distance-one or flagged existing phonetic neighbors in the pre-coinage audit. Twenty-seven carry `ecological-systems-and-material-life`; `howenu`, `muralo`, and `panuri` carry no module field and remain in base vocabulary.

## Concepts Kept Compositional

The pass keeps several expressions compositional because their parts expose scope, direction, criteria, and cross-module boundaries that a single label could hide. These are current recommendations rather than prohibitions against later coinage.

| Concept | Phi strategy | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ecosystem boundary | `litero norui` | Keeps the selected ecosystem and the act of drawing its spatial, temporal, and relational limit visible. |
| Ecological niche | State the `menuro` habitat, `kelitho` functions, `pherami` dependencies, resources, conditions, and interactions relevant to the organism | Niche concepts differ in what dimensions they include; explicit relations prevent one word from pretending the analysis is complete. |
| Species range | State where and when the `likori` or `liloni` dwells, with an explicit `norui` boundary | Keeps occurrence, season, life stage, uncertainty, and sampling limits recoverable. |
| Abundance | Use `lorua` with the organism, place, time, count, density, or comparison | Existing abundance vocabulary remains sufficient when the measured basis is stated. |
| Groundwater | `muila phialu`, earth-water, with explicit depth, movement, storage, and source clauses | Prevents a broad compound from claiming a specific aquifer, flow path, or legal category. |
| Wetland | `wirua muila menuro`, wet-land habitat, or an exact source classification | Keeps ordinary wet ground distinct from a scientific, legal, or community-defined wetland category. |
| Upstream and downstream | Use the water's source, goal, `selu` flow, and `sekaru` transfer direction explicitly | Direction remains relative to the selected water path instead of becoming an absolute spatial axis. |
| Infiltration | `phialu muo muila koema`, water enters earth | States the physical event without claiming rate, storage, recharge, or destination. |
| Heatwave | `sharoi sukaro mosha`, a period of excessive heat, with the place, duration, reference, and exact threshold | Makes unusual heat relative to a stated climate and impact rather than defining it by one universal number. |
| Emission | `leiro thirulo`, released output, with source, agent, amount, and destination | Reuses Systems directionality and keeps every output from becoming an environmental emission by default. |
| Pollutant | `pesenu muralo`, polluting material, or the relevant polluting organism or energy form | Keeps the agent tied to demonstrated pollution rather than making contaminant status sufficient. |
| Organic matter | `shaemu muralo` | Reuses the existing organic quality while exact scientific fractions and methods remain in source material outside the Phi passage. |
| Compost | `thepalu morume muralo`, garden decomposing material, with management and maturity stated | Distinguishes a practical material and process without treating all decomposition as composting. |
| Reuse | Return an object or material to a stated use or make it a `turema koewi`, returned input | Keeps direct reuse distinct from `tukelu` recycling, which requires processing and transformation. |
| Food web | `nuola phaliso`, eating network, with organisms and directions stated | Makes feeding relations visible without assuming energy transfer, complete coverage, stability, or harmony. |
| Ecological carrying capacity | `litero henora` or `menuro henora`, with the population, function, time, and conditions stated | Reuses technical capacity while preventing a number from becoming an inherent limit independent of conditions and values. |
| Indicator | `mirela siru`, state marker, with method and interpretation stated | Keeps an observed marker separate from the ecological condition it is used to infer. |
| Forecast | `wireo mirela kethira`, infer a future state, with model, evidence, and uncertainty stated | Exposes prediction as an inference rather than a guaranteed future. |
| Monitoring interval | `somethi mosha` | Names the monitoring practice and its period without hiding duration or frequency. |
| Precaution | State the possible `pemaru` hazard, uncertainty, limited protective action, burdens, refusal, and review | Keeps precaution as a reasoned practice rather than a word that automatically authorizes intervention. |
| Environmental justice, land or water authority, rights, and restoration obligations | Use Commons terms for `karami` authority, `norulo` jurisdiction, `shereni` entitlement, `naseru` obligation, `lothoni` accountability, and `helolu` redress with Philosophical Reasoning resources and exact source terms | Ecological terminology must not settle legitimacy, rights, remedy, responsibility, or justice by itself. |

## Source Material Guidance

Keep species and taxonomic names, chemical names and formulas, analytes, scientific classifications, measurements, units, dates, site and sample identifiers, coordinates, dataset fields, model names and versions, methods, standards, thresholds, protocols, citations, legal categories, map or record labels, technical quotations, and terms from a community or knowledge tradition outside the Phi passage when source form matters. Preserve spelling, script, notation, precision, version, and provenance rather than silently normalizing or translating them.

Use existing vocabulary, a transparent expression, a module term, or a fully admitted new word when Phi needs the concept. A temporary pronunciation aid remains an outside conversational convention and does not acquire a Phi taxonomy, definition, evidential status, or claim of universality.

A place, water body, project, community, model, dataset, or other named entity may use `ne` with a listed content name or a valid Phi-form onym when that form follows the productive-name charter and the bearer or relevant naming community accepts it. The name identifies the referent; it does not translate ecological type, ownership, jurisdiction, scientific classification, or source authority.

Describe the practical relations in core and module Phi where useful: which beings, materials, and places are involved; what moved or changed; the relevant boundary, time, and scale; who sampled, observed, measured, inferred, modeled, or reported; which alternative explanations remain; who may act; what action is proposed; and how its effects will be monitored and reviewed. Do not claim that this analysis exhausts the separately preserved source concept.

Exact preservation does not make a classification correct, a sample representative, a concentration harmful, a model valid, a threshold applicable, a record complete, or an intervention legitimate. Module words identify analytical roles around source material; they do not replace the artifact or its qualified interpretation.

## Continued Development

Use the module in ecological essays, land and water discussions, climate and material narratives, monitoring notes, infrastructure decisions, and stories about inhabited places. Coin another word when a recurring ecological concept deserves lexical presence or when the available compound is cumbersome, ambiguous, or aesthetically wrong for Phi. Retain a compound when its parts expose a useful boundary, direction, condition, source, or responsibility. Every new entry must still satisfy the vocabulary schema, phonology, collision checks, semantic contrasts, examples, and reference generation, but it does not need to pass an external burden-of-proof process.
