# Ecological Systems and Material Life

**Status:** **Profile** - domain-oriented ecological organization, zero new roots.

## Charter

This profile gathers existing Phi resources for describing land, water, weather, living beings, habitat relations, material conditions, flows, transformations, ecological evidence, protection, restoration, regeneration, and decisions under uncertainty. It tests whether speakers can analyze ecological situations without treating a named place as a complete system, a sequence as a demonstrated cycle, or an exact scientific term or measurement as its own interpretation.

The profile is not a scientific ontology, environmental standard, conservation doctrine, climate model, land-management plan, professional credential, or claim that solarpunk values settle an ecological question. It does not certify that an action is sustainable, regenerative, harmless, just, or effective merely because a valued Phi root describes its intention.

## Boundaries

- Phi retains one grammar. Ecological discussion uses ordinary clauses, evidentials, conditions, modality, comparison, discourse repair, and external frames; this profile adds no ecological particle, system syntax, scientific classifier, parser mode, or incompatible construction.
- "Material life" means material conditions, flows, transformations, uses, and afterlives within lived systems. It does not add grammatical animacy or assert that every material is biologically alive.
- `sila` names a human-scale community and must not silently become the technical ecological sense of *community*. `shelira` names a forest, but naming a forest does not identify an ecosystem boundary, population, habitat, food web, or causal model.
- `lorea`, `phaliso`, `selu`, `nuri`, `turema`, `kelu`, and `sunoe` broadly name connection, network, flow, cycle, return, becoming, and regeneration. The Systems module can separately name a system, dependency, transfer, control, and feedback relation, but those words still do not establish an ecological boundary, material conservation, recurrence, recovery, causal mechanism, or emergence.
- `muila` covers earth and soil broadly. It does not distinguish planet, soil horizon, sediment, substrate, mineral fraction, organic matter, contamination, or a source-specific land category.
- `hiso`, `peloma`, `pukea`, and `shurano` support discussion of cleanliness, harmfulness, danger, and safety. The Systems module adds `pelomaru` for a potential source or condition of harm, but neither that word nor the core terms define pollutant, toxicity, exposure, ecological threshold, hazard class, acceptable risk, or a suitable response.
- Ordinary roots for plants, animals, fungi, insects, birds, fish, trees, forests, and groves are not a scientific taxonomy. Exact species names, classifications, and source-specific organism categories remain external when their identity or distinctions matter.
- `shorupo`, `theluo`, `thenoki`, `talome`, and `sunoe` name protection, stewardship, sustaining, restoration, and regeneration without proving ecological benefit or granting authority. Resource allocation, land or water authority, responsibility, and review overlap [Commons and Collective Governance](commons_and_collective_governance.md).
- Ecological decisions affect bodies, access, work, shelter, water, food, and infrastructure. Those consequences remain shared with [Medical and Bodily Care](medical_and_bodily_care.md), [Accessibility and Participation](accessibility_and_participation.md), [Household and Daily Life](household_and_daily_life.md), [Systems and Shared Infrastructure](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md), and [Work, Craft, and Repair](work_craft_and_repair.md).
- Causal attribution, competing explanations, uncertainty, values, and revision overlap [Philosophical Reasoning](philosophical_reasoning.md). An evidential identifies a claimed source; it does not establish scientific validity, confidence, expertise, or consensus.
- Names, classifications, testimony, and ecological concepts belonging to Indigenous peoples, local communities, or knowledge traditions require source-faithful carriage and attribution when a core paraphrase would erase origin, authority, identity, or contested history. Phi's values do not license extraction or universalization of that knowledge.

## Current Practices and Limits

| Need | Current Phi strategy | Important limit |
|---|---|---|
| Describe an ecological place | Name the land, water, weather, organisms, materials, and observed relations in separate clauses. | An inventory of entities does not identify a system boundary, scale, habitat relation, relevant absence, or interaction. |
| Trace water or material movement | Use source and goal prepositions, `selu`, `roke`, `lorea`, explicit places, `koemari`, `thirulo`, `selokaru`, and ordered clauses. | Input, output, and transfer clarify an analytical boundary and direction but do not conventionalize upstream, downstream, runoff, drainage, accumulation, exposure, conservation, or a complete ecological pathway. |
| Describe habitat relations | State where an organism dwells, what it eats, what supports it, and which conditions change. | These clauses may be useful without defining habitat type, quality, range, seasonality, population, niche, or dependence. |
| Describe decomposition or a cycle | State death, feeding, transformation, growth, return, recurrence, and causal relations explicitly; compare with an exact source term. | A sequence is not necessarily recurrent or closed, `kelu` does not identify conserved material, and `nuri` or `turema` does not explain what returns. |
| Report scientific data | Preserve the complete site, identifier, analyte, value, unit, date, method label, or source statement in `patha ... patho`; state who measured, observed, inferred, or reported it. | Exact data does not establish sampling validity, uncertainty, comparability, reference condition, causal relevance, expertise, or threshold interpretation. |
| Discuss pollution or ecological harm | State the material, source, movement, exposure, observed change, possible harm, evidence, and competing explanations separately. | Broad cleanliness and harmfulness language does not distinguish contaminant, pollutant, concentration, dose, cumulative load, ecological effect, or regulated finding. |
| Choose a limited intervention | State the immediate danger or need, the material action, expected result, uncertainty, affected people and beings, refusal or disagreement, and review time. | A limited or precautionary action is not proof of ecological success, legitimacy, consent, proportionality, or responsibility. |
| Observe and revisit change | Identify what will be observed, where, when, by whom, how it will be recorded, and which choice may be revised. | Observation plus writing does not establish a monitoring design, baseline, indicator, duration, comparison rule, publication duty, or review authority. |
| Discuss a source-specific ecological concept | Carry its exact term, spelling, script, taxonomy, quotation, or community wording first, then offer a bounded core analysis. | A Phi analysis must not be presented as exhaustive when it drops scientific criteria, cultural context, legal force, identity, or source authority. |

## Scenario Evidence

- [The river above and below the workshop](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#13-the-river-above-and-below-the-workshop) tests exact site measurements, a directly observed dye flow, field water after rain, fish deaths, competing causal paths, broad harmfulness, source control, repeated measurement, and action without a responsibility finding.
- [The fallen tree and the living grove](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#14-the-fallen-tree-and-the-living-grove) tests exact habitat, nutrient-cycle, and biodiversity terms; organism and material relations; an incomplete return explanation; path safety; partial retention; and observation without a claim of ecological success.
- [Water for the garden](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#1-water-for-the-garden) supplies boundary evidence for competing ecological goods: naming the garden and river as values does not supply priority, compromise, or an optimal allocation.
- [Rain and the river](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#3-rain-and-the-river) supplies boundary evidence for causal revision: report, direct observation, inference, alternative possibility, error, and changed belief remain distinct around ecological harm.

These are fictional regression texts written by one maintainer. They demonstrate available compositions and recurring failures, not scientific accuracy for another site, ecological expertise, successful conservation, permission to act on land or water, or the adequacy of Phi for a knowledge tradition.

## Comparative Findings

| Finding | Evidence status | Consequence for this profile |
|---|---|---|
| Naming entities and local relations does not establish an ecological system boundary | **Recurring** across river and grove scales | Require speakers to state the selected place, time, scale, included beings and materials, excluded alternatives, and relation under analysis where they matter. |
| Exact data and exact ecological terms preserve source material without interpreting it | **Recurring** across both ecological scenarios and earlier technical cases | Keep identity and precision external when needed, then state method, relevance, evidence, and practical relation separately in core Phi. |
| Connection, flow, sequence, transformation, and return do not by themselves explain a pathway or closed cycle | **Recurring** across the river source paths, grove material-return chain, and systems dialogues | Reuse Systems terms for selected boundaries, inputs, outputs, transfers, dependencies, and feedback while retaining ecological pathway, conservation, trophic relation, cumulative effect, and emergence as unresolved distinctions. |
| Causal claims require more than proximity, sequence, or one changed measurement | **Recurring** across the two river scenarios and supported by the grove dependency problem | Preserve observation, inference, alternative paths, uncertainty, and revision instead of allowing ecological values to select a cause. |
| Limited action can proceed while cause or ecological success remains uncertain | **Recurring** across source control and partial wood relocation | Teach explicit scope, expected result, uncertainty, affected parties, and review rather than treating intervention as proof. |
| Observation and writing remain thinner than ecological monitoring | **Recurring** across both new scenarios | Record what current Phi can state while testing baseline, indicator, method, duration, comparison, and review in later cases. |
| Watershed, pollution, concentration, threshold, and source attribution remain thin | **Provisional**, one dedicated source-path scenario plus earlier river evidence | Transform sites, sources, analytes, timing, and observed effects before considering any conventional term. |
| Habitat, decomposition, nutrient return, biodiversity, and mitigation remain thin | **Provisional**, one dedicated grove scenario | Transform organisms, material states, path conditions, and intervention extent before treating these as stable lexical needs. |
| Climate, heat, broader waste streams, and habitat change remain under-tested | **Open evidence gap** | Use an inhabited-place heat scenario next rather than extrapolating from water and dead wood. |

## External Register Guidance

Use `patha ... patho` when exact source form matters for species and taxonomic names, chemical names and formulas, scientific classifications, measurements, units, dates, site and sample identifiers, coordinates, dataset fields, model names, standards, thresholds, protocols, citations, legal categories, map or record labels, technical quotations, and terms from a community or knowledge tradition. Preserve spelling, script, notation, precision, version, and provenance rather than silently normalizing or translating them.

Use `hasha ... hasho` for a temporary Phi-pronounceable adaptation when source spelling is not authoritative and participants need a speakable scientific, geographic, or ecological term. A guest adaptation remains external and does not acquire a Phi taxonomy, definition, evidential status, or claim of universality.

A place, water body, project, community, or other named entity may use `ne` with a listed content name or a valid Phi-form onym when that form follows the productive-name charter and the bearer or relevant naming community accepts it. The name identifies the referent; it does not translate its ecological type, ownership, jurisdiction, or scientific classification.

After any external form, describe the practical relations in core Phi where useful: which beings, materials, and places are involved; what moved or changed; the relevant time and scale; who observed, measured, inferred, or reported; which alternative explanations remain; who may act; what action is proposed; and how its effects will be reviewed. Do not claim that this analysis exhausts the source concept.

An exact phrase such as `patha watershed patho`, `patha nutrient cycle patho`, `patha biodiversity patho`, or `patha urban heat island patho` preserves a comparison target. It does not identify the system boundary, close a material loop, define a diversity measure, prove a causal mechanism, or authorize an intervention.

## Vocabulary Pass Starting Point

An original inhabited-place heat and cooling scenario can provide examples for the first ecological module terms. It may compare exact temperature readings from identified places and times, distinguish weather from a longer climate pattern, include vegetation, materials, water and energy limits, a technical warning, and people whose bodily, communication, mobility, work, or rest needs differ.

The scenario can compare interventions such as shade, changed work time, a cooling place, water use, reflective material, ventilation, or planting without assuming that one serves everyone or that a green intervention has no material cost. It can preserve direct, reported, inferred, and exact external information alongside refusal, urgent action, longer proposals, and review.

Use the profile and scenario to inventory places, times, readings, sources, affected people, body or access needs, material conditions, causal claims, uncertainties, proposed actions, authority claims, burdens, refusals, and review conditions that need clear vocabulary.

Retain compositions that expose ecological relations clearly and coin module terms wherever lexical presence helps. Scientific fidelity, bodily autonomy, access, testimony, place identity, participation, and criticism remain definition requirements rather than barriers to beginning the pass.
