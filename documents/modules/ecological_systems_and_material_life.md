# Ecological Systems and Material Life

**Status:** **Established vocabulary module** - 30 module words, ordinary Phi grammar.

## Charter

This module gives Phi speakers optional vocabulary for describing ecological organization, land and water processes, weather and climate, pollution and exposure, material uses and afterlives, and repeated ecological observation. It supports discussion of living beings and nonliving conditions together without treating a named place as a complete system, a green intention as an ecological result, or scientific terminology as self-interpreting authority.

The module is not a scientific ontology, environmental standard, conservation doctrine, climate model, land-management plan, professional credential, or claim that solarpunk values settle an ecological question. It does not certify that an action is sustainable, regenerative, harmless, just, or effective merely because a valued Phi word describes its intention.

The speaker-facing guide is [Ecological Systems and Material Life](../../manual/part7_reference/modules/ecological_systems_and_material_life.md), and the complete generated inventory appears in the [module lexicon](../../manual/part7_reference/lexicon/by_module.md#ecological-systems-and-material-life). Canonical definitions remain in `vocabulary/content/`.

## Boundaries

- Phi retains one grammar. The module adds no ecological particle, scientific classifier, system syntax, inflection, parser mode, or incompatible construction.
- A module word is ordinary Phi vocabulary. Speakers outside the module may ask for a core paraphrase and are not obliged to learn its specialized terms.
- "Material life" means material conditions, flows, transformations, uses, and afterlives within lived systems. It adds no grammatical animacy and does not assert that every material is biologically alive.
- `limatero` names a selected ecosystem, not a naturally bounded, balanced, healthy, self-sufficient, or harmonious whole. Its place, time, scale, components, relations, and omissions remain part of the analysis.
- `limakori`, `limaloni`, `limaweso`, and `phiralima` require stated categories and scope where those choices matter. They do not create a universal taxonomy, rank kinds of life, translate source-community knowledge, or make a list of organisms into proof of biodiversity or ecological health.
- `panumori` resource and `whemorali` waste are relational classifications, not inherent material essences. Neither grants ownership, disposal authority, access, entitlement, or permission to erase another ecological, cultural, or future relation.
- `hisophira` contaminant, `pelosenu` pollution, `pelokira` toxicity, `pelorawi` exposure, and `pelomaru` hazard remain distinct. Technical purity language must not be used to stigmatize people, cultures, migrants, or disliked organisms.
- `somethiru` monitoring concerns stated ecological indicators, places, and conditions. It does not authorize surveillance of people, establish complete coverage, or grant authority to interpret or publish a record.
- Exact species names, taxonomies, chemical names, analytes, values, units, site and sample identifiers, methods, datasets, models, standards, thresholds, legal categories, and source quotations remain exact external material when their identity or precision matters.
- Infrastructure and technical system relations overlap [Systems and Shared Infrastructure](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md); causation, evidence, definitions, uncertainty, and values overlap [Philosophical Reasoning](philosophical_reasoning.md); authority and allocation overlap [Commons and Collective Governance](commons_and_collective_governance.md).
- Ecological decisions affect bodies, access, work, shelter, water, food, and shared places. Those consequences remain shared with [Medical and Bodily Care](medical_and_bodily_care.md), [Accessibility and Participation](accessibility_and_participation.md), [Household and Daily Life](household_and_daily_life.md), and [Work, Craft, and Repair](work_craft_and_repair.md).
- Names, classifications, testimony, and ecological concepts belonging to Indigenous peoples, local communities, or knowledge traditions require source-faithful carriage and attribution when a core paraphrase would erase origin, authority, identity, restriction, or contested history. Phi's values do not license extraction or universalization of that knowledge.

## Optional Cross-Module Dependencies

The established [Systems and Shared Infrastructure module](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md) supplies `terulora` system, `monakeli` component, `nomirela` state, `phelorani` dependency, `koemari` input, `thirulo` output, `selokaru` transfer, `ketorami` technical control, `turekali` feedback, `henoraki` capacity, `pheluremi` storage, `poruseli` efficiency, `nilakiro` inspection, `kolutheri` reliability, `pelomaru` hazard, and `kirosholu` specification. Ecological speakers may use these ordinary Phi words wherever their definitions fit, but they do not by themselves define an ecosystem, habitat relation, exposure pathway, ecological capacity, resilience, scientific validity, or legitimate intervention.

The established [Philosophical Reasoning module](philosophical_reasoning.md) supplies claims, evidence, reasons, inference, validity, contradiction, definition, criteria, doubt, confidence, tradeoffs, and compromise. These terms help examine ecological conclusions without making an evidential, exact value, model, trend, community decision, or preferred value prove the conclusion.

The established [Commons and Collective Governance module](commons_and_collective_governance.md) supplies membership, representation, institutions, roles, authority, authorization, jurisdiction, legitimacy, rules, procedures, decisions, allocation, entitlement, ownership, obligation, accountability, records, review, coercion, and retaliation. Ecological speakers may use these terms to examine land, water, resource, pollution, monitoring, and restoration decisions, but governance vocabulary does not make an ecological claim valid or an intervention effective.

## First Vocabulary Pass

The first pass admits words for recurring ecological distinctions whose ordinary paraphrases would be cumbersome, ambiguous, or unable to make the relation itself available for sustained discussion. Four-syllable forms are used freely for broad or internally layered concepts. The pass does not attempt to minimize coinage, and its choices may be revised through ecological writing, observation, land and water discussion, or material practice.

| Family | Established module words | What the pass now distinguishes |
|---|---|---|
| Ecological organization | `limatero` ecosystem, `menulora` habitat, `limakori` species, `limaloni` population, `limaweso` ecological community, `phiralima` biodiversity | Ecosystem versus named place or harmony; habitat versus observed location; species versus exact taxon; population versus species; ecological versus human community; biodiversity versus list, abundance, or health. |
| Land, water, weather, and climate | `luphanori` watershed, `wiraselu` runoff, `wherakelu` erode, `muhenari` sediment, `howenari` weather, `melunaro` climate, `phaluwero` flood, `kurathimo` drought | Drainage area versus water body; surface flow versus rain or stream; erosion versus weathering or excavation; sediment versus soil or pollution; weather versus climate; flood and drought versus their exact thresholds or consequences. |
| Pollution and exposure | `pelosenu` pollute, `hisophira` contaminant, `pelokira` toxic, `nomiraki` concentration, `pelorawi` expose | Pollution versus contamination; contaminant versus pollutant; toxicity versus hazard or harm; concentration versus total amount or load; exposure versus dose, uptake, or effect. |
| Material life and afterlives | `mueralo` material, `panumori` resource, `whemorali` waste, `moralume` decompose, `lumeraki` nutrient, `turekelu` recycle | Material versus thing or substance grade; resource and waste as relational statuses; decomposition versus breakage, death, or composting; nutrient versus food or fertilizer; recycling versus return, reuse, or a closed cycle. |
| Observation and interpretation | `phisumari` sample, `mothanomi` baseline, `helurani` trend, `reloteru` model, `somethiru` monitor | Sample versus example or evidence; baseline versus original or ideal state; trend versus cause or forecast; model versus represented reality; monitoring versus one observation, inspection, proof, or surveillance. |

All 30 entries follow the complete vocabulary schema, include explicit semantic contrasts and grammatical examples, carry the `ecological-systems-and-material-life` module classification, pass the lexical validator, and have no character edit-distance-one or flagged existing phonetic neighbors in the pre-coinage audit.

## Concepts Kept Compositional

The pass keeps several expressions compositional because their parts expose scope, direction, criteria, and cross-module boundaries that a single label could hide. These are current recommendations rather than prohibitions against later coinage.

| Concept | Phi strategy | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ecosystem boundary | `limatero norui` | Keeps the selected ecosystem and the act of drawing its spatial, temporal, and relational limit visible. |
| Ecological niche | State the `menulora` habitat, `kelithora` functions, `phelorani` dependencies, resources, conditions, and interactions relevant to the organism | Niche concepts differ in what dimensions they include; explicit relations prevent one word from pretending the analysis is complete. |
| Species range | State where and when the `limakori` or `limaloni` dwells, with an explicit `norui` boundary | Keeps occurrence, season, life stage, uncertainty, and sampling limits recoverable. |
| Abundance | Use `lorua` with the organism, place, time, count, density, or comparison | Existing abundance vocabulary remains sufficient when the measured basis is stated. |
| Groundwater | `muila phialu`, earth-water, with explicit depth, movement, storage, and source clauses | Prevents a broad compound from claiming a specific aquifer, flow path, or legal category. |
| Wetland | `wirua muila menulora`, wet-land habitat, or an exact source classification | Keeps ordinary wet ground distinct from a scientific, legal, or community-defined wetland category. |
| Upstream and downstream | Use the water's source, goal, `selu` flow, and `selokaru` transfer direction explicitly | Direction remains relative to the selected water path instead of becoming an absolute spatial axis. |
| Infiltration | `phialu muo muila koema`, water enters earth | States the physical event without claiming rate, storage, recharge, or destination. |
| Heatwave | `sharoi sulae mosha`, exceeding-warm period, with the place, duration, reference, and exact threshold | Makes unusual heat relative to a stated climate and impact rather than defining it by one universal number. |
| Emission | `leiro thirulo`, released output, with source, agent, amount, and destination | Reuses Systems directionality and keeps every output from becoming an environmental emission by default. |
| Pollutant | `pelosenu mueralo`, polluting material, or the relevant polluting organism or energy form | Keeps the agent tied to demonstrated pollution rather than making contaminant status sufficient. |
| Organic matter | `shaemu mueralo` | Reuses the existing organic quality while exact scientific fractions and methods remain external. |
| Compost | `thepalu moralume mueralo`, garden decomposing material, with management and maturity stated | Distinguishes a practical material and process without treating all decomposition as composting. |
| Reuse | Return an object or material to a stated use or make it a `turema koemari`, returned input | Keeps direct reuse distinct from `turekelu` recycling, which requires processing and transformation. |
| Food web | `nuola phaliso`, eating network, with organisms and directions stated | Makes feeding relations visible without assuming energy transfer, complete coverage, stability, or harmony. |
| Ecological carrying capacity | `limatero henoraki` or `menulora henoraki`, with the population, function, time, and conditions stated | Reuses technical capacity while preventing a number from becoming an inherent limit independent of conditions and values. |
| Indicator | `nomirela siru`, state marker, with method and interpretation stated | Keeps an observed marker separate from the ecological condition it is used to infer. |
| Forecast | `wireo nomirela kethira`, infer a future state, with model, evidence, and uncertainty stated | Exposes prediction as an inference rather than a guaranteed future. |
| Monitoring interval | `somethiru mosha` | Names the monitoring practice and its period without hiding duration or frequency. |
| Precaution | State the possible `pelomaru` hazard, uncertainty, limited protective action, burdens, refusal, and review | Keeps precaution as a reasoned practice rather than a word that automatically authorizes intervention. |
| Environmental justice, land or water authority, rights, and restoration obligations | Use Commons terms for `kanorami` authority, `noruwela` jurisdiction, `sherenori` entitlement, `naserumi` obligation, `lonathoni` accountability, and `helokelu` redress with Philosophical Reasoning resources and exact source terms | Ecological terminology must not settle legitimacy, rights, remedy, responsibility, or justice by itself. |

## External Register Guidance

Use `patha ... patho` when exact source form matters for species and taxonomic names, chemical names and formulas, analytes, scientific classifications, measurements, units, dates, site and sample identifiers, coordinates, dataset fields, model names and versions, methods, standards, thresholds, protocols, citations, legal categories, map or record labels, technical quotations, and terms from a community or knowledge tradition. Preserve spelling, script, notation, precision, version, and provenance rather than silently normalizing or translating them.

Use `hasha ... hasho` for a temporary Phi-pronounceable adaptation when source spelling is not authoritative and participants need a speakable scientific, geographic, ecological, or material term. A guest adaptation remains external and does not acquire a Phi taxonomy, definition, evidential status, or claim of universality.

A place, water body, project, community, model, dataset, or other named entity may use `ne` with a listed content name or a valid Phi-form onym when that form follows the productive-name charter and the bearer or relevant naming community accepts it. The name identifies the referent; it does not translate ecological type, ownership, jurisdiction, scientific classification, or source authority.

After any external form, describe the practical relations in core and module Phi where useful: which beings, materials, and places are involved; what moved or changed; the relevant boundary, time, and scale; who sampled, observed, measured, inferred, modeled, or reported; which alternative explanations remain; who may act; what action is proposed; and how its effects will be monitored and reviewed. Do not claim that this analysis exhausts the source concept.

Exact carriage does not make a classification correct, a sample representative, a concentration harmful, a model valid, a threshold applicable, a record complete, or an intervention legitimate. Module words identify analytical roles around exact material; they do not replace the source payload or its qualified interpretation.

## Continued Development

Use the module in ecological essays, land and water discussions, climate and material narratives, monitoring notes, infrastructure decisions, and stories about inhabited places. Coin another word when a recurring ecological concept deserves lexical presence or when the available compound is cumbersome, ambiguous, or aesthetically wrong for Phi. Retain a compound when its parts expose a useful boundary, direction, condition, source, or responsibility. Every new entry must still satisfy the vocabulary schema, phonology, collision checks, semantic contrasts, examples, and reference generation, but it does not need to pass an external burden-of-proof process.
