# Commons and Collective Governance

**Status:** **Profile** - domain-oriented experimental organization, zero new roots.

## Charter

This profile gathers existing Phi resources for describing commons, communities, councils, participation, shared resources, allocation, collective choices, commitments, responsibility, repair, records, and review. It tests whether speakers can coordinate shared life while keeping visible who acts, who is affected, what authority is claimed, which procedure is used, and which disagreements remain.

The profile is not a constitution, legal system, property regime, economic doctrine, institutional template, or claim that one form of governance fits every community. It does not infer legitimacy from a council label, plural action, necessity, consensus language, stewardship, fairness, justice, or a practical outcome.

## Boundaries

- Phi retains one grammar. Collective discussion uses ordinary clauses, questions, evidentials, modality, proposition frames, discourse markers, and external frames; this profile adds no governance particle, voting syntax, authority operator, parser mode, or incompatible construction.
- `laenu` names a commons or shared resource, not a government, owner, legal person, territory, or decision-making body. `sila` names a community without defining membership, standing, borders, jurisdiction, or who may speak for it.
- `kowela` names a council characterized by deliberation for the community, while `lona` names a meeting event. Neither word by itself states who may participate, how agreement is tested, what happens to objections, or when a decision becomes binding.
- `lo mia` and other plural forms identify several participants in an action. They do not create an institution, authorize representation, establish jurisdiction, or prove that every affected person was included.
- `thua`, `theloma`, and `kolo` support evaluation of fairness, justice, and equality. They do not silently choose a distributive criterion, identify a valid finding, specify a remedy, or settle a disagreement.
- `kuri` means right-hand direction only. It does not mean correctness, entitlement, or a legal or moral right; source-specific rights language requires faithful external carriage and separate core analysis unless later shared-core evidence justifies another convention.
- `na` states necessity, not legitimate authority. `seru` states self-binding commitment, not an assigned office; `thonai` presents responsibility as willingly carried duty, not imposed liability, and does not fix whether its basis is causal, moral, role-based, individual, or collective.
- A completed allocation, repair, or written commitment does not by itself establish consent, legitimacy, accountability, restored trust, or justice. Practical action and unresolved criticism may coexist.
- Collective choice does not override bodily refusal or authorize examination, touch, treatment, disclosure, or care by implication. Those relations remain governed by the distinctions in [Medical and Bodily Care](medical_and_bodily_care.md).
- Access to information and participation in decisions overlap [Accessibility and Participation](accessibility_and_participation.md). No collective decision is presumed inclusive merely because a meeting occurred or material was available in one form.
- Technical operation and maintenance overlap [Systems and Shared Infrastructure](systems_and_shared_infrastructure.md). Stewardship of a device or resource does not itself establish ownership, public authority, an office, or liability.
- Land, water, organisms, material flows, pollution, restoration, and ecological evidence overlap [Ecological Systems and Material Life](ecological_systems_and_material_life.md). A protection or regeneration claim does not establish jurisdiction, consent, allocation authority, responsibility, or a fair distribution of environmental burdens.
- Definitions, evidence challenges, competing values, revision, and understood disagreement overlap [Philosophical Reasoning](philosophical_reasoning.md). A collective choice is not an argument for its own legitimacy, and a discourse consequence is not proof that a decision follows.
- Source-specific political, legal, economic, and cultural institutions must not be flattened into supposedly universal core paraphrases. Preserve exact terminology when its wording, provenance, force, identity, or contested history matters.

## Shared Core Dependencies

These are existing Phi roots and function words. The profile indexes them without moving them from the shared lexicon, narrowing their ordinary meanings, or assigning them technical political or legal definitions.

| Area | Existing resources | Current reach |
|---|---|---|
| Community, commons, and collective agency | `sila` community, `laenu` commons, `miona` person, `lo` plural, `nuawe` together, `wesoma` mutual | Speakers can identify people, a community, a shared resource, and action by several participants. These forms do not conventionalize membership, constituency, affected-party standing, institutional identity, jurisdiction, or representation. |
| Assembly, inquiry, and deliberation | `kowela` council, `lona` meeting, `shareo` discuss, `thilou` inquire, `lilea` clarify, `lonae` respond, `numike` negotiate, `thorae` facilitator, `thereo` mediator | Speakers can gather, ask, clarify, respond, negotiate, facilitate, and mediate. The roots do not specify appointment, agenda, proposal status, speaking order, consensus test, vote, quorum, abstention, closure, or appeal. |
| Participation, choice, and stance | `pawela` participate, `shorema` contribute, `kanu` choose, `nawo` agree, `naweri` refuse, `kelomi` accept, `thekao` acknowledge, `ralu` free, `nawea` respect | Speakers can take part, contribute, choose, agree, refuse, accept, and acknowledge. Participation, consultation, representation, agreement, endorsement, consent, compliance, and authority to decide still require separate clauses and context. |
| Resources, needs, and distribution | `phelu` hold, `phowe` share, `phanoi` portion, `loa` give, `howela` receive, `phelona` require, `henoi` enough, `theula` all, `theli` each, `shelami` most, `soli` some, `riola` labor | Speakers can describe shared holding, quantities, needs, contributions, portions, and transfers. These resources do not define ownership, custody, claim, eligibility, entitlement, priority, scarcity, sufficiency for a particular claimant, or an allocation rule. |
| Evaluation and competing goods | `thua` fair, `theloma` justice, `kolo` equal, `weilo` balanced, `sone` value, `rolia` worth, `noetha` essential, `peloma` harmful | Speakers can compare outcomes and name fairness, justice, equality, balance, value, necessity, and harm. They must still state the criterion, affected relation, acceptable loss, tradeoff, remedy, or unresolved conflict. |
| Commitment, responsibility, and repair | `seru` commit, `thonai` responsible, `theluo` steward, `kanelu` err, `thiranu` maintain, `shiroka` repair, `talome` restore, `warosha` reconcile, `tholira` forgive, `ka` cause, `na` necessity, `po` possibility, `ma` negation | Speakers can distinguish commitment, willingly carried responsibility, error, causation, upkeep, repair, restoration, reconciliation, forgiveness, possibility, and necessity in broad terms. They cannot yet conventionalize office, mandate, assigned duty, imposed obligation, institutional responsibility, finding, remedy, sanction, enforcement, or restored standing. |
| Evidence, notice, and records | `hi` direct evidence, `ke` inference, `ti` report, `ho` assumption, `sano` know, `shane` tell, `thekiro` write, `siru` marker, `shekoi` specify, `masue` measure, `patha ... patho` exact material | Speakers can identify a claimed source, knowledge, telling, writing, markers, specifications, measurements, and exact records. They cannot conventionalize notice, delivery, publication, provenance, access to a record, confidentiality, version, official status, or the authority of a source. |
| Sequence, change, and review | `phoe` before, `pheo` after, `weno` when, `hasi` wait, `wireo` future, `teru` process, `helui` change, `turema` return | Speakers can order actions, pause, revisit a topic, describe a process, and change a choice or commitment. These forms do not establish a provisional decision, review period, amendment, repeal, appeal, deadline, succession, or duty to reopen a matter. |

## Current Practices and Limits

| Need | Current Phi strategy | Important limit |
|---|---|---|
| Identify a commons and its participants | Name the community, resource, affected people, and concrete relations with `sila`, `laenu`, ordinary possession or holding, plural forms, and explicit clauses. | A community or commons noun does not determine membership, title, exclusion, standing, boundaries, or authority over the resource. |
| Convene collective deliberation | Use `lona` or `kowela`, then state who participates, asks, responds, clarifies, negotiates, facilitates, or mediates. | A place or event of discussion is not a complete procedure; agenda, proposal, representation, consensus test, decision rule, and closure remain unstated. |
| State a collective choice | Use plural agents with `kanu`, proposition frames for the chosen action, and separate clauses for agreement, refusal, acceptance, or acknowledgement. | Plural agency does not prove authorization, inclusion, legitimacy, consensus, or whether a decision applies to nonparticipants. |
| Allocate a shared resource | State the resource, quantities or portions, recipients, needs, contributions, present and future consequences, and the actual transfer or holding. | The resulting distribution does not reveal its criterion, relative shares, sufficiency, eligibility, priority, entitlement, or treatment of rejected claims. |
| Preserve disagreement during action | State `nawo`, `ma nawo`, `naweri`, `kelomi`, `thekao`, and commitments in separate clauses; schedule later discussion explicitly. | Acceptance, compliance, agreement, consent, endorsement, reservation, objection, and provisional cooperation remain unconventionalized relations. |
| Assign or contest responsibility | State causation, knowledge, warning, commitment, action or omission, capacity, and proposed response separately; allow refusal of an imposed action. | `thonai` does not identify the basis or scope of responsibility, and `na` does not establish who may impose an obligation or consequence. |
| Repair harm or shared infrastructure | Describe repair, restoration, changed practice, shared labor, reconciliation, and future commitments as distinct actions. | Material repair may leave loss, trust, institutional failure, remedy, accountability, enforcement, and justice unresolved. |
| Record and revisit a decision | Write the relevant commitment or choice, preserve exact source material, state when it will be discussed again, and identify later changes explicitly. | Writing does not establish notice, access, authenticity, official status, interpretation, review rights, or authority to amend the record. |
| Discuss an external institution or rule | Preserve its exact name, wording, identifier, or source category with `patha ... patho`, then analyze concrete relations in core Phi. | Exact carriage does not import source grammar, enact the rule, validate the institution, or establish that the core analysis is a complete translation. |

## Scenario Evidence

- [Grain for hunger and seed](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#11-grain-for-hunger-and-seed) tests allocation under scarcity among present hunger, future seed, and a labor contribution. It reaches a concrete provisional action without a ruler, owner, complete agreement, or stated allocation rule.
- [The marker that was not reported](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#12-the-marker-that-was-not-reported) tests responsibility after shared infrastructure fails. It separates causation, warning, knowledge, commitment, maintenance, refusal, shared repair, written commitments, and unresolved justice without assuming unilateral authority.
- [Words before the meeting](../philosophical_test_corpus.md#10-words-before-the-meeting) supplies boundary evidence: receipt of information, contribution, participation, representation, agreement, and readiness to decide remain distinct, and governance practice must account for access rather than treating meeting presence as inclusion.

These are scripted regression texts by one maintainer. They demonstrate available compositions and recurring failures, not the legitimacy of a real institution, the adequacy of one governance model, or another community's agreement with Phi's values.

## Comparative Findings

| Finding | Evidence status | Consequence for this profile |
|---|---|---|
| A commons can hold resources without becoming a government, owner, or decision-making body | **Recurring** across shared grain and the shared water device | Keep resource relations separate from institution, membership, jurisdiction, and authority. |
| Plural action and council discussion do not establish legitimate collective authority | **Recurring** across both commons scenarios and supported by the access scenarios | Require speakers to state participants, affected people, claimed authorization, procedure, objections, and decision status where they matter. |
| Broad fairness and justice terms identify evaluation without supplying criteria | **Recurring** across allocation and accountability | Pair `thua`, `theloma`, or `kolo` with the concrete needs, contributions, harms, procedures, findings, and remedies under dispute. |
| Concrete action can proceed while legitimacy or accountability remains disputed | **Recurring** across provisional allocation and shared repair | Preserve unresolved criticism rather than treating an outcome, acceptance, or written commitment as proof of agreement or justice. |
| Agreement, refusal, acceptance, acknowledgement, commitment, and participation are different stances | **Recurring** across scenarios 10 through 12 | State each relevant stance separately and do not infer consent or endorsement from cooperation. |
| Authority, institutional role, decision procedure, and review are recurring weak points | **Recurring** across both commons scenarios | Organize current compositions here, but require another institution outside a council before considering any conventional form. |
| Allocation criteria, claims, priority, and urgency pressure remain thin | **Provisional**, one scarce-resource scenario | Transform the claimants and criteria before treating any distinction as a lexical candidate. |
| Causal, informational, role-based, and collective responsibility remain thin | **Provisional**, one accountability scenario | Independently vary causation, knowledge, commitment, capacity, and shared holding in the next tests. |
| Exact instructions and written commitments help only when provenance, access, interpretation, and relevance are stated | **Recurring** across technical, access, and accountability scenarios | Keep records and exact material separate from authority, successful notice, understanding, and justified decision. |

## External Register Guidance

Use `patha ... patho` when exact source form matters for the names or wording of governments, nations, peoples, communities, institutions, offices, laws, bylaws, policies, treaties, contracts, rights claims, decision rules, ballots, budgets, currencies, dates, deadlines, identifiers, records, testimony, and contested political or legal categories. Preserve source spelling, script, notation, wording, and provenance when normalization or paraphrase would change identity, force, scope, or the object of criticism.

A community, institution, place, project, or other bearer may instead choose a valid Phi-form onym introduced by `ne` under the productive-name charter. That name designates the bearer; it does not translate the bearer's institutional type, legal status, membership rule, or authority.

Use `hasha ... hasho` only for a temporary Phi-pronounceable adaptation when the source form is not authoritative and participants need a speakable external name or term. A guest adaptation does not create a Phi office, institution, legal category, or grant of authority.

After either frame, describe the practical relations in core Phi where useful: who acts, who is affected, what resource or action is at issue, what source or rule is claimed, who knew or received it, how a choice was reached, what was accepted or refused, what response follows, and when review occurs. Do not claim that this analysis exhausts the source institution or category.

An exact phrase such as `patha majority vote patho`, `patha public trust patho`, or the wording of a law preserves an external rule or concept. It does not establish who may vote, whether the procedure was followed, whether the rule is legitimate, or whether the law is true, just, accessible, or binding in the situation under discussion.

## Gap Inventory, Not a Word Queue

| Gap family | Distinctions currently missing or thin | Evidence still required |
|---|---|---|
| Membership and standing | member, resident, affected party, constituency, inclusion, exclusion, eligibility to participate, representation | Cases in which participants, affected people, resource users, and authorized decision makers are not the same set. |
| Institution and authority | institution, government, office, mandate, jurisdiction, authorization, delegation, legitimacy, term, succession | A standing institution outside a council whose role, scope, continuity, and claimed authority can be varied independently. |
| Collective decision procedure | proposal, agenda, deliberation, consensus, vote, quorum, abstention, objection, decision, closure, minority position | Parallel decisions using different procedures while holding the issue and participants constant, followed by blind recovery of decision status. |
| Allocation and political economy | claim, entitlement, ownership, property, custody, scarcity, priority, allocation criterion, sufficiency, common, public, private | Transformations of the grain case that vary need, contribution, future cost, share size, and rejected claims without changing the resource. |
| Obligation and accountability | willingly accepted duty versus assigned or imposed obligation, law, policy, rule, violation, causal responsibility, role responsibility, investigation, finding, remedy, sanction, enforcement, appeal | Cases that independently vary cause, knowledge, commitment, capacity, authority, repair, and consequence rather than treating responsibility as one scale. |
| Notice and public record | notice, delivery, disclosure, publication, record, minutes, provenance, version, confidentiality, official status | Conflicting testimony and records where access, source authority, timing, and later amendment change the decision. |
| Review and institutional change | provisional decision, reservation, review, appeal, amendment, repeal, recall, handoff, institutional failure | A decision that remains actionable while challenged, then changes through a stated review process without erasing the original objection. |
| Power, conflict, and institutional harm | coercion, domination, corruption, abuse of office, retaliation, capture, organized exclusion, resistance | Critical scenarios in which a valued community or council causes harm and cooperative vocabulary cannot be allowed to soften responsibility. |

No new root follows from this profile. Any future form needed for ordinary participation, consent, safety, testimony, criticism of authority, or protection from institutional harm is a shared-core question, not vocabulary owned by a governance module. Specialized institutional terminology must still pass the ordinary admission protocol and remain explainable to speakers outside the profile.

## Next Use Test

Write an original shared-storehouse scenario outside a council without adding vocabulary. A standing role or institution follows an exact allocation rule; at least one affected person did not receive or could not use the notice; a written record conflicts with testimony; the role holder claims authorization; and the participants must distinguish rule compliance, legitimacy, harm, repair, and review. Include one delegated task, one refusal, one provisional action that protects an urgent need, and an ending in which practical action occurs without complete agreement.

Read the Phi without its translation after a delay and recover every participant, affected person, resource, rule, evidence source, claimed authority, decision step, objection, responsibility relation, remedy, and review condition. Then transform the case by changing authorization, knowledge, scarcity, and repair capacity one at a time while holding the other facts constant.

Record every English rescue and classify it as retrieval, composition, missing convention, external carriage, shared-core gap, or profile-oriented candidate. Do not propose governance vocabulary until the same distinction fails in both the storehouse case and at least one transformed case, core and external alternatives have been tested, and the repair improves blind recovery without weakening participation, consent, safety, testimony, criticism, or source fidelity.
