# 4. Coordination and Subordination: Announcing Relationships

Following the modifier-first principle, Phi announces how clauses and phrases relate to each other BEFORE presenting their content. This creates transparent logical structures where relationships are explicit from the outset.

## 4.1. Coordination: Announcing Equal Relationships

Coordinating conjunctions announce the type of connection between equal elements, appearing between them to signal their relationship:

**Basic (`nela`):** Used for "and."
```
mia sela nela weola lothea
1SG peace COORD community love
(I love peace and community.)
```

**Adversative (`thona`):** Used for "but."
```
mia lothea kela thona shia ma kela
1SG love understand BUT 3SG NEG understand
(I understand love, but the other does not.)
```

**Alternative (`sola`):** Used for "or."
```
wa [shia miro] sola [philo]
Q [3SG beautiful] OR [calm]
(Is it beautiful or calm?)
```

### 4.1.1. Correlative Constructions
To add emphasis to a coordinated phrase, a logical adverb may be placed before the main verb.

- **Both... and...:** Use `nela` to link the items and add the adverb `sila` (together).
  ```
  mia [sela nela weola] sila lothea
  1SG [peace and community] together love
  (I love both peace and community.)
  ```
- **Either... or...:** Use `sola` to link the items and add the adverb `sona` (as a choice).
  ```
  thi [thola sola sela] sona po shele
  2SG [story or peace] as-a-choice POSS receive
  (You can have either the story or the peace.)
  ```
- **Neither... nor...:** Use `sola` and `sona`, and negate the main verb with `ma`.
  ```
  mia [thola sola sela] sona ma kela
  1SG [story or peace] as-a-choice NEG understand
  (I understand neither the story nor the peace.)
  ```

### 4.1.2. Discourse Adverbs: Announcing Logical Connections

Discourse adverbs announce how the current sentence relates to what came before, appearing early in the sentence to prepare listeners for the logical connection.

**Position (Layered Announcement):** Discourse adverbs appear *after* any Slot 0 particle but *before* the subject, creating layers of announcement: `[Slot 0 Particle] [Discourse Adverb] [Subject]...`

**Common Discourse Adverbs:**
- `thelao` (therefore, for this reason)
- `kewai` (however, on the other hand)
- `sheno` (in addition, furthermore)
- `phisu` (for example, for instance)

## 4.2. Subordination: Announcing Dependent Relationships

Subordination in Phi follows the modifier-first principle—dependent clauses announce their relationship and appear before or within the main clause they modify.

### 4.2.1. Indirect Speech (Clause as Object)
To report the content of speech indirectly, the reported statement is treated as the direct object of a verb of speech or thought.
```
mia [thi to thopa] kela
1SG [2SG PST speak] understand
(I understand what you said.)
```

### 4.2.2. Direct Speech (Quotation)

The quotative particle `sho` announces that what follows is direct speech, maintaining the principle of announcing before delivering:
```
shia sho "thino wela" thopa
3SG QUOT "the sun is beautiful" say
(She says, "The sun is beautiful.")
```

### 4.2.3. Temporal Clauses ("announce then deliver")
A temporal clause is formed by using a preposition that functions as a temporal subordinator to announce the relationship, followed by the clause.
```
pheo [mia kealo] mia sela hisu
AFTER [1SG create] 1SG peace feel
(After I create, I feel peace.)

phoe [mia shele] mia nolu
BEFORE [1SG help] 1SG think
(Before I help, I think.)
```

### 4.2.4. Causal and Concessive Clauses
Causal ("because") and concessive ("although") clauses are formed by using the prepositions `lao` and `shai` respectively, followed by the subordinate clause.

**Structure:** `[Preposition [Clause]], [Main Clause]`

- **Causal:**
  ```
  lao [shia to wepu], mia nolou hisu
  BECAUSE [3SG PST leave], 1SG sadness feel
  (Because she left, I feel sad.)
  ```
- **Concessive:**
  ```
  shai [shiro to wela ma nio], mia ketho ma hisu
  ALTHOUGH [forest PST beautiful NEG be], 1SG fear NEG feel
  (Although the forest was not beautiful, I felt no fear.)
  ```

### 4.2.5. Indirect Questions
An indirect question is formed by using a clause containing an interrogative pronoun (e.g., `sua`, `kua`) as the direct object of a verb like `kela` (to know) or `kewha` (to ask). The main sentence remains a statement.

**Structure:** `S [S-interrogative O V] V`

**Examples:**
- **Expressing knowledge of a question:**
  ```
  mia [sua to wepu] kela
  1SG [who PST leave] know
  (I know who left.)
  ```
- **Expressing uncertainty:**
  ```
  mia [kua thola nio] ma kela
  1SG [where story is] NEG know
  (I don't know where the story is.)
  ```

## 4.3. The Philosophy of Relational Announcement

Phi's approach to coordination and subordination embodies the modifier-first principle at the clause level:

### 4.3.1. Transparency in Logic
By announcing logical relationships with conjunctions and subordinators BEFORE the content they connect, Phi makes the structure of complex thoughts transparent. Listeners know immediately whether information is being added, contrasted, or conditioned.

### 4.3.2. Conscious Construction
The requirement to announce relationships forces speakers to be conscious of how their ideas connect. One cannot accidentally imply causation or contrast—these relationships must be explicitly announced.

### 4.3.3. Collaborative Understanding
When relationships are announced upfront, listeners can actively participate in constructing meaning. They know from the first word whether to expect addition, exception, cause, or condition, allowing them to process complex ideas more easily.

This systematic announcement of relationships transforms complex sentence construction from a potential source of confusion into an exercise in transparent, conscious communication.
