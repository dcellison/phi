> - Old particles `re` (relativizer) and `sho` (quotative) are now the multi-syllable complementizers `rena` and `shola…sholo` (with required closers) — the "27 particle forms" count and the Slot 2 list have changed.
> - `shia` is no longer the numeral three (it is the 3SG pronoun); the scale units are `shao` (3), `phoi` (9), `lau` (27), `rei` (81), and the base glyph list should read `mu`, `ta`, `wi` + those four.
> - Example vocabulary has moved on: `kofe` (help) is now `naphe`; `wela` (good) is retired for `welao`; `kea` (knowledge) is not a current word; `ke` is not a current particle; `thela` is now "how".
> - The sample sentence `wa pi mia lo melu so ko kofe` violates the settled Slot 0 order — politeness comes first (`pi wa …`).
> - The syllabogram count is ~70, not ~50: 14 onsets (10 consonants including k, 4 fricative digraphs) × 5 vowels.
> - The lexicon is 906 words and growing, so "~300 logograms" wants rethinking (or an explicit frequency cutoff).
> - The hiatus-marking problem this document worries about is solved elegantly in the Tengwar mode (see `tengwar_mode.md`): the second vowel hangs below the same glyph. A block-internal below-position may be the Mayan-mode answer too.
> - Per the 2026 punctuation and letters rulings: no capitals exist in any mode, and the period is the only visible punctuation — the "question arc" idea for `wa` must remain the *rendering of the word* `wa`, not a silent mark (mode-invariance).

# A Mayan-Inspired Glyph Block System for Phi

## Executive Summary

This document explores the development of a unique writing system for Phi based on Mayan hieroglyphic principles. The proposed system would combine logographic and syllabic elements in a flexible block structure, allowing particles to attach visually to content words while maintaining clarity about grammatical scope and relationships.

## Background

Phi currently has:
- A romanized writing system (existing)
- A Tengwar mode specifically adapted for vowel hiatus (created)
- A desire for a more unique, philosophically-aligned writing system

The Mayan hieroglyphic system offers an compelling model because it:
- Combines logographic and syllabic elements flexibly
- Arranges symbols in block structures
- Allows affixes to attach visually to main glyphs
- Provides artistic flexibility while maintaining linguistic precision

## Why Mayan-Style Blocks Suit Phi

### 1. Structural Alignment

**Block structure matches Phi's analytical nature:**
- Main glyph = content word (noun, verb, adjective)
- Attached affixes = particles (lo, na, se, ke, etc.)
- Visual hierarchy reflects grammatical relationships

**Phi's CV syllable structure is ideal for syllabograms:**
- Each CV syllable could have its own sub-glyph
- Vowel hiatus could be shown through spacing or special markers
- Much simpler than Mayan since Phi has only ~50 possible CV combinations

### 2. Philosophical Alignment

- Each content word glyph could visually embody its philosophical pillars
- Art Nouveau organic forms could inspire the glyph designs
- The image prompts already developed for vocabulary could inform the visual vocabulary
- The act of writing becomes a meditative, artistic practice reflecting the language's deeper principles

### 3. Practical Advantages

- SOV word order maps nicely to left-to-right block reading
- Particles attached to their head words visually
- Compound concepts could share glyph elements
- Flexibility in arrangement (horizontal, vertical, adapted to different surfaces)

## Phi's Three-Slot Particle System

Understanding Phi's particle system is crucial for designing the visual attachment system:

### Slot 0 (Sentence-frame particles)
- **Particles**: wa (question), no (imperative), lu (conditional), su (optative), pi (politeness)
- **Scope**: Entire sentence/clause
- **Examples**: Question markers, mood markers

### Slot 1 (Verb phrase particles)
- **Tense**: to (past), so (future)
- **Aspect**: ki (perfective), si (imperfective), pa (inchoative), te (cessative)
- **Voice**: se (passive)
- **Modality**: po (possibility), na (necessity), ka (causative)
- **Negation**: ma (negative)
- **Scope**: Verb phrase

### Slot 2 (Word-level particles)
- **Particles**: lo (plural), ko (focus), mo (comparative), ha (proximal), ra (distal), sa/ni/le (honorifics), re (relativizer), sho (quotative)
- **Scope**: Individual words or small phrases

## Visual Solutions for Particle Scope

The key challenge is representing particles that affect different scopes (word, phrase, sentence) within a block-based system.

### Proposed Solutions by Scope:

**Slot 0 (sentence-wide scope):**
- Margin glyphs: Place in left margin, affecting all blocks to their right
- Overhead arcs: Curved lines above affected blocks
- Sentence cartouches: Frame entire sentences with distinctive borders

**Slot 1 (verb phrase scope):**
- Verb block modification: Attach as larger affixes to verb blocks
- Color/shading: Different visual weight for verb phrase modifiers
- Connecting bridges: Link to all words in the verb phrase

**Slot 2 (word-level):**
- Direct attachment: Small affixes on individual word blocks
- Internal modification: Modify the internal structure of the glyph
- Special cases:
  - "re" (relativizer): Visual bracket around relative clause
  - "sho" (quotative): Speech-bubble-like container

### Visual Example

For the sentence: `wa pi mia lo melu so ko kofe`
(Q POL 1SG PL friend FUT FOC help)
"Will I please help the friends (emphasized)?"

- "wa" creates question arc over everything
- "pi" adds politeness marker in margin
- "mia" is a single glyph block
- "melu" has "lo" attached as small affix
- "kofe" has "so" as medium affix (verb modifier) and "ko" as small affix (focus)

## Logographic vs. Syllabic Writing

### Fully Flexible Mixed System

Every word in Phi can be written in multiple ways:

**Logographic mode** - used for:
- Efficiency: Common words are faster as single glyphs
- Artistic expression: Visual poetry, calligraphy, art
- Semantic emphasis: When meaning matters more than sound
- Visual balance: Fitting text into specific spaces

**Syllabic mode** - used for:
- Clarity: Teaching, dictionaries, pronunciation guides
- Transparency: New learners, children's texts
- Precision: Names, technical terms, borrowed words
- Phonetic emphasis: Poetry focusing on sound patterns

**Mixed mode** - used for:
- Disambiguation: Logogram + phonetic complement
- Learning bridges: Helping readers recognize unfamiliar logograms
- Compound transparency: Showing word formation
- Stylistic variety: Avoiding repetition

### Implementation Scale

- **~300 logograms** for content words (manageable learning curve)
- **~50 syllabograms** for CV combinations (complete coverage)
- **~27 particle forms** that can attach or frame other elements
- **7 number glyphs** for the ternary system (mu, ta, wi, shia, phoi, lau, rei)

## Representing Ternary Numbers

Phi's ternary number system presents unique opportunities for visual representation:

### Base Number Glyphs (0-2)
- **mu (0)**: Empty circle or void shape, representing absence/potential
- **ta (1)**: Single vertical stroke or dot, the fundamental unit
- **wi (2)**: Two linked elements or paired marks, showing duality

### Scale Unit Glyphs (groups)
- **shia (3)**: Triangle or three-part mandala
- **phoi (9)**: Nine-cell grid or triple triangle
- **lau (27)**: Complex nested form suggesting abundance
- **rei (81)**: Ornate symbol suggesting "countless"

### Compound Number Representation
Numbers could be written either:
1. **Analytically**: Separate glyphs for each component (e.g., "ta shia wi" = 1×3+2 = 5)
2. **Synthetically**: Combined forms where scale units contain base numbers
3. **Positionally**: Using position within a frame to indicate scale (similar to an abacus)

The visual design should emphasize Phi's philosophy of approximate rather than precise counting—making large numbers deliberately complex to write, encouraging the use of approximative expressions instead.

## Vowel Hiatus Representation

Phi's strict vowel hiatus (each vowel is a separate syllable) requires clear visual marking:

### Proposed Solutions
1. **Spacing gaps**: Physical space between syllable blocks containing adjacent vowels
2. **Hiatus markers**: Special small glyphs (like apostrophes) between vowel-containing blocks
3. **Block boundaries**: Stronger visual boundaries when blocks contain vowels in sequence
4. **Color/shading**: Different visual treatment for vowel-initial syllables

Example: "kea" (knowledge) would show clear separation between "ke" and "a" blocks, preventing misreading as a diphthong.

## Compound Word Transparency

Compound words should optionally reveal their components:

### Visual Strategies
1. **Nested blocks**: Component words appear as sub-blocks within a larger frame
   - Example: "thela" could show [no][the][la] within a unified border
2. **Shared elements**: Overlapping glyphs where components share visual features
3. **Gradient complexity**: Simple compounds use touching blocks, complex ones use fusion
4. **Etymology markers**: Small indicators showing word boundaries for learners

This allows readers to choose their level of analysis—seeing either the whole concept or its constituent parts.

## Design Principles

1. **No hierarchy of "worthiness"**: All words deserve equal treatment - no "sacred" words requiring special treatment

2. **Writer's choice**: Like choosing fonts, writers select based on:
   - Audience (learners vs. fluent readers)
   - Purpose (clarity vs. aesthetics)
   - Medium (carved stone vs. digital text)

3. **Systematic design**: Logograms could share visual elements based on:
   - Semantic domains (emotion words share curved elements)
   - Phonetic hints (words starting with 'm' share a base shape)
   - Philosophical pillars (solarpunk words have growth motifs)

4. **Practical evolution**: Common words naturally develop simplified forms through use, not through hierarchical assignment

## Managing Visual Complexity

The interaction of multiple particle scopes poses a significant design challenge. Consider the sentence `wa pi mia lo melu so ko kofe` with 7 grammatical elements affecting different scopes.

### Strategies for Clarity

1. **Visual Hierarchy Through Size**
   - Sentence-level particles (wa, pi): Largest/boldest markers
   - Verb phrase particles (so): Medium-sized attachments
   - Word-level particles (lo, ko): Smallest attachments
   
2. **Consistent Positioning Rules**
   - Slot 0: Always in margins or overhead
   - Slot 1: Always left-side of verb blocks
   - Slot 2: Always right-side or internal to blocks

3. **Progressive Complexity**
   - Basic texts: Minimize particle stacking
   - Intermediate: Gradual introduction of complex interactions
   - Advanced: Full system with all possible combinations

4. **Alternative Notation for Dense Texts**
   - Linear mode: Particles written separately when too many stack
   - Footnote system: Complex grammatical information moved to margins
   - Color coding: Different particle types in different colors (for digital/printed texts)

### Visual Breathing Room

Just as Phi's phonology uses vowel hiatus to slow speech, the writing system should use visual spacing to prevent crowding:
- Minimum spacing between blocks
- Larger gaps between phrases
- Clear sentence boundaries
- White space as a grammatical tool

## Advantages of This System

1. **Philosophical coherence**: Visual representation aligns with Phi's core values
2. **Practical flexibility**: Adaptable to different contexts and skill levels
3. **Artistic potential**: Enables beautiful calligraphy and visual poetry
4. **Learning pathway**: Natural progression from syllabic to mixed to logographic
5. **Cultural uniqueness**: Distinctive identity while building on proven principles
6. **Spatial grammar**: Grammatical relationships visible through spatial arrangement

## Addressing the Learning Curve

With ~377 total symbols (300 logograms + 50 syllabograms + 27 particles + 7 numbers), the system requires a structured learning approach:

### Phased Learning Path

**Phase 1: Syllabic Foundation (Month 1-2)**
- Master 50 CV syllabograms
- Write any Phi word phonetically
- Learn basic particle attachments
- Focus: Sound-symbol correspondence

**Phase 2: Core Logograms (Month 3-4)**
- Learn 50 most frequent content words as logograms
- Practice mixed syllabic-logographic writing
- Introduce particle scope markers
- Focus: Reading fluency for basic texts

**Phase 3: Extended Logograms (Month 5-8)**
- Add 100-150 more logograms
- Learn compound word visual patterns
- Master complex particle interactions
- Focus: Writing efficiency

**Phase 4: Full System (Month 9-12)**
- Complete ~300 logogram repertoire
- Develop personal calligraphic style
- Create artistic variations
- Focus: Aesthetic expression

### Pedagogical Tools

1. **Mnemonic Design**: Logograms incorporate visual hints:
   - Words starting with 'm' share a base curved element
   - Emotion words contain heart-like shapes
   - Movement words show directional arrows

2. **Progressive Texts**: Reading materials designed for each phase:
   - Phase 1: All syllabic with wide spacing
   - Phase 2: Common words as logograms, rest syllabic
   - Phase 3: Mixed based on frequency
   - Phase 4: Full artistic expression

3. **Digital Learning Aids**:
   - Hover-to-reveal: Mouse over logogram to see syllabic form
   - Adaptive display: System adjusts complexity to reader level
   - Practice modes: Switch between writing systems instantly

## Conclusion

A Mayan-inspired glyph block system offers Phi a writing system that is:
- Linguistically sophisticated yet learnable
- Visually beautiful and philosophically meaningful
- Flexible enough for both practical and artistic use
- True to Phi's principles of accessibility and mindful communication

This system would give Phi a unique visual identity while maintaining the language's core values of clarity, inclusivity, and aesthetic harmony. The flexibility to write each word either logographically or syllabically ensures the system remains practical and egalitarian rather than hierarchical or exclusive.
