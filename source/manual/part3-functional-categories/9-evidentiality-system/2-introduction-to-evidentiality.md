# Introduction to Evidentiality

## What is Evidentiality?

Evidentiality is a grammatical system that indicates **how the speaker knows** the information they are presenting. It's one of phi's most sophisticated and distinctive features, requiring speakers to explicitly mark the source of their knowledge.

### Evidentiality in Human Languages

Evidentiality exists in many world languages but is often overlooked by speakers of languages like English that don't grammatically require it:

- **Quechua**: Distinguishes direct experience (`-mi`), inference (`-si`), hearsay (`-shi`)
- **Turkish**: Marks witnessed (`-di`) vs. non-witnessed (`-miş`) past events  
- **Tibetan**: Complex system with multiple evidential distinctions
- **German**: Uses modal particles like `wohl` (presumably) for evidential meaning

#### Example from Turkish

```
Witnessed:     Ali gel-di.        (Ali came - I saw it)
Non-witnessed: Ali gel-miş.       (Ali came - I heard/inferred)
```

### Why Evidentiality Matters

Evidentiality serves crucial communicative functions:

1. **Precision**: Prevents miscommunication about information reliability
2. **Responsibility**: Speakers must acknowledge their information sources
3. **Clarity**: Distinguishes facts from assumptions, reports, or guesses
4. **Cultural value**: Promotes intellectual honesty and careful communication

### Evidentiality vs. Related Concepts

#### Evidentiality ≠ Certainty
- **Evidentiality**: *How* you know something
- **Certainty/Modality**: *How sure* you are about something

#### Evidentiality ≠ Tense
- **Evidentiality**: Source of knowledge
- **Tense**: Time of the event

#### Example Distinction

English: "John left" (no evidential information)

Turkish options:
- `John gitti` (John left - I witnessed this)
- `John gitmiş` (John left - I heard/inferred this)

Both express the same event but different knowledge sources.

## Cross-Linguistic Distribution

### Languages with Evidentiality Systems

- **Widespread in**: Andean languages, some Native American languages, 
  Tibeto-Burman languages, some Balkan languages
- **Rare in**: Most European languages, East Asian languages
- **Emerging in**: Contact situations, creoles

### Types of Evidential Systems

1. **Two-way systems**: Direct vs. indirect (Turkish, Bulgarian)
2. **Three-way systems**: Direct vs. inference vs. hearsay (Quechua)
3. **Four+ way systems**: Multiple fine-grained distinctions (Tibetan, Tuyuca)

## Phi's Position

Phi employs a **six-way evidential system** that covers the major ways humans acquire knowledge, making it one of the most detailed evidential systems among constructed languages.

This detailed system reflects phi's design philosophy of promoting:
- **Intellectual precision**
- **Clear communication**
- **Acknowledgment of knowledge limitations**
- **Cross-cultural accessibility** (drawing from multiple human language patterns)
