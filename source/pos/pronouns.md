# pronouns

> pronouns are words used in place of nouns to avoid repetition or to refer back to nouns previously mentioned in a sentence or discourse. they represent persons, animals, things, or concepts and can function as subjects, objects, possessives, or reflexives in sentences. examples include "he," "she," "it," "they," "me," "you," "himself," "ourselves," and "whose." pronouns help to make sentences more concise and fluent by replacing specific nouns with more general terms.

## introduction to phi pronouns

phi pronouns represent a deliberate exception to the language's otherwise strict 
phonotactic patterns. unlike other parts of speech that follow specific consonant-vowel 
structures, pronouns maintain simpler, more compact forms due to their extremely high 
frequency in everyday speech.

the phi pronoun system is intentionally minimal, with only three core forms: `mia` (first 
person), `thi` (second person), and `sha` (third person). this simplicity eliminates 
the gender distinctions found in many natural languages, avoiding unnecessary 
complexity and potential social issues. the third person pronoun `sha` refers to all 
entities regardless of gender or animacy.

plurality in pronouns is achieved through the standard particle `lo`, which precedes 
the pronoun to create forms like `lo mia` (we) and `lo thi` (you all). this follows 
phi's consistent approach to pluralization across all nominal elements.

while these pronouns do not conform to the phonotactic patterns that distinguish nouns, 
verbs, adjectives, and other parts of speech, their exceptional status is justified 
by both practical considerations (frequency of use favors brevity) and linguistic 
naturalness (pronouns often maintain irregular forms across natural languages).

## personal pronouns

personal pronouns in phi refer to the speaker (first person), the addressee (second person), 
and any other entity (third person). these core forms can be combined with various particles 
to express grammatical relationships.

| phi word | english translation | funcrion | 
 | -------- | ------------------- | ---------- |
| mia      | me/I                | 1st person |
| thi      | you                 | 2nd person |
| sha      | it/he/she/they      | 3rd person |

## pronoun usage with particles

phi pronouns can be modified by particles to indicate grammatical function, plurality, emphasis, 
and other features. these combinations are essential for understanding how pronouns operate 
in sentences.

### plurality

plurality is expressed by adding the particle `lo` before the pronoun:

| form     | 1st person | 2nd person | 3rd person |
| -------- | ---------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| singular | mia        | thi        | sha        |
| plural   | lo mia     | lo thi     | lo sha     |

### grammatical function

grammatical function can be explicitly marked with particles:

| funcrion | 1st person  | 2nd person | 3rd person | english equivalent |
| -------- | ---------- | ---------- | ---------- | ------------------ |
| hubject | si mia      | si thi     | si sha     | I, you, it (as subject) |
| object   | na mia     | na thi     | na sha     | me, you, it (as object) |

### emphasis

emphasis is added with the particle `ma`:

| phi       | english translation      |
| --------- | ------------------------ |
| ma mia    | *I* / *me* (emphatic)    |
| ma thi    | *you* (emphatic)         |
| ma sha    | *it/he/she* (emphatic)   |
| ma lo mia | *we* / *us* (emphatic)   |

### exclusivity in first person plural

to express "we/us" excluding the addressee, phi uses:

| phi           | english translation   |
| ------------- | --------------------- |
| lo mia me thi | we (but not you)      |

## special pronouns

while the core three-pronoun system covers most needs, phi also provides combinations to express 
certain specialized pronoun functions:

| phi formation | english translation    | funcrion | 
 | ------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------------- |
| thue mia      | my/mine                | possessive (1st person) |
| thue thi      | your/yours             | possessive (2nd person) |
| thue sha      | its/his/hers/theirs    | possessive (3rd person) |

The possessive forms in phi use the preposition `thue` ("of") combined with the pronoun, 
rather than dedicated possessive determiners. This creates constructions like "miwhoa thue mia" 
(friend of me) instead of "my friend". This approach maintains phi's principle of using 
consistent syntactic patterns rather than introducing new word classes, and follows the 
same possessive structure used with nouns.

| phi formation | english translation    | funcrion | 
 | ------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------------- |
| mia na mia    | mysulf | reflexive (1st person)   |
| thi na thi    | yoursulf | reflexive (2nd person)   |
| sha na sha    | itself/himself/herself | reflexive (3rd person)  |

The reflexive pronoun formations follow a systematic pattern of "[pronoun] na [same pronoun]", 
where "na" is the object marker particle. This construction literally means "[pronoun] to 
[same pronoun]" (e.g., "I to me"), explicitly showing that the subject and object are the 
same entity. Rather than introducing dedicated reflexive words, phi elegantly repurposes 
its existing pronoun system combined with the object marker to create reflexivity through 
a consistent and predictable syntactic pattern.

## pronoun system notes

phi's pronoun system is deliberately minimal to reduce learning burden while maintaining 
sufficient expressive power. the lack of gender distinction in the third person (`sha`) 
reflects phi's simplified approach to grammatical categories and avoids encoding social 
distinctions into the language's core grammar.

the pronoun forms themselves are phonologically exceptional as they do not follow the 
standard phonotactic patterns of phi. this exception is linguistically sound, as pronouns 
in many natural languages often display irregular forms due to their high frequency of use.

## remaining pronoun count

phi pronouns are one of the few closed classes in the language, with only three core forms.
there are no plans to expand this system, as it provides adequate expressive power when
combined with the language's particle system. the simplicity of the pronoun system is
a deliberate design choice that supports phi's goal of being systematic and easy to learn.
