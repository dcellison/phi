# conjunctions

> conjunctions are words that join together other words, phrases, or clauses in sentences. they can connect items of the same kind, such as two words, two phrases, or two clauses, indicating relationships like addition, contrast, choice, cause, and effect.

## introduction to phi conjunctions

all conjunctions in phi adhere to the structure `[C][V][C][V]` where:

- C = consonant (h, l, m, n, p, r, s, t, w)
- V = vowel (i, u, e, o, a)

this pattern creates words like `nene` (and), `wetu` (if), and `matu` (after), 
which consist of two syllables with a consistent consonant-vowel alternation and no 
fricative digraphs. this gives conjunctions a clear, direct sound that distinguishes 
them from other parts of speech while maintaining phi's syllable structure rules.

conjunctions serve as connectors between words, phrases, or clauses, expressing 
relationships such as addition, contrast, choice, condition, cause, and effect. 
their distinctive two-syllable pattern creates an immediate phonological cue that 
helps listeners identify connecting functions in speech.

in phi, conjunctions do not change form based on the elements they connect, maintaining 
consistent forms regardless of context. this regularity, combined with their 
distinctive phonological pattern, makes them easily identifiable in both speech and 
writing.

### correlative conjunction usage

| english example                | phi translation              |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------------------- |

| either ball or cup             | miwa mophui lote tuphai      |
| neither bag nor basket         | nopa pashia pisu pashui      |
| not only bread but also butter | watu nophai tena pashia      |
| whether brick or bottle        | moro mathe wete niphui       |
