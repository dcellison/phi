# Evidentiality System

## Six-Way Evidential Distinction

Phi's evidentiality system is one of its most sophisticated features, requiring speakers to explicitly mark the **source of their knowledge** for every assertion. This six-way distinction covers all major epistemological categories found across human languages.

## The Complete Evidential Paradigm

| Particle | Type | English Gloss | Information Source | Reliability |
|----------|------|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| `hi` | Direct Evidence | "I see/experience" | Personal observation | Highest |
| `ro` | Inference | "I conclude/deduce" | Logical reasoning | High |
| `mu` | Memory | "I remember" | Personal recollection | Moderate-High |
| `ti` | Reported Speech | "X said that" | Specific attribution | Variable |
| `nu` | Hearsay | "I heard/they say" | General sources | Low-Moderate |
| `pe` | Presumption | "I assume/expect" | General knowledge | Variable |

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. `hi` - Direct Evidence

**Function**: Speaker directly witnessed or experienced the event through sensory perception.

**Usage principles**:
- Applies to all five senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell)
- Includes internal mental states accessible only to the speaker
- Cannot be used for future events (logical impossibility)

**Validated examples**:
```
hi ne riwhea ta whuru.
DIR INAN wind PRS blow
"The wind is blowing." (I can see/feel it)

hi mia ta whemo.
DIR 1SG PRS think
"I am thinking." (direct access to my own thoughts)

hi he nowhea na he phiphea li shelu.
DIR HUM person OBJ HUM child PST teach
"A person taught a child." (I witnessed the teaching)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Turkish `-di` (witnessed past), Estonian `-nud` (perfect)

### 2. `ro` - Inference Evidentiality

**Function**: Speaker deduces information through logical reasoning from observable evidence.

**Usage principles**:
- Based on visible/audible traces or effects
- Speaker did not witness the actual event
- Logical deduction from reliable evidence

**Validated examples**:
```
ro he thephoa ta phoni.
INF HUM person PRS live
"A person lives here." (I see signs of habitation)

ro ne raoshea ta waphe phera.
INF INAN sun PRS warm be
"The sun is warm." (I can see/feel its effects)

ro sha na ne lophui li whesa.
INF 3SG OBJ INAN art PST create
"S/he created art." (I see the finished artwork)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Quechua `-si` (inferential), Georgian perfect evidentials

### 3. `mu` - Memory Evidentiality

**Function**: Speaker recalls information from personal memory.

**Usage principles**:
- Personal recollection of past events
- Acknowledges potential fallibility of memory
- Distinguished from direct evidence by temporal distance

**Validated examples**:
```
mu mia na ne whethea li whera.
MEM 1SG OBJ INAN book PST learn
"I learned from a book." (I remember this happening)

mu he nowhea li riphe phera.
MEM HUM person PST important be
"The person was important." (I remember this being the case)

mu lo mia na thueta li whisi.
MEM PL 1SG OBJ that PST see
"We saw that." (I remember us seeing it)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Some Tibetan evidentials, certain Turkish constructions

### 4. `ti` - Reported Speech Evidentiality

**Function**: Information is attributed to a specific source.

**Usage principles**:
- Explicit attribution to particular person or document
- Reliability depends on the credibility of the source
- Often used in academic/formal contexts

**Validated examples**:
```
ti he nowhea na ne whethea ta whera.
REP HUM person OBJ INAN book PRS learn
"A person learns from books." (according to the teacher)

ti ne riwhea su waphe phera.
REP INAN weather FUT warm be
"The weather will be warm." (the forecast says)

ti mia su na ne lophui whera.
REP 1SG FUT OBJ INAN art learn
"I will learn about art." (someone told me I would)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Korean quotative constructions, Japanese reporting particles

### 5. `nu` - Hearsay Evidentiality

**Function**: Information comes from general, unspecified sources.

**Usage principles**:
- Multiple transmission steps possible
- Cannot identify specific source
- Common for cultural knowledge or rumors

**Validated examples**:
```
nu he thephoa na ne lophui li whesa.
HEAR HUM person OBJ INAN art PST create
"A person created art." (people say/it's said that)

nu ne riwhea su thilu phera.
HEAR INAN weather FUT bad be
"The weather will be bad." (I heard this around)

nu thueta ne whethea riphe ta phera.
HEAR that INAN book important PRS be
"That book is important." (word is that...)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Turkish `-miş` (non-witnessed), Quechua hearsay markers

### 6. `pe` - Presumption Evidentiality

**Function**: Information based on general knowledge, expectations, or assumptions.

**Usage principles**:
- Default expectations about how things work
- Cultural or world knowledge
- Reasonable assumptions without specific evidence

**Validated examples**:
```
pe he nowhea ta whemo.
PRES HUM person PRS think
"People think." (general assumption about humans)

pe ne raoshea su phela.
PRES INAN sun FUT rise
"The sun will rise." (based on general knowledge)

pe mia na ne whethea su whera.
PRES 1SG OBJ INAN book FUT learn
"I will learn from books." (reasonable expectation)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Some epistemic modal constructions, presumptive evidentials

## Systematic Constraints

### Evidential Exclusivity Rule
**Only one evidential per clause is permitted.** Mixing evidentials creates contradictory epistemological claims.

**Incorrect**: `*hi ro ne lowhai ta riphe.` (conflicting knowledge sources)
**Correct**: `hi ne lowhai ta riphe.` OR `ro ne lowhai ta riphe.`

### Temporal Compatibility
Certain evidential-tense combinations are logically impossible:

- **`hi` + `su` (future)**: Cannot directly observe future events
- **`mu` + `ta` (present)**: Memory applies to past events

### Register Sensitivity
- **Formal contexts**: Evidentials often required for precision
- **Informal speech**: May be omitted for efficiency
- **Academic writing**: Precision highly valued

## Reliability Hierarchy

In contexts where multiple evidentials could apply, speakers typically choose based on reliability:

1. **`hi` (direct)** - Highest reliability, personal observation
2. **`mu` (memory)** - High reliability, but acknowledges temporal distance
3. **`ro` (inference)** - Moderate reliability, logical but potentially incomplete
4. **`ti` (reported)** - Variable reliability, depends on source credibility
5. **`nu` (hearsay)** - Lower reliability, multiple transmission steps
6. **`pe` (presumption)** - Variable reliability, assumptions may be incorrect

## Advanced Usage Patterns

### Evidential Chains in Discourse
In extended discourse, evidentials create epistemological coherence:

```
hi mia na ne whethea li whera. ro thueta ne whethea riphe phera.
DIR 1SG OBJ INAN book PST learn. INF that INAN book important be.
"I learned from a book (direct). That book is important (I infer)."
```

### Politeness Interactions
Evidentials interact with politeness to create nuanced social meanings:

```
so hi ne riwhea ta whuru.
POL DIR INAN wind PRS blow
"The wind is politely blowing." (formal observation)
```

### Conditional Constructions
Evidentials specify the epistemological status of both condition and consequence:

```
tu ro ne riwhea ta thilu, mia su me sharo.
COND INF INAN weather PRS bad, 1SG FUT NEG go
"If the weather is bad (I infer), I won't go."
```

## Cultural and Philosophical Implications

Phi's evidentiality system promotes:

### Intellectual Honesty
- Prevents claims to false authority
- Acknowledges limitations of knowledge
- Distinguishes certainty levels

### Precision in Communication
- Reduces ambiguity about information sources
- Enables accurate evaluation of claims
- Supports critical thinking

### Cross-Cultural Accessibility
- Draws from patterns found in many human languages
- Makes epistemological distinctions explicit
- Aids in intercultural communication
