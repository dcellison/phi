# Sentence Type Markers

## Four Core Sentence Types

Phi's sentence type system transforms the basic **communicative function** of utterances through four specialized particles. These markers determine whether a sentence is a question, exclamation, conditional statement, or expression of uncertainty.

## The Complete Paradigm

| Particle | Type | Function | English Equivalent |
|----------|------|----------|-------------------|
| `wa` | Interrogative | Questions and information requests | "?" / question words |
| `ho` | Exclamation | Emotional emphasis and surprise | "!" / interjections |
| `tu` | Conditional | Hypothetical conditions | "if" / hypotheticals |
| `hu` | Possibility | Uncertainty and speculation | "perhaps" / "maybe" |

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. `wa` - Interrogative Marker

**Function**: Transforms statements into questions, specifically for yes/no questions and confirmation requests.

**Usage principles**:
- Applied to entire propositions for yes/no questions
- Creates polite information requests when combined with `so`
- **Note**: Wh-questions use question words (`hamite`, `phiato`, `walime`, etc.) and do not require `wa` since they are already inherently interrogative

**Basic yes/no questions**:
```
wa si mia ta whera.
Q SUBJ 1SG PRS learn
"Am I learning?" / "Do I learn?"

wa ne riwhea ta whuru.
Q INAN wind PRS blow
"Is the wind blowing?"

wa he nowhea na ne whethea ta whera.
Q HUM person OBJ INAN book PRS learn
"Does the person learn from books?"
```

**Polite yes/no questions (with `so`)**:
```
so wa si mia na ne whethea ta whera.
POL Q SUBJ 1SG OBJ INAN book PRS learn
"May I ask - am I learning from books?"
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: Japanese `ka`, Mandarin `ma`, Polish `czy`

### 2. `ho` - Exclamation Marker

**Function**: Adds emotional emphasis, surprise, or strong feeling to statements.

**Usage principles**:
- Expresses speaker's emotional reaction to information
- Intensifies the emotional content of utterances
- Often combined with emphasis particles for maximum effect

**Basic exclamatory patterns**:
```
ho ne riwhea ta waphe phera.
EXCL INAN weather PRS warm be
"How warm the weather is!" / "The weather is so warm!"

ho mia na ne whethea li whera.
EXCL 1SG OBJ INAN book PST learn
"I learned from a book!" (with surprise/emphasis)

ho he nowhea riphe ta phera.
EXCL HUM person important PRS be
"What an important person!" / "How important the person is!"
```

**With emphasis particles**:
```
ho ma he nowhea riphe ta phera.
EXCL EMPH HUM person important PRS be
"What a very important person!"

ho ne riwhea ma waphe ta phera.
EXCL INAN weather EMPH warm PRS be
"How very warm the weather is!"
```

**Emotional responses**:
```
ho mia na thueta li whisi.
EXCL 1SG OBJ that PST see
"I saw that!" (with amazement/excitement)
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: English exclamation patterns, Spanish exclamatory constructions

### 3. `tu` - Conditional Marker

**Function**: Introduces hypothetical conditions and counterfactual scenarios.

**Usage principles**:
- Establishes "if-then" logical relationships
- Creates hypothetical scenarios for reasoning
- Often appears in complex conditional constructions

**Basic conditional patterns**:
```
tu ne riwhea ta thilu, mia su me sharo.
COND INAN weather PRS bad, 1SG FUT NEG go
"If the weather is bad, I won't go."

tu mia na ne whethea ta whera, mia su whemo riphe.
COND 1SG OBJ INAN book PRS learn, 1SG FUT think important
"If I learn from books, I will think it's important."

tu he nowhea ta phoni, sha su whema.
COND HUM person PRS live, 3SG FUT work
"If a person lives (there), s/he will work."
```

**Counterfactual conditions**:
```
tu mia li na ne whethea whera, mia li su riphe whemo.
COND 1SG PST OBJ INAN book learn, 1SG PST FUT important think
"If I had learned from books, I would have thought it important."
```

**Complex conditional chains**:
```
tu he nowhea ta whema, tu sha ta phoni riphe, sha su waphe phera.
COND HUM person PRS work, COND 3SG PRS live important, 3SG FUT warm be
"If a person works, if s/he lives well, s/he will be comfortable."
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: English "if", German "wenn", Mandarin "ruguo"

### 4. `hu` - Possibility Marker

**Function**: Expresses uncertainty, speculation, or tentative assertions.

**Usage principles**:
- Indicates speaker's uncertainty about the truth value
- Softens assertions to show tentativeness
- Distinguished from evidentials by focusing on certainty rather than information source
- **Quick reference**: Use `hu` when uncertain about truth ("Maybe X is true"); use evidentials when you know your information source ("X is true because I saw/heard/infer/remember")

**Basic possibility patterns**:
```
hu ne riwhea su waphe phera.
PSB INAN weather FUT warm be
"The weather might be warm." / "Perhaps the weather will be warm."

hu mia shio ne whethea su whera.
PSB 1SG from INAN book FUT learn
"I might learn from books." / "Maybe I'll learn from books."

hu he nowhea ta riphe phera.
PSB HUM person PRS important be
"The person might be important."
```

**Speculative reasoning**:
```
hu sha li na ne lophui whesa.
PSB 3SG PST OBJ INAN art create
"S/he possibly created art." (tentative conclusion)

hu lo mia su na thueta whisi.
PSB PL 1SG FUT OBJ that see
"We might see that."
```

**Polite suggestions (with `so`)**:
```
so hu mia shio lo ne whethea ta whera.
POL PSB 1SG from PL INAN book PRS learn
"Perhaps I might politely learn from books."
```

**Cross-linguistic parallels**: English "maybe/perhaps", German "vielleicht", Mandarin "keneng"

## Interaction Patterns

### Sentence Type + Evidentiality
Sentence type markers can combine with evidential information, though this requires careful discourse management:

```
wa hi ne riwhea ta whuru.
Q DIR INAN wind PRS blow
"Is the wind blowing?" (asking about something directly observable)

ho ro he nowhea riphe ta phera.
EXCL INF HUM person important PRS be
"How important the person is!" (exclaiming about an inference)
```

### Sentence Type + Politeness
The politeness marker `so` frequently combines with sentence types for social appropriateness:

```
so wa + [statement] = polite yes/no question
so ho + [statement] = polite exclamation  
so tu + [condition] = polite conditional
so hu + [statement] = polite speculation
```

### Complex Discourse Patterns
Multiple sentence types can appear in discourse sequences:

```
wa he nowhea monite ta phoni. hu sha ta whema. tu sha ta whema, sha su riphe phera.
Q HUM person there PRS live. PSB 3SG PRS work. COND 3SG PRS work, 3SG FUT important be.
"Does a person live there? Maybe s/he works. If s/he works, s/he will be important."
```

## Structural Constraints

### Exclusivity Rule
**Only one sentence type marker per clause is permitted.** Multiple sentence types create contradictory communicative functions.

**Incorrect**: `*wa ho ne riwhea ta whuru.` (question + exclamation conflict)
**Correct**: `wa ne riwhea ta whuru.` OR `ho ne riwhea ta whuru.`

### Scope Properties
Sentence type markers have **clausal scope**, applying to the entire grammatical unit:

- **Wide scope**: The sentence type applies to the complete proposition
- **Non-compositional**: Cannot be negated or modified
- **Discourse function**: Primarily communicative rather than propositional

### Register Sensitivity
- **Formal contexts**: Explicit sentence type marking preferred
- **Informal contexts**: May rely on intonation and context
- **Written communication**: Sentence type markers essential for clarity

## Semantic Relationships

### Certainty Spectrum
The sentence types create a spectrum of speaker certainty:

1. **Declarative** (unmarked): Neutral assertion
2. **`hu` (possibility)**: Tentative assertion
3. **`wa` (interrogative)**: Uncertainty seeking resolution
4. **`tu` (conditional)**: Hypothetical/counterfactual
5. **`ho` (exclamatory)**: Emphatic assertion

### Logical Relationships
Sentence types interact with logical structure:

- **`wa`**: Requests information to resolve uncertainty
- **`tu`**: Establishes logical conditions and consequences  
- **`hu`**: Qualifies truth conditions with uncertainty
- **`ho`**: Emphasizes already-established information

## Pedagogical Recommendations

### Learning Sequence
1. **Start with `wa`**: Most frequent and essential for basic communication
2. **Add `ho`**: Emotional expression and emphasis
3. **Master `tu`**: Logical reasoning and complex thinking
4. **Refine with `hu`**: Nuanced uncertainty and politeness

### Practice Patterns
- **Question-answer pairs**: Systematic `wa` practice
- **Emotional responses**: Natural `ho` usage contexts
- **Logical reasoning**: `tu` in argumentative contexts
- **Tentative discussion**: `hu` for speculation and politeness
