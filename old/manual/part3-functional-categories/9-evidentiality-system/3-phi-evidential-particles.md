# Phi's Evidential Particles

## Six-Way Evidential System

Phi employs a **six-way evidential distinction** that covers the major ways humans acquire knowledge:

| Particle | Type | English Equivalent | Source of Knowledge |
|----------|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| `hi` | Direct Evidence | "I saw/experienced" | Direct sensory experience |
| `ro` | Inference | "I conclude/deduce" | Logical reasoning from evidence |
| `nu` | Hearsay | "I heard that" | Second-hand information |
| `ti` | Reported Speech | "X said that" | Attributed to specific source |
| `mu` | Memory | "I remember" | Personal recollection |
| `pe` | Presumption | "I assume/expect" | General knowledge/assumption |

## Detailed Analysis of Each Evidential

### 1. `hi` - Direct Evidence Marker

**Use**: Speaker directly witnessed or experienced the event

**Validated Examples**:
```
hi ne riwhea ta whuru.
DIR inanimate wind present blow
"The wind is blowing." (I can see/feel it directly)

hi mia ta whemo.
DIR I present think
"I am thinking." (I have direct access to my own thoughts)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: Similar to Turkish `-di` (witnessed past)

**Usage Notes**:
- Used for events the speaker directly perceives through any of the five senses
- Includes internal mental states (thoughts, emotions) that only the speaker can access
- Highest reliability level in typical contexts

### 2. `ro` - Inference Evidentiality

**Use**: Speaker deduces the information from available evidence

**Validated Examples**:
```
ro he thephoa ta phoni.
INF human person present live
"A person lives." (I can see signs of habitation)

ro ne raoshea ta waphe phera.
INF inanimate sun present warm is
"The sun is warm." (I can see its effects, feel the heat)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: Similar to Quechua `-si` (inferential)

**Usage Notes**:
- Based on logical reasoning from observable evidence
- Speaker has not directly witnessed the event itself
- Evidence may be physical traces, logical connections, or patterns

### 3. `nu` - Hearsay Evidentiality

**Use**: Speaker heard the information from others (general hearsay)

**Validated Examples**:
```
nu he thephoa na ne lophui li whesa.
HEAR human person object inanimate art past create
"A person created art." (I heard this from various people)

nu he thephoa ta whemo.
HEAR human person present think
"A person thinks." (People told me this about someone)

nu sha na ne noshea li theso.
HEAR they object inanimate food past cook
"They cooked food." (I heard this happened)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: Similar to Quechua `-shi` (hearsay)

**Usage Notes**:
- Information obtained from multiple or unspecified sources
- Speaker cannot or does not want to identify specific sources
- Lower reliability due to potential transmission errors

### 4. `ti` - Reported Speech Evidentiality

**Use**: Speaker attributes information to a specific source

**Validated Examples**:
```
ti he nowhea li shuso mia ta whera.
REP human teacher past say I present learn
"The teacher said I am learning." (Direct quotation/report)

ti mia li shuso thi ta whera.
REP I past say you present learn
"I said you are learning." (Reporting my own past statement)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: More specific than general hearsay systems

**Usage Notes**:
- Attribution to a particular person, document, or source
- Can be direct quotation or paraphrase
- Speaker takes responsibility for accurate reporting but not for truth of content

### 5. `mu` - Memory Evidentiality

**Use**: Speaker recalls the information from personal memory

**Validated Examples**:
```
mu mia na ne raoshea li whona.
MEM I object inanimate sun past look
"I looked at the sun." (I remember doing this)

mu he phiphea li whera.
MEM human child past learn
"The child learned." (I remember witnessing this)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: Rare cross-linguistically, found in some Amazonian languages

**Usage Notes**:
- Speaker previously had direct knowledge but is now recalling it
- Acknowledges potential fallibility of memory
- Distinguishes between current direct knowledge and remembered knowledge

### 6. `pe` - Presumption Evidentiality

**Use**: Speaker makes assumptions based on general knowledge or expectations

**Validated Examples**:
```
pe ne lashea su whale.
PRES inanimate rain future flow
"It will rain." (Based on weather patterns/expectations)

pe pi mathai su whuwe.
PRES animate cat future sleep
"The cat will sleep." (This is what cats typically do)
```

**Cross-linguistic comparison**: Similar to German modal particles like `wohl`

**Usage Notes**:
- Based on general world knowledge, cultural expectations, or typical patterns
- Often used for predictions about future events
- Acknowledges that assumptions may be wrong

## Semantic Distinctions

### Direct vs. Indirect Evidence

- **Direct** (`hi`): Sensory experience of the event itself
- **Indirect** (`ro`, `nu`, `ti`, `mu`, `pe`): Information obtained through other means

### Source Specificity

- **Specific source** (`ti`): Attributed to particular person/document  
- **General source** (`nu`): Unspecified people/sources
- **No external source** (`hi`, `ro`, `mu`, `pe`): Speaker's own experience/reasoning

### Reliability Hierarchy

From most to least reliable in typical contexts:
1. `hi` (direct) - highest reliability
2. `mu` (memory) - personal but potentially fallible  
3. `ro` (inference) - logical but potentially incomplete
4. `ti` (reported) - depends on source reliability
5. `nu` (hearsay) - multiple transmission steps
6. `pe` (presumption) - assumptions may be wrong

## Comparison with English Approximations

| Phi | English Equivalents |
|-----|-------------------|
| `hi` | "I see/know firsthand" |
| `ro` | "I conclude/deduce/figure" |
| `nu` | "I hear/they say" |
| `ti` | "According to X" |
| `mu` | "I recall/remember" |
| `pe` | "I assume/expect/suppose" |
