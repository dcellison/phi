# Philosophical Audit of Phi Vocabulary

## Evaluation Criteria
1. **Sound-Meaning Correspondence**: Do the phonetics embody the concept?
2. **Pillar Alignment**: Does it reflect its philosophical foundations?
3. **Memorability**: Will speakers feel the meaning in the sound?
4. **Speaking as Practice**: Does pronunciation cultivate mindfulness?
5. **Language Independence**: Is it phonetically distinct from English (and other major languages)?

## Categories
- **KEEP**: Word perfectly embodies its meaning through sound
- **REVISE**: Good foundation but needs adjustment
- **REPLACE**: Fundamental mismatch between sound and meaning

## Pattern Analysis

### Initial Consonant Distribution (191 words)
- 'sh' words: 28 (15%) - Often used for soft/gentle concepts
- 'th' words: 38 (20%) - Often used for thoughtful/mindful concepts  
- 'ph' words: 22 (12%) - Often used for gentle/giving concepts
- 'wh' words: 8 (4%) - Underutilized
- 'k' words: 14 (7%) - Often too sharp for the concepts
- 'w' words: 25 (13%) - Good for communal/together concepts

### Emerging Issues
1. **Overuse of certain patterns**: Too many 'th' and 'sh' beginnings
2. **Sound confusion**: Similar-sounding words for different concepts
3. **Missed opportunities**: Some words don't embody their meanings
4. **Length mismatches**: Important concepts sometimes too short

### Initial Statistics (30 words analyzed)
- **KEEP**: 15 words (50%)
- **REVISE**: 10 words (33%)
- **REPLACE**: 5 words (17%)

### Strong Patterns in KEEP Category
- Words beginning with 'we' for communal concepts
- Thoughtful 'th' for mindful actions
- Flowing liquids 'l' and 'r' for movement/connection
- Open vowel endings for spacious concepts
- Sound journeys that mirror meaning

### Common Issues in REVISE/REPLACE
- Sharp consonants ('k') for soft concepts
- Generic sounds that could mean anything
- Too short for important concepts
- Missing energy/character of the concept
- No family resemblance between related words
